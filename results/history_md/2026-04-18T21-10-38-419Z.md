## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Are Worth Fighting For

## A 55-Minute Middle School ELA Lesson

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Core Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Embedded Skill:** Curiosity — cultivating the habit of asking deeper questions, exploring the unknown, and valuing the pursuit of understanding
**Essential Question:** *What happens when we stop asking "why?" — and what happens when we don't?*

---

## MATERIALS NEEDED

- Whiteboard or projector
- Printed copies of the "Curiosity Case Files" (one per student — described below)
- Sticky notes (two colors per student — e.g., yellow and pink)
- Student writer's notebooks or lined paper
- Pens/pencils
- Timer (visible to students)
- Optional: short video clip (suggested: 90-second NASA clip about the Mars Curiosity Rover, or a clip of a scientist explaining why they pursued their field)

---

## STANDARDS ALIGNMENT

| Standard | How It's Addressed |
|---|---|
| CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 | Students construct a written argument with a claim, reasons, evidence, counterargument, and rebuttal |
| CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1a | Students introduce a claim and distinguish it from opposing claims |
| CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1b | Students support claims with logical reasoning and relevant evidence |
| CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1c | Students use transitional phrases to create cohesion |
| CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1e | Students provide a concluding statement |

---

## LESSON OBJECTIVES

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. **Define** curiosity and explain why it matters in academic and real-world contexts
2. **Identify** the components of an effective argumentative piece of writing (claim, reasons, evidence, counterargument, rebuttal, conclusion)
3. **Draft** a structured argumentative paragraph or short essay arguing *for* the value of curiosity
4. **Practice** the habit of curiosity by generating genuine questions throughout the lesson

---

## TEACHER PREPARATION NOTES

Before class, prepare the **"Curiosity Case Files"** — a one-page handout containing three short scenarios (approximately 3–4 sentences each). These scenarios should show moments where curiosity either changed or could have changed an outcome. Sample scenarios are written into the lesson below. You may print these directly or adapt them.

Also prepare the **Argument Framework Anchor Chart** on the board or projector before students arrive:

```
CLAIM → REASON → EVIDENCE → COUNTERARGUMENT → REBUTTAL → CONCLUSION
```

---

**[0:00 – 0:03] — The Sticky Note Storm**

As students walk in, they find two sticky notes on their desks — one yellow, one pink.

Written on the board:

> **Yellow sticky note:** Write ONE thing you are genuinely curious about. It can be about anything — science, people, history, your own life, the universe. Anything.
>
> **Pink sticky note:** Write ONE question you've always been afraid to ask — either because you thought it was a "dumb" question or because you weren't sure anyone could answer it.

Give students 2–3 minutes to write silently. No wrong answers. No names required on the sticky notes.

**[0:03 – 0:06] — Gallery Wall**

Ask students to walk up and place their sticky notes on designated sections of the board — yellow on the left wall section, pink on the right.

Read a few aloud yourself (without identifying students). Let the room absorb the variety and depth of the questions.

Say something like:

> *"Look at this. Look at how many real, fascinating, important questions are in this room right now. Every single one of these questions is worth pursuing. And yet — most school days, we never ask them. Today, we're going to argue that this is a problem worth fixing."*

**[0:06 – 0:08] — Opening Provocation**

Pose this question to the class and let students briefly turn and talk for 60 seconds with a neighbor:

> **"Is curiosity a choice? Or is it something that just happens to you?"**

Take 2–3 quick shares. Don't resolve the debate — let it simmer.

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 2: BUILD THE KNOWLEDGE BASE — "Curiosity Case Files"

**CURIOSITY CASE FILES (Handout Text — Print or Project)**

---

**Case File #1: The Boy Who Lit Up the World**

When Nikola Tesla was a young student in Croatia, his teachers thought he was disruptive. He constantly asked "but why?" and "what if?" during lectures. His professors often found him annoying. But Tesla refused to simply memorize what he was told. Instead, he kept asking questions — what if electricity could travel through the air? What if machines could run without wires? His relentless curiosity led to the invention of alternating current electricity, which is how your home, school, and phone are powered today. Many historians argue that Tesla's curiosity literally changed the modern world.

**Questions to consider:** What might have happened if Tesla stopped asking questions? Who told him to stop — and why? Was his curiosity easy or uncomfortable for the people around him?

---

**Case File #2: The Classroom That Stopped Asking**

A middle school teacher in Ohio conducted an experiment. For one week, she tracked how often her students asked questions during class — not answering questions, but *asking* them unprompted. The average? Three questions per student for the entire week. She then watched kindergartners for a week. The average? Over twenty questions *per hour*. She wrote in her notes: "Somewhere between kindergarten and seventh grade, we teach children that questions are interruptions, not contributions."

**Questions to consider:** What do you think causes this change? Is this a problem? Who is responsible for fixing it?

---

**Case File #3: The Doctor Who Saved Millions by Being Nosy**

In the 1840s, a Hungarian doctor named Ignaz Semmelweis noticed something that everyone else had ignored: women were dying at much higher rates in hospitals where doctors delivered babies than in clinics where midwives did. Instead of accepting this as "just the way things are," Semmelweis became obsessed with figuring out *why*. He asked question after question, followed the data, and eventually discovered that doctors were transmitting germs from autopsies to patients — before anyone even knew germs existed. He required doctors to wash their hands. Death rates dropped dramatically. The medical establishment mocked him. He was eventually fired. But his curiosity — his refusal to accept unexplained suffering — saved millions of lives.

**Questions to consider:** What would have happened if Semmelweis had just accepted "the way things are"? Why did people resist his curious questioning? What does this tell us about the cost of curiosity — and the cost of its absence?

**[0:10 – 0:16] — Reading and Annotation**

Students read independently. As they read, they use the following annotation symbols (written on the board):

- **!** = This surprises me
- **?** = This makes me want to know more
- **★** = This could be evidence in an argument

Give students 5–6 minutes to read and annotate.

**[0:16 – 0:20] — Whole-Class Discussion: What Did Curiosity Do?**

Facilitate a quick discussion using these questions:

1. *"What patterns did you notice across the three case files?"*
2. *"What did curiosity cost these people — and what did it gain them?"*
3. *"If you had to argue that curiosity matters, which case file gives you the strongest evidence? Why?"*

Guide students toward recognizing: curiosity is not passive — it is a choice, sometimes a brave one, and it produces real consequences in the world.

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 3: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — "The Architecture of an Argument"
**Time: 0:20 – 0:30 (10 minutes)**

---

**[0:20 – 0:23] — Activate Prior Knowledge**

Ask students:

> *"Has anyone ever had to convince someone of something — a parent, a friend, a teacher? What did you do to make your argument work?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Students will likely mention: giving reasons, using examples, admitting the other person had a point.

Say:

> *"You already know how to argue. Today we're going to formalize what you already do — and apply it to something you now actually care about: curiosity."*

**[0:23 – 0:28] — Introduce the Argument Framework**

Reveal or point to the anchor chart:

```
CLAIM → REASON → EVIDENCE → COUNTERARGUMENT → REBUTTAL → CONCLUSION
```

Walk through each component using a non-threatening, relatable example first — then connect it to the curiosity topic:

---

**CLAIM**
*What you believe to be true — your position.*

**Everyday example:** "School should start later."
**Today's example:** "Curiosity is one of the most important skills students can develop."

> *"A claim is not a fact. It's a position. It's something someone could disagree with — which is exactly why you have to argue for it."*

---

**REASON**
*Why your claim is true — the logical backbone.*

**Example:** "Curiosity leads to deeper learning and real-world problem-solving."

> *"Reasons are the 'because' behind your claim. They explain the thinking."*

---

**EVIDENCE**
*The proof — facts, examples, quotes, data.*

**Example:** "Ignaz Semmelweis's curiosity about unexplained patient deaths directly led to the discovery of germ transmission, saving millions of lives."

> *"Evidence is what turns opinion into argument. Without it, you're just stating what you think. With it, you're making a case."*

---

**COUNTERARGUMENT**
*The other side — what someone who disagrees would say.*

**Example:** "Some might argue that curiosity is a distraction in school — that students should focus on learning established knowledge rather than chasing questions."

> *"This is the part most writers skip — and it's actually the most powerful part. When you bring up the other side, you show that you've thought carefully and you're not afraid of disagreement. It makes you sound smarter, not weaker."*

---

**REBUTTAL**
*Your response to the counterargument — why your position still holds.*

**Example:** "However, established knowledge was built entirely by curious people. Eliminating curiosity from education would be like removing the engine from a car and expecting it to still move."

---

**CONCLUSION**
*The "so what" — why this matters.*

**Example:** "Schools that nurture curiosity don't just teach students what to think — they teach students how to think, preparing them for a world full of problems that don't yet have answers."

---

**[0:28 – 0:30] — Quick Check for Understanding**

Give students 90 seconds to turn to a partner and explain: *"What is the difference between a reason and evidence? Use an example."*

Take one or two shares. Clarify as needed.

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 4: GUIDED PRACTICE — "Building the Argument Together"
**Time: 0:30 – 0:38 (8 minutes)**

---

**[0:30 – 0:33] — Whole-Class Claim Crafting**

Write the following two claim options on the board. Ask students to vote (raise hand) for which one they find more compelling, and briefly explain why:

> **Option A:** "Schools should actively teach curiosity as a skill, not just a personality trait."
>
> **Option B:** "Curiosity is essential to academic success and should be encouraged in every classroom."

After a brief 60-second vote and discussion, tell students:

> *"For our practice today, we're going to use this claim: 'Schools should actively teach and reward curiosity because it leads to deeper learning, innovation, and real-world problem solving.'"*

Write this on the board. This becomes their shared claim.

**[0:33 – 0:38] — Collaborative Framework Building**

As a class, work together to fill in the argument framework. The teacher facilitates while students contribute. Write all responses on the board.

Use guiding prompts:

- *"What's a reason that supports this claim? Think about the case files."*
- *"Which case file gives us the best evidence? How would we quote or reference it?"*
- *"Who would disagree with this claim? What would they say? Let's steelman the opposition — make their argument as strong as possible."*
- *"How do we respond to that counterargument without dismissing it?"*
- *"What's the big-picture reason this all matters?"*

By the end of this segment, the board should have a collaborative skeleton of an argument that students can reference during independent writing.

**Model language and transitions** by interjecting phrases as students offer ideas:

- "For example, as shown in Case File #2..."
- "While some may argue that..."
- "However, this overlooks the fact that..."
- "Ultimately, the evidence suggests..."

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments (55 minutes)**

**Grade:** Middle School (7th Grade)  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.  
**Topic:** Curiosity — What it is, why it matters, and how it fuels strong arguments

---

### Objectives:
- Students will understand the concept of curiosity and its importance in learning and critical thinking.
- Students will practice writing an argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence.
- Students will learn to acknowledge and address opposing viewpoints in their writing.
- Students will organize their reasoning logically.

---

### Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Projector or chart paper (optional)  
- Handout with a short text about curiosity (definition, benefits, examples)  
- Writing paper or notebooks  
- Pens/pencils

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *What does curiosity mean to you?*  
- Write their ideas on the board.  
- Share a brief definition: *Curiosity is a strong desire to learn or know something.*  
- Discuss why curiosity is important: it helps us discover new things, ask questions, and think deeply.  
- Give examples of curiosity in everyday life and famous discoveries (e.g., scientists, inventors).  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives good questions and strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us gather reasons and evidence to support our opinions.  
- When we are curious, we explore different sides of an issue, which helps us understand opposing views.  
- This makes our arguments stronger and more convincing.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Review the components of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - Clear claim (your opinion or position)  
  - Reasons supporting the claim  
  - Evidence or examples backing up each reason  
  - Acknowledgment of an opposing claim  
  - Logical organization of ideas  
- Use a simple example on the board:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* Studies show students feel safer and more equal.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Response:* However, students can express themselves through other ways like hairstyles or accessories.

**4. Guided Practice: Writing an Argument About Curiosity (15 minutes)**  
- Present the prompt: *“Curiosity is the most important quality for students to have.” Do you agree or disagree? Write a paragraph supporting your claim.*  
- Distribute the handout with a short text about curiosity to help students gather ideas and evidence.  
- As a class, brainstorm reasons for both agreeing and disagreeing with the statement. Write these on the board.  
- Model writing a claim and one reason with evidence on the board.  
- Students begin writing their own argumentative paragraph, including:  
  - Their claim (agree or disagree)  
  - At least two reasons with evidence  
  - One opposing viewpoint and a response to it

**5. Sharing and Peer Feedback (10 minutes)**  
- Pair students to read each other’s paragraphs.  
- Provide a simple checklist for feedback:  
  - Is the claim clear?  
  - Are there reasons and evidence?  
  - Is an opposing view acknowledged?  
  - Is the argument organized logically?  
- Students give one positive comment and one suggestion for improvement.

**6. Wrap-Up and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Ask a few volunteers to share their claims and one reason.  
- Discuss how curiosity helped them think about different sides of the argument.  
- Reinforce that being curious helps us become better writers and thinkers.  
- Assign a short homework: Think of a question you are curious about and write down three reasons why you want to learn more about it.

---

### Extension Ideas:
- Have students research their curiosity question and write a longer argumentative essay.  
- Create a “Curiosity Wall” in the classroom where students post questions they want to explore.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with the practice of writing clear, well-supported arguments, meeting the CCSS writing standard in an engaging and meaningful way.
