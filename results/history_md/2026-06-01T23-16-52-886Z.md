## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with 4 real-world scenarios (smoothie recipes, car travel, phone plans, paint mixing), exit ticket.

---

### 1. Warm-Up & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a*
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A store sells 3 apples for $2. How much for 12 apples?"
- **Scenario B:** "A baby is 20 inches at birth. How tall at age 10?"

**Activity:** Students turn-and-talk: "Which scenario is proportional? What information do we need to clarify?"

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Circulate and listen for students who precisely articulate what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through origin). Call on 2–3 students to clarify reasoning aloud. Note whether students restate problem details with precision vs. vague generalizations.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Organizing Information (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b*
Introduce proportional relationships using a smoothie recipe (2 cups berries : 3 cups yogurt). Build a **four-representation chart** on the board:

| Table | Equation | Graph | Verbal |
|-------|----------|-------|--------|

Fill in each section together, showing how y = kx connects every representation, with k = 3/2.

**Activity:** Students copy the organizer into notebooks and add a second example (4 tickets : $20).

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks at end of class. Look for whether students arranged the four representations in a logical, connected structure (e.g., showing the constant of proportionality consistently across all four). Score 2 = clear logical flow; 1 = basic but inconsistent organization.

---

### 3. Source Investigation & Assessing Validity (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a and CRI2b*
Distribute a handout showing **three "advertisements"** for phone plans:
- Ad 1: "Only $0.10/minute!" (with fine print)
- Ad 2: A table showing minutes vs. cost
- Ad 3: A graph with no labeled axes

**Activity:** In pairs, students decide: Which source gives the most reliable information for determining if the plan is proportional? Why?

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Listen for students cross-checking the ad's claim against the table values (does $0.10 × minutes match the table?). Strong students will verify across all three sources.

**Assessment of CRI2b:** In pair shares, note students who identify Ad 3 as low-quality (missing labels) and Ad 2 as high-quality (verifiable data). Document on a quick checklist.

---

### 4. Pros and Cons Activity & Sound Reasoning (15 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a and CRI4a*
**Scenario:** "You need to choose between two paint mixing ratios for an art project. Brand A uses 2 oz red : 5 oz white. Brand B claims '40% red paint.' Which gives a more consistent pink color across batches?"

**Activity:** Small groups (4 students) must:
1. Create a table and equation for each option
2. List pros and cons of each
3. Decide which is truly proportional and justify

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Collect group worksheets. Look for systematic trade-off analysis: cost, consistency, ease of scaling. Level 2 work compares both quantitatively (showing Brand A = 2/7 ≈ 28.6% red, not matching Brand B's 40%) and considers long-term implications (large batches).

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Evaluate written justifications. Level 2 reasoning anticipates complexities like rounding errors or batch size effects. Level 1 reasoning states a choice with partial logic. Level -1 makes unsupported claims (e.g., "Brand B is better because percentages are easier").

---

### 5. Reflection & Metacognition (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a*
**Exit Ticket prompt:**
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x + 2 proportional? Explain."
2. **Reflection question:** "Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What made you change your mind? What strategy will you use next time you see a proportional relationship problem?"

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read exit tickets after class. Level 2 responses show deep metacognitive awareness ("I first thought any equation with x was proportional, but I realized the +2 means it doesn't pass through the origin, so now I'll always check the y-intercept first"). Level 1 mentions reasoning without depth ("I changed my mind because my partner explained it"). Level -1 shows no reflection or repeats earlier errors.

---

### 6. Closure (4 minutes)
Recap the key insight: proportional relationships have a constant ratio (k), pass through the origin, and can be represented in five connected ways. Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying k from graphs.

---

## Summary of Critical Thinking Assessment Tools
- **Observation notes** during warm-up (CRI1a)
- **Notebook check** for organizer (CRI1b)
- **Pair-share checklist** during ad analysis (CRI2a, CRI2b)
- **Group worksheet rubric** for paint task (CRI3a, CRI4a)
- **Exit ticket reflection** (CRI5a)

Each subcompetency is scored on the -1 to 2 scale, with cited evidence from student work or recorded verbal contributions, providing a complete critical thinking profile aligned with the math standard.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportional Relationships: Connecting Tables, Graphs, Equations, and Words
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will recognize, represent, and translate proportional relationships across verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations. Throughout the lesson, students will apply critical thinking to organize data, validate mathematical claims, weigh representational choices, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Hook (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents a messy, confusing verbal description on the board: *"So, I went to the store, and I think I paid $15 for 3 pounds of apples, but my receipt was crumpled. Later, I bought 5 pounds and it cost $25. I need to know how much 10 pounds cost, but I don't know the rule."* 

**Targeting CRI1a: Clarifying information**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Translation Time." Students are tasked with stripping away the irrelevant narrative and rewriting the scenario into a precise, clear mathematical statement that identifies the core quantities (cost and pounds) and the unit rate. 
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will circulate and observe students' whiteboards, looking for students who successfully clarify the messy text into a precise statement (e.g., "The apples cost $5 per 1 pound") to support deeper mathematical understanding.

### **Part 2: Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher demonstrates how to take the clarified apple data and represent it in a table, a coordinate graph, and an equation ($y = 5x$). 

**Targeting CRI1b: Organizing information**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Proportionality Graphic Organizer." Students take a new set of raw, out-of-order data points about a car's speed (e.g., 2 hours for 120 miles, 1 hour for 60 miles, 4 hours for 240 miles) and arrange them into a logically structured, sequential input/output table, followed by a properly scaled graph.
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will assess student organizers to ensure they have arranged the x-values sequentially (1, 2, 4) to strengthen the logical flow of the data, making it easier to identify the constant of proportionality.

**Targeting CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Fact-Check the Math." The teacher introduces a fictional student's claim: *"A car traveling 50 miles in 1 hour and 120 miles in 2 hours is a proportional relationship."* Students must rigorously cross-check this claim using multiple "authoritative sources" in math: building a table, plotting a graph, and checking for a single constant equation ($y = kx$). 
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will observe whether students rely on just a quick glance or if they systematically cross-check the data against the graph (not a straight line through the origin) and the table (no constant multiplier) to invalidate the fictional student's claim.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Practice (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students work in small groups on a "Real-World Proportions" task. They are given a packet of mixed information about a local pizza shop's pricing. 

**Targeting CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Data Sorting." The packet includes high-quality, authoritative data (the official store menu) and low-quality, unreliable data (a Yelp review saying "I think I paid $20 for 2 pizzas," and a friend's text message with a typo). Students must demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting only the authoritative source (the menu) to build their mathematical models.
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will review group worksheets to ensure students explicitly discarded the hearsay and irrelevant text messages, using only the verified menu prices to calculate their constant of proportionality.

**Targeting CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Choose Your Representation." Groups are asked to predict the cost of 150 pizzas for a school event. They must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using a table, a graph, or an equation to find this specific answer, considering the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table to 150 is time-consuming; a graph requires massive paper; an equation is fast but abstract).
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will read the groups' written justifications to ensure they didn't just pick a method randomly, but systematically evaluated the pros and cons of all three alternatives before deciding that the equation ($y = kx$) is the most efficient choice for large inputs.

### **Part 4: Independent Practice (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a final scenario: A streaming service charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $5 per month. 

**Targeting CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
*   **Specific Activity:** "The Cell Phone Plan Decision." Students must decide if this relationship is proportional and write a paragraph justifying their conclusion. They must anticipate complexities, specifically the presence of the $10 initial fee.
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will evaluate the written responses for sophisticated reasoning. A successful student will logically explain that while the rate of change is constant ($5/month), the starting fee means the graph will not pass through the origin (0,0), and therefore the ratio of $y/x$ is not constant, making it non-proportional.

### **Part 5: Closure & Metacognition (5 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Lesson wrap-up and Exit Ticket.

**Targeting CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
*   **Specific Activity:** "Metacognitive Minute." On their exit ticket, students respond to the prompt: *"Think about a time today when you thought a relationship was proportional, but it wasn't. How did you adjust your thinking process to catch that error?"*
*   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. High-level responses will demonstrate self-awareness (e.g., "I initially assumed the streaming service was proportional because it went up by $5 every time, but I realized I needed to adjust my thinking to always check what happens at zero months. I changed my strategy to always check for (0,0) first.").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal explanations.

### Materials
Projector/board, graph paper, calculators, “Proportional or Not?” task cards, exit ticket, chart paper or notebook paper.

---

## Lesson Plan

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Deal Is Better? — 5 minutes
Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $6  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $9  

Ask students: “Which deal has the better price per bar? How do you know?” Students solve independently, then share with a partner.

Teacher highlights that proportional relationships have a constant ratio or unit rate. For example, Deal A is $2 per bar, and Deal B is $1.80 per bar.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify what the quantities are: number of bars and total cost.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Clarifies ideas precisely to support deeper understanding.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Listen for or collect written statements such as: “The two quantities are bars and dollars. I divided dollars by bars to find the cost for 1 bar.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Introduce three tests for proportional relationships:

1. **Table:** The ratio between quantities is constant.  
2. **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin.  
3. **Equation:** The relationship can be written as \( y = kx \), where \( k \) is the constant of proportionality.

Example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |
| 4 | 48 |

Ask students to find the unit rate: \( 12 \). Write the equation:  
\[
y = 12x
\]

Graph the points and show that the line goes through \((0,0)\).

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 15 |
| 2 | 25 |
| 3 | 35 |
| 4 | 45 |

Discuss why this is not proportional: the rate of change may look constant, but it does not fit \( y = kx \), and the graph would not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students create a three-column note organizer: table test, graph test, equation test.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Arranges information to strengthen logical flow.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Student notes should show organized comparisons, such as: “Table: same ratio. Graph: straight line through origin. Equation: \( y = kx \).”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 10 minutes
Give students this verbal description:

“A cyclist travels 18 miles in 2 hours at a constant speed.”

As a class, represent the situation in five ways:

1. **Verbal:** The cyclist travels 9 miles each hour.  
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 9 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 27 |

3. **Equation:** \( y = 9x \)  
4. **Graph:** Points form a straight line through the origin.  
5. **Diagram:** A double number line showing hours and miles.

Ask: “How can we check that all five representations match?”

Students should explain that each representation shows the same unit rate of 9 miles per hour.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check the verbal description, table, graph, equation, and diagram.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Rigorously cross-checks information using multiple sources/representations.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Look for statements like: “The equation \( y = 9x \) matches the table because when \( x = 2 \), \( y = 18 \), and the graph also includes \((2,18)\).”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes
Students work in pairs with four task cards. Each card gives a relationship in a different form.

Example cards:

**Card 1: Table**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |

**Card 2: Graph**  
A line passing through \((0,2)\), \((1,4)\), and \((2,6)\).

**Card 3: Verbal**  
“A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.”

**Card 4: Diagram**  
A double number line showing 3 notebooks cost $7.50, 6 notebooks cost $15, and 9 notebooks cost $22.50.

For each card, students answer:

1. Is it proportional?  
2. What evidence supports your answer?  
3. If proportional, write the equation.  
4. Which representation helped you decide most clearly?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose the most useful information from each representation, such as unit rate, origin, or constant ratio.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Selects high-quality, relevant mathematical evidence.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Student writing may say: “The starting fee makes the taxi relationship not proportional because proportional graphs must pass through \((0,0)\).”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare which representation—table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description—is most helpful for deciding proportionality.  
- **Target Level:** 1–2, depending on depth.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** A level 2 response may state: “The graph is fastest for seeing the origin, but the table is better for checking exact ratios. I used both because the graph alone could be hard to read.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion: Defend Your Reasoning — 7 minutes
Invite pairs to share one card. Ask students to agree, disagree, or add evidence.

Discussion prompts:
- “What makes you sure?”
- “Can another representation confirm your answer?”
- “What mistake might someone make here?”
- “How would you revise your explanation to be clearer?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students defend whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical reasoning.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Demonstrates strong reasoning and anticipates possible errors.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Listen for explanations such as: “Even though the taxi cost increases by $2 each mile, it is not proportional because there is a $4 starting value, so the equation is \( y = 2x + 4 \), not \( y = kx \).”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 8 minutes
Students complete the following individually:

**Exit Ticket**

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

Is the relationship proportional? Explain using at least two representations.

2. Write the equation.

3. Reflection: “What strategy helped you decide? What did you check to avoid mistakes?”

Expected answer: Yes, it is proportional. The constant ratio is 4, the equation is \( y = 4x \), and the graph would be a line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students explain their strategy and how they checked their reasoning.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Demonstrates metacognitive awareness and adjusts thinking.  
- **Assessment Evidence:** Look for reflections such as: “At first I only checked that y increased by 4, but then I remembered that proportional relationships need \( y = kx \), so I checked the ratio and the origin.”

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Three Tests for Proportionality (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b*
Introduce three tests: (1) **Table** — constant ratio; (2) **Graph** — straight line through origin; (3) **Equation** — fits y = kx.

Build a four-representation organizer on the board using a smoothie recipe (2 cups berries : 3 cups yogurt), showing how y = (3/2)x connects all representations.

**Activity:** Students copy a three-column organizer (table test, graph test, equation test) into notebooks and add a second example (4 tickets : $20).

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks. Look for logical arrangement where the constant of proportionality appears consistently across all representations. Level 2 = clear logical flow; Level 1 = basic but inconsistent organization.

---

### 3. Fact-Check the Math (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a*
Present a fictional student's claim: *"A car traveling 50 miles in 1 hour and 120 miles in 2 hours is a proportional relationship."*

**Activity:** In pairs, students cross-check the claim using multiple representations — building a table, plotting points, and checking for a constant y = kx.

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Listen for systematic cross-checking. Strong students verify across table (no constant multiplier: 50 vs. 60), graph (not through origin if extended), and equation. Level 2 students rigorously check all three; Level 1 students rely on one quick check.

---

### 4. Pizza Shop Data Sorting (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2b and CRI3a*
Distribute a packet about a pizza shop containing: the official menu (authoritative), a Yelp review ("I think I paid $20 for 2 pizzas"), and a friend's text with a typo.

**Activity:** Groups must (1) select only authoritative data to build a proportional model, then (2) predict the cost of 150 pizzas, weighing trade-offs of using a table, graph, or equation.

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Review worksheets to confirm students discarded hearsay and used only verified menu prices. Level 2 = explicit justification for source choice.

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Read written justifications. Level 2 students systematically evaluate all three representations (extending a table to 150 is tedious; a graph needs huge paper; the equation y = kx is fastest but abstract) before choosing.

---

### 5. Sound Reasoning: The Streaming Plan (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI4a*
**Scenario:** A streaming service charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $5 per month.

**Activity:** Students individually decide whether this is proportional and write a paragraph justifying their answer, anticipating the complication of the $10 fee.

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Evaluate written responses. Level 2 reasoning explains that although the rate ($5/month) is constant, the $10 fee means the graph does not pass through (0,0) and y/x is not constant, so the equation is y = 5x + 10, not y = kx. Level 1 states a partial answer; Level -1 makes unsupported claims.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Metacognition (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a*
**Exit Ticket:**
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x + 2 proportional? Explain using at least two representations."
2. **Reflection:** "Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What strategy will you use next time to check whether a relationship is proportional?"

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read after class. Level 2 responses show metacognitive awareness: *"I first thought any equation with x was proportional, but the +2 means it doesn't pass through the origin, so now I'll always check the y-intercept first."* Level 1 mentions reasoning without depth; Level -1 shows no reflection.
