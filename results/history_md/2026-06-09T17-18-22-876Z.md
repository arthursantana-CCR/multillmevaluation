## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A smoothie shop charges $5 for the first smoothie and $2 for each additional one."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner for 2 minutes, then share aloud.

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students must clearly articulate what "proportional" means and identify the relevant features (constant ratio, passes through origin).
**Assessment:** Listen for student explanations during pair-share. Look for precise language like "the ratio stays the same" vs. vague responses. Record observations on a clipboard rubric (Level 1 = partial clarity; Level 2 = precise clarification with reference to constant ratio).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the five ways to represent proportional relationships using Scenario A:
- **Table:** 1 smoothie → $3, 2 → $6, 3 → $9
- **Graph:** Plot points; note straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x (where k = 3 is the constant of proportionality)
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal $3 segments
- **Verbal:** "Cost is three times the number of smoothies"

Explicitly model how each representation connects: the "3" appears in every form.

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing Information):** Students copy a graphic organizer mapping the five representations side-by-side.
**Assessment:** Collect graphic organizers. Level 2 work shows clear alignment across representations with arrows/notes connecting the constant of proportionality in each form. Level 1 work shows representations listed but disconnected.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Spot the Imposter" (10 minutes)
Present four representations claiming to describe the same relationship. One is incorrect (e.g., a table that doesn't maintain a constant ratio). Students work in groups of 3 to identify the imposter and justify.

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing Validity) and CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** Students must cross-check each representation against the others to verify validity.
**Assessment:** Circulate and listen for students testing multiple ratios (e.g., "2/6 = 1/3, but 5/12 ≠ 1/3"). Level 2 students systematically check every data point across all representations; Level 1 students check only one or two. Capture quotes on observation log.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Smoothie Shop Decision (15 minutes)
Distribute the handout: *"You're opening a smoothie stand. Choose between two pricing models: (A) $3 per smoothie, or (B) $10 membership fee + $1 per smoothie. Which model is proportional? Which would you choose for a 2-hour event with ~20 customers? Justify with at least three representations."*

Groups create tables, graphs, and equations for both models, then write a recommendation.

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Students must compare the two pricing models considering customer volume, fairness, and revenue.
**Assessment:** Review written recommendations. Level 2 responses analyze trade-offs across multiple scenarios (e.g., "If we have fewer than 5 customers, Model A earns more; for 20+ customers, Model B…"). Level 1 responses pick one without comparison.

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Reasoning must be grounded in mathematical evidence from the representations.
**Assessment:** Check that conclusions reference specific data (slope, y-intercept, ratio test). Level 2 reasoning anticipates edge cases ("What if fewer customers show up?"). Level 1 reasoning states a choice with only one supporting calculation. Flag faulty logic (e.g., "B is better because it has a membership fee") as Level -1.

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection: Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students respond individually to two prompts:
1. **Math check:** Given the table (x: 2, 4, 6 / y: 5, 10, 15), is the relationship proportional? Write the equation.
2. **Reflection:** *"What was one moment today when you changed your thinking? What caused the shift? What strategy will you use next time you check if a relationship is proportional?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on Thinking Processes):** The metacognitive prompt requires students to identify shifts in reasoning.
**Assessment:** Read exit tickets. Level 2 responses describe specific moments ("I thought Model B was proportional until I graphed it and saw it didn't go through the origin—now I'll always check the origin first"). Level 1 responses mention reasoning generally ("I learned about graphs"). Level -1 responses show no awareness of errors.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-filled tables and sentence stems ("This relationship is/is not proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create their own "imposter" problem for peers.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.). Represent it using all five forms.

---

**Critical Thinking Coverage Summary:** CRI1a (Warm-Up), CRI1b (Direct Instruction organizer), CRI2a & CRI2b (Spot the Imposter), CRI3a (Smoothie Shop comparison), CRI4a (Justification writing), CRI5a (Exit ticket reflection).

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proving Proportions: Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to recognize, represent, and verify proportional relationships across multiple mathematical formats while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate, organize, and reflect on mathematical data.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Introduction (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents a verbal description of two local pizzerias. "Pizza A charges $15 per pizza with free delivery. Pizza B charges $12 per pizza plus a flat $10 delivery fee." Students are asked to determine which scenario represents a proportional relationship and explain why. 

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must translate the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical ideas, clarifying the difference between a constant rate and a rate with a starting fee. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to student discussions and assess their written warm-up responses to see if they can precisely clarify that proportionality requires a constant ratio ($y/x$) and passing through the origin $(0,0)$, rather than just identifying "which is cheaper."

### **Part 2: Guided Practice (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher provides students with a scattered, unorganized list of data points representing the distance a car travels over time (e.g., 2 hours = 120 miles, 5 hours = 300 miles, 1 hour = 60 miles). Students must arrange this data into a chronological input/output table, calculate the unit rate, and then plot the points on a coordinate plane to visually represent the relationship.

*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students take scattered, disorganized data and arrange it into a structured table and a sequential graph to strengthen the logical flow of the mathematical evidence.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' guided notes to ensure the table is organized sequentially by the independent variable (time) and that the graph accurately reflects this logical progression, forming a straight line through the origin.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Group Investigation (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are divided into small groups and given a "Math Detective" case. They receive a claim from a fictional student: *"The relationship between the number of movie tickets bought and the total cost is proportional."* To prove or disprove this, groups are given three "sources" of information: a hand-drawn sketch of a graph, a detailed data table from the theater's website, and an algebraic equation ($y = 10.5x$). 

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must rigorously cross-check the fictional student's claim by comparing the table, the graph, and the equation to ensure all three mathematical "sources" align and validate the proportionality.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group dialogue to ensure students are actively comparing the unit rate across all three representations (e.g., checking if the table's ratio matches the equation's constant of proportionality) rather than relying on just one source.

*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** During the investigation, students must evaluate which piece of evidence is the most reliable. They must recognize that the hand-drawn sketch lacks specific coordinates and is therefore lower quality evidence compared to the authoritative data table and the precise algebraic equation.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will ask groups to rank their sources by reliability. Assessment is based on students' ability to articulate that the equation and table provide sophisticated, high-quality proof, whereas the sketch is insufficient for verification.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** The teacher asks the groups: *"If you needed to find the cost of 5,000 movie tickets, which representation (table, graph, or equation) would you choose to use and why?"* Students must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each option.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess group responses on their worksheets. A successful assessment occurs when students note that while a graph is great for quick visual confirmation (pro), it is highly inefficient for large numbers (con), making the equation the best long-term choice for large-scale predictions.

### **Part 4: Independent Application (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a complex real-world scenario: A streaming service offers a "proportional" plan of $5 per month, but the fine print states that after 12 months, the price increases to $8 per month. Students must decide whether to sign a 2-year contract with this company or a competitor offering a flat $6 per month forever. They must justify their decision using proportional reasoning.

*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by anticipating the complexity of the price jump. They must recognize that the first plan is *not* truly proportional over the 2-year period and use mathematical logic to make a sound financial decision.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect the independent practice worksheets and evaluate the written justifications. Mastery is demonstrated when a student uses calculations (e.g., $5 \times 12 + $8 \times 12$ vs. $6 \times 24$) to logically prove which choice is better, avoiding the trap of the initial lower price.

### **Part 5: Closure & Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket with the following prompt: *"Think about how you solved today's problems. How did translating a word problem into a graph or equation change your understanding of the problem? Did your initial assumptions about any of the scenarios change once you organized the data?"*

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students engage in deep metacognitive awareness, reflecting on how utilizing different mathematical representations strategically adjusted their thinking and helped them catch initial errors or assumptions.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for explicit self-awareness. A high-level response will detail a specific shift in thinking, such as, "I initially thought Pizza B was cheaper because of the $12 price, but once I created the equation, I realized the $10 fee made it non-proportional and more expensive for small orders."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Representation Match” cards  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes
Display:

**Store A:** 3 notebooks cost $6.  
**Store B:** 5 notebooks cost $12.  

Ask students: “Which store has the better deal? How do you know?”

Students solve independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Bring the class together and highlight that comparing **unit rates** helps us understand relationships between quantities.

Introduce the key idea:  
A relationship is **proportional** if one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Use a simple example:

**Apples cost $2 per pound.**

Create a table:

| Pounds | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Ask:
- What is the ratio of cost to pounds?
- What is the constant of proportionality?
- What equation represents this relationship?

Guide students to identify:

\[
\frac{y}{x} = 2
\]

So:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Then graph the points \((1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8)\). Emphasize that a proportional relationship graphs as a **straight line through the origin**.

Also show a nonexample:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 7 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 17 |

Explain that this is not proportional because the ratio is not constant and the relationship does not fit \(y = kx\).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes
Present this verbal description:

“A cyclist travels 15 miles every hour.”

Students work with the teacher to create:

1. **Table**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 15 |
| 2 | 30 |
| 3 | 45 |
| 4 | 60 |

2. **Equation**

\[
y = 15x
\]

3. **Graph**  
Students plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.

4. **Diagram**  
Use a double number line:

Hours: 0 — 1 — 2 — 3 — 4  
Miles: 0 — 15 — 30 — 45 — 60

5. **Verbal description**  
“For every 1 hour, the cyclist travels 15 miles.”

Ask students to explain how each representation shows the same proportional relationship.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Match — 15 minutes
Give each pair a set of cards. Each set includes tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Some are proportional; some are not.

Students must:
1. Sort cards into “proportional” and “not proportional.”
2. Match different representations of the same proportional relationship.
3. Write one explanation for a match using evidence from at least two representations.

Example student sentence frame:

“This relationship is proportional because the ratio ___ to ___ is always ___. The equation is ___, and the graph shows ___.”

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- “How do you know the constant stays the same?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “What would the equation be?”
- “Which representation gives you the clearest evidence?”

---

### 5. Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration — Throughout Lesson

**CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Lesson moment:** During guided practice, students identify what each quantity represents and label \(x\), \(y\), and \(k\).  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students precisely stating meanings, such as: “\(x\) is hours, \(y\) is miles, and 15 is miles per hour.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student clearly explains variables and constant.  
  - 1: Student identifies some parts but is unclear.  
  - -1: Student reverses or mislabels quantities in a way that causes confusion.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students organize a verbal description into a table, graph, equation, and diagram.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether their work follows a logical structure and whether representations align.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student arranges information consistently across all representations.  
  - 1: Student has a basic table or graph but incomplete connections.  
  - -1: Student work is scattered or contradictory.

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Lesson moment:** In the card sort, students verify whether a relationship is proportional by checking multiple representations.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for students cross-checking: “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph goes through the origin.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student uses multiple checks to confirm proportionality.  
  - 1: Student checks only one representation.  
  - -1: Student accepts a relationship as proportional without valid evidence.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students decide which representation gives the strongest evidence for proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to justify why a table, graph, or equation is most useful in a specific case.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student selects relevant evidence, such as constant ratio or equation \(y = kx\).  
  - 1: Student uses somewhat relevant evidence but lacks depth.  
  - -1: Student relies on irrelevant or inaccurate information.

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students compare representations and discuss which is best for identifying proportionality quickly.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for trade-off language: “A graph is visual, but a table makes the ratio easier to calculate.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student compares strengths and limits of multiple representations.  
  - 1: Student gives a simple preference with little explanation.  
  - -1: Student ignores alternatives.

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students decide whether each card is proportional or not and justify their decision.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Review written explanations for logical claims supported by mathematical evidence.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student gives a correct decision with clear evidence.  
  - 1: Student’s reasoning is partly correct but incomplete.  
  - -1: Student uses faulty logic, such as “it is proportional because the numbers go up.”

**CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Lesson moment:** Exit ticket reflection asks students how they checked their answer and what representation helped most.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for metacognitive comments, such as: “At first I thought it was proportional, but then I checked the ratios and changed my answer.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student explains and adjusts their thinking.  
  - 1: Student mentions a strategy without reflection.  
  - -1: Student repeats an error without noticing.

---

### 6. Independent Practice and Exit Ticket — 8 minutes
Students complete:

1. Determine whether the table is proportional:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |
| 8 | 40 |

Write the equation.

2. Determine whether this situation is proportional:  
“A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month.”

Explain why or why not.

3. Reflection:  
“What is one way you can prove a relationship is proportional? Which representation helps you most?”

---

### Closure — 3 minutes
Review the main criteria:

A proportional relationship:
- Has a constant ratio \(\frac{y}{x}\)
- Can be written as \(y = kx\)
- Graphs as a straight line through the origin
- Can be represented consistently in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words

End by reminding students that proportional reasoning is useful for comparing prices, speeds, recipes, maps, and real-world rates.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes
Display:

**Store A:** 3 notebooks cost $6.  
**Store B:** 5 notebooks cost $12.  

Ask students: “Which store has the better deal? How do you know?”

Students solve independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Bring the class together and highlight that comparing **unit rates** helps us understand relationships between quantities.

Introduce the key idea:  
A relationship is **proportional** if one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Use a simple example:

**Apples cost $2 per pound.**

Create a table:

| Pounds | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Ask:
- What is the ratio of cost to pounds?
- What is the constant of proportionality?
- What equation represents this relationship?

Guide students to identify:

\[
\frac{y}{x} = 2
\]

So:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Then graph the points \((1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8)\). Emphasize that a proportional relationship graphs as a **straight line through the origin**.

Also show a nonexample:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 7 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 17 |

Explain that this is not proportional because the ratio is not constant and the relationship does not fit \(y = kx\).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes
Present this verbal description:

“A cyclist travels 15 miles every hour.”

Students work with the teacher to create:

1. **Table**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 15 |
| 2 | 30 |
| 3 | 45 |
| 4 | 60 |

2. **Equation**

\[
y = 15x
\]

3. **Graph**  
Students plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.

4. **Diagram**  
Use a double number line:

Hours: 0 — 1 — 2 — 3 — 4  
Miles: 0 — 15 — 30 — 45 — 60

5. **Verbal description**  
“For every 1 hour, the cyclist travels 15 miles.”

Ask students to explain how each representation shows the same proportional relationship.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Match — 15 minutes
Give each pair a set of cards. Each set includes tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Some are proportional; some are not.

Students must:
1. Sort cards into “proportional” and “not proportional.”
2. Match different representations of the same proportional relationship.
3. Write one explanation for a match using evidence from at least two representations.

Example student sentence frame:

“This relationship is proportional because the ratio ___ to ___ is always ___. The equation is ___, and the graph shows ___.”

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- “How do you know the constant stays the same?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “What would the equation be?”
- “Which representation gives you the clearest evidence?”

### 5. Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration — Throughout Lesson

- **Lesson moment:** During guided practice, students identify what each quantity represents and label \(x\), \(y\), and \(k\).  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students precisely stating meanings, such as: “\(x\) is hours, \(y\) is miles, and 15 is miles per hour.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student clearly explains variables and constant.  
  - 1: Student identifies some parts but is unclear.  
  - -1: Student reverses or mislabels quantities in a way that causes confusion.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students organize a verbal description into a table, graph, equation, and diagram.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether their work follows a logical structure and whether representations align.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student arranges information consistently across all representations.  
  - 1: Student has a basic table or graph but incomplete connections.  
  - -1: Student work is scattered or contradictory.

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Lesson moment:** In the card sort, students verify whether a relationship is proportional by checking multiple representations.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for students cross-checking: “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph goes through the origin.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student uses multiple checks to confirm proportionality.  
  - 1: Student checks only one representation.  
  - -1: Student accepts a relationship as proportional without valid evidence.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students decide which representation gives the strongest evidence for proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to justify why a table, graph, or equation is most useful in a specific case.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student selects relevant evidence, such as constant ratio or equation \(y = kx\).  
  - 1: Student uses somewhat relevant evidence but lacks depth.  
  - -1: Student relies on irrelevant or inaccurate information.

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students compare representations and discuss which is best for identifying proportionality quickly.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for trade-off language: “A graph is visual, but a table makes the ratio easier to calculate.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student compares strengths and limits of multiple representations.  
  - 1: Student gives a simple preference with little explanation.  
  - -1: Student ignores alternatives.

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Lesson moment:** Students decide whether each card is proportional or not and justify their decision.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Review written explanations for logical claims supported by mathematical evidence.  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student gives a correct decision with clear evidence.  
  - 1: Student’s reasoning is partly correct but incomplete.  
  - -1: Student uses faulty logic, such as “it is proportional because the numbers go up.”

**CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Lesson moment:** Exit ticket reflection asks students how they checked their answer and what representation helped most.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for metacognitive comments, such as: “At first I thought it was proportional, but then I checked the ratios and changed my answer.”  
- **Level evidence:**  
  - 2: Student explains and adjusts their thinking.  
  - 1: Student mentions a strategy without reflection.  
  - -1: Student repeats an error without noticing.

Students complete:

1. Determine whether the table is proportional:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |

Write the equation.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A smoothie shop charges $5 for the first smoothie and $2 for each additional one."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner (2 minutes), then share aloud. Guide students to articulate that proportional relationships have a constant ratio y/x = k and pass through the origin (0,0).

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students must precisely articulate what "proportional" means.
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share. Level 2 = precise language ("the ratio y/x stays constant and the graph passes through the origin"); Level 1 = partial clarity ("the numbers go up the same way"); Level -1 = mislabels quantities or reverses the ratio.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the five representations using Scenario A:
- **Table:** 1 → $3, 2 → $6, 3 → $9
- **Graph:** Plot points; straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x (k = 3 is the constant of proportionality)
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal $3 segments
- **Verbal:** "Cost is three times the number of smoothies"

Show a nonexample (Scenario B) and explicitly model how the "3" appears in every form for Scenario A but how Scenario B fails the constant-ratio test.

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing Information):** Students complete a graphic organizer mapping all five representations side-by-side.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers. Level 2 work shows clear alignment across representations with arrows connecting k in each form; Level 1 lists representations but disconnected; Level -1 = scattered or contradictory entries.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Spot the Imposter" (10 minutes)
Present four representations of the same supposed relationship; one is incorrect (e.g., a table that doesn't maintain a constant ratio). Groups of 3 identify the imposter and rank the remaining sources by reliability.

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing Validity):** Students cross-check each representation against the others.
**Assessment:** Listen for systematic ratio testing ("2/6 = 1/3, but 5/12 ≠ 1/3"). Level 2 = checks every data point across all representations; Level 1 = checks only one or two; Level -1 = accepts proportionality without valid evidence.

**🎯 Targets CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** Groups rank the representations by reliability (e.g., a precise equation vs. a hand-drawn sketch without scale).
**Assessment:** Level 2 = articulates that the equation and verified table provide highest-quality proof while a rough sketch is insufficient; Level 1 = ranks without clear justification; Level -1 = relies on irrelevant evidence.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Smoothie Shop Decision (15 minutes)
Handout: *"You're opening a smoothie stand. Choose between (A) $3 per smoothie, or (B) $10 setup fee + $1 per smoothie. Which model is proportional? Which would you choose for a 2-hour event with ~20 customers? Justify with at least three representations."*

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Students compare pricing models considering customer volume, fairness, and revenue.
**Assessment:** Level 2 = analyzes trade-offs across scenarios ("Below 5 customers, A earns more; above 20, B…"); Level 1 = picks one without comparison; Level -1 = ignores alternatives.

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Reasoning must reference mathematical evidence (slope, y-intercept, ratio test).
**Assessment:** Level 2 = anticipates edge cases ("What if fewer customers show up?"); Level 1 = states a choice with one calculation; Level -1 = faulty logic ("B is better because membership fees sound professional").

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection: Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
1. **Math check:** Given the table (x: 2, 4, 6 / y: 5, 10, 15), is the relationship proportional? Write the equation.
2. **Reflection:** *"What was one moment today when you changed your thinking? What caused the shift? What strategy will you use next time you check if a relationship is proportional?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on Thinking Processes):**
**Assessment:** Level 2 = specific shift described ("I thought Model B was proportional until I graphed it and saw it didn't pass through the origin—now I'll check the origin first"); Level 1 = general mention ("I learned about graphs"); Level -1 = no awareness of errors.

- **Extension:** Students create their own "imposter" problem for peers.
