## Generator
# The Curiosity Argument: Asking Better Questions to Write Stronger Arguments

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan for 7th Grade ELA

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Central Idea:** The best arguments begin not with answers, but with genuine curiosity about a question. When writers wonder deeply about a topic, they naturally seek evidence, consider other perspectives, and build more honest, compelling arguments.

**Learning Objectives:** By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Explain how curiosity strengthens argumentative writing
2. Identify and acknowledge opposing claims in an argument
3. Draft a structured argumentative paragraph with a claim, reasoning, evidence, and a counterargument acknowledgment
4. Practice intellectual humility — the willingness to stay curious even when they have an opinion

**Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard or projector
- Printed or projected "Curiosity Argument" graphic organizer (described below)
- Sticky notes (two colors per student, e.g., yellow and pink)
- Student journals or lined paper
- Timer visible to students
- Optional: a physical object like a locked box or an interesting photograph to open the lesson

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### OPENING HOOK — *The Wondering Moment* (7 minutes)

**[0:00 – 7:00]**

As students walk in, display a photograph or image on the projector that is genuinely ambiguous or surprising — for example, a black-and-white photograph of children working in a coal mine from the early 1900s, an aerial view of a perfectly circular lake in a desert, or a photograph of a species of animal most students have never seen.

Do NOT label or explain the image.

**Say to students:**

> "Before I tell you anything about what's on the screen, I want you to spend sixty seconds writing every single question that pops into your head. Don't try to answer them. Just ask. Fill the page with question marks if you have to."

*Allow 60–90 seconds of silent writing.*

**Then ask:** "Who has a question they're willing to share?"

Call on 5–6 students. Write their questions on the board word-for-word without commenting on whether they are "good" or "right."

**Now say:**

> "Look at this list. Every single one of these questions came out of you in under two minutes. That feeling — that pull toward wanting to know something — is called **curiosity**. Now here's the important thing I want you to think about today: what if curiosity isn't just something you feel when you look at a weird picture? What if it's actually a *skill* — one of the most powerful tools a writer can use?"

**Brief Discussion (2 minutes):** Ask students: *"When you write an argument essay, do you usually feel curious? Or do you feel like you already know what you want to say before you start?"* Take a few honest hands/responses. Acknowledge that most people feel the second way — and that's exactly what this lesson is going to challenge.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *The Curious Arguer* (10 minutes)

**[7:00 – 17:00]**

**Say:**

> "Today we're going to look at what makes an argument strong according to your writing standards, and we're going to discover that every single ingredient of a strong argument actually requires you to be curious."

Write on the board (or reveal a slide):

**The 4 Parts of a Strong Argument (CCSS W.7.1)**
1. **Claim** — What do you believe?
2. **Reasons & Evidence** — Why do you believe it? What proof supports it?
3. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claims** — What do people who disagree say?
4. **Logical Organization** — How do you put it all together clearly?

**Now draw a second column next to this list and label it: "The Curious Question Behind Each Part."**

Fill it in as you talk through each one:

| Argument Component | The Curious Question |
|---|---|
| Claim | *What do I actually think — and am I willing to be wrong?* |
| Reasons & Evidence | *What would prove this? What would disprove it?* |
| Opposing Claims | *What does someone who completely disagrees with me believe — and why might they have a point?* |
| Logical Organization | *If someone confused read this, what questions would they still have?* |

**Explain each row briefly:**

- **On Claim:** "Curious writers don't always start with a firm claim. They start with a real question and let their research sharpen their opinion. That makes their claim more honest and harder to knock down."

- **On Evidence:** "A curious writer asks: 'What would DISPROVE my argument?' If you only look for evidence that agrees with you, you're not doing research — you're just confirming what you already thought. Real thinkers ask uncomfortable questions."

- **On Opposing Claims:** "This is the part most students skip or rush through. But think about it — if you've never truly wondered *why* someone might disagree with you, your counterargument is going to be weak and unfair. Curiosity about the other side actually makes YOUR side stronger."

- **On Organization:** "A curious writer reads their own draft like a confused stranger would. They ask: 'Where does this get muddy? Where does this skip a step?' That question is what revision really is."

**Pause and ask the class:** *"Which of these four do you think is hardest to be genuinely curious about?"* Take a quick informal poll (hands or verbal). Most students will say opposing claims. Note this — you'll return to it.

---

### MODELED PRACTICE — *Watching Curiosity in Action* (8 minutes)

**[17:00 – 25:00]**

**Say:**

> "I'm going to show you two versions of the same argument paragraph. Your job is to decide which writer was more curious — and to explain why."

Display or read aloud the two paragraphs on a topic students can relate to immediately:

---

**Topic: Should students have homework?**

**Paragraph A (The Incurious Argument):**

*Students should not have homework. Homework is stressful and takes too much time. Students are tired after school and need a break. Therefore, schools should stop giving homework. Anyone who disagrees just doesn't care about students' mental health.*

---

**Paragraph B (The Curious Argument):**

*Schools should significantly reduce the amount of homework assigned to middle school students. Research from Stanford University found that students who have more than one to two hours of homework per night experience higher stress levels, reduced sleep, and fewer opportunities for physical activity — all of which can negatively affect learning. While some argue that homework helps students practice skills and stay academically competitive, this concern is worth taking seriously. However, research also suggests that quality of practice matters more than quantity, and that exhausted students retain very little. A well-rested student who did thirty minutes of focused review may learn more than a burned-out student who spent three hours completing busywork. Schools that want students to succeed should look carefully at whether the homework they assign is truly serving students — or simply keeping up a tradition.*

---

**Ask students:**
- "Which paragraph sounds more confident to you? Why?" *(Most will say B, even though A sounds more forceful on the surface — dig into this.)*
- "Where in Paragraph B can you see the writer being curious about the opposing side?"
- "What questions do you think the writer of Paragraph B asked themselves before writing?"

Guide students to notice: Paragraph B is stronger NOT because the writer was more certain, but because the writer was more curious. The writer asked: *What does research actually say? What would someone who disagrees argue? Is that argument fair? How do I respond to it honestly?*

**Key takeaway to write on the board:**

> **"Curiosity doesn't make your argument weaker. It makes your argument honest — and honest arguments are the hardest to defeat."**

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — *The Sticky Note Think* (8 minutes)

**[25:00 – 33:00]**

Give each student two sticky notes — one yellow, one pink (or any two colors available).

Display the following prompt:

> **"Middle school students should be allowed to use their phones in class for any purpose, at any time."**

**Instructions:**

- On the **yellow sticky note**, write the strongest argument you can think of *in favor* of this statement. Push yourself — even if you personally disagree, try to argue for it as thoughtfully as you can.
- On the **pink sticky note**, write the strongest argument you can think of *against* this statement. Again, push yourself even if you personally agree with it.

*Give students 4 minutes to write both.*

**Then say:**

> "Now flip both sticky notes over. On the back of each one, write one genuine question you still have — something you'd actually need to know or research to make this argument really strong."

*Give students 2 more minutes.*

**Brief share-out:** Ask 3–4 students to read one of their questions aloud. Celebrate specificity. Point out questions like *"What percentage of students actually use phones for learning vs. distractions?"* or *"Do schools with phone bans see different test scores?"* as examples of curious questions that would lead to strong evidence.

**Say:** "Notice that when you tried to argue for BOTH sides, you probably had to think harder. That discomfort — that moment where you're not sure what you think — that's curiosity doing its job."

---

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — *Write Your Curious Argument* (15 minutes)

**[33:00 – 48:00]**

Distribute or have students draw the **Curiosity Argument Graphic Organizer** in their journals. The organizer has the following sections:

---

**CURIOSITY ARGUMENT ORGANIZER**

**My Topic/Question I'm Exploring:**
*(Write it as a genuine question, not a statement.)*

**My Claim:**
*(After thinking it through, what do I believe?)*

**Reason 1 + Evidence:**
*(What supports my claim? What specific facts, examples, or data?)*

**Reason 2 + Evidence:**
*(What else supports my claim?)*

**The Opposing Claim I Take Seriously:**
*(What is the strongest thing someone who disagrees would say?)*

**My Curious Response:**
*(I acknowledge their point has some merit, BUT... because...)*

**My Conclusion:**
*(What do I want my reader to walk away thinking or doing?)*

---

**Choose a topic from this list (or propose your own to the teacher):**

1. Should our school have a later start time?
2. Should students be allowed to choose their own books for English class?
3. Should junk food be banned in school cafeterias?
4. Should competitive letter grades be replaced with pass/fail in middle school?

**Say to students:**

> "Before you write anything in the 'My Claim' box, I want you to write your topic as a real question — one you're genuinely not 100% sure about. Then do your thinking. If you're certain about everything before you start, push yourself to steelman the other side — that means making the opposing argument as STRONG as you possibly can."

*Circulate during this time. Check in particularly with students who are rushing to fill in the organizer without pausing to think. Ask probing questions: "What would someone who completely disagrees with you say? Does that argument have any merit?" Push students who write weak counterarguments to try again.*

---

### CLOSING — *The Curiosity Debrief* (7 minutes)

**[48:00 – 55:00]**

**Ask students to pause their writing** (they can finish the argument as homework or during the next class session).

**Whole-class reflection — ask these questions one at a time, taking 2–3 responses each:**

1. *"What was the hardest part of this organizer to fill in? Why?"*
2. *"Did anyone change their mind — even a little — about their topic while they were writing? What made you shift?"*
3. *"How is writing an argument with curiosity different from writing an argument when you're just trying to win?"*

**Closing thought — say to students:**

> "There's a famous scientist named Richard Feynman who said: 'I'd rather have questions that can't be answered than answers that can't be questioned.' That's what curiosity in writing looks like. The best argumentative writers aren't the ones who are most sure of themselves. They're the ones who asked the hardest questions — including the ones that challenged their own thinking. When you do that, your reader trusts you. And when your reader trusts you, your argument actually lands."

**Exit Ticket (last 2 minutes):**

On a scrap of paper or index card, students write:

> *"One question I'm still curious about on my topic is ________________________."*

Collect these as students leave. These can be used to open the next class session and to check for students who may need more support developing genuine curiosity about their topic.

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For students who need additional support:**
- Provide a completed sample organizer using the phone policy topic from the guided practice so they have a model to follow
- Offer sentence frames for the counterargument section: *"Some people argue that ________, and this concern makes sense because ________. However, ________."*
- Allow verbal processing with a partner before writing

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to find and cite a real piece of evidence (from memory or a provided article) for both their argument AND the opposing argument
- Ask them to write a second paragraph that strengthens their counterargument response with additional logical reasoning
- Prompt: *"Can you imagine a scenario where the opposing claim is actually RIGHT? What would that scenario look like?"*

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the two sample paragraphs in both text and audio if possible
- Allow students to draft their organizer in their home language first, then translate key ideas
- Partner with a peer for the sticky note activity

- Printed or projected \"Curiosity Argument\" graphic organizer (described below)
- Sticky notes (two colors per student, e.g., yellow and pink)
- Student journals or lined paper
- Timer visible to students
- Optional: a physical object like a locked box or an interesting photograph to open the lesson

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### OPENING HOOK — *The Wondering Moment* (7 minutes)

**[0:00 – 7:00]**

As students walk in, display a photograph or image on the projector that is genuinely ambiguous or surprising — for example, a black-and-white photograph of children working in a coal mine from the early 1900s, an aerial view of a perfectly circular lake in a desert, or a photograph of a species of animal most students have never seen.

Do NOT label or explain the image.

**Say to students:**

> \"Before I tell you anything about what's on the screen, I want you to spend sixty seconds writing every single question that pops into your head. Don't try to answer them. Just ask. Fill the page with question marks if you have to.\"

*Allow 60–90 seconds of silent writing.*

**Then ask:** \"Who has a question they're willing to share?\"

Call on 5–6 students. Write their questions on the board word-for-word without commenting on whether they are \"good\" or \"right.\"

**Now say:**

> \"Look at this list. Every single one of these questions came out of you in under two minutes. That feeling — that pull toward wanting to know something — is called **curiosity**. Now here's the important thing I want you to think about today: what if curiosity isn't just something you feel when you look at a weird picture? What if it's actually a *skill* — one of the most powerful tools a writer can use?\"

**Brief Discussion (2 minutes):** Ask students: *\"When you write an argument essay, do you usually feel curious? Or do you feel like you already know what you want to say before you start?\"* Take a few honest hands/responses. Acknowledge that most people feel the second way — and that's exactly what this lesson is going to challenge.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *The Curious Arguer* (10 minutes)

**[7:00 – 17:00]**

**Say:**

> \"Today we're going to look at what makes an argument strong according to your writing standards, and we're going to discover that every single ingredient of a strong argument actually requires you to be curious.\"

Write on the board (or reveal a slide):

**The 4 Parts of a Strong Argument (CCSS W.7.1)**
1. **Claim** — What do you believe?
2. **Reasons & Evidence** — Why do you believe it? What proof supports it?
3. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claims** — What do people who disagree say?
4. **Logical Organization** — How do you put it all together clearly?

**Now draw a second column next to this list and label it: \"The Curious Question Behind Each Part.\"**

Fill it in as you talk through each one:

| Argument Component | The Curious Question |
|---|---|
| Claim | *What do I actually think — and am I willing to be wrong?* |
| Reasons & Evidence | *What would prove this? What would disprove it?* |
| Opposing Claims | *What does someone who completely disagrees with me believe — and why might they have a point?* |
| Logical Organization | *If someone confused read this, what questions would they still have?* |

**Explain each row briefly:**

- **On Claim:** \"Curious writers don't always start with a firm claim. They start with a real question and let their research sharpen their opinion. That makes their claim more honest and harder to knock down.\"

- **On Evidence:** \"A curious writer asks: 'What would DISPROVE my argument?' If you only look for evidence that agrees with you, you're not doing research — you're just confirming what you already thought. Real thinkers ask uncomfortable questions.\"

- **On Opposing Claims:** \"This is the part most students skip or rush through. But think about it — if you've never truly wondered *why* someone might disagree with you, your counterargument is going to be weak and unfair. Curiosity about the other side actually makes YOUR side stronger.\"

- **On Organization:** \"A curious writer reads their own draft like a confused stranger would. They ask: 'Where does this get muddy? Where does this skip a step?' That question is what revision really is.\"

**Pause and ask the class:** *\"Which of these four do you think is hardest to be genuinely curious about?\"* Take a quick informal poll (hands or verbal). Most students will say opposing claims. Note this — you'll return to it.

---

### MODELED PRACTICE — *Watching Curiosity in Action* (8 minutes)

**[17:00 – 25:00]**

**Say:**

> \"I'm going to show you two versions of the same argument paragraph. Your job is to decide which writer was more curious — and to explain why.\"

Display or read aloud the two paragraphs on a topic students can relate to immediately:

---

**Topic: Should students have homework?**

**Paragraph A (The Incurious Argument):**

*Students should not have homework. Homework is stressful and takes too much time. Students are tired after school and need a break. Therefore, schools should stop giving homework. Anyone who disagrees just doesn't care about students' mental health.*

---

**Paragraph B (The Curious Argument):**

*Schools should significantly reduce the amount of homework assigned to middle school students. Research from Stanford University found that students who have more than one to two hours of homework per night experience higher stress levels, reduced sleep, and fewer opportunities for physical activity — all of which can negatively affect learning. While some argue that homework helps students practice skills and stay academically competitive, this concern is worth taking seriously. However, research also suggests that quality of practice matters more than quantity, and that exhausted students retain very little. A well-rested student who did thirty minutes of focused review may learn more than a burned-out student who spent three hours completing busywork. Schools that want students to succeed should look carefully at whether the homework they assign is truly serving students — or simply keeping up a tradition.*

---

**Ask students:**
- \"Which paragraph sounds more confident to you? Why?\" *(Most will say B, even though A sounds more forceful on the surface — dig into this.)*
- \"Where in Paragraph B can you see the writer being curious about the opposing side?\"
- \"What questions do you think the writer of Paragraph B asked themselves before writing?\"

Guide students to notice: Paragraph B is stronger NOT because the writer was more certain, but because the writer was more curious. The writer asked: *What does research actually say? What would someone who disagrees argue? Is that argument fair? How do I respond to it honestly?*

**Key takeaway to write on the board:**

> **\"Curiosity doesn't make your argument weaker. It makes your argument honest — and honest arguments are the hardest to defeat.\"**

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — *The Sticky Note Think* (8 minutes)

**[25:00 – 33:00]**

Give each student two sticky notes — one yellow, one pink (or any two colors available).

Display the following prompt:

> **\"Middle school students should be allowed to use their phones in class for any purpose, at any time.\"**

**Instructions:**

- On the **yellow sticky note**, write the strongest argument you can think of *in favor* of this statement. Push yourself — even if you personally disagree, try to argue for it as thoughtfully as you can.
- On the **pink sticky note**, write the strongest argument you can think of *against* this statement. Again, push yourself even if you personally agree with it.

*Give students 4 minutes to write both.*

**Then say:**

> \"Now flip both sticky notes over. On the back of each one, write one genuine question you still have — something you'd actually need to know or research to make this argument really strong.\"

*Give students 2 more minutes.*

**Brief share-out:** Ask 3–4 students to read one of their questions aloud. Celebrate specificity. Point out questions like *\"What percentage of students actually use phones for learning vs. distractions?\"* or *\"Do schools with phone bans see different test scores?\"* as examples of curious questions that would lead to strong evidence.

**Say:** \"Notice that when you tried to argue for BOTH sides, you probably had to think harder. That discomfort — that moment where you're not sure what you think — that's curiosity doing its job.\"

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — *Write Your Curious Argument* (15 minutes)

**[33:00 – 48:00]**

Distribute or have students draw the **Curiosity Argument Graphic Organizer** in their journals. The organizer has the following sections:

---

**CURIOSITY ARGUMENT ORGANIZER**

**My Topic/Question I'm Exploring:**
*(Write it as a genuine question, not a statement.)*

**My Claim:**
*(After thinking it through, what do I believe?)*

**Reason 1 + Evidence:**
*(What supports my claim? What specific facts, examples, or data?)*

**Reason 2 + Evidence:**
*(What else supports my claim?)*

**The Opposing Claim I Take Seriously:**
*(What is the strongest thing someone who disagrees would say?)*

**My Curious Response:**
*(I acknowledge their point has some merit, BUT... because...)*

**My Conclusion:**
*(What do I want my reader to walk away thinking or doing?)*
