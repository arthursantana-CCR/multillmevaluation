## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Sticky notes (2 per student)
- Lined paper or writer's notebooks
- "Mystery Box" (a small closed box on the teacher's desk)

---

## Lesson

### Hook — The Mystery Box (5 minutes)

Place a closed box on your desk before students arrive. Say nothing about it. When class begins, let students wonder and whisper. After two minutes, ask:

*"How many of you are thinking about what's inside?"*

Nearly every hand will go up. Tell them:

**"That feeling — that pull — is curiosity. And today, curiosity is your most important writing tool."**

Reveal the box contains a simple rock or pencil. Watch their reactions. Explain that their reaction to the ordinary object teaches them something: curiosity is not about having answers. It is about staying in the question long enough to discover something worth saying.

---

### Direct Instruction — What Curious Writers Do (10 minutes)

Write this on the board:

> **Uncurious writer:** "School uniforms are bad. Students should wear what they want."
> **Curious writer:** "School uniforms might reduce some problems, but do they actually help students learn — or do they just make adults feel better?"

Ask students which writer they would rather read. Discuss briefly.

Explain that argumentative writing is not about shouting your opinion the loudest. It is about being genuinely curious enough to ask hard questions, look at the problem from multiple sides, and then use evidence to build a well-reasoned answer.

Introduce the three habits of a curious argumentative writer:

1. **Ask "But what if I'm wrong?"** — This leads to acknowledging opposing claims.
2. **Ask "How do I actually know that?"** — This leads to finding real evidence.
3. **Ask "What am I missing?"** — This leads to stronger, more logical organization.

Write these on the board and leave them visible throughout the lesson.

---

### Guided Practice — Sticky Note Debate (15 minutes)

Give each student two sticky notes — one blue (or any color), one yellow.

Pose this question: **"Should students have phones in class?"**

- On the **first sticky note**, students write their immediate gut opinion in one sentence.
- On the **second sticky note**, they write the strongest possible argument for the *opposite* side.

Students place both notes on the board in two columns. Read several aloud.

Ask: *"Did anyone write something on the second note that actually made them reconsider, even a little?"*

This is the moment. Explain that real curiosity means being willing to be changed by what you discover. An argument that ignores the other side is not an argument — it is just noise. The most powerful argumentative writers are the ones brave enough to take the opposing view seriously and respond to it honestly.

Model a brief claim with a counterclaim using student sticky notes as material:

*"While some argue phones distract students, research suggests that when used with clear guidelines, phones can actually support personalized learning — which means the real issue isn't the phone itself, but the structure around it."*

Point out: claim, counterclaim acknowledged, evidence hinted at, logical pivot.

---

### Independent Writing — First Draft Argument (20 minutes)

Students choose one of the following prompts:

- Should homework be eliminated in middle school?
- Should schools start later in the morning?
- Should students grade their own tests?

Before writing, students spend **two minutes silently asking questions** about their topic using the three curious writer habits from the board. They jot these questions at the top of their paper.

Then they write a one-paragraph argument that includes:
- A clear claim
- At least one piece of reasoning or evidence
- One acknowledged opposing view
- A logical response to that opposing view

Circulate and coach. If students are stuck, ask them: *"What would someone who disagrees with you say? Now — how do you answer them?"*

---

### Closing — The Best Question Wins (5 minutes)

Return to the mystery box briefly. Ask:

*"What made the box interesting?"*

Students will say: not knowing what was inside. Connect this back to writing. The best arguments begin with a writer who doesn't completely know the answer yet — someone curious enough to investigate, honest enough to consider other views, and skilled enough to build a logical case from what they find.

Close with this sentence for students to copy:

> **"Curiosity doesn't weaken your argument. It builds the only kind of argument worth reading."**

Ask two or three volunteers to share their strongest sentence from their draft.

---

*Extension: Students expand their paragraph into a full five-paragraph argument for homework.*

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt and example paragraph  
- Sticky notes or index cards  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board. Highlight key ideas such as asking questions, wanting to learn more, exploring new ideas.  
- Explain: Curiosity is the desire to learn and understand more about the world. It helps us ask good questions and find strong reasons to support our ideas.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie or a scientist who made a discovery by asking questions). Emphasize how curiosity led them to explore different viewpoints and gather evidence.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our claim  
  - Consider other opinions or opposing claims  
- Write the standard on the board: *“Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.”*  
- Briefly define key terms: claim, reasons, evidence, opposing claims, logical organization.

**3. Mini Lesson: Structure of an Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple structure for an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim:** State your opinion clearly.  
  2. **Reason:** Give a reason that supports your claim.  
  3. **Evidence:** Provide facts, examples, or details that back up your reason.  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claim:** Mention a different opinion.  
  5. **Refutation:** Explain why your claim is stronger.  
- Show a short example paragraph on the board or handout. Read it aloud and identify each part together.

**4. Guided Practice: Generating Curiosity Questions (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple argumentative prompt related to a familiar topic, for example:  
  *“Should school start later in the morning?”*  
- Ask students to brainstorm questions they are curious about regarding this topic. Write their questions on the board. Examples:  
  - How does starting school later affect students’ sleep?  
  - Do teachers prefer later start times?  
  - What do parents think about changing school hours?  
- Explain that these curiosity questions help us find reasons and evidence for our argument.

**5. Writing Activity: Drafting an Argumentative Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Students choose a side on the prompt (for or against later school start times).  
- Using the curiosity questions and the argument structure, students write a paragraph supporting their claim.  
- Encourage them to:  
  - State their claim clearly  
  - Use at least two reasons with evidence  
  - Acknowledge an opposing claim and explain why their claim is stronger  
- Circulate and provide support as needed.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing claim in each.  
- End with a brief discussion:  
  *“How did being curious help you write your argument? What did you learn about considering other opinions?”*  

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to think of a topic they are curious about and write a short argument paragraph using the same structure, applying curiosity to find reasons and evidence.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson helps students see curiosity as a tool for strong argument writing. By asking questions and exploring different viewpoints, they learn to support their claims with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing ideas in a logical way.
