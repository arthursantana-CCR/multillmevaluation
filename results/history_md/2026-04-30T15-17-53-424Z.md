## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade:** 7 | **Time:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Materials
- Sticky notes (2 colors per student)
- Lined paper or notebooks
- Whiteboard/markers
- "Mystery Object" (a simple item like a compass, abacus, or unusual tool)

---

## Lesson

### Hook — The Object Nobody Understands (8 minutes)

Place the mystery object on a desk at the front of the room. Say nothing about it. Give students 90 seconds to silently write down every question that pops into their head about it.

Then ask: *"Who wrote more than three questions? More than five? More than eight?"*

Tell students: **"Curiosity is a superpower — and today it's going to make you a better arguer."**

Explain that the best argument writers aren't just people who are confident. They're people who are genuinely curious — about their own ideas *and* about the people who disagree with them. A curious writer asks: *What do I believe? Why? Who thinks differently, and why might they be right?*

---

### Direct Instruction — What Makes an Argument? (10 minutes)

Write on the board:

**Claim → Reasons → Evidence → Counterclaim → Rebuttal**

Walk through each part briefly using a relatable example:

*"School should start later."*

- **Claim:** School should start no earlier than 8:30 a.m.
- **Reason:** Teenagers are scientifically shown to need more sleep.
- **Evidence:** The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends start times of 8:30 or later for middle and high schoolers.
- **Counterclaim:** Some argue earlier schedules help parents with work drop-off routines.
- **Rebuttal:** While that concern is valid, adjusting transportation and childcare options is more practical than sacrificing student health.

Point out that the **counterclaim** only exists because the writer was *curious* enough to ask: *"Who disagrees with me, and do they have a point?"* That curiosity makes the argument stronger, not weaker.

---

### Curiosity Activity — Two-Color Sticky Notes (10 minutes)

Give each student two different colored sticky notes.

Post two statements on the board:

1. *"Students should be allowed to choose their own homework assignments."*
2. *"Social media does more harm than good for teenagers."*

Students pick **one statement**. On the **first color** sticky note, they write their own position and one reason. On the **second color**, they write the strongest possible argument for the *opposite* side — trying to genuinely understand it, not mock it.

Pair students up. Partners share their sticky notes. Challenge them: *"Did your partner's opposing argument change your thinking even a little? Why or why not?"*

Debrief quickly as a class. Ask: *"What does it feel like to try to understand a view you disagree with? Is it uncomfortable? Useful?"*

Connect the feeling directly to curiosity: real curiosity means being open to being wrong.

---

### Guided Writing — Building the Argument (20 minutes)

Students now draft a short argument paragraph (or two) on the topic they chose. They must include:

- A clear **claim**
- At least **two reasons** with evidence or logical support
- A **counterclaim** that takes the other side seriously
- A **rebuttal** that responds thoughtfully

Encourage students to keep asking themselves the "curiosity questions" as they write:

- *"Why do I actually believe this?"*
- *"What am I assuming without checking?"*
- *"What would someone smarter than me say against this?"*

Circulate the room. When you see a student skip the counterclaim or write something dismissive like *"Some people think this, but they're wrong,"* kneel down and ask: *"Can you be more curious here? What's the real reason someone might believe that?"*

---

### Share & Reflect (7 minutes)

Ask two or three volunteers to read their counterclaim and rebuttal sections aloud — not their claim, specifically these sections.

After each one, class responds with a quick thumbs up if the writer seemed genuinely curious about the opposing view versus just dismissing it.

Close with this question for students to answer in writing (2–3 sentences in their notebook):

**"How does being curious make your argument stronger? Give one specific example from what you wrote today."**

---

### Closing (2 minutes)

Return to the mystery object. Tell students what it is. Ask: *"Did asking questions about it help you understand it better than if I'd just told you immediately?"*

**Leave them with this:** Every argument you'll ever write is a mystery object. The curious writer is always the one who figures it out first.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt and example paragraph  
- Sticky notes or index cards  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us become better thinkers and learners.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie or Thomas Edison) who used curiosity to discover or invent something important.  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different viewpoints — all skills important for writing strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our claims  
  - Consider other opinions or opposing claims  
- Tell students that today they will practice using curiosity to write an argument that is clear, logical, and fair.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- On the board, outline the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim:** Your main point or opinion  
  2. **Reasons:** Why you believe your claim is true  
  3. **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claims:** Recognizing other viewpoints  
  5. **Conclusion/Closing:** Restate your claim or summarize your argument  
- Read a short example paragraph aloud (provided on handout).  
- Highlight each part as you read it, showing how curiosity helped the writer find evidence and consider other opinions.

**4. Guided Practice: Generating Questions and Claims (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple, relatable argumentative prompt, for example:  
  *“Should school uniforms be required?”*  
- Ask students to brainstorm questions they are curious about related to this topic. Write some on the board (e.g., “Do uniforms improve student behavior?” “How do uniforms affect students’ self-expression?”).  
- Have students choose a side (for or against uniforms) and write a clear claim sentence in their notebooks.  
- Encourage them to use their curiosity questions to think about reasons and evidence.

**5. Independent Writing: Drafting an Argument Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Students write a paragraph supporting their claim, including:  
  - At least two reasons with evidence  
  - One sentence acknowledging an opposing claim  
  - A concluding sentence  
- Remind them to organize their ideas logically and use curiosity to explore different viewpoints.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each, ask the class:  
  - What curious questions might the writer have asked?  
  - How did the writer acknowledge other opinions?  
- End with a brief discussion:  
  *“How does being curious help us become better writers and thinkers?”*

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to find an article or video about a topic they are curious about and write down two questions they have after learning about it. They will bring these questions to the next class to discuss how curiosity leads to deeper understanding.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson encourages students to embrace curiosity as a tool for inquiry and critical thinking, which strengthens their ability to write clear, evidence-based arguments. By practicing questioning, considering multiple viewpoints, and organizing their reasoning, students meet the CCSS writing standard while developing a lifelong skill.
