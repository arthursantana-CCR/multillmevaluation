## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade:** 7 | **Time:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Sticky notes (2 per student)
- Lined paper or notebooks
- "Mystery Object" — a sealed envelope labeled *DO NOT OPEN*

---

## Lesson

### HOOK — The Envelope (8 minutes)

Place a sealed envelope on the front desk before students arrive. Write on the board: *What's inside? You have 3 minutes to write your best guess and why.*

Let students wonder. Let them argue with each other. After three minutes of buzzing curiosity, open it — inside is a card that reads: **"A really great argument."**

When students react (confusion, laughter, groaning), pause and ask: *"What just happened in your brain when you didn't know what was inside?"*

Guide them toward the word **curiosity**. Explain: that restless, hungry feeling — that *need* to know — is one of the most powerful tools a writer has. Today, they're going to use it.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Makes an Argument? (10 minutes)

Write on the board:

> **Claim → Reasons → Evidence → Counterclaim → Rebuttal**

Tell students: a great argument doesn't start with an answer. It starts with a *question* you genuinely want to explore.

Walk through each component briefly using a relatable example:

- **Claim:** Schools should have a later start time.
- **Reason:** Teenagers are biologically wired to sleep later.
- **Evidence:** Studies show teen brains release melatonin later than adult brains.
- **Counterclaim:** Some say later school disrupts after-school activities and parents' work schedules.
- **Rebuttal:** However, a well-rested student performs better academically, which matters more long-term.

Emphasize: the **counterclaim** is where curiosity lives. Ask students: *"What would someone who disagrees think? Can you get genuinely curious about their side — not just to defeat it, but to understand it?"*

A writer who only knows their own side writes a weak argument. A curious writer understands *everyone's* side.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — The Sticky Note Debate (15 minutes)

Give each student two sticky notes — one blue (or any color), one yellow.

Post three debatable questions on the board:
1. Should students be allowed to use phones during lunch?
2. Is homework necessary for learning?
3. Should junk food be banned from school cafeterias?

Students choose **one question**. On the blue sticky note, they write their claim and one reason. On the yellow sticky note, they write the *strongest possible argument against their own position* — not a weak version, the best version.

Students post both notes in two columns on the board under their chosen question.

Read a few aloud. Point out when a counterclaim is genuinely strong. Ask the class: *"Does knowing this argument make your original claim harder or easier to defend?"*

Answer: harder — and that's exactly the point. Curiosity means sitting with difficulty instead of avoiding it.

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — The 15-Minute Argument (15 minutes)

Students now write a short structured argument (approximately one paragraph per section) using this framework:

1. **Opening claim** — state your position clearly
2. **Two reasons with evidence** — explain *why*, using facts, examples, or logical reasoning
3. **Counterclaim** — honestly present the opposing view
4. **Rebuttal** — respond thoughtfully, not dismissively
5. **Closing** — reinforce your claim

Encourage them to pick a topic they have **genuine questions about**, not just one they already feel certain about. A little uncertainty makes for better writing.

Circulate the room. When students seem stuck, ask: *"What would your smartest critic say to you right now?"* That question usually unlocks the counterclaim.

---

### CLOSING — The Curiosity Reflection (7 minutes)

Bring the class back together. Ask two or three volunteers to share their counterclaim section — specifically.

Then pose this final question for a brief class discussion:

*"Did writing the other side change your thinking at all — even slightly?"*

Most will say yes. Close with this idea: **Curiosity isn't weakness. In argument writing, it's your greatest strength.** The writers who ask the hardest questions, who genuinely wonder if they might be wrong, write the most convincing arguments — because readers trust them.

Return to the envelope. Ask: *"Would this lesson have worked if I'd just told you what was inside from the start?"*

No. The question pulled you in. **That's what good arguments do.**

---

### Exit Ticket
On a scrap of paper: *Write one question about your topic you still don't fully know the answer to.*

Collect and celebrate the uncertainty.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt and example paragraph  
- Sticky notes or index cards  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us become better thinkers and learners.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci) who used curiosity to discover or create something important.  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different viewpoints — all skills important for writing strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our claims  
  - Consider other opinions or opposing claims  
- Tell students that today they will practice using curiosity to write an argument that is clear, logical, and fair.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- On the board, outline the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim:** Your main point or opinion  
  2. **Reasons:** Why you believe your claim is true  
  3. **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claims:** Recognizing other viewpoints  
  5. **Conclusion:** A sentence that wraps up your argument logically  
- Read a short example paragraph aloud (provided on handout).  
- Highlight each part as you read it, showing how curiosity helped the writer ask questions and find evidence.

**4. Guided Practice: Generating Questions and Claims (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple, relatable prompt for argument writing, for example:  
  *“Should school start later in the morning?”*  
- Ask students to brainstorm questions they have about this topic on sticky notes or index cards (e.g., “How does sleep affect learning?” “What do students and teachers think about start times?”).  
- Collect a few questions and discuss how these questions show curiosity and can help find reasons and evidence.  
- Next, have students write a clear claim related to the prompt (e.g., “School should start later because students need more sleep to focus.”).

**5. Independent Writing: Drafting an Argumentative Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Students write a paragraph supporting their claim using:  
  - At least two reasons  
  - Evidence or examples to back up each reason  
  - One sentence acknowledging an opposing claim (e.g., “Some people think starting school early helps with after-school activities, but…”).  
- Encourage students to organize their ideas logically and use curiosity-driven questions to find strong reasons and evidence.

**6. Sharing and Reflecting (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each reading, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing claim.  
- Discuss how curiosity helped the writer develop their argument.

**7. Closing Discussion (5 minutes)**  
- Ask students: *“How can being curious help you in writing and in other parts of your life?”*  
- Summarize key points: Curiosity leads to better questions, stronger evidence, and fairer arguments.  
- Encourage students to keep practicing curiosity in their writing and learning.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Write a second argumentative paragraph on a different topic of their choice, using curiosity to guide their reasoning and evidence.

---

**End of Lesson**
