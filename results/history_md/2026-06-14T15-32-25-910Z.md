## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, and justify their reasoning using critical thinking.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Lemonade Stand" handout, exit ticket slips, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two recipes on the board:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups lemon juice + 6 cups water
- **Recipe B:** 3 cups lemon juice + 8 cups water

Ask: *"If you doubled Recipe A, what would you need? Are these recipes proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must clarify what "proportional" means before answering. Push them to define terms precisely (e.g., "the ratio stays constant"). 
*Assessment:* Listen for students who move from vague statements ("they're similar") to precise ones ("the ratio of lemon to water is 1:3 in Recipe A but 3:8 in B, so only A is proportional"). Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Introduce proportional relationships using four representations of the same scenario: *"A bike travels 15 miles every hour."*

- **Table:** Hours (1, 2, 3, 4) vs. Miles (15, 30, 45, 60)
- **Equation:** y = 15x
- **Graph:** A straight line through the origin
- **Verbal:** "Miles is 15 times the hours."

Explicitly highlight the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule that proportional graphs pass through (0,0).

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Model how the same relationship is organized across representations using a four-quadrant graphic organizer on the board.
*Assessment:* Have students replicate the organizer in notebooks for a new scenario ("$4 per smoothie"). Check that all four representations align logically.

### 3. Guided Practice: "Detective Work" (10 minutes)
Distribute four data sets (two proportional, two non-proportional). Students work in pairs to determine which are proportional and justify using **multiple representations**.

Example sets:
- Set 1: (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9) ✓
- Set 2: (1, 2), (2, 5), (3, 8) ✗
- Set 3: A graph passing through origin
- Set 4: A table claiming "$5 entrance fee + $2 per ride"

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** Students must verify each claim by cross-checking using at least two methods (ratio test AND graph test).
*Assessment:* Collect written justifications. Look for students who use **two or more methods** to confirm validity rather than relying on a single check.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Ask: *"Which representation gave you the clearest evidence? Why?"*
*Assessment:* In pair discussions, listen for students who evaluate which representation is most authoritative for a given situation (e.g., "the equation form let me test any value, not just the ones in the table").

### 4. Collaborative Task: Lemonade Stand Decision (15 minutes)
**Scenario:** Maya is choosing between two suppliers.
- **Supplier A:** Charges proportionally — $2 per pound of sugar.
- **Supplier B:** Charges $5 flat fee + $1.50 per pound.

Groups of 3 must:
1. Build a table, graph, and equation for each.
2. Decide which supplier Maya should use if she buys 4 lbs, 10 lbs, or 20 lbs.
3. Write a recommendation.

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Groups must analyze trade-offs across different purchase amounts.
*Assessment:* Read group recommendations. Look for systematic comparison ("Supplier B is cheaper after 10 lbs because…") rather than a single-quantity judgment.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students must justify their final recommendation using mathematical evidence.
*Assessment:* Use a rubric: Does the reasoning identify the break-even point? Does it anticipate future scenarios (e.g., "If Maya expands, Supplier B becomes more economical")? Strong responses anticipate complexity.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Each student completes:
1. One proportional relationship problem (identify k and write the equation).
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"What strategy did you use to decide if a relationship was proportional? Did your thinking change during the lesson? What would you do differently next time?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The metacognitive prompt requires students to describe and evaluate their own reasoning.
*Assessment:* Score reflections on a 0–2 scale:
- **2:** Names a specific strategy shift (e.g., "At first I only checked one ratio; now I check all of them and verify with the graph passing through origin").
- **1:** Mentions reasoning but without deep reflection.
- **0:** No reflection evidence.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-made tables for struggling students.
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to construct a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain the trick.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price tag, etc.). Represent it in all four forms and write one sentence reflecting on how you verified it was proportional.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Great Pizza Debate: Proving Proportional Relationships
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to recognize and represent proportional relationships using multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking skills to validate mathematical claims, organize data, and justify their reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook and Clarification (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces a real-world verbal description: "A local pizzeria claims their pricing is perfectly proportional. Their menu states: 1 slice is $2.50, 3 slices are $7.50, 5 slices are $12.00, and 8 slices are $20.00." The teacher asks the class to break down this verbal description into mathematical facts. 

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students work in pairs to clarify the pizzeria’s verbal claim by identifying the relevant quantities (slices and cost) and calculating the unit rate for each menu item to see if a constant ratio exists. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher circulates, listening for students who precisely clarify the mathematical definition of proportionality. Mastery is observed when students state, "To be proportional, every slice must cost exactly $2.50. The 5-slice deal breaks this rule because $12.00 divided by 5 is $2.40."

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Data (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are handed a blank worksheet with a table, a coordinate plane, and an equation box. They must translate the pizzeria's verbal menu into these three new representations.

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students arrange the raw menu data into a logical, chronological input/output table (x = slices, y = cost) and then plot these ordered pairs on a coordinate graph.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses student worksheets to ensure the information is organized with a strong logical flow—checking that the x-axis and y-axis are properly labeled, scaled consistently, and that the data points are plotted accurately to reveal the break in the straight line.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students cross-check the pizzeria's printed menu (Source 1) against their newly created mathematical graph (Source 2) and their unit rate calculations (Source 3) to rigorously test the validity of the restaurant's "perfectly proportional" claim.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher observes group discussions, looking for students who explicitly point out the discrepancy between the sources. A successful assessment occurs when a student notes, "The graph bends at 5 slices, and the unit rate changes. By cross-checking the math against the menu, we can prove the menu's claim is invalid."

### **Part 3: Quality of Evidence and Trade-offs (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces a *new* scenario: A competing pizzeria, "Proportion Pete’s," charges exactly $3.00 per slice, no matter what. Students are given three pieces of "evidence" proving Pete's pricing is proportional: a messy hand-drawn diagram, a partial table, and the equation *y = 3x*. 

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students evaluate the three pieces of evidence and must select the highest-quality, most authoritative mathematical representation to prove Pete's proportionality to the class.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses this by having students vote and defend their choice. Mastery is demonstrated when students show sophisticated judgment by selecting the equation *y = 3x*, explaining that it is the most rigorous and universally applicable proof compared to a limited table or a messy diagram.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity:** The teacher asks, "If you needed to find the cost of 1,000 slices for a school dance, which representation (table, graph, or equation) would you choose?" Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each option.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Students write a short paragraph weighing the pros and cons. The teacher evaluates these responses, looking for comprehensive analysis (e.g., "A graph is great for a quick visual check of a straight line through the origin, but extending a graph to 1,000 is impractical. The equation *y = 3x* is the best choice because it allows for instant, precise calculation of large numbers").

### **Part 4: Decision Making and Reflection (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students act as "Mathematical Health Inspectors." They are given one final, complex scenario involving a catering company’s delivery fee plus a per-pizza cost. They must decide if the total cost is proportional to the number of pizzas ordered.

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students write a final "verdict" letter to the catering company. They must use sound mathematical reasoning to explain why a starting delivery fee (a y-intercept other than zero) means the relationship is not proportional, even if the cost per pizza remains constant.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher collects the verdict letters, assessing them for sophisticated reasoning. Mastery is shown when students avoid faulty logic (e.g., "It goes up by $10 every time, so it's proportional") and instead correctly argue that the graph will not pass through the origin (0,0), thus violating the rule of proportionality.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Before the bell rings, students complete a Metacognitive Exit Ticket with the prompt: "How did your understanding of proportional relationships change today? What initial assumptions did you have to adjust?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reads the exit tickets to gauge self-awareness. A high-level reflection will demonstrate strategic adjustment, such as, "Initially, I thought any relationship where numbers increase steadily was proportional. I had to adjust my thinking to realize that the ratio between y and x must remain exactly the same, and the graph must start at zero."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** Middle School, Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain their reasoning using mathematical evidence.
4. Compare different representations and choose which one best supports their conclusion.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Rulers  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Procedure

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes

Display this situation:

> “A smoothie shop sells 2 smoothies for $6, 4 smoothies for $12, and 6 smoothies for $18.”

Ask students:

- What do you notice?
- What is the cost per smoothie?
- How could we organize this information?

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite a few students to explain.

Guide students to see that the cost per smoothie is always $3, so the relationship is proportional.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students identify the key quantities: number of smoothies and total cost.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for students accurately naming the two variables and explaining the meaning of each number. Evidence may sound like: “The number of smoothies changes, and the total cost changes with it.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities stays constant. This constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by `k`.

Use the smoothie example:

| Smoothies | Cost |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

Ask students to calculate:

- 6 ÷ 2 = 3  
- 12 ÷ 4 = 3  
- 18 ÷ 6 = 3  

Since the ratio is always 3, the equation is:

> `y = 3x`

Explain that proportional relationships can be represented in several ways:

- **Table:** constant ratio  
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin  
- **Equation:** `y = kx`  
- **Diagram:** equal groups or repeated scaling  
- **Verbal description:** “Each smoothie costs $3.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students organize the same relationship into multiple representations.  
**Observe/assess:** Check whether students can keep the quantities consistent across table, equation, graph, and words.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Proportional and Non-Proportional Relationships — 12 minutes

Give students three examples on a handout.

**Example A**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |

**Example B**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 7 |
| 3 | 10 |

**Example C**

A graph showing a straight line through the origin.

Students work in pairs to decide whether each relationship is proportional. They must justify their decision using at least two forms of evidence, such as a table and equation, or a graph and verbal explanation.

Discuss answers as a class:

- Example A is proportional because `y ÷ x = 5`.
- Example B is not proportional because the ratio is not constant.
- Example C is proportional if the line is straight and passes through `(0, 0)`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students check whether their conclusion is valid by using more than one representation.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for students cross-checking: “The table has a constant ratio, and the equation would be `y = 5x`, so it is proportional.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students decide which evidence is most useful for proving proportionality.  
**Observe/assess:** Ask: “Which representation gives the strongest evidence?” Strong responses mention constant ratios, origin on graph, or equation form `y = kx`.

---

### 4. Group Task: Represent One Relationship Five Ways — 15 minutes

Present this scenario:

> “A printer prints 12 pages in 3 minutes. It continues printing at the same rate.”

In groups of 3–4, students must represent the relationship in five ways:

1. **Table**
2. **Graph**
3. **Equation**
4. **Diagram**
5. **Verbal description**

Students should determine the rate:

> 12 pages ÷ 3 minutes = 4 pages per minute

Possible equation:

> `y = 4x`, where `x` is minutes and `y` is pages printed.

Students create a small poster or notebook display.

After 8 minutes, groups compare their representation with another group. They must identify one strength and one possible limitation of a representation. For example, a graph shows the pattern visually, but a table may be easier for exact values.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
**Targeted moment:** Students compare representations and discuss advantages and disadvantages.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for comments such as: “The graph is good for seeing the pattern, but the equation is faster for finding any value.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
**Targeted moment:** Students decide whether their relationship is proportional and justify it logically.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for complete reasoning: “It is proportional because the rate is always 4 pages per minute, the equation is `y = 4x`, and the graph goes through the origin.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Bring the class together. Ask:

- How can a table show proportionality?
- How can a graph show proportionality?
- Why does the equation need to look like `y = kx`?
- Which representation helped you understand the relationship best?

Emphasize that no single representation is always best; mathematicians choose representations based on the question they are trying to answer.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A bicycle travels 15 miles in 3 hours at a constant speed. Write an equation for the relationship between hours and miles.
2. Create a table with at least three values.
3. Explain how you know the relationship is proportional.
4. Reflection: Which representation helped you most today, and why?

Expected equation:

> `y = 5x`

where `x` is hours and `y` is miles.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
**Targeted moment:** Students reflect on which representation helped them reason most clearly.  
**Observe/assess:** Read student reflections for metacognition, such as: “The table helped me because I could divide miles by hours each time and check that the ratio stayed the same.”

---

## Assessment

Students are assessed through:

- Partner explanations during guided practice
- Group representation task
- Class discussion responses
- Exit ticket accuracy and reflection

Look for evidence that students can identify constant ratios, connect representations, justify proportionality, and explain their thinking clearly.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** Middle School, Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain their reasoning using mathematical evidence.
4. Compare different representations and choose which one best supports their conclusion.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Rulers  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Procedure

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes

Display this situation:

> “A smoothie shop sells 2 smoothies for $6, 4 smoothies for $12, and 6 smoothies for $18.”

Ask students:

- What do you notice?
- What is the cost per smoothie?
- How could we organize this information?

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite a few students to explain.

Guide students to see that the cost per smoothie is always $3, so the relationship is proportional.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students identify the key quantities: number of smoothies and total cost.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for students accurately naming the two variables and explaining the meaning of each number. Evidence may sound like: “The number of smoothies changes, and the total cost changes with it.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities stays constant. This constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by `k`.

Use the smoothie example:

| Smoothies | Cost |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

Ask students to calculate:

- 6 ÷ 2 = 3  
- 12 ÷ 4 = 3  
- 18 ÷ 6 = 3  

Since the ratio is always 3, the equation is:

> `y = 3x`

Explain that proportional relationships can be represented in several ways:

- **Table:** constant ratio  
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin  
- **Equation:** `y = kx`  
- **Diagram:** equal groups or repeated scaling  
- **Verbal description:** “Each smoothie costs $3.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students organize the same relationship into multiple representations.  
**Observe/assess:** Check whether students can keep the quantities consistent across table, equation, graph, and words.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Proportional and Non-Proportional Relationships — 12 minutes

Give students three examples on a handout.

**Example A**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |

**Example B**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 7 |
| 3 | 10 |

**Example C**

A graph showing a straight line through the origin.

Students work in pairs to decide whether each relationship is proportional. They must justify their decision using at least two forms of evidence, such as a table and equation, or a graph and verbal explanation.

Discuss answers as a class:

- Example A is proportional because `y ÷ x = 5`.
- Example B is not proportional because the ratio is not constant.
- Example C is proportional if the line is straight and passes through `(0, 0)`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students check whether their conclusion is valid by using more than one representation.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for students cross-checking: “The table has a constant ratio, and the equation would be `y = 5x`, so it is proportional.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
**Targeted moment:** Students decide which evidence is most useful for proving proportionality.  
**Observe/assess:** Ask: “Which representation gives the strongest evidence?” Strong responses mention constant ratios, origin on graph, or equation form `y = kx`.

---

### 4. Group Task: Represent One Relationship Five Ways — 15 minutes

Present this scenario:

> “A printer prints 12 pages in 3 minutes. It continues printing at the same rate.”

In groups of 3–4, students must represent the relationship in five ways:

1. **Table**
2. **Graph**
3. **Equation**
4. **Diagram**
5. **Verbal description**

Students should determine the rate:

> 12 pages ÷ 3 minutes = 4 pages per minute

Possible equation:

> `y = 4x`, where `x` is minutes and `y` is pages printed.

Students create a small poster or notebook display.

After 8 minutes, groups compare their representation with another group. They must identify one strength and one possible limitation of a representation. For example, a graph shows the pattern visually, but a table may be easier for exact values.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
**Targeted moment:** Students compare representations and discuss advantages and disadvantages.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for comments such as: “The graph is good for seeing the pattern, but the equation is faster for finding any value.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
**Targeted moment:** Students decide whether their relationship is proportional and justify it logically.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for complete reasoning: “It is proportional because the rate is always 4 pages per minute, the equation is `y = 4x`, and the graph goes through the origin.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Bring the class together. Ask:

- How can a table show proportionality?
- How can a graph show proportionality?
- Why does the equation need to look like `y = kx`?
- Which representation helped you understand the relationship best?

Emphasize that no single representation is always best; mathematicians choose representations based on the question they are trying to answer.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A bicycle travels 15 miles in 3 hours at a constant speed. Write an equation for the relationship between hours and miles.
2. Create a table with at least three values.
3. Explain how you know the relationship is proportional.
4. Reflection: Which representation helped you most today, and why?

Expected equation:

> `y = 5x`

where `x` is hours and `y` is miles.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
**Targeted moment:** Students reflect on which representation helped them reason most clearly.  
**Observe/assess:** Read student reflections for metacognition, such as: “The table helped me because I could divide miles by hours each time and check that the ratio stayed the same.”

- Group representation task
- Class discussion responses
- Exit ticket accuracy and reflection

Look for evidence that students can identify constant ratios, connect representations, justify proportionality, and explain their thinking clearly.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, and justify their reasoning using critical thinking.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Lemonade Stand" handout, exit ticket slips, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display a pizzeria's claim: *"Our pricing is perfectly proportional: 1 slice = $2.50, 3 slices = $7.50, 5 slices = $12.00, 8 slices = $20.00."* Ask: *"Is this claim true? How would you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must clarify what "proportional" means before answering. Push them to define terms precisely (e.g., "the ratio stays constant").
*Assessment:* Listen for students moving from vague ("the prices look similar") to precise ("every slice must cost exactly the same — $12 ÷ 5 = $2.40, which breaks the rule"). Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Introduce proportional relationships using **five representations** of the scenario *"A bike travels 15 miles every hour."*

- **Table:** Hours (1, 2, 3, 4) vs. Miles (15, 30, 45, 60)
- **Equation:** y = 15x
- **Graph:** A straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Equal-group bars showing repeated scaling
- **Verbal:** "Miles is 15 times the hours."

Explicitly highlight the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule that proportional graphs pass through (0,0).

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Model how the same relationship is organized across representations using a graphic organizer on the board.
*Assessment:* Have students replicate the organizer in notebooks for "$4 per smoothie." Check that all five representations align logically with consistent labels and scales.

### 3. Guided Practice: "Detective Work" (10 minutes)
Distribute four data sets (two proportional, two not). Pairs determine which are proportional and justify using **multiple representations**.

- Set 1: (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9) ✓
- Set 2: (1, 2), (2, 5), (3, 8) ✗
- Set 3: A graph passing through the origin
- Set 4: A table claiming "$5 entrance fee + $2 per ride"

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** Students cross-check each claim using at least two methods (ratio test AND graph test).
*Assessment:* Collect written justifications. Look for students who use two or more methods rather than a single check (e.g., "The unit rates differ AND the graph would not pass through the origin").

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Ask: *"Which representation gave you the strongest evidence? Why?"*
*Assessment:* Listen for evaluations of authoritativeness (e.g., "The equation lets me test any value, not just the ones in the table"). Strong responses select y = kx as the most rigorous proof.

### 4. Collaborative Task: Lemonade Stand Decision (15 minutes)
**Scenario:** Maya is choosing between two sugar suppliers.
- **Supplier A:** $2 per pound (proportional).
- **Supplier B:** $5 flat fee + $1.50 per pound.

Groups of 3 must:
1. Build a table, graph, and equation for each supplier.
2. Decide which Maya should use for 4 lbs, 10 lbs, or 20 lbs.
3. Write a recommendation "verdict letter."

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Groups analyze trade-offs across purchase amounts and representations.
*Assessment:* Read recommendations for systematic comparison ("B is cheaper after 10 lbs because…") rather than single-quantity judgments. Listen for comments like "The equation is faster for large values, but the graph reveals the break-even point."

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence, correctly arguing that Supplier B is not proportional because the graph does not pass through (0,0).
*Assessment:* Use a rubric: Does the reasoning identify the break-even point? Does it avoid faulty logic (e.g., "B goes up by the same amount, so it's proportional")? Does it anticipate future scenarios?

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Each student completes:
1. One proportional problem (identify k, write the equation, sketch a quick diagram).
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"What strategy did you use to decide if a relationship was proportional? Did your thinking change during the lesson? What would you do differently next time?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The metacognitive prompt requires students to evaluate their own reasoning.
*Assessment:* Score reflections 0–2:
- **2:** Names a specific strategy shift ("At first I assumed any steady increase was proportional; now I verify the ratio AND that the graph passes through the origin").
- **1:** Mentions reasoning without deep reflection.
- **0:** No reflection evidence.

- **Support:** Provide pre-made tables for struggling students.
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to construct a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain the trick.
