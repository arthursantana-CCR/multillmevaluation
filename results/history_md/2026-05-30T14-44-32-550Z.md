## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **A:** "A bakery sells 3 muffins for $6, 6 muffins for $12, 9 muffins for $18."
- **B:** "A taxi charges $5 to start, plus $2 per mile."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Guide them toward the definition: a proportional relationship has a constant ratio (y/x = k) and passes through the origin.

**→ Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what makes a relationship proportional vs. non-proportional. **Assessment:** Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "passes through origin"). Note whether students distinguish the two scenarios with clear reasoning vs. vague guesses.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Using the muffin example, model all five representations on the board:
- **Verbal:** "Each muffin costs $2."
- **Table:** Muffins (1,2,3,4) → Cost ($2,$4,$6,$8)
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin, slope 2
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 1 muffin = $2 block

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k)** and how it appears in each form.

**→ Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy the five representations into a graphic organizer (T-chart with labels). **Assessment:** Collect organizers; check that students arrange representations clearly, label k consistently, and show logical connections between forms (e.g., arrows linking the slope to k to the unit rate).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Smoothie Shop Investigation" (15 minutes)
Distribute handout with three smoothie shops:
- **Shop X:** Verbal description ("$3 per smoothie")
- **Shop Y:** Table (1→$4, 2→$7, 3→$10)
- **Shop Z:** Graph (line through origin, passing through (2, $5))

Students work in pairs to:
1. Determine which shops are proportional.
2. Identify k for the proportional ones.
3. Write equations for each.
4. Decide which shop offers the best deal for buying 8 smoothies.

**→ Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity) & CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must verify whether each representation truly shows proportionality by cross-checking ratios in tables, testing the origin on graphs, and confirming equations match. **Assessment:** Circulate and ask probing questions: *"How do you know Shop Y isn't proportional? Did you check more than one ratio?"* Look for students who systematically test multiple data points rather than relying on a single check.

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** The "best deal for 8 smoothies" question forces comparison across shops. **Assessment:** Review written justifications. Level 2 responses will compare cost per smoothie *and* consider implications (e.g., "Shop Z is cheapest per unit, but only proportional shops let us predict reliably"). Level 1 responses pick a shop with limited comparison.

---

### 4. Independent Practice & Reasoning Challenge (12 minutes)
Pose this problem on the board:
*"Maria claims that if a table shows the ratio 6:9 and another row shows 10:15, the relationship must be proportional. Do you agree? Justify using at least two representations."*

Students work individually, writing a justification with a table check AND either a graph or equation.

**→ Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must construct a logical argument using multiple representations. **Assessment:** Collect work. Level 2 responses confirm proportionality by checking that 6/9 = 10/15 = 2/3, write y = (2/3)x, AND note the graph would pass through the origin. Level 1 responses verify one ratio without connecting representations. Watch for faulty logic (e.g., claiming proportionality because "the numbers go up").

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (9 minutes)

**Metacognitive Reflection (5 min):** On the exit ticket, students respond to:
1. Solve: "A car travels 150 miles in 3 hours at a constant rate. Write an equation and predict the distance in 7 hours."
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, verbal, diagram) helps YOU understand proportional relationships best, and why? What was one mistake or confusion you had today, and how did you work through it?"*

**→ Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** **Assessment:** Read reflections for evidence of metacognition. Level 2 responses identify a specific moment of struggle (e.g., "I first thought Shop Y was proportional because I only checked one ratio—then I realized I needed to check all of them") and describe strategy adjustment. Level 1 responses state a preference without explaining reasoning. Level 0 responses leave it blank or write surface-level comments.

**Closing (4 min):** Share 2–3 student reflections aloud. Reinforce: proportional relationships are everywhere, and recognizing them across representations builds flexible mathematical thinking.

---

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price, speed). Represent it in all five forms.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.
3. Explain how they know a relationship is or is not proportional using evidence such as constant ratios, unit rate, and graphs through the origin.

### Materials
- Board/projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and word problems  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 8 | 16 | 24 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 10 | 18 | 26 | 34 |

Ask students to answer silently:  
- Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
- How do you know?

Then students turn and talk with a partner. Invite two students to share.

Emphasize: “A proportional relationship has equivalent ratios. In a table, the ratio between corresponding quantities stays constant. On a graph, the points lie on a straight line through the origin. In an equation, it can be written as `y = kx`, where `k` is the constant of proportionality.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify the important information in each table and explain which quantities are being compared.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for precise statements such as, “I compared dollars to hours, and Table A has a constant rate of $8 per hour.” Assign level -1 to 2 based on whether students clarify correctly, partially, or misleadingly.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Multiple Representations — 10 minutes
Use the situation: “A bike rental costs $6 per hour.”

Guide students through five representations:

1. **Verbal description:** The cost is $6 for every 1 hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours, x | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost, y | 0 | 6 | 12 | 18 | 24 |

3. **Equation:** `y = 6x`
4. **Graph:** Plot points `(0,0), (1,6), (2,12), (3,18), (4,24)`. Draw the straight line.
5. **Diagram:** Use a double number line showing hours on top and dollars below.

Ask: “How do all five representations show the same relationship?” Students write one sentence in notebooks.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students connect the same relationship across a table, graph, equation, diagram, and words.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether students arrange their notes logically and connect representations, such as “The table gives points for the graph, and the equation shows the cost for any number of hours.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Representations — 12 minutes
Give each pair a handout with mixed representations. Examples:

- Table: `x: 2, 4, 6`; `y: 5, 10, 15`
- Equation: `y = 2.5x`
- Graph: straight line through origin with points `(2,5), (4,10)`
- Verbal: “A recipe uses 5 cups of flour for every 2 batches.”

Students match representations that belong to the same proportional relationship. They must write the constant of proportionality for each set.

Then include one “trap” representation, such as a graph that is a straight line but does not pass through the origin, or a table with a nonconstant ratio.

Ask students to justify one match and one rejection.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students check whether representations truly match by comparing ratios, graph features, and equations.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students cross-checking across at least two representations, such as, “The graph and table both show 2.5 as the unit rate, so they match.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students decide which information is useful for proving proportionality and which is insufficient or misleading.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Students should prioritize reliable mathematical evidence: constant ratio, `y = kx`, and graph through `(0,0)`. Listen for rejection of weak reasoning like “It looks close.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Choose the Better Deal — 12 minutes
Present this scenario:

“You need to buy sports drink mix for a team. Store A sells 3 bottles for $9. Store B sells 5 bottles for $14. Store C sells 4 bottles for $13.”

Students work with partners to:
1. Make a table of bottles and cost.
2. Find the unit rate for each store.
3. Represent at least one store with an equation.
4. Decide which store is the best deal.
5. Explain their reasoning in complete sentences.

Students must also answer: “Is every store’s pricing proportional? Why or why not?” Clarify that if each store charges the same price per bottle within that store, the relationship can be modeled proportionally, but different stores may have different constants of proportionality.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students compare Store A, B, and C using unit rates.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for comparison beyond one calculation, such as “Store B is $2.80 per bottle, Store A is $3.00, and Store C is $3.25, so Store B is cheapest.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students justify their final choice using proportional reasoning.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether their decision follows logically from evidence, not preference. Strong evidence: correct unit rates, accurate comparison, and a clear conclusion.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes
Invite pairs to share their best-deal conclusions. Record their unit rates on the board. Ask:
- “What made this comparison fair?”
- “Why is unit rate useful?”
- “Could two students use different representations and still reach the same conclusion?”

Reinforce that proportional relationships can be represented in multiple ways, but the constant of proportionality connects them all.

---

### 6. Individual Reflection and Exit Ticket — 8 minutes
Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A taxi company charges $4 per mile. Complete a table for 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 miles.  
2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. Explain how the graph would look.  
4. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
5. Reflection: “At first I thought ___, but then I realized ___ because ___.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students reflect on how their understanding changed or how they checked their work.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for metacognitive statements, such as “I first only looked for a pattern, but then I checked the unit rate to be sure.”

### 3. Guided Practice: "Smoothie Shop Investigation" (15 minutes)
Distribute a handout with three smoothie shops:
- **Shop X (verbal):** "$3 per smoothie"
- **Shop Y (table):** 1→$4, 2→$7, 3→$10
- **Shop Z (graph):** Line through origin passing through (2, $5)

Include one "trap": a graph that is a straight line but does NOT pass through the origin.

Pairs must: (1) determine which shops are proportional, (2) identify k for each proportional one, (3) write equations, and (4) decide which shop offers the best deal for 8 smoothies.

**→ Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check multiple ratios in the table, test the origin on graphs, and confirm equations match. **Assessment:** Circulate and probe: *"How do you know Shop Y isn't proportional? Did you check more than one ratio?"* Look for systematic checking across representations.

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** The "best deal" question forces comparison across shops and representations. **Assessment:** Level 2 responses compare cost per smoothie *and* consider trade-offs (e.g., "Shop Z is cheapest per unit, but Shop Y's pricing isn't proportional so we can't reliably predict cost for 8"). Level 1 responses pick a shop with superficial comparison.

---

### 4. Independent Reasoning Challenge (12 minutes)
Pose this problem:
*"The t-shirt company charges $5 per shirt AND a one-time $20 shipping fee. Is this relationship proportional? Justify using at least two representations (table, graph, or equation)."*

Students work individually, building a table, checking ratios, and either graphing or writing an equation.

**→ Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must recognize that the $20 fee shifts the line off the origin, breaking proportionality even though the per-unit rate is constant. **Assessment:** Level 2 responses anticipate the complexity: "y = 5x + 20 is not y = kx; the graph crosses the y-axis at 20, not the origin; ratios like 25/1 and 30/2 are not equal." Level 1 responses verify only one feature. Watch for faulty logic ("it's proportional because each shirt still costs $5").

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (11 minutes)

**Exit Ticket (7 min):** 
1. A car travels 150 miles in 3 hours at a constant rate. Write an equation and predict the distance in 7 hours.
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"At first I thought ___, but then I realized ___ because ___. Which representation helps YOU best understand proportional relationships, and what will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"*

**→ Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** **Assessment:** Read for metacognition. Level 2 responses identify a specific moment of struggle and strategy adjustment (e.g., "I first only looked for a pattern, but then I realized I had to check both the unit rate AND whether the graph passes through the origin"). Level 1 responses state preferences without explanation.

**Closing (4 min):** Share 2–3 student reflections aloud. Reinforce: proportional relationships appear across many forms, and the constant of proportionality connects them all.
