## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Are the Best Evidence You Have

### A 55-Minute Lesson for 7th Grade ELA

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA / Argumentative Writing
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Curiosity Integration:** Students will practice using genuine curiosity as a tool for building stronger arguments — learning that the best arguers are not those with the loudest opinions, but those who ask the most interesting questions.

---

## LEARNING OBJECTIVES

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Identify the components of a strong written argument (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)
2. Generate curious, open-ended questions as a strategy for finding evidence and strengthening claims
3. Acknowledge and respond to opposing viewpoints in writing
4. Draft a short argumentative paragraph using a logical structure

---

## MATERIALS NEEDED

- Whiteboard or projector
- Sticky notes (two colors — one per student of each color)
- "Curiosity Starter" handout (described in lesson)
- Lined paper or writer's notebooks
- Pencils/pens
- Optional: Timer for structured activities

---

## VOCABULARY TO POST ON THE BOARD

| Word | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | Your main argument — what you believe to be true |
| **Reason** | Why you believe your claim |
| **Evidence** | Facts, examples, or data that support your reason |
| **Counterclaim** | An opposing argument someone else might make |
| **Rebuttal** | Your response to the counterclaim |
| **Curiosity** | A strong desire to know or learn something |

---

## LESSON PLAN

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 1: THE HOOK — "THE WORST ARGUMENT EVER" (0–7 minutes)

**Purpose:** Grab attention, establish the problem, and introduce curiosity as a concept

**Teacher Script / Facilitation:**

Begin class by writing this statement on the board in large letters:

> *"Pizza is the best food. It just is. Anyone who disagrees is wrong."*

Ask the class: *"Raise your hand if you find this argument convincing."*

Pause. (Few or no hands will go up.)

Then ask: *"Why isn't this convincing? What's missing?"*

Take 2–3 student responses. Guide them toward noticing there are no reasons, no evidence, and no acknowledgment that other people might think differently.

Now write a second version on the board:

> *"Pizza is one of the most culturally significant foods in America. Because it can be adapted to nearly any dietary preference and is affordable for many families, it has become a shared food experience across different communities. Some might argue that other foods like tacos or sushi are equally significant — and they make a fair point. However, pizza's unique combination of accessibility, variety, and history gives it a strong case."*

Ask: *"What's different about this one?"*

After a brief discussion, introduce the key idea for today:

> **"The secret to writing a great argument isn't having a strong opinion. It's being curious enough to ask great questions — about your own side AND the other side."**

Write on the board: **CURIOSITY = The Arguer's Superpower**

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — BUILDING THE ARGUMENT (7–17 minutes)

**Purpose:** Teach the structure of a written argument explicitly

**Teacher introduces the CRERC framework** (write it on the board):

```
C — Claim
R — Reason
E — Evidence
R — (Acknowledge) Rival/Opposing Claim (Counterclaim)
C — Counter back (Rebuttal)
```

Walk through each component using a fun, relatable example. Use the following topic:

**Topic: "Middle schoolers should have a longer lunch period."**

Model each piece aloud:

- **Claim:** "Middle school students should have a longer lunch period — at least 40 minutes instead of the current 20."

- **Reason:** "Students need adequate time to eat, socialize, and mentally reset before afternoon classes."

- **Evidence:** "According to the American Academy of Pediatrics, students who feel rushed during meals are more likely to skip eating altogether, which negatively affects concentration and academic performance."

- **Counterclaim (pause here):** Ask students — *"What would someone who DISAGREES say? Be curious about the other side."* Take responses. Summarize a counterclaim: "Some administrators argue that a longer lunch period takes valuable time away from instruction."

- **Rebuttal:** "However, research consistently shows that well-rested and well-fed students actually absorb more during instruction, meaning the time is ultimately gained back, not lost."

**Check for understanding:** Ask students to turn to a partner and restate what each letter in CRERC stands for. (1 minute)

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 3: CURIOSITY SPOTLIGHT — "QUESTION STORMS" (17–28 minutes)

**Purpose:** Teach curiosity as a deliberate skill connected to argument writing

**Setup:**
Tell students: *"Before a good arguer writes a single sentence, they ask questions. Not just 'What do I think?' but 'What don't I know yet? What might someone else think? What proof would change my mind?'"*

**Introduce the three types of Curiosity Questions for arguing:**

Write on the board:

> 🔍 **Type 1 — Digging Deeper:** "What evidence exists that supports my claim?"
> 🔄 **Type 2 — Flipping It:** "What would someone who disagrees say, and why might they be right?"
> 🌍 **Type 3 — Zooming Out:** "Why does this topic even matter? Who is affected by it?"

**Activity — The Two-Color Sticky Note Challenge:**

Give each student one sticky note of each color. Assign the following topic (post it on the board):

> **"Students should be allowed to use their phones during school hours."**

- On **Color 1** sticky note: Write one Type 1 or Type 3 question (supporting the argument)
- On **Color 2** sticky note: Write one Type 2 question (flipping it / exploring the other side)

Give students 4 minutes to write independently.

Then have students post their sticky notes in two columns on the board (or on a designated wall space): one column labeled **"FOR"** and one labeled **"AGAINST / WONDERING."**

Do a quick gallery scan as a class — read a few aloud. Notice patterns. Ask:

- *"Did anyone's question make you think differently about the topic?"*
- *"Which questions would make the BEST evidence to look up?"*
- *"Did anyone write a question that made their OWN side more complicated? That's actually a sign of a strong arguer — not a weak one."*

**Bridge the skill:** Say — *"This is exactly what good writers do before they argue. They get curious first. The questions you just wrote are the building blocks of a great argumentative essay."*

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 4: GUIDED PRACTICE — ARGUMENT SKELETON (28–40 minutes)

**Purpose:** Students apply CRERC structure with teacher support

**Give students a Curiosity Starter Handout** (or have them copy this into their notebooks):

---

**CURIOSITY STARTER — ARGUMENT PLANNER**

**Topic:** *(Choose one: phones in school / longer lunch period / homework on weekends)*

**Step 1 — Get Curious First**
Before you write your argument, write THREE questions you genuinely want answered about this topic:
1. _______________________________________________
2. _______________________________________________
3. _______________________________________________

**Step 2 — Build Your Argument**

**My CLAIM is:**
_______________________________________________

**My REASON is:**
_______________________________________________

**My EVIDENCE is (or would be — what would prove this?):**
_______________________________________________

**A COUNTERCLAIM someone might make:**
_______________________________________________

**My REBUTTAL to that counterclaim:**
_______________________________________________

---

Give students **8 minutes** to complete the planner independently.

**Teacher circulates** and looks for:
- Students who state their claim too vaguely (prompt: *"Can you be more specific? What exactly are you arguing?"*)
- Students who struggle with counterclaims (prompt: *"Pretend you're your parents or your principal. What would they say against your argument?"*)
- Students who skip curiosity questions (prompt: *"What's something about this topic you genuinely don't know yet? Write that down."*)

After 8 minutes, pair students up. Each partner shares their planner and the other gives ONE piece of feedback using this sentence frame:

> *"Your counterclaim made me think _____________, and I wonder if you could strengthen your rebuttal by _______________."*

(2 minutes per partner = 4 minutes total)

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 5: INDEPENDENT WRITING — DRAFT PARAGRAPH (40–50 minutes)

**Purpose:** Students produce an original argumentative paragraph

**Directions** (post on board):

> Using your Curiosity Starter planner, write a complete argumentative paragraph of at least **8–10 sentences.** Your paragraph should include:
> ✅ A clear claim (opening sentence)
> ✅ At least one reason
> ✅ At least one piece of evidence or example
> ✅ An acknowledgment of an opposing viewpoint
> ✅ A rebuttal
> ✅ A closing sentence that reinforces your claim

Remind students: *"You don't have to be right. You have to be curious, logical, and honest about the other side."*

**Play soft background music if appropriate for your classroom culture.** Give students the full 10 minutes to write without interruption.

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 6: CLOSING — "THE CURIOSITY CONFESSION" (50–55 minutes)

**Purpose:** Reflect, synthesize, and connect curiosity to real-world thinking

**With 5 minutes left**, ask students to stop writing and put their pencils down.

Ask three closing questions in rapid succession — students respond by a show of hands or a quick written exit ticket (one sentence each):

1. *"What was ONE question you asked today that you didn't have before class started?"*

2. *"Did thinking about the other side make your argument weaker or stronger? How?"*

3. *"In your own words — what does curiosity have to do with writing a good argument?"*

Take 2–3 verbal responses for question 3. Then close with this statement:

> **"The smartest arguers in history — scientists, lawyers, writers, leaders — weren't people who refused to ask questions. They were people who couldn't stop asking them. Curiosity isn't the enemy of a strong opinion. It IS what makes your opinion strong. Next time you're asked to argue for something, don't start with your answer. Start with your questions."**

**Collect argument planners and/or paragraphs** as a formative assessment before students leave.

---

## DIFFERENTIATION

### For Students Who Need Support:
- Provide a sentence frame bank:
  - *"I argue that _______ because _______."*
  - *"Evidence of this can be seen when _______."*
  - *"Some people believe _______, but _______."*
- Reduce paragraph requirement to 5–6 sentences
- Allow verbal brainstorming with a partner before writing

### For Students Who Need a Challenge:
- Require two pieces of evidence from different types (a statistic AND a real-world example)
- Ask students to write a second paragraph that argues the opposite position with equal effort
- Have students rate the strength of their own argument on a scale of 1–10 and justify the score in writing

### For English Language Learners:
- Allow planning in first language before writing in English
- Pair with a supportive partner during sticky note activity
- Provide a visual anchor chart with CRERC structure and examples posted visibly throughout the lesson
