## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout: "Smoothie Shop Scenarios"
- Whiteboard and markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Flow

### Opening Hook (5 minutes)
Display two recipes on the board: Recipe A uses 2 cups of strawberries for 3 smoothies; Recipe B uses 5 cups of strawberries for 8 smoothies. Ask: *"If we double Recipe A, will it still taste the same? What about Recipe B—does it scale the same way?"* Students turn-and-talk for 90 seconds.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means in everyday context. **Assessment:** Listen during turn-and-talk for students who precisely articulate the idea of a constant ratio versus those who only vaguely describe "scaling up." Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

### Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities are proportional if their ratio (y/x) is constant, called the constant of proportionality (k), giving the equation y = kx. Demonstrate using Recipe A: 2/3, 4/6, 6/9 all equal 2/3. Show this as a table, then plot points on a graph, emphasizing that proportional relationships pass through the origin as a straight line.

Contrast with Recipe B: 5/8 ≠ 10/16... wait, that IS equal. Adjust to a non-example: 5 cups for 8 smoothies, 7 cups for 10 smoothies (5/8 ≠ 7/10).

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model a four-column organizer (Verbal → Table → Equation → Graph). **Assessment:** Later in independent work, check whether students replicate this logical organization in their notebooks.

### Guided Practice (12 minutes)
Distribute the "Smoothie Shop Scenarios" handout with four situations. For each, students determine if the relationship is proportional and represent it in all four forms. Work through Scenario 1 together, then have students attempt Scenario 2 with a partner.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Include one scenario with a "claim" from a fictional shop owner: *"My pricing is fair and proportional—$3 for 2 drinks, $5 for 4 drinks, $7 for 6 drinks."* Students must verify by cross-checking ratios across the table AND testing whether the graph passes through the origin. **Assessment:** Collect written justifications; look for students using at least two representations to validate or refute the claim.

### Collaborative Challenge (13 minutes)
Pose a decision-making task: *"You're opening a lemonade stand. Choose between three supplier pricing plans."*
- Plan A: $4 per 2 lbs of lemons (proportional)
- Plan B: $5 flat fee + $1.50 per lb (non-proportional)
- Plan C: $2 per lb for first 5 lbs, $1 per lb after (non-proportional)

Groups of 3-4 must recommend a plan and justify with tables, graphs, and equations.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must list at least two advantages and two disadvantages of each plan based on quantity needs. **Assessment:** Review group posters/notes for evidence of trade-off analysis (e.g., "Plan A is best for large orders, but Plan C is cheaper for small quantities").

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students must identify which representation (table, graph, or equation) most clearly reveals proportionality. **Assessment:** During group share-outs, ask: "Which representation was MOST useful for your decision and why?" Listen for sophisticated reasoning about why graphs reveal the origin-line property quickly.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Groups present their recommendation with mathematical justification. **Assessment:** Use a rubric scoring (1) accurate identification of proportional vs. non-proportional, (2) correct equation/graph, (3) reasoning that anticipates different purchase quantities.

### Closing Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Whole-class debrief: "Did any group change their mind during the discussion? Why?" Then distribute exit tickets with two parts:

**Part 1 (Content):** Given the table {(3,12), (5,20), (8,32)}, write the equation and explain how you know it's proportional.

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"Describe one moment today when your thinking about proportions changed. What caused the shift?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Part 2 of the exit ticket explicitly demands metacognition. **Assessment:** Score reflections on depth—level 2 responses describe a specific misconception corrected (e.g., "I thought any straight-line table was proportional, but I learned it must go through zero"); level 1 responses are vague ("I learned a lot"). Use these to plan tomorrow's warm-up addressing persistent confusions.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed organizers and sentence frames ("This is/is not proportional because___")
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a scenario that *looks* proportional in a table but isn't (e.g., includes a y-intercept)

## Homework
Students find a real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, gas mileage, etc.) and represent it in all four forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proving Proportions in the Real World
**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Objective:** Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, organize mathematical models, and justify their reasoning.

**Materials:** Graph paper, rulers, tablets/laptops for research, whiteboard.

---

### **1. Hook & Introduction (10 minutes)**
Begin with a verbal description on the board: "A local streaming service charges $8 per month. Another service charges $15 for the first month and $5 for every month after." Ask students to predict which service is a "better deal" over time and whether the cost grows evenly. Introduce the mathematical concept of a proportional relationship: a constant rate of change where the ratio between quantities remains the same, and the graph passes through the origin (0,0). 

### **2. Data Collection & Source Evaluation (15 minutes)**
Students transition to tablets to investigate a real-world scenario: bulk pricing at grocery stores. They must determine if buying produce in larger quantities represents a truly proportional relationship.

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   *Activity:* Provide students with three potential sources to find the price of apples: a random user's blog post from 2018, an official weekly ad from a local supermarket's verified website, and a Wikipedia article on the history of apples. Students must select the highest-quality, most authoritative source to build their math model.
    *   *Assessment:* Observe group discussions. Check that students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting the official supermarket website, justifying their choice based on the source's currency, authority, and relevance to current local pricing.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   *Activity:* Once students find the price of a 3-lb bag of apples on the supermarket site, they must rigorously cross-check this unit rate against a second authoritative source (e.g., a different verified grocery app like Target or Walmart) to validate if the proportional pricing model holds true across the industry or if stores use different pricing structures.
    *   *Assessment:* Review students' digital notes to ensure they have recorded data from at least two verified sources and accurately compared the unit rates to check for consistency before proceeding.

### **3. Organizing & Clarifying the Math (15 minutes)**
Students take their validated grocery data and represent it mathematically.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   *Activity:* Students translate the raw data (e.g., "$4.50 for 3 lbs") into a precise verbal description of the unit rate ("The cost is $1.50 per pound of apples"). They must clarify this idea precisely to support a deeper understanding of the constant of proportionality ($k$).
    *   *Assessment:* Listen to student explanations during guided practice. Assess their ability to clearly articulate the unit rate and explain how it acts as the multiplier in their equation ($y = 1.5x$).

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   *Activity:* Students arrange their clarified data into a basic structure: a T-chart (table) and a coordinate plane (graph). They must arrange the information to strengthen logical flow, ensuring the independent variable (pounds) is on the x-axis and the dependent variable (cost) is on the y-axis.
    *   *Assessment:* Collect the students' graph paper. Evaluate if the table is sequentially ordered and if the graph is properly scaled, labeled, and plotted in a straight line through the origin.

### **4. Analyzing & Decision-Making (10 minutes)**
Present a new scenario on the board: A taxi charges a $3 flat fee just to get in the car, plus $2 per mile driven.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   *Activity:* Ask students to figure out the cost of a 50-mile ride. They must discuss the trade-offs of using a table, a graph, or an equation to solve this specific problem. 
    *   *Assessment:* Observe groups systematically evaluating the options. They should conclude that while a table or graph is great for visualizing early values, an equation ($y = 2x + 3$) is the most efficient choice for comprehensive long-term implications (calculating large numbers), despite being more abstract.

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   *Activity:* Students must decide if the taxi scenario is a proportional relationship. They must apply sophisticated reasoning, anticipating complexities like the flat starting fee.
    *   *Assessment:* Read students' written justifications on their whiteboards. They must correctly conclude the relationship is *not* proportional, using sound logic: it does not pass through the origin (0,0) because at 0 miles, the cost is already $3, meaning the ratio of $y/x$ is not constant.

### **5. Closure & Metacognition (5 minutes)**
Wrap up the lesson with an exit ticket focused on self-reflection.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   *Activity:* Students write a brief reflection answering: "At the beginning of class, how did you decide if two things were proportional? How has your thinking process changed now that you have used tables, graphs, and equations to test your assumptions?"
    *   *Assessment:* Evaluate the exit tickets for deep metacognitive awareness. Look for students who acknowledge their initial superficial assumptions (e.g., "I used to just guess if numbers looked like they were going up evenly") and explain how they now strategically adjust their thinking by actively checking for a constant rate of change and an origin point of (0,0).

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Proportional or Not?” handout  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Deal Is Better? — 7 minutes

Display the following:

- **Store A:** 3 notebooks for $6  
- **Store B:** 5 notebooks for $12  
- **Store C:** 4 notebooks for $8  

Ask students: “Which store has the best price per notebook? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Bring the class together and ask students to share strategies. Guide students toward finding the unit rate.

Emphasize: “A proportional relationship has a constant ratio or constant unit rate.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the relevant quantities: number of notebooks and total cost.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying what each number means, such as “$6 is the total cost for 3 notebooks, so I need the cost for 1 notebook.”  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student clearly explains the quantities and their relationship.  
  - 1: Student identifies some relevant numbers but explanation is incomplete.  
  - -1: Student misuses quantities, such as comparing 3 to 12 directly.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Teach students that two quantities are proportional if one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other.

Use this example:

| Cups of Juice | Cost in Dollars |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Ask: “What pattern do you notice?”  
Students should identify that the cost is always 2 times the number of cups.

Write:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Explain:
- The constant of proportionality is 2.
- The graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line through the origin, \((0,0)\).
- In a table, the ratio \(y/x\) stays the same.
- In words: “Each cup of juice costs $2.”

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 25 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask: “Is this proportional?” Students should notice the ratio is not constant because \(25/3\) is not 8.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship in a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students can connect the representations logically: table → constant ratio → equation → graph → verbal description.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student arranges representations clearly and accurately.  
  - 1: Student completes some representations but with limited connection.  
  - -1: Student’s representations contradict each other.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Work through this situation as a class:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours.”

Ask students to create:

1. A table  
2. A unit rate  
3. An equation  
4. A graph  
5. A verbal description  

Expected responses:
- Unit rate: 6 miles per hour  
- Equation: \(y = 6x\), where \(x\) is hours and \(y\) is miles  
- Graph: line through \((0,0)\), \((1,6)\), \((2,12)\), etc.  
- Verbal description: “The cyclist travels 6 miles each hour.”

Then ask: “How can we verify that this relationship is proportional?”

Students should say:
- The unit rate is constant.
- The equation is in the form \(y = kx\).
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students verify proportionality using more than one representation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify their answer using at least two checks, such as table ratios and graph behavior.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student cross-checks using multiple mathematical sources of evidence.  
  - 1: Student checks only one representation.  
  - -1: Student claims proportionality without evidence or uses an incorrect rule.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give students a handout with four scenarios:

**A.** A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cookies.  
**B.** A taxi charges a $4 starting fee plus $2 per mile.  
**C.** A phone plan costs $30 for 3 GB, $40 for 4 GB, and $50 for 5 GB.  
**D.** A runner travels 7 miles every hour.

For each scenario, students must:
- Decide whether it is proportional.
- Explain why.
- Represent proportional relationships in at least two ways.

Encourage students to debate with their partners if they disagree.

For example, scenario B is not proportional because the starting fee means the graph does not pass through the origin. Scenario D is proportional because the rate is constant and can be written as \(y = 7x\).

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which information matters most when judging proportionality.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students focusing on mathematically useful evidence, such as constant ratios, unit rate, equation form, or graph through the origin.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student selects strong mathematical evidence and ignores irrelevant details.  
  - 1: Student uses somewhat relevant information but explanation lacks depth.  
  - -1: Student relies on irrelevant or misleading information.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Partners compare different ways to represent a relationship and choose the clearest method.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students, “Which representation helped you decide fastest: table, graph, equation, or words? Why?”  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student compares trade-offs, such as “The table made the ratios easy to check, but the graph showed the starting fee clearly.”  
  - 1: Student names a preferred representation with minimal explanation.  
  - -1: Student refuses to consider another method.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Review the partner activity. For each scenario, ask one pair to explain their reasoning. Press students to justify:

- “How do you know the ratio is constant?”
- “Would the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Can this be written as \(y = kx\)?”

Highlight that proportional relationships must have no added starting value. For example, \(y = 2x + 4\) is not proportional because of the added 4.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students defend whether each relationship is proportional.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for logical reasoning that connects evidence to conclusion.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student gives a clear claim, evidence, and mathematical reasoning.  
  - 1: Student gives a mostly correct answer but reasoning is incomplete.  
  - -1: Student uses faulty logic, such as “It increases, so it must be proportional.”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete:

1. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |
| 8 | 40 |

2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. Describe one mistake you almost made or one strategy that helped you decide.

Expected:
- Yes, proportional.
- \(y = 5x\)
- The ratio \(y/x = 5\) each time.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students explain their own strategy or possible mistake.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Review written reflections for metacognition.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student explains how they checked their thinking and adjusted if needed.  
  - 1: Student briefly mentions a strategy without much detail.  
  - -1: Student repeats an error without recognizing it.

---

## Assessment

Students demonstrate understanding if they can:
- Identify constant ratios in tables.
- Recognize that proportional graphs pass through the origin.
- Write equations in the form \(y = kx\).
- Explain proportional relationships verbally.
- Justify their reasoning using more than one representation.

- **Store C:** 4 notebooks for $8

Emphasize: “A proportional relationship has a constant ratio or constant unit rate.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the relevant quantities: number of notebooks and total cost.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying what each number means, such as “$6 is the total cost for 3 notebooks, so I need the cost for 1 notebook.”

  - 1: Student identifies some relevant numbers but explanation is incomplete.  
  - -1: Student misuses quantities, such as comparing 3 to 12 directly.

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Teach students that two quantities are proportional if one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other.

Use this example:

| Cups of Juice | Cost in Dollars |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Ask: “What pattern do you notice?”  
Students should identify that the cost is always 2 times the number of cups.

Write:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Explain:
- The constant of proportionality is 2.
- The graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line through the origin, \((0,0)\).
- In a table, the ratio \(y/x\) stays the same.
- In words: “Each cup of juice costs $2.”

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 25 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask: “Is this proportional?” Students should notice the ratio is not constant because \(25/3\) is not 8.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship in a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students can connect the representations logically: table → constant ratio → equation → graph → verbal description.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student arranges representations clearly and accurately.  
  - 1: Student completes some representations but with limited connection.  
  - -1: Student’s representations contradict each other.

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Work through this situation as a class:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours.”

Ask students to create:

1. A table  
2. A unit rate  
3. An equation  
4. A graph  
5. A verbal description  

Expected responses:
- Unit rate: 6 miles per hour  
- Equation: \(y = 6x\), where \(x\) is hours and \(y\) is miles  
- Graph: line through \((0,0)\), \((1,6)\), \((2,12)\), etc.  
- Verbal description: “The cyclist travels 6 miles each hour.”

Then ask: “How can we verify that this relationship is proportional?”

Students should say:
- The unit rate is constant.
- The equation is in the form \(y = kx\).
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students verify proportionality using more than one representation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify their answer using at least two checks, such as table ratios and graph behavior.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student cross-checks using multiple mathematical sources of evidence.  
  - 1: Student checks only one representation.  
  - -1: Student claims proportionality without evidence or uses an incorrect rule.

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give students a handout with four scenarios:

**A.** A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cookies.  
**B.** A taxi charges a $4 starting fee plus $2 per mile.  
**C.** A phone plan costs $30 for 3 GB, $40 for 4 GB, and $50 for 5 GB.  
**D.** A runner travels 7 miles every hour.

For each scenario, students must:
- Decide whether it is proportional.
- Explain why.
- Represent proportional relationships in at least two ways.

Encourage students to debate with their partners if they disagree.

For example, scenario B is not proportional because the starting fee means the graph does not pass through the origin. Scenario D is proportional because the rate is constant and can be written as \(y = 7x\).

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which information matters most when judging proportionality.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students focusing on mathematically useful evidence, such as constant ratios, unit rate, equation form, or graph through the origin.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student selects strong mathematical evidence and ignores irrelevant details.  
  - 1: Student uses somewhat relevant information but explanation lacks depth.  
  - -1: Student relies on irrelevant or misleading information.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Partners compare different ways to represent a relationship and choose the clearest method.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students, “Which representation helped you decide fastest: table, graph, equation, or words? Why?”  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student compares trade-offs, such as “The table made the ratios easy to check, but the graph showed the starting fee clearly.”  
  - 1: Student names a preferred representation with minimal explanation.  
  - -1: Student refuses to consider another method.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Review the partner activity. For each scenario, ask one pair to explain their reasoning. Press students to justify:

- “How do you know the ratio is constant?”
- “Would the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Can this be written as \(y = kx\)?”

Highlight that proportional relationships must have no added starting value. For example, \(y = 2x + 4\) is not proportional because of the added 4.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students defend whether each relationship is proportional.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for logical reasoning that connects evidence to conclusion.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student gives a clear claim, evidence, and mathematical reasoning.  
  - 1: Student gives a mostly correct answer but reasoning is incomplete.  
  - -1: Student uses faulty logic, such as “It increases, so it must be proportional.”

2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. Describe one mistake you almost made or one strategy that helped you decide.

Expected:

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students explain their own strategy or possible mistake.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Review written reflections for metacognition.  
- **Look-for levels:**  
  - 2: Student explains how they checked their thinking and adjusted if needed.  
  - 1: Student briefly mentions a strategy without much detail.  
  - -1: Student repeats an error without recognizing it.

## Lesson Flow

### Opening Hook (5 minutes)
Display two recipes: Recipe A uses 2 cups of strawberries for 3 smoothies; Recipe B uses 5 cups of strawberries for 8 smoothies, then 7 cups for 10 smoothies. Ask: *"If we double Recipe A, will it still taste the same? Does Recipe B scale the same way?"* Students turn-and-talk for 90 seconds.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means in context. **Assessment:** Listen during turn-and-talk. Level 2: student precisely articulates a constant ratio (e.g., "2/3 = 4/6"). Level 1: vaguely describes "scaling up." Level -1: confuses quantities (e.g., comparing 2 to 8 directly). Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

### Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define: two quantities are proportional if their ratio (y/x) is constant — the constant of proportionality (k) — giving y = kx. Demonstrate with Recipe A: 2/3, 4/6, 6/9 all equal 2/3. Show as a table, then plot points, emphasizing proportional relationships graph as a straight line through the origin. Contrast with the non-example (5/8 ≠ 7/10).

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model a four-column organizer (Verbal → Table → Equation → Graph). **Assessment:** Level 2: students replicate logical flow across all four representations. Level 1: completes some with limited connection. Level -1: representations contradict each other.

### Guided Practice (12 minutes)
Distribute "Smoothie Shop Scenarios" with four situations. Work Scenario 1 together; partners attempt Scenario 2. Include a fictional shop owner's claim: *"My pricing is fair and proportional—$3 for 2 drinks, $5 for 4 drinks, $7 for 6 drinks."* Students verify by cross-checking ratios AND testing whether the graph passes through the origin.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** **Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Level 2: uses at least two representations (ratio check + graph behavior) to validate/refute. Level 1: checks only one. Level -1: claims proportionality without evidence.

### Collaborative Challenge (13 minutes)
*"You're opening a lemonade stand. Choose between three supplier pricing plans."*
- Plan A: $4 per 2 lbs of lemons (proportional, y = 2x)
- Plan B: $5 flat fee + $1.50 per lb (non-proportional)
- Plan C: $2 per lb for first 5 lbs, $1 per lb after (non-proportional)

Groups of 3–4 recommend a plan with tables, graphs, and equations.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Groups must identify which representation most clearly reveals proportionality. **Assessment:** During share-outs ask, "Which representation was MOST useful and why?" Level 2: sophisticated reasoning about graphs revealing the origin-line property. Level 1: names a preference with shallow rationale.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups list at least two advantages and disadvantages of each plan based on quantity needs. **Assessment:** Look for trade-off analysis (e.g., "Plan A is best for large orders, but Plan C is cheaper for small quantities").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Groups present their recommendation with mathematical justification, including why Plan B is *not* proportional (it doesn't pass through (0,0) — at 0 lbs the cost is already $5). **Assessment:** Rubric scores (1) accurate proportional/non-proportional identification, (2) correct equation/graph, (3) reasoning that anticipates different purchase quantities. Level -1: faulty logic like "it increases, so it must be proportional."

### Closing Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Whole-class debrief: "Did any group change their mind during discussion? Why?" Then distribute exit tickets:

**Part 1 (Content):** Given the table {(3,12), (5,20), (8,32)}, write the equation and explain how you know it's proportional.

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"At the start of class, how did you decide if two things were proportional? How has your thinking changed now that you've used tables, graphs, and equations to test your assumptions?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** **Assessment:** Score reflections on depth. Level 2: describes a specific misconception corrected (e.g., "I thought any straight-line table was proportional, but I learned it must go through zero") and a strategy adjustment. Level 1: vague ("I learned a lot"). Level -1: no awareness, repeats an error. Use these to plan tomorrow's warm-up.
