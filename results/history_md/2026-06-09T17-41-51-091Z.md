## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while exercising critical thinking throughout.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout (with pricing scenarios), exit tickets, journal notebooks.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Shop A:** 2 smoothies for $6, 4 for $12, 6 for $18
- **Shop B:** 1 smoothie for $4, 3 for $10, 5 for $16

**Prompt:** "Which shop charges proportionally? How do you know?"

Students think-pair-share. The teacher records student observations on the board, asking, "What information matters here? What can we ignore?"

🎯 **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must isolate the relevant data (price-per-unit ratios) from extra wording.
**Assessment:** Listen for students who articulate, "I need to check if every pair has the same ratio." Collect pair-share notes.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Teacher introduces the constant of proportionality (k) and shows how Shop A's relationship appears as:
- **Table:** ratios all equal 3
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing equal groupings
- **Verbal:** "Each smoothie costs $3"

Teacher models organizing these five representations side-by-side on a graphic organizer.

🎯 **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy the organizer and fill it out, structuring information logically across representations.
**Assessment:** Review graphic organizers for clear alignment between forms; check that the constant k=3 appears consistently across all five.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Validity Check (10 minutes)
Distribute three "student work samples" (fictional) claiming different relationships are proportional:
- Sample 1: A correct table with y = 5x
- Sample 2: A graph that doesn't pass through the origin but is labeled "proportional"
- Sample 3: A table where ratios are 2, 2.1, and 2 (close but not equal)

Students work in pairs to verify each claim using **at least two representations** (e.g., check the table AND graph it).

🎯 **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check claims using multiple representations rather than trusting the label.
🎯 **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must judge whether "close to equal" ratios qualify as proportional.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How did you verify this? What other representation could confirm your answer?" Students who use two or more methods demonstrate Level 2.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Plan (12 minutes)
Present the **"Class Field Trip" problem:**
> The class needs to rent transportation. Plan A: $5 per student, no flat fee. Plan B: $20 flat fee plus $3 per student. Plan C: $4 per student, no flat fee.

Students must:
1. Identify which plans are proportional and which are not (justify with equations and graphs).
2. Recommend the best plan for a group of 25 students AND argue trade-offs.

🎯 **Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare three options, considering both proportionality AND total cost.
🎯 **Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must justify their recommendation with mathematical evidence, anticipating that group size changes the "best" answer.
**Assessment:** Collect written recommendations. Look for: (1) correct identification of proportional plans (A and C), (2) reasoning that considers different group sizes, (3) acknowledgment that Plan B becomes competitive for large groups despite not being proportional.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Students complete a brief journal entry:
> "Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What evidence made you reconsider? If you could redo one problem, what strategy would you try differently?"

Then complete a 3-question exit ticket:
1. Is y = 2x + 1 proportional? Justify.
2. Sketch a graph showing a proportional relationship with k = 4.
3. Write the equation for a table showing (2, 10), (5, 25), (8, 40).

🎯 **Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** The journal prompt requires metacognitive awareness — students articulate how their reasoning evolved.
**Assessment:** Read journals for evidence of strategic adjustment (Level 2) vs. surface-level statements like "I learned about graphs" (Level 1). Look for phrases such as "At first I thought… but then I realized…"

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Teacher highlights two strong student justifications from the field trip task and asks the class, "What made these arguments convincing?" This models high-quality reasoning publicly and reinforces standards for mathematical argument.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers; pair struggling students with peer mentors.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to design their own non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance, challenging classmates to detect the flaw.

## Homework
Students find a real-world example of a proportional relationship (recipe, unit price, distance/time) and represent it in all five forms, including a one-paragraph reflection on how they verified proportionality.

**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Decoding Real-World Data
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to identify, represent, and verify proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data, weigh problem-solving options, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Clarifying the Concept (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** "The Pizza Problem"
The teacher presents a vague verbal description on the board: *"A local pizzeria says their prices are perfectly fair because the more slices you buy, the more you pay. Two slices cost $3.00, and five slices cost $7.50."* Students must translate this verbal description into a precise mathematical definition to determine if the relationship is truly proportional. 

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify the vague claim of "perfectly fair" by calculating the unit rate ($1.50 per slice) and defining proportionality precisely (a constant ratio between quantities). 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students' whiteboards to ensure they are not just guessing, but precisely clarifying the relationship by writing the constant of proportionality ($k = 1.50$) to support a deeper understanding of the text.

### **Part 2: Direct Instruction & Organizing Data (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The Multi-Representation Graphic Organizer
The teacher models how to take the pizza data and represent it in four interconnected ways: a table, a coordinate plane graph, an equation ($y = 1.5x$), and a diagram. Students follow along, filling out a blank graphic organizer.

*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students arrange the raw data into a structured, four-quadrant graphic organizer. This strengthens the logical flow of information, showing how a single constant rate visually translates into a straight line through the origin and algebraically into a $y = kx$ equation.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the students' graphic organizers to ensure the information is structured consistently, checking that the table's logical flow directly matches the plotted points on the graph.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Case Study (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** "The Cell Phone Plan Investigation"
Students are placed in small groups and given a "Case Study" file containing mixed data from two fictional cell phone companies. Company A provides a promotional flyer with a table of data. Company B provides a customer's billing graph. Students must determine which company offers a truly proportional data plan and which is the better deal.

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** The promotional flyer for Company A contains a hidden fee (the table shows 1 GB for $10, 2 GB for $20, but 0 GB for $5). Students must rigorously cross-check the flyer's claims by graphing the table to see if it passes through the origin $(0,0)$.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group discussions to verify that students are not blindly accepting the flyer's claim. Assessment is based on students actively cross-checking the table against a graph to prove the information is invalid (non-proportional).

*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students must decide which pieces of data are most reliable. They must recognize that the promotional flyer (Company A) is misleading, while the raw billing graph (Company B) provides high-quality, authoritative mathematical evidence of a proportional relationship.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the groups' final written conclusions to see if they demonstrate sophisticated judgment, explicitly stating why Company B's data was of higher quality and more mathematically reliable than Company A's misleading table.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** To predict the cost of 50 GB of data, groups must choose whether to extend their graph, continue their table, or write and solve an equation. They must discuss the trade-offs of each method.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will ask groups to explain their chosen method. High-level mastery is observed when students systematically evaluate the trade-offs (e.g., "A graph is great for visualizing, but an equation is much faster and more accurate for large, long-term numbers like 50 GB").

*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Groups must make a final recommendation on which plan a consumer should buy, anticipating complexities (e.g., "Company B is proportional and cheaper per GB, but if a user uses zero data, Company A charges a $5 base fee").
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the groups' final presentations. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by justifying their decision using the constant of proportionality, while acknowledging the real-world complexities of base fees versus per-unit rates.

### **Part 4: Independent Reflection & Closure (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Metacognitive Math Journaling
Students independently complete an exit ticket with two prompts: 
1. *Prove that $y = 4x$ is proportional using two different representations.*
2. *Think about how you solved the Cell Phone Case Study today. Did you initially make any assumptions that turned out to be wrong? How did you adjust your thinking once you graphed the data?*

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students are required to look inward at their own cognitive process, identifying any initial biases (like assuming Company A was proportional just because 10/1 and 20/2 equal 10) and explaining how they strategically adjusted their thinking when new representations revealed a flaw.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. A successful reflection will explicitly state how the student recognized an error in their initial assumption and used the multi-representation strategy to correct their own mathematical thinking.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Topic:** Is It Proportional? Multiple Representations of Relationships  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Time:** 55 minutes

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and can be represented by an equation in the form **y = kx**.
3. Use evidence from multiple representations to justify whether a relationship is proportional.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, diagrams, and word problems  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which One Belongs? — 5 minutes

Display three relationships:

A. A table showing:  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 3, 6, 9, 12  

B. A graph showing a straight line through the origin  

C. A table showing:  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 5, 7, 9, 11  

Ask students:  
“Which relationships look proportional? What makes you think so?”

Students write silently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

**Teacher move:** Listen for students mentioning “same multiplier,” “constant ratio,” “line,” or “starts at zero.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship means two quantities have a constant ratio. In other words, one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other.

Write:

**y = kx**

Explain:

- **x** is the independent quantity.
- **y** is the dependent quantity.
- **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- In a graph, a proportional relationship is a straight line through the origin, **(0, 0)**.
- In a table, the ratio **y/x** stays the same.
- In a verbal description, the situation often uses language like “for each,” “per,” or “at a constant rate.”

Use example:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

Table:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Equation:  
**y = 2x**

Graph:  
A straight line through **(0,0)** and points such as **(1,2), (2,4), (3,6)**.

Verbal description:  
“The cost is $2 per notebook.”

Diagram:  
Draw groups showing each notebook paired with $2.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Same Relationship, Five Representations — 15 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A bike rental shop charges $4 per hour.”

As a class, complete the five representations:

1. **Verbal:** The rental costs $4 for each hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

3. **Equation:**  
**y = 4x**

4. **Graph:** Students plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.

5. **Diagram:** Students draw one “hour” box matched with four dollar signs, then repeat.

Ask guiding questions:

- “What does the 4 mean in every representation?”
- “How do we know this relationship is proportional?”
- “Why is the point (0,0) important?”
- “If we know the equation, how can we check the table?”

Students should see that all representations tell the same mathematical story.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs. Each pair receives four relationship cards:

**Card 1: Table**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |
| 8 | 24 |

**Card 2: Verbal Description**  
“A taxi charges a $3 starting fee plus $2 per mile.”

**Card 3: Equation**  
**y = 5x**

**Card 4: Graph**  
A straight line that does not pass through the origin.

For each card, students must:

1. Decide whether the relationship is proportional.
2. Identify the representation type.
3. Explain their reasoning using at least two pieces of evidence.
4. If proportional, identify the constant of proportionality.

Students record answers in a chart:

| Card | Representation | Proportional? | Evidence | Constant of Proportionality |
|---|---|---|---|---|

After 10 minutes, ask a few pairs to share. Emphasize that the taxi example is not proportional because of the starting fee, even though the cost increases at a constant rate.

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes

Students complete two problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

a. Make a table for 1, 2, 3, and 4 batches.  
b. Write an equation relating cups of sugar, **x**, to cups of flour, **y**.  
c. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

Expected equation:  
**y = 1.5x**

**Problem 2:**  
A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 13 |
| 4 | 16 |

Ask:  
“Is this proportional? Explain using ratios.”

Students should notice that **13/3** does not match the other ratios.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 3 minutes

Students answer:

1. “How can you tell from a table if a relationship is proportional?”
2. “How can you tell from a graph?”
3. “One mistake I almost made or corrected today was…”

Collect exit tickets to check understanding.

---

## Critical Thinking Subcompetency Connections

### CRI1a — Clarifying Information

**Lesson moment:** During guided practice, students identify what each quantity means: hours, cost, x, y, and constant of proportionality.

**Assessment/observation:** Listen for students using precise language such as “x is hours,” “y is total cost,” and “4 means dollars per hour.” Check whether students label axes and include units.

---

### CRI1b — Organizing Information

**Lesson moment:** Students organize the bike rental situation into a verbal description, table, graph, equation, and diagram.

**Assessment/observation:** Review whether students’ representations are consistent with one another and arranged clearly. A strong response shows the same rate across all formats.

---

### CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information

**Lesson moment:** In the partner activity, students cross-check each card using mathematical tests: constant ratio, equation form, and graph through the origin.

**Assessment/observation:** Look for students verifying claims instead of guessing. For example, they should compute ratios or check whether the graph passes through **(0,0)**.

---

### CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information

**Lesson moment:** Students decide which evidence best supports their conclusion: table ratios, graph features, equation form, or context.

**Assessment/observation:** Check if students choose strong mathematical evidence. For example, “The graph is a line through the origin” is stronger than “It looks straight.”

---

### CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices

**Lesson moment:** Students compare representation methods and discuss which is most useful for deciding proportionality.

**Assessment/observation:** Ask: “Would a table, graph, or equation be easiest here? Why?” Listen for students explaining trade-offs, such as tables making ratios easy and graphs making the origin easy to see.

---

### CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making

**Lesson moment:** Students decide whether each relationship is proportional and justify their answer with evidence.

**Assessment/observation:** Review written explanations for logical reasoning. Students should connect evidence to conclusions, such as “This is not proportional because y/x is not constant.”

---

### CRI5a — Reflecting on Thinking Processes

**Lesson moment:** Exit ticket question: “One mistake I almost made or corrected today was…”

**Assessment/observation:** Look for students identifying how their thinking changed, such as realizing that a constant increase is not always the same as a proportional relationship.

---

## Closing Teacher Note

If students struggle, reteach the difference between “constant difference” and “constant ratio.” Many students think any steady increase is proportional, so continue emphasizing that proportional relationships must have a constant multiplier and pass through the origin.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which One Belongs? — 5 minutes

Display three relationships:

A. A table showing:  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 3, 6, 9, 12  

B. A graph showing a straight line through the origin  

C. A table showing:  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 5, 7, 9, 11  

Ask students:  
"Which relationships look proportional? What makes you think so?"

Students write silently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

**Teacher move:** Listen for students mentioning "same multiplier," "constant ratio," "line," or "starts at zero."

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship means two quantities have a constant ratio. In other words, one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other.

Write:

**y = kx**

Explain:

- **x** is the independent quantity.
- **y** is the dependent quantity.
- **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- In a graph, a proportional relationship is a straight line through the origin, **(0, 0)**.
- In a table, the ratio **y/x** stays the same.
- In a verbal description, the situation often uses language like "for each," "per," or "at a constant rate."

Use example:

"A store sells notebooks for $2 each."

Table:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Equation:  
**y = 2x**

Graph:  
A straight line through **(0,0)** and points such as **(1,2), (2,4), (3,6)**.

Verbal description:  
"The cost is $2 per notebook."

Diagram:  
Draw groups showing each notebook paired with $2.

### 3. Guided Practice: Same Relationship, Five Representations — 15 minutes

Give students this situation:

"A bike rental shop charges $4 per hour."

As a class, complete the five representations:

1. **Verbal:** The rental costs $4 for each hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

3. **Equation:**  
**y = 4x**

4. **Graph:** Students plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.

5. **Diagram:** Students draw one "hour" box matched with four dollar signs, then repeat.

Ask guiding questions:

- "What does the 4 mean in every representation?"
- "How do we know this relationship is proportional?"
- "Why is the point (0,0) important?"
- "If we know the equation, how can we check the table?"

Students should see that all representations tell the same mathematical story.

**Card 1: Table**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |
| 8 | 24 |

**Card 2: Verbal Description**  
"A taxi charges a $3 starting fee plus $2 per mile."

**Card 3: Equation**  
**y = 5x**

**Card 4: Graph**  
A straight line that does not pass through the origin.

For each card, students must:

1. Decide whether the relationship is proportional.
2. Identify the representation type.
3. Explain their reasoning using at least two pieces of evidence.
4. If proportional, identify the constant of proportionality.

Students record answers in a chart:

| Card | Representation | Proportional? | Evidence | Constant of Proportionality |
|---|---|---|---|---|

After 10 minutes, ask a few pairs to share. Emphasize that the taxi example is not proportional because of the starting fee, even though the cost increases at a constant rate.

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes

Students complete two problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

a. Make a table for 1, 2, 3, and 4 batches.  
b. Write an equation relating cups of sugar, **x**, to cups of flour, **y**.  
c. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

Expected equation:  
**y = 1.5x**

**Problem 2:**  
A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 13 |
| 4 | 16 |

Ask:  
"Is this proportional? Explain using ratios."

Students should notice that **13/3** does not match the other ratios.

Students answer:

1. "How can you tell from a table if a relationship is proportional?"
2. "How can you tell from a graph?"
3. "One mistake I almost made or corrected today was…"

Collect exit tickets to check understanding.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while exercising critical thinking throughout.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, student work samples, exit tickets, journal notebooks.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Shop A:** 2 smoothies for $6, 4 for $12, 6 for $18
- **Shop B:** 1 smoothie for $4, 3 for $10, 5 for $16

**Prompt:** "Which shop charges proportionally? How do you know?"

Students think-pair-share. Teacher records observations, asking, "What information matters here? What can we ignore?"

🎯 **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students isolate the relevant data (price-per-unit ratios) from extra wording and define proportionality precisely as a constant ratio.
**Assessment:** Listen for statements like, "I need to check if every pair has the same ratio." Collect pair-share notes; check that students articulate the constant of proportionality (k = 3 for Shop A).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes)
Introduce the constant of proportionality and the form **y = kx**. Show Shop A's relationship across five representations:
- **Table:** ratios all equal 3
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin (0,0)
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing equal groupings
- **Verbal:** "Each smoothie costs $3"

Emphasize that proportional relationships always pass through the origin and have a constant multiplier — not just a constant increase. Students copy a graphic organizer aligning all five forms.

🎯 **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students structure information logically across representations.
**Assessment:** Review organizers for clear alignment; check that k = 3 appears consistently across all five forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Validity Check (10 minutes)
Distribute three fictional "student work samples":
- Sample 1: A correct table with y = 5x
- Sample 2: A graph that doesn't pass through the origin but is labeled "proportional"
- Sample 3: A table where ratios are 2, 2.1, and 2 (close but not equal)

Pairs verify each claim using **at least two representations** (e.g., check the table AND graph it).

🎯 **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check claims using multiple representations rather than trusting the label.
🎯 **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students judge whether "close to equal" ratios qualify as proportional, choosing strong mathematical evidence over surface appearance.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How did you verify this? What other representation could confirm your answer?" Students who use two or more methods and explicitly reject Sample 2 (no origin) and Sample 3 (inconsistent ratios) demonstrate Level 2.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Plan (12 minutes)
Present the **"Class Field Trip" problem:**
> The class needs to rent transportation. Plan A: $5 per student, no flat fee. Plan B: $20 flat fee plus $3 per student. Plan C: $4 per student, no flat fee.

Groups must:
1. Identify which plans are proportional (justify with equations and graphs).
2. Recommend the best plan for 25 students AND discuss trade-offs.
3. Choose whether a table, graph, or equation is the most efficient tool for predicting costs at larger group sizes.

🎯 **Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students systematically compare three options, considering proportionality, total cost, and the trade-offs of different representation methods (e.g., "An equation is faster than extending a graph for large numbers").
🎯 **Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify their recommendation with mathematical evidence, anticipating that group size changes the "best" answer — Plan B becomes competitive at scale despite not being proportional.
**Assessment:** Collect written recommendations. Look for: (1) correct identification that Plans A and C are proportional, (2) reasoning that explores multiple group sizes, (3) acknowledgment of real-world complexities like the $20 base fee.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students complete a brief journal entry:
> "Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What evidence made you reconsider? If you could redo one problem, what strategy would you try differently?"

Then a 3-question exit ticket:
1. Is y = 2x + 1 proportional? Justify.
2. Sketch a graph showing a proportional relationship with k = 4.
3. Write the equation for a table showing (2, 10), (5, 25), (8, 40).

🎯 **Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Students articulate how their reasoning evolved and identify initial assumptions they revised.
**Assessment:** Read journals for evidence of strategic adjustment (Level 2: "At first I thought… but then I realized…") vs. surface-level statements (Level 1: "I learned about graphs").

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Highlight two strong student justifications from the field trip task. Ask, "What made these arguments convincing?" Reinforce the key distinction: a *constant difference* is not the same as a *constant ratio* — only relationships with a constant multiplier passing through the origin are proportional.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers; pair struggling students with peer mentors.
- **Extension:** Have advanced students design a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance for classmates to detect.

## Homework
Students find a real-world proportional relationship (recipe, unit price, distance/time), represent it in all five forms, and write a one-paragraph reflection on how they verified proportionality.
