## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to analyze and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout with 4 real-world scenarios (smoothie recipe, car travel, paint mixing, phone plan)
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 min)
Display two tables on the board: one proportional (cups of juice vs. servings) and one non-proportional (age vs. height). Ask: *"Which relationship is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner, then share. Introduce the day's goal: identifying and representing proportional relationships in multiple forms.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio). 
- *Assessment:* Listen for precise language during pair-share ("the ratio y/x stays the same"). Note students who clarify vs. those who give vague answers.

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 min)
Define proportional relationship: y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality. Demonstrate with a smoothie recipe (2 cups fruit : 3 cups yogurt) across all five representations:
- **Verbal:** "For every 2 cups of fruit, use 3 cups of yogurt."
- **Table:** Show ratios 2:3, 4:6, 6:9.
- **Equation:** y = 1.5x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin.
- **Diagram:** Bar model.

Emphasize that the graph passes through the origin and the ratio is constant.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to organize the same relationship across five formats in a logical sequence.
- *Assessment:* Review students' notes for clear labeling and consistent structure across representations.

### 3. Guided Practice — Carousel Activity (15 min)
Four stations around the room, each presenting a scenario in only ONE representation:
- **Station A (Table):** Car travels 60, 120, 180 miles in 1, 2, 3 hours.
- **Station B (Graph):** Plotted line of paint mixture (blue vs. white).
- **Station C (Equation):** c = 0.15m (phone plan cost per minute).
- **Station D (Verbal):** "A printer prints 25 pages every 2 minutes."

In groups of 4, students rotate every 3 minutes. At each station, they must (a) determine if the relationship is proportional, (b) identify the constant of proportionality, and (c) translate it into a different representation.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must verify proportionality by cross-checking ratios across multiple data points—not just one.
- *Assessment:* Circulate and ask, "How do you know this isn't a coincidence between two points?" Look for students checking multiple ratios.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students must select which data points or features are most useful (e.g., recognizing the origin point on a graph as authoritative evidence).
- *Assessment:* Review station worksheets for justified selection of evidence (e.g., "The line passes through (0,0), confirming proportionality").

### 4. Critical Thinking Discussion (10 min)
Pose a scenario: *"A gym offers two memberships: Plan A charges $5 per visit. Plan B charges $20 flat fee plus $2 per visit. Which is proportional? Which is the better deal?"*

Students work in pairs to:
1. Graph both plans.
2. Identify which is proportional and why.
3. Recommend a plan based on usage.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students must compare both plans, considering different usage scenarios (low vs. high visits).
- *Assessment:* Collect written recommendations. Look for trade-off language: "If you visit fewer than X times, Plan A is better because…"

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence.
- *Assessment:* Evaluate reasoning chains. Strong responses anticipate edge cases (e.g., "At exactly 6.67 visits, the plans cost the same").

### 5. Independent Practice & Exit Ticket (10 min)
Students complete an exit ticket with three tasks:
1. Given a table, determine if it's proportional and write the equation.
2. Graph a proportional relationship from a verbal description.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"What strategy helped you most today when deciding if a relationship was proportional? What confused you, and how did you work through it?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** The reflection prompt requires metacognitive awareness.
- *Assessment:* Read reflections for evidence of strategy awareness ("I checked the ratio in every row instead of just one") and self-correction ("At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but then I realized it has to go through the origin").

### 6. Closure (3 min)
Quick whole-class share: "What's one way you'll remember to check for proportionality?" Preview tomorrow's lesson on solving real-world proportional problems.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence starters.
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to create a non-proportional relationship that "looks" proportional and explain the trick.

## Homework
Find one proportional relationship at home (recipe, allowance, etc.) and represent it in all five forms.

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Materials:** Projector/board, graph paper, rulers, student handout, colored pencils, exit ticket

---

### 0–5 Minutes: Warm-Up — “Is It Proportional?”

Display two situations:

1. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, and 180 miles in 3 hours.  
2. A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.

Ask students:  
- “Which relationship seems proportional? Why?”  
- “What information helps you decide?”

Students write a quick response, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify relevant quantities and explain what makes a relationship proportional.  
- **Assessment:** Teacher listens for precise clarification such as: “The first situation is proportional because the ratio of miles to hours is always 60.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: clarifies ideas precisely to support deeper understanding.

---

### 5–15 Minutes: Mini-Lesson — What Makes a Relationship Proportional?

Teacher explains:

A **proportional relationship** has a constant ratio between two quantities. This constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**, often written as **k**.

If y is proportional to x, then:

**y = kx**

A proportional relationship can be shown in several ways:

- **Table:** The ratio y/x is constant.
- **Graph:** The points lie on a straight line through the origin, (0, 0).
- **Equation:** The equation has the form y = kx.
- **Diagram:** Equal groups or repeated equal ratios are shown.
- **Verbal description:** The situation describes a constant rate with no starting fee or added amount.

Use the warm-up examples:

For the car:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

Ratios: 60/1 = 60, 120/2 = 60, 180/3 = 60.  
Equation: y = 60x.

For the taxi:

Cost = 2x + 4.  
Because there is a starting fee of $4, the graph would not pass through the origin, so it is not proportional.

---

### 15–25 Minutes: Guided Practice — Multiple Representations

Give students this situation:

“A store sells apples for $3 per pound.”

As a class, create:

1. **Verbal description:** Apples cost $3 for each pound.  
2. **Table:**

| Pounds | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 6 |
| 3 | 9 |
| 4 | 12 |

3. **Equation:** y = 3x  
4. **Graph:** Plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing 1 pound costs $3, 2 pounds cost $6, etc.

Ask: “How can we check that all five representations match?”

Students should compare the unit rate across the table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship into five representations.  
- **Assessment:** Check whether student work is logically arranged and consistent. Evidence might include: “My table, graph, and equation all show the cost increases by 3 for every 1 pound.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: arranges information to strengthen logical flow.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description agree.  
- **Assessment:** Look for students verifying information across representations, such as: “The point (2, 6) is on the graph, and it also fits y = 3x because 3 × 2 = 6.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: cross-checks information using multiple reliable mathematical representations.

---

### 25–38 Minutes: Partner Task — Proportional or Not?

Students work in pairs. Give each pair four cards:

**Card A:**  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 5, 10, 15, 20

**Card B:**  
x: 1, 2, 3, 4  
y: 6, 8, 10, 12

**Card C:**  
Equation: y = 7x

**Card D:**  
A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month.

For each card, students must decide:
1. Is it proportional?  
2. What evidence proves it?  
3. Which representation would best show the relationship: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description? Why?

Students record answers on the handout.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which information is most useful for proving proportionality.  
- **Assessment:** Look for students selecting relevant evidence instead of surface details. Strong evidence: “Card B is not proportional because y/x changes: 6/1 = 6, but 8/2 = 4.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: selects high-quality, relevant mathematical evidence.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare representations and choose the best one for each card.  
- **Assessment:** Listen for trade-off reasoning, such as: “A graph is helpful because we can see if it goes through the origin, but a table is faster for checking ratios.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: evaluates trade-offs between options.

---

### 38–48 Minutes: Whole-Class Discussion — Defend Your Decision

Choose several pairs to present one card. Require students to use sentence frames:

- “I know this is proportional because…”
- “I checked by…”
- “The best representation is… because…”
- “At first I thought…, but then I noticed…”

Teacher asks probing questions:
- “Does the graph have to pass through the origin? Why?”
- “Can a relationship have a constant increase but not be proportional?”
- “How does the equation help us decide?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students defend whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical reasoning.  
- **Assessment:** Look for logical justification, such as: “Card D is not proportional because the $20 sign-up fee means when x = 0, y = 20, not 0.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: uses sound reasoning and anticipates common complications.

---

### 48–55 Minutes: Exit Ticket — Reflect and Represent

Students complete independently:

1. A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cups of sugar. Complete a table for 0, 1, 2, and 3 batches.  
2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. Explain how you know it is proportional.  
4. Reflection: “What did I check first? Did I change my thinking at any point? Why?”

Expected equation: y = 5x if x represents batches and y represents cups of sugar, or y = 2x for flour depending on the chosen quantity. Students should clearly define variables.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on how they decided whether the relationship was proportional.  
- **Assessment:** Look for metacognitive evidence, such as: “At first I only looked for a pattern, but then I checked the ratio to make sure it stayed the same.”  
- **Level Focus:** Aim for Level 2: shows awareness of thinking and adjusts strategy.

Emphasize: the graph must pass through the origin, and the ratio must be constant across ALL data points.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model logical sequencing across five formats.
- *Assessment:* Check guided notes for clear labeling, ordered x-values, and consistent structure across representations.

### 3. Guided Practice — Carousel Stations (15 min)
Four stations, each presenting ONE representation. Groups rotate every 3 minutes:
- **Station A (Table):** Car travels 60, 120, 180 miles in 1, 2, 3 hours.
- **Station B (Graph):** Plotted line of paint mixture.
- **Station C (Equation):** c = 0.15m (phone plan).
- **Station D (Verbal):** "A printer prints 25 pages every 2 minutes."

At each station, students (a) determine proportionality, (b) identify k, and (c) translate into a different representation.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students verify proportionality by cross-checking ratios across multiple data points and representations.
- *Assessment:* Circulate and ask, "How do you know this isn't a coincidence between two points?" Look for explicit cross-checks like: "The point (2,6) is on the graph AND fits y = 3x."

### 4. Critical Thinking Discussion: Decision-Making (13 min)
Pose: *"A gym offers two memberships: Plan A charges $5 per visit. Plan B charges $20 flat fee plus $2 per visit. Which is proportional? Which is the better deal?"*

In pairs, students: (1) graph both plans, (2) identify which is proportional and why, (3) recommend a plan based on usage.

Use sentence frames during share-out:
- "I know this is proportional because…"
- "At first I thought…, but then I noticed…"

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students systematically compare plans across usage scenarios.
- *Assessment:* Collect written recommendations. Strong responses use trade-off language: "If you visit fewer than 6.67 times, Plan A is better because…"

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students justify recommendations using mathematical evidence, recognizing the $20 fee prevents the line from passing through the origin.
- *Assessment:* Evaluate reasoning chains. Strong responses anticipate edge cases ("At exactly 6.67 visits, costs are equal").

### 5. Exit Ticket: Reflection (10 min)
Students complete independently:
1. Given a recipe (2 cups flour per 5 cups sugar), build a table for 0–3 batches and write the equation.
2. Explain how you know it is proportional.
3. **Reflection:** *"Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional versus now. What strategy helped most today? What confused you, and how did cross-checking the table, graph, and equation change your thinking?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** The prompt demands metacognitive awareness.
- *Assessment:* Read for strategy awareness and self-correction (e.g., "At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but then I realized it must go through the origin, so I'll always check that first").

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence starters.
- **Challenge:** Create a non-proportional relationship that "looks" proportional and explain the trick.
