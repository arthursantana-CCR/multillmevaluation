## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: The Art of Argument — Building Persuasive Writing

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Construct a clear argumentative claim supported by reasons and evidence
- Acknowledge and respond to an opposing viewpoint
- Organize their argument using a logical structure

---

## Materials Needed
- Whiteboard and markers
- Argument graphic organizer (one per student)
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- Sample argument paragraph (printed or projected)
- Writing journals or loose-leaf paper

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### HOOK — Open With Wonder (5 Minutes)

Begin class by posing this question on the board before students even sit down:

**"Should students have homework over school breaks?"**

As students settle in, ask them to stand on one side of the room if they agree and the other side if they disagree. Once divided, pick one student from each side and give them exactly 30 seconds to convince the class to join their side.

After both students speak, ask the class: *"What made one argument more convincing than the other? What was missing from either one?"*

This physical, spontaneous activity taps into **imaginative thinking** — students aren't just answering a question, they're inhabiting a position and defending it on the spot. It sets the emotional and intellectual tone for everything that follows.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Makes an Argument? (10 Minutes)

Transition to the front of the room and explain that what just happened is exactly what argumentative writing tries to do on paper — persuade a reader with logic, evidence, and awareness of the other side.

Introduce the **AREA Framework** on the board:
- **A** — Assertion (your claim)
- **R** — Reasoning (why it's true)
- **E** — Evidence (proof that supports it)
- **A** — Acknowledgment (the other side — and your response)

Walk through a projected sample paragraph using this framework. Use color coding — highlight each component a different color so students can visually see how the pieces connect. Ask students to call out which color belongs where.

Emphasize: *"A strong argument doesn't ignore the other side — it faces it, names it, and respectfully pushes back."* This moment of acknowledging opposition is what separates a rant from a real argument.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — Deconstruct and Rebuild (12 Minutes)

Distribute the argument graphic organizer. Present students with a **broken argument** — one that has a claim but missing reasoning, weak evidence, and no acknowledgment of the opposing view.

**Example broken argument:** *"School should start later because students are tired."*

Working in pairs, students must:
1. Identify what's weak or missing
2. Add at least one piece of real reasoning
3. Suggest what the opposing claim might be
4. Write one sentence that responds to that opposing claim

**Creativity competency addressed — Collaborative Creativity:** Partners must negotiate ideas, challenge each other's thinking, and co-construct something stronger than either could alone. Walking around the room, prompt pairs with questions like: *"What would someone who disagrees say? Have you thought about why a parent might push back on this?"* This encourages **perspective-shifting**, a core creative thinking skill.

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — Build Your Own Argument (20 Minutes)

Now students work independently. Provide them with three topic choices so every student can connect to something that feels personally relevant:

1. Should students be allowed to use phones during lunch?
2. Should all middle schoolers be required to take an art class?
3. Should the school week be four days instead of five?

Students use their graphic organizer to plan, then write a full argumentative paragraph (or two, for advanced students) that includes all four AREA components.

**Creativity competency addressed — Original Thinking and Risk-Taking:** By offering choice, students are invited to select the topic that sparks genuine feeling. Encourage them to take a position they actually believe in — even if it feels unpopular. Remind them: *"The best arguments come from writers who actually care about what they're saying."*

Circulate the room. Offer targeted feedback: push students who have a claim but no counterargument, or those who have evidence but haven't connected it back to their claim.

---

### SHARE AND REFLECT — The Sticky Note Gallery (6 Minutes)

Ask three or four volunteers to read their arguments aloud. After each one, distribute two sticky notes to the class — one color for *"something that convinced me"* and one for *"a question I still have."* Students post their notes near the speaker's desk after each reading.

Readers briefly respond to one question they received. This builds **audience awareness**, which is essential both in writing and in creative communication.

---

### CLOSING — Exit Ticket (2 Minutes)

On a slip of paper, students answer:
- What is your claim?
- What is the strongest piece of evidence you used?
- What opposing argument did you acknowledge?

Collect as a formative assessment to guide the next day's revision lesson.

---

## Teacher Notes

Students who struggle with the graphic organizer may benefit from sentence starters. Advanced students should be challenged to include two pieces of evidence and a more nuanced response to the counterargument. Tomorrow's lesson will focus on revision using peer feedback from today's sticky notes.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Writing Strong Arguments with Clear Reasons and Relevant Evidence**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objectives:
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:  
- Understand the components of a strong argumentative essay.  
- Write a clear claim supported by logical reasons and relevant evidence.  
- Acknowledge and address opposing viewpoints respectfully.  
- Organize their argument in a logical sequence.  
- Demonstrate creativity through idea generation, flexible thinking, and effective communication.

---

### Creativity Competencies Addressed:
- **Idea Generation:** Encouraging students to brainstorm multiple claims and supporting reasons.  
- **Flexibility:** Teaching students to consider and incorporate opposing viewpoints.  
- **Originality:** Guiding students to develop unique arguments and evidence.  
- **Elaboration:** Helping students expand on their reasons with detailed evidence and explanations.  
- **Communication:** Practicing clear and persuasive writing and speaking.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Chart paper or projector for displaying examples  
- Handouts with a sample argumentative prompt and graphic organizer  
- Sticky notes  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction (10 minutes)**  
- **Hook:** Begin by asking students a question related to a familiar topic, e.g., “Should school start later in the morning?”  
- Write two opposing claims on the board:  
  - Claim A: School should start later.  
  - Claim B: School should start at the current time.  
- Ask students to quickly share reasons for each claim. Record their ideas on the board.  
- Explain that today’s lesson will focus on how to write strong arguments that include clear claims, reasons, evidence, and acknowledgment of opposing views.

**Creativity Focus:** Idea Generation — Students brainstorm multiple reasons for both sides.

---

#### 2. **Mini-Lesson: Components of an Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple graphic organizer on the board or handout with these sections:  
  1. Claim  
  2. Reason 1 + Evidence  
  3. Reason 2 + Evidence  
  4. Opposing Claim + Rebuttal  
  5. Conclusion  
- Define each part clearly:  
  - **Claim:** Your main point or opinion.  
  - **Reason:** Why you believe your claim is true.  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or quotes that support your reason.  
  - **Opposing Claim:** A viewpoint that disagrees with your claim.  
  - **Rebuttal:** Your response explaining why the opposing claim is less convincing.  
- Show a brief example paragraph that includes all these parts.  
- Emphasize logical organization: each reason should connect clearly to the claim, and the opposing claim should be addressed respectfully.

**Creativity Focus:** Elaboration — Students learn to expand reasons with evidence and develop rebuttals.

---

#### 3. **Guided Practice: Brainstorming and Organizing (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute a prompt related to a middle school-relevant topic, e.g., “Should students have homework every night?”  
- In pairs, students brainstorm:  
  - One clear claim (for or against homework every night).  
  - Two reasons supporting their claim.  
  - Evidence or examples for each reason.  
  - One opposing claim they might hear.  
  - A rebuttal to that opposing claim.  
- Use a graphic organizer handout to record their ideas.  
- Circulate and support students, encouraging flexible thinking by asking, “What might someone who disagrees say?” and “How can you respond to that?”

**Creativity Focus:** Flexibility — Students consider alternate viewpoints and adapt their thinking.

---

#### 4. **Independent Writing: Drafting an Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Students use their graphic organizer to write a short argumentative paragraph (5-7 sentences) that includes:  
  - A clear claim statement.  
  - Two reasons with supporting evidence.  
  - An acknowledgment of an opposing claim with a rebuttal.  
- Remind students to organize their writing logically and use transition words (because, however, although, therefore).  
- Encourage originality by asking them to use their own words and examples.

**Creativity Focus:** Originality and Communication — Students create unique arguments and practice clear writing.

---

#### 5. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each reading, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, opposing claim, and rebuttal.  
- Discuss how the writer organized their ideas and how effectively they addressed the opposing viewpoint.  
- End with a quick reflection: “What was one new thing you learned about writing arguments today?” and “How did thinking about the other side help your argument?”

**Creativity Focus:** Communication — Students practice speaking and listening skills; Reflection encourages metacognition.

---

### Assessment:
- Informal assessment through observation during brainstorming and writing.  
- Review of students’ written paragraphs to check for inclusion of claim, reasons, evidence, opposing claim, and rebuttal.  
- Participation in discussion and reflection.

---

### Extensions/Homework:
- Assign students to write a full argumentative essay on a topic of their choice, using the structure learned.  
- Encourage them to research additional evidence to support their claims.

---

### Summary:
This 55-minute lesson guides 7th graders through the process of writing a well-structured argumentative paragraph. It explicitly teaches the components of argument writing aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 and integrates creativity competencies by encouraging idea generation, flexible thinking, originality, elaboration, and communication. Through brainstorming, guided practice, independent writing, and sharing, students develop skills to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence while acknowledging opposing views logically and respectfully.
