## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (Grade 7)

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities.
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Materials:** Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with data tables, exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 for 2 cups, $6 for 4 cups, $9 for 6 cups."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join, plus $5 per visit."

Ask students: *"Which scenario shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**→ Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through origin). 
**Assessment:** Listen for clear, precise language during pair-share. Note whether students distinguish *"the price per cup stays the same"* vs. vague responses like *"the numbers go up."*

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships and introduce the four representations: **table, graph, equation (y = kx), verbal description**. Use the smoothie example to model each representation, emphasizing the constant of proportionality (k = 1.5).

Walk students through organizing the smoothie data into:
- A table with a third column for the ratio y/x
- A coordinate graph (showing the line through the origin)
- The equation y = 1.5x

**→ Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Modeling demonstrates how arranging data into structured columns reveals the constant ratio.
**Assessment:** During the next activity, check student notebooks for organized tables with labeled columns and consistent structure.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Sort (12 minutes)
Distribute a handout with six relationships (mix of tables, graphs, equations, and word problems). In pairs, students classify each as proportional or non-proportional and justify their answer using the constant ratio test.

Example items:
- Table: (1, 4), (2, 8), (3, 14) — *not proportional*
- Equation: y = 7x — *proportional, k = 7*
- Graph passing through (0, 2) and (4, 10) — *not proportional*

**→ Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must cross-check evidence across multiple representations. Does the table's ratio match what the graph shows? Does the equation fit the verbal description?
**Assessment:** Collect handouts. Look for justifications that reference *multiple* checks (e.g., "I checked every ratio AND confirmed it passes through the origin").

**→ Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some items contain "distractor" data — a partial pattern that looks proportional. Strong students will recognize that two matching ratios aren't enough; all ratios must match.
**Assessment:** Note which students catch the distractors versus those who stop at surface-level evidence.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task — "Pick the Better Deal" (15 minutes)
Pose this problem: *"Plan A: 5 movie tickets for $40. Plan B: 8 tickets for $60. Plan C: A flat $25 fee plus $4 per ticket."*

Groups of 3 must:
1. Identify which plans are proportional.
2. Determine which plan is the best deal for someone buying 12 tickets.
3. Defend their choice using at least two representations.

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must compare trade-offs — Plan C is cheaper for small purchases but more expensive for large ones.
**Assessment:** Circulate and listen for statements like *"It depends on how many tickets — Plan B is better long-term but Plan C wins if you only buy a few."* Award full credit when students articulate conditions, not just a single "best" answer.

**→ Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must build a logical argument tying their math to their conclusion.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a one-paragraph justification. Look for reasoning chains: *"Because k = 8 for Plan A and k = 7.5 for Plan B, and Plan C costs $73 for 12 tickets while Plan B costs $90… therefore…"*

---

### 5. Closing Reflection & Exit Ticket (11 minutes)

**Reflection prompt (5 min discussion):** *"What was one moment today when you changed your mind or realized your first answer was wrong? What made you reconsider?"*

Students share examples aloud.

**→ Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** This prompt forces metacognitive review — students articulate how their reasoning evolved.
**Assessment:** On the exit ticket, students answer in writing:
1. Define a proportional relationship in your own words.
2. Describe one strategy you used today and whether you'd use it again. Why or why not?

Score Question 2 for depth: A "2" response identifies a *specific* strategy adjustment (e.g., *"I started by only checking two ratios, but I realized I need to check all of them — next time I'll make a ratio column right away"*). A "1" response is generic (*"I'll study more"*).

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Detective Agency
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and verify proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook & Data Verification (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world scenario. The teacher presents a claim: "The city's new water billing system charges a perfectly proportional rate for water usage." Students are given two printed data sources to investigate this claim: a screenshot of a random social media complaint (Source A) and an official municipal water rate schedule combined with a verified consumer protection report (Source B). Students must determine if the relationship between gallons used and total cost is proportional.

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Before doing any math, students evaluate the two sources. They must discuss in pairs which source provides high-quality, reliable data to build their mathematical models, ultimately discarding the social media post in favor of the municipal schedule.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to pair discussions and assess whether students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by explicitly stating that Source B is authoritative and reliable for mathematical modeling, while Source A lacks verification and depth.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students rigorously cross-check the pricing information by comparing the municipal rate schedule with the independent consumer protection report to ensure the data points (gallons vs. cost) match before plotting them.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students actively comparing the numbers across the multiple authoritative documents. Success is assessed when students confidently validate the data points as accurate and consistent across both reliable sources.

### **Part 2: Guided Practice & Structuring Data (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Using the validated municipal water data, the teacher guides the class in translating the verbal description of the water rate into a structured table and a graph. 

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** The teacher provides a complex verbal description of the water rate: "For every 500 gallons of water, the city charges $15, and this rate remains constant regardless of the volume." Students must clarify this idea precisely by translating it into a simplified unit rate (cost per 100 gallons) and drawing a diagram (tape diagram) to support deeper understanding of the constant ratio.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the students' diagrams and unit rate calculations. Mastery is shown when a student precisely clarifies the wordy description into a clear, mathematically accurate visual model that proves the constant ratio ($3 per 100 gallons).
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students are given a scrambled list of data points representing water usage and cost. They must arrange this scattered information into a sequential, logical input/output table (x = gallons, y = cost) and subsequently plot it on a coordinate plane to reveal the straight line passing through the origin.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to ensure the table is organized in ascending order, strengthening the logical flow of the data and making the constant of proportionality ($y/x$) immediately visible.

### **Part 3: Collaborative "Proportionality Detectives" (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students work in small groups. Each group receives a "case file" containing a new scenario (e.g., a "Buy 2 Get 1 Free" shoe sale, a streaming service subscription with a base fee, or a recipe scaling). They must represent their scenario using an equation ($y = kx$), a graph, and a table.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Groups must choose which representation (table, graph, or equation) is the *best* tool to predict the cost of 10,000 items. They must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each option (e.g., a table takes too long to extend, a graph might lack precision at large scales, but an equation allows for instant, precise calculation).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess this during a whole-class share-out. Students must articulate the long-term implications of their choice, demonstrating that while graphs are great for quick visual checks, equations are the most efficient choice for large-scale predictions.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Groups analyzing the "Buy 2 Get 1 Free" or "Base Fee" scenarios must decide if the relationship is proportional. They must apply sophisticated mathematical reasoning, anticipating complexities (e.g., recognizing that a base fee means the graph will not pass through the origin, or that a free item changes the unit rate).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect the groups' final written verdicts. Assessment is based on their ability to justify their conclusion using sound logic (e.g., "The streaming service is not proportional because the ratio of $y/x$ is not constant due to the $5 startup fee, which is proven by the graph starting at (0,5) instead of (0,0).").

### **Part 4: Closure & Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that requires them to look back at a mistake they made or a misconception they held during the lesson (e.g., assuming all straight lines are proportional).

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students write a short reflection paragraph responding to the prompt: "Describe a moment today when you had to adjust your mathematical thinking. How did creating a graph or table help you realize your initial assumption about proportionality was incorrect?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will demonstrate deep self-awareness, with the student explicitly stating how they strategically adjusted their thinking process (e.g., "I initially thought the shoe sale was proportional because the cost went up steadily, but when I organized the data into a table, I saw the unit rate changed on the third pair. I adjusted my thinking to always check $y/x$ for every point.").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

---

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- “Proportional or Not?” handout  
- Exit ticket  
- Colored pencils or markers  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Spot the Pattern — 5 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Ask students:  
1. What patterns do you notice?  
2. Which table might show a proportional relationship? Why?

Students write a quick response, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify relevant information from the tables, such as ratios, patterns, and constant change.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying that in Table A, each y-value is 3 times the x-value. Look for precise statements such as, “The ratio y/x is always 3,” rather than vague statements like, “It goes up by 3.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain:

A proportional relationship is a relationship between two quantities where one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other. It can be written as:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Key features:
- In a table, the ratio y/x is constant.
- In a graph, the points form a straight line through the origin.
- In an equation, the relationship can be written as y = kx.
- In a verbal description, one quantity is described as “per” one unit of another.
- In a diagram, equal groups or repeated ratios show the same multiplicative relationship.

Use Table A to show:

y/x = 3/1 = 6/2 = 9/3 = 12/4 = 3

So the relationship is proportional, and the equation is:

**y = 3x**

Then explain why Table B is not proportional:

4/1 = 4, but 7/2 = 3.5, so the ratio is not constant.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize proportional relationship features into a five-part reference chart: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check that students arrange information logically and consistently. For example, under “graph,” they should write “straight line through the origin,” not just “straight line.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: One Relationship, Five Representations — 15 minutes
Present this situation:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

As a class, represent the relationship in five ways.

1. **Verbal description:** The total cost is $2 per notebook.  
2. **Table:**

| Notebooks | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost ($) | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |

3. **Equation:** y = 2x  
4. **Graph:** Plot points (0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8). Draw a straight line through the origin.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing each notebook connected to $2.

Ask students:  
- How do we know this is proportional?  
- What does the constant of proportionality mean in this situation?  
- What would the graph look like if there were a starting fee of $3?

Discuss how a relationship such as y = 2x + 3 would not be proportional because the graph would not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify whether the notebook relationship is proportional using evidence from multiple representations.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for logical reasoning: “It is proportional because the cost per notebook is always $2, the equation is y = 2x, and the graph passes through the origin.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes
Give pairs a handout with four scenarios. Students must decide whether each is proportional and represent at least two of them in multiple ways.

Example scenarios:

**A.** A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
**B.** A taxi charges $5 to start and $2 per mile.  
**C.** A bike travels 12 miles every hour at a constant speed.  
**D.** A plant is already 4 inches tall and grows 2 inches per week.

For each scenario, students complete:
- Is it proportional?  
- Evidence from a table  
- Evidence from an equation  
- Evidence from a graph or diagram  
- Explanation in words

Encourage students to compare tricky cases, especially taxi fare and plant growth, because both have a starting amount and therefore are not proportional.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check their decision using more than one representation, such as table and graph, instead of relying on only one clue.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students verifying conclusions with multiple pieces of mathematical evidence, such as constant ratio and graph through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose the strongest evidence to support their answer. For example, they decide whether “the numbers go up” is enough, or whether “the ratio is constant” is better evidence.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review written explanations for high-quality mathematical evidence. Strong responses should include specific ratios, equations, or graph features.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare different representations and decide which one best proves proportionality for each situation.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask pairs, “Which representation made your decision easiest, and why?” Students should explain trade-offs, such as “The table helped us see the ratios, but the graph made it clear there was a starting amount.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes
Bring the class together. Review one proportional and one non-proportional example from the partner activity.

Ask:
- What mistake might someone make with this problem?
- How can we check our answer?
- Why does a starting value usually mean the relationship is not proportional?

Emphasize that proportional relationships must have a constant ratio and pass through the origin when graphed.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes
Students answer:

1. A gym charges $10 per month. Is the relationship between months and total cost proportional? Represent it with an equation.  
2. A different gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month. Is this proportional? Why or why not?  
3. Reflection: “When deciding if a relationship was proportional, the strategy that helped me most was ______ because ______.”

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on which strategy helped them determine proportionality.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for metacognitive responses that name a strategy and explain why it helped, such as “Checking y/x helped me because I could see if the ratio stayed the same.”

---

## Assessment Summary
Students demonstrate mastery when they can:
- Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
- Explain that proportional relationships have the form **y = kx**.
- Identify the constant of proportionality.
- Distinguish proportional relationships from relationships with starting values.
- Justify conclusions using clear mathematical evidence.

---

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Spot the Pattern — 5 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Ask students:  
1. What patterns do you notice?  
2. Which table might show a proportional relationship? Why?

Students write a quick response, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify relevant information from the tables, such as ratios, patterns, and constant change.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying that in Table A, each y-value is 3 times the x-value. Look for precise statements such as, “The ratio y/x is always 3,” rather than vague statements like, “It goes up by 3.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain:

A proportional relationship is a relationship between two quantities where one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other. It can be written as:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Key features:
- In a table, the ratio y/x is constant.
- In a graph, the points form a straight line through the origin.
- In an equation, the relationship can be written as y = kx.
- In a verbal description, one quantity is described as “per” one unit of another.
- In a diagram, equal groups or repeated ratios show the same multiplicative relationship.

Use Table A to show:

y/x = 3/1 = 6/2 = 9/3 = 12/4 = 3

So the relationship is proportional, and the equation is:

**y = 3x**

Then explain why Table B is not proportional:

4/1 = 4, but 7/2 = 3.5, so the ratio is not constant.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize proportional relationship features into a five-part reference chart: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check that students arrange information logically and consistently. For example, under “graph,” they should write “straight line through the origin,” not just “straight line.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: One Relationship, Five Representations — 15 minutes
Present this situation:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

As a class, represent the relationship in five ways.

1. **Verbal description:** The total cost is $2 per notebook.  
2. **Table:**

| Notebooks | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost ($) | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |

3. **Equation:** y = 2x  
4. **Graph:** Plot points (0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8). Draw a straight line through the origin.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing each notebook connected to $2.

Ask students:  
- How do we know this is proportional?  
- What does the constant of proportionality mean in this situation?  
- What would the graph look like if there were a starting fee of $3?

Discuss how a relationship such as y = 2x + 3 would not be proportional because the graph would not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify whether the notebook relationship is proportional using evidence from multiple representations.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for logical reasoning: “It is proportional because the cost per notebook is always $2, the equation is y = 2x, and the graph passes through the origin.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes
Give pairs a handout with four scenarios. Students must decide whether each is proportional and represent at least two of them in multiple ways.

Example scenarios:

**A.** A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
**B.** A taxi charges $5 to start and $2 per mile.  
**C.** A bike travels 12 miles every hour at a constant speed.  
**D.** A plant is already 4 inches tall and grows 2 inches per week.

For each scenario, students complete:
- Is it proportional?  
- Evidence from a table  
- Evidence from an equation  
- Evidence from a graph or diagram  
- Explanation in words

Encourage students to compare tricky cases, especially taxi fare and plant growth, because both have a starting amount and therefore are not proportional.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check their decision using more than one representation, such as table and graph, instead of relying on only one clue.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students verifying conclusions with multiple pieces of mathematical evidence, such as constant ratio and graph through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose the strongest evidence to support their answer. For example, they decide whether “the numbers go up” is enough, or whether “the ratio is constant” is better evidence.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review written explanations for high-quality mathematical evidence. Strong responses should include specific ratios, equations, or graph features.

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare different representations and decide which one best proves proportionality for each situation.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask pairs, “Which representation made your decision easiest, and why?” Students should explain trade-offs, such as “The table helped us see the ratios, but the graph made it clear there was a starting amount.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes
Bring the class together. Review one proportional and one non-proportional example from the partner activity.

Ask:
- What mistake might someone make with this problem?
- How can we check our answer?
- Why does a starting value usually mean the relationship is not proportional?

Emphasize that proportional relationships must have a constant ratio and pass through the origin when graphed.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes
Students answer:

1. A gym charges $10 per month. Is the relationship between months and total cost proportional? Represent it with an equation.  
2. A different gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month. Is this proportional? Why or why not?  
3. Reflection: “When deciding if a relationship was proportional, the strategy that helped me most was ______ because ______.”

**Critical Thinking Target: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on which strategy helped them determine proportionality.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for metacognitive responses that name a strategy and explain why it helped, such as “Checking y/x helped me because I could see if the ratio stayed the same.”

1. Identify which plans are proportional.
2. Determine the best deal for 12 tickets.
3. Defend their choice using at least two representations.

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare trade-offs — Plan C is cheaper for small purchases but more expensive for large ones.
