## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Learning Objective:**  
Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

**Materials:**  
Graph paper, rulers, calculators if appropriate, projector/board, student handout, exit ticket.

---

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two situations:

1. A cyclist travels 12 miles in 1 hour, 24 miles in 2 hours, and 36 miles in 3 hours.  
2. A gym charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $5 per visit.

Ask students:  
- “Which situation shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”  
- “What patterns do you notice?”

Students write independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity:** Students identify important details: constant rate, starting value, and whether the relationship starts at zero.  
- **Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying that the cyclist’s distance is always 12 miles per hour and starts at 0, while the gym situation has an initial fee, so it is not proportional.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship has two key features:

1. The ratio between the two quantities is constant.  
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin, or point `(0, 0)`.

Use the cyclist example to build a table:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |

Ask students to find the ratio `miles ÷ hours`. Guide them to see the constant of proportionality is 12.

Write the equation:

`y = 12x`

Explain:
- `x` = hours  
- `y` = miles  
- `12` = constant of proportionality, or unit rate

Then graph the points and show that the line passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity:** Students organize the same relationship as a table, graph, equation, and verbal description.  
- **Assessment:** Check student notes to see whether representations are clearly connected and logically arranged.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this problem:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

Students complete:

1. Make a table for 0, 2, 4, 6, and 8 cups of sugar.  
2. Write an equation for cups of flour in terms of cups of sugar.  
3. Graph the relationship.  
4. Write a sentence explaining the relationship.

Guide the class through each step.

Expected table:

| Sugar | Flour |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 2 | 3 |
| 4 | 6 |
| 6 | 9 |
| 8 | 12 |

Equation: `y = 1.5x`, where `x` is sugar and `y` is flour.

Ask: “How can we check that all of these representations match?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity:** Students cross-check the table, graph, and equation to confirm they show the same relationship.  
- **Assessment:** Ask students to point to one value in the table, locate it on the graph, and test it in the equation.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence of proportionality.  
- **Assessment:** Students explain whether the table, graph, or equation best proves the relationship is proportional and why.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Which Plan Is Better? — 13 minutes

Give students this scenario:

“Two printing companies charge for posters.”

- **Company A:** 4 posters cost $10, 8 posters cost $20, 12 posters cost $30.  
- **Company B:** Charges a $6 setup fee plus $2 per poster.

Partners answer:

1. Which company has a proportional relationship?  
2. Represent each company with a table and equation.  
3. If you need 20 posters, which company is cheaper?  
4. Is the cheaper company always the better choice? Explain.

Expected:
- Company A is proportional: `y = 2.5x`
- Company B is not proportional: `y = 2x + 6`
- For 20 posters: A costs $50; B costs $46, so B is cheaper for 20 posters.

Discuss that proportional does not always mean cheapest.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity:** Students compare Company A and Company B, considering cost, setup fee, and number of posters.  
- **Assessment:** Look for students explaining trade-offs, such as “Company B is cheaper for 20 posters, but it is not proportional because of the setup fee.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity:** Students justify which company to choose using equations and calculations.  
- **Assessment:** Require students to support conclusions with mathematical evidence, not just preference.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion: Common Misconceptions — 6 minutes

Discuss these questions:

- “Can a relationship be linear but not proportional?”  
- “Does a straight line always mean proportional?”  
- “Why must a proportional graph go through the origin?”

Emphasize:
- A proportional relationship must be a straight line through `(0, 0)`.
- A relationship with an added fee can be linear but not proportional.
- The constant of proportionality is the unit rate.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 7 minutes

Students complete independently:

1. A table shows:  

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |
| 8 | 20 |

Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

2. Write an equation for the relationship.

3. Reflection: “What representation helped you most today—table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”

Expected:
- Yes, proportional because `y ÷ x = 2.5` for all pairs.
- Equation: `y = 2.5x`

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity:** Students reflect on which representation helped them understand proportionality.  
- **Assessment:** Review responses for evidence that students can describe their own thinking, such as “The table helped me because I could compare ratios.”

---

## Differentiation

**Support:**  
Provide partially completed tables and graph axes. Use color-coding to connect matching values across table, graph, and equation.

**Challenge:**  
Ask students to create their own proportional and non-proportional scenarios, then trade with a partner to identify and represent each one.

## Assessment Summary

Students demonstrate mastery if they can:
- Identify proportional relationships using constant ratios.
- Represent proportional relationships with tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.
- Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional.
- Use mathematical reasoning to compare real-world situations.
