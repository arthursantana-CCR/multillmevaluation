## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A coffee shop sells 3 muffins for $6."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students think-pair-share.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must identify what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through origin). 
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for precise language: "the ratio stays the same" vs. vague statements. Collect 2–3 student explanations on whiteboards.

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the example: *"Maria earns $15 for every 2 hours of babysitting."*

Model all five representations side-by-side on the board:
- **Table:** Hours (2, 4, 6) → Dollars (15, 30, 45)
- **Graph:** Plot points, draw line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 7.5x
- **Diagram:** Bar/tape diagram showing the ratio
- **Verbal:** "Earnings are proportional to hours worked at $7.50/hour"

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule that proportional graphs pass through (0,0).

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Students copy this organized 5-representation chart into their notebooks.
**Assessment:** Circulate and check notebook layouts. Look for clear labeling and logical arrangement linking representations (e.g., arrows connecting table values to graph points).

### 3. Guided Practice: "Spot the Proportion" (10 minutes)
Hand out four scenarios:
1. A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 miles in 3 hours, 240 miles in 4 hours.
2. A taxi charges $3 flat fee plus $2 per mile.
3. 5 apples cost $2.50; 10 apples cost $5.00.
4. A plant grows 2 cm the first week, 5 cm the second, 9 cm the third.

In pairs, students determine which are proportional and justify using **at least two representations**.

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** Students must verify their answers by cross-checking the table against the equation or graph.
**Assessment:** Ask probing questions: *"How did you confirm scenario 1 is proportional? Did you check more than one way?"* Strong responses verify with both ratio comparison and the origin test.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence of proportionality.
**Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Look for statements like *"The graph is most convincing because it shows a straight line through the origin"* rather than surface-level picks.

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Best Deal (12 minutes)
Present: *"You need to buy notebooks for a club. Store A sells 4 notebooks for $10. Store B sells 6 notebooks for $14. Store C charges $3 per notebook plus a $1 membership."*

Students must:
- Determine which deals are proportional
- Build a table/graph to compare
- Recommend the best option for buying 12 notebooks AND for buying 30 notebooks

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Students compare three options across different quantities, noticing that the "best" choice may change.
**Assessment:** Review written recommendations for trade-off language: *"Store B is cheaper for small amounts, but Store A becomes better at higher quantities because..."* Score using a quick rubric (superficial = 1; systematic trade-off analysis = 2).

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students must justify their choice mathematically.
**Assessment:** Check that reasoning uses the constant of proportionality and unit rates accurately. Flag faulty logic such as comparing totals without per-unit analysis.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
**Exit Ticket (3 questions):**
1. Give an equation, table, or graph that represents a proportional relationship and explain why.
2. Describe one mistake you almost made today and how you corrected it.
3. Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, words) helps YOU best identify proportionality? Why?

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** Questions 2 and 3 require metacognition.
**Assessment:** Review exit tickets. Score deep reflections (level 2) when students articulate strategy shifts—e.g., *"I first thought Scenario 4 was proportional because the numbers grew, but I checked the ratios and they weren't equal, so I changed my answer."* Score level 1 for surface mentions without explanation.

### 6. Closure (3 minutes)
Quick whole-class recap: *"What are the two key tests for a proportional relationship?"* (constant ratio + passes through origin).

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables; sentence frames for justifications.
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Students find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, distances), represent it in three ways, and write a one-paragraph reflection on how they verified it was proportional.

---

This lesson integrates all five critical thinking subcompetencies (CRI1a–CRI5a) through structured tasks where each cognitive skill is both practiced and observable through student artifacts (notebooks, written justifications, exit tickets).

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Detective: Investigating Real-World Relationships
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to recognize, verify, and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Introduction and Clarifying the Problem (10 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a verbal description of a real-world scenario: "A local farm claims their water pump fills a reservoir at a rate of 15 gallons every 2 minutes, but a neighboring farm says their pump is faster, filling 40 gallons in 5 minutes." The verbal description is intentionally wordy and slightly confusing. Students must translate this verbal description into a precise mathematical definition, identifying the independent variable (time in minutes) and the dependent variable (volume in gallons). 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** Students rewrite the wordy prompt into clear, precise "if/then" statements and define their variables ($x$ = time, $y$ = gallons) to support a deeper mathematical understanding of the rates.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and review students' whiteboards to ensure they have stripped away irrelevant words and precisely clarified the variables and rates before moving to the math.

### **Part 2: Data Gathering and Source Evaluation (15 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
To explore proportionality further, students are given a new task: determining the proportional relationship between the distance driven and fuel consumed (MPG) for a specific hybrid car. Instead of being handed the math problem, students are given three printed sources: a random internet forum post, an official Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) data sheet, and a promotional flyer from a used car salesman. Students must select the best source, cross-check the MPG claims, and extract the raw data points.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** In small groups, students evaluate the three sources, demonstrating sophisticated judgment by discarding the forum post and salesman flyer, and selecting the EPA data sheet as the highest-quality, authoritative source for their mathematical model.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group discussions, assessing whether students justify their choice based on the authority and objectivity of the EPA versus the bias of the salesman.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** Before graphing, students rigorously cross-check the EPA’s MPG claim by comparing it against a secondary authoritative source provided on their tablets (e.g., the official car manufacturer’s engineering specifications) to validate the constant of proportionality ($k$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check students' guided notes to verify that they have cited two reliable sources that confirm the same unit rate before proceeding.

### **Part 3: Organizing and Representing Data (15 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Once the valid data is collected, students find that the data points are scattered (e.g., 3 gallons for 150 miles, 1 gallon for 50 miles, 4 gallons for 200 miles). Students must organize this data and represent it using a table, a graph, and an equation ($y = 50x$). 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** Students arrange the scattered, randomized data points into a sequential, logical table (ascending order of $x$) to strengthen the logical flow and easily spot the constant ratio ($y/x$). 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the students' tables to ensure the information is structured consistently, making the proportional relationship visually obvious.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** The teacher introduces a "curveball" data point representing a different car that costs a flat $10 rental fee plus fuel. Students must use sophisticated mathematical reasoning to decide if this new relationship is proportional. They must justify their decision by proving the graph does not pass through the origin $(0,0)$ and the ratio $y/x$ is not constant.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' written justifications to ensure they use sound mathematical logic and the strict definition of proportionality to support their conclusions.

### **Part 4: Evaluating Representations (10 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students now have a table, a graph, an equation, and a verbal description of the hybrid car's fuel efficiency. The teacher asks: "If you were presenting this data to a 5th-grade class, versus presenting it to a team of automotive engineers, which representation would you use and why?"

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each representation. For example, they might argue that a graph is highly visual and best for 5th graders, but an equation ($y = 50x$) is more precise and efficient for engineers. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect a T-chart from each group where they compare the pros and cons of tables, graphs, and equations, assessing their ability to weigh alternative choices based on the audience.

### **Part 5: Metacognitive Closure (5 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an exit ticket reflecting on the lesson's challenges and their own learning progression regarding proportional relationships.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Moment:** Students write a brief reflection answering: "What was a moment today where your initial assumption about proportionality was wrong? How did you strategically adjust your thinking process to understand the concept better?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess students' deep metacognitive awareness, looking for specific examples of how they recognized a misunderstanding (e.g., confusing a straight line with a proportional line) and corrected it.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Learning Target:**  
Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.

**Materials:**  
Graph paper, pencils, projector/board, student handout, rulers, exit ticket.

---

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 5 minutes

Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $6  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $10  
- Deal C: 4 granola bars for $9

Ask students: “Which deals have the same price per bar? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students identify the relevant quantities: number of bars and total cost.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying what needs to be compared, such as: “I need the cost per 1 bar,” or “The important information is bars and dollars.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — clarifies ideas precisely to support understanding.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when the ratio between two quantities stays constant. In a proportional relationship:

- The table has equivalent ratios.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- The equation can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- A diagram can show equal groups or repeated scaling.
- A verbal description explains a constant rate.

Use the snack example:

| Bars | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 5 | 10 |

The cost per bar is always $2, so the equation is **y = 2x**.

Graph the points: (1, 2), (3, 6), (5, 10). Show that the line goes through the origin.

Then compare with Deal C: 4 bars for $9 means $2.25 per bar, so it does not match the same relationship.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students see the same relationship arranged in a table, equation, graph, and words.  
**Observe/Assess:** Check that students organize values consistently, labeling x as bars and y as cost. Look for student work saying: “x = bars, y = cost, so y = 2x.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — arranges information to strengthen logical flow.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A bike rental shop charges $8 per hour.”

As a class, complete the following:

1. Verbal description: “The total cost is $8 for each hour.”
2. Table:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

3. Equation: **y = 8x**
4. Graph: Plot points and draw a line through the origin.
5. Diagram: Draw equal groups of $8 for each hour.

Ask: “How can each representation prove the relationship is proportional?”

Expected responses:

- “The cost per hour is always 8.”
- “The graph is a straight line through zero.”
- “The equation is y = 8x.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and words all match.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to point to at least two representations that confirm proportionality. Look for statements like: “The table and graph both show the same constant rate.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — cross-checks information using multiple mathematical sources.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide which information is useful for proving proportionality.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask: “Is one point enough to prove it is proportional?” Students should explain that one point alone is not enough; they need a constant rate or a graph through the origin.  
**Target Level:** 2 — selects relevant, high-quality mathematical evidence.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Which Representation Is Most Helpful? — 13 minutes

Give partners three relationship cards:

**Card A:**  
A babysitter earns $12 per hour.

**Card B:**  

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

**Card C:**  
A graph shows points (0, 2), (1, 5), (2, 8), (3, 11).

Partners must:

1. Decide whether each relationship is proportional.
2. Represent each proportional relationship in at least two other ways.
3. Explain which representation helped them decide fastest and why.

Correct ideas:

- Card A is proportional: **y = 12x**.
- Card B is proportional: **y = 4x**.
- Card C is not proportional because it does not pass through the origin, even though it increases by 3 each time.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare representations: table, graph, equation, verbal description.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for partner discussion such as: “The table is faster for checking ratios, but the graph makes it easy to see if it goes through zero.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — evaluates trade-offs between choices.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students justify whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
**Observe/Assess:** Require each pair to write one complete justification. Strong evidence might say: “Card C is not proportional because the graph does not include (0,0), so it cannot be written as y = kx.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — uses logical reasoning and addresses complexity, such as constant increase versus proportionality.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 7 minutes

Invite pairs to share one card. Emphasize a common misconception: a relationship can increase by the same amount but still not be proportional if it does not go through the origin.

Ask:

- “Why is Card C not proportional even though it has a pattern?”
- “What is the difference between a constant difference and a constant ratio?”

Students should conclude that proportional relationships require a constant ratio and an origin point on the graph.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 8 minutes

Students complete:

1. A taxi charges $5 to start and $3 per mile. Is the total cost proportional to miles? Explain.
2. A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar. Make a table for 1, 2, and 4 batches.
3. Write one sentence: “At first I thought ___, but now I know ___.”

Expected answer for #1: Not proportional because when miles = 0, cost = $5, not $0.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on how their understanding changed.  
**Observe/Assess:** Review reflection sentences. Evidence may include: “At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but now I know it must go through the origin.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — shows metacognitive awareness and adjustment of thinking.

---

By the end of the lesson, students should be able to:

- Identify proportional relationships using constant ratios.
- Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.
- Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional.
- Use evidence from multiple representations to support conclusions.

## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Learning Target:**  
Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.

**Materials:**  
Graph paper, pencils, projector/board, student handout, rulers, exit ticket.

---

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 5 minutes

Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $6  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $10  
- Deal C: 4 granola bars for $9

Ask students: “Which deals have the same price per bar? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students identify the relevant quantities: number of bars and total cost.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying what needs to be compared, such as: “I need the cost per 1 bar,” or “The important information is bars and dollars.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — clarifies ideas precisely to support understanding.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when the ratio between two quantities stays constant. In a proportional relationship:

- The table has equivalent ratios.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- The equation can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- A diagram can show equal groups or repeated scaling.
- A verbal description explains a constant rate.

Use the snack example:

| Bars | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 5 | 10 |

The cost per bar is always $2, so the equation is **y = 2x**.

Graph the points: (1, 2), (3, 6), (5, 10). Show that the line goes through the origin.

Then compare with Deal C: 4 bars for $9 means $2.25 per bar, so it does not match the same relationship.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students see the same relationship arranged in a table, equation, graph, and words.  
**Observe/Assess:** Check that students organize values consistently, labeling x as bars and y as cost. Look for student work saying: “x = bars, y = cost, so y = 2x.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — arranges information to strengthen logical flow.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A bike rental shop charges $8 per hour.”

As a class, complete the following:

1. Verbal description: “The total cost is $8 for each hour.”
2. Table:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

3. Equation: **y = 8x**
4. Graph: Plot points and draw a line through the origin.
5. Diagram: Draw equal groups of $8 for each hour.

Ask: “How can each representation prove the relationship is proportional?”

Expected responses:

- “The cost per hour is always 8.”
- “The graph is a straight line through zero.”
- “The equation is y = 8x.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and words all match.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to point to at least two representations that confirm proportionality. Look for statements like: “The table and graph both show the same constant rate.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — cross-checks information using multiple mathematical sources.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide which information is useful for proving proportionality.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask: “Is one point enough to prove it is proportional?” Students should explain that one point alone is not enough; they need a constant rate or a graph through the origin.  
**Target Level:** 2 — selects relevant, high-quality mathematical evidence.

### 4. Partner Task: Which Representation Is Most Helpful? — 13 minutes

Give partners three relationship cards:

**Card A:**  
A babysitter earns $12 per hour.

**Card B:**  

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

**Card C:**  
A graph shows points (0, 2), (1, 5), (2, 8), (3, 11).

Partners must:

1. Decide whether each relationship is proportional.
2. Represent each proportional relationship in at least two other ways.
3. Explain which representation helped them decide fastest and why.

Correct ideas:

- Card A is proportional: **y = 12x**.
- Card B is proportional: **y = 4x**.
- Card C is not proportional because it does not pass through the origin, even though it increases by 3 each time.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare representations: table, graph, equation, verbal description.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for partner discussion such as: “The table is faster for checking ratios, but the graph makes it easy to see if it goes through zero.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — evaluates trade-offs between choices.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students justify whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
**Observe/Assess:** Require each pair to write one complete justification. Strong evidence might say: “Card C is not proportional because the graph does not include (0,0), so it cannot be written as y = kx.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — uses logical reasoning and addresses complexity, such as constant increase versus proportionality.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 7 minutes

Invite pairs to share one card. Emphasize a common misconception: a relationship can increase by the same amount but still not be proportional if it does not go through the origin.

Ask:

- “Why is Card C not proportional even though it has a pattern?”
- “What is the difference between a constant difference and a constant ratio?”

Students should conclude that proportional relationships require a constant ratio and an origin point on the graph.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 8 minutes

Students complete:

1. A taxi charges $5 to start and $3 per mile. Is the total cost proportional to miles? Explain.
2. A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar. Make a table for 1, 2, and 4 batches.
3. Write one sentence: “At first I thought ___, but now I know ___.”

Expected answer for #1: Not proportional because when miles = 0, cost = $5, not $0.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on how their understanding changed.  
**Observe/Assess:** Review reflection sentences. Evidence may include: “At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but now I know it must go through the origin.”  
**Target Level:** 2 — shows metacognitive awareness and adjustment of thinking.

# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2  
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, scenario handout, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Fair?" (7 minutes)
Display three snack deals:
- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $6
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $10
- Deal C: 4 granola bars for $9

Ask: *"Which deals have the same price per bar? How do you know?"* Students work independently (2 min), then think-pair-share.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students identify the relevant quantities (bars, cost) and the operation needed (unit rate).  
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for precise language: *"the ratio stays the same,"* or *"I need cost per 1 bar."* Collect 2–3 student explanations on whiteboards. Level 2 = precise variable identification; Level 1 = vague comparison.

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce: *"Maria earns $15 for every 2 hours of babysitting."* Model all five representations side-by-side:
- **Table:** Hours (2, 4, 6) → Dollars (15, 30, 45)
- **Graph:** Plot points; draw line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 7.5x
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal groups
- **Verbal:** "Earnings are proportional to hours worked at $7.50/hour"

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule that proportional graphs pass through (0,0). State the two tests: constant ratio AND passes through origin.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Students copy this 5-representation chart into their notebooks, labeling x and y consistently.  
**Assessment:** Circulate and check notebook layouts. Look for arrows connecting table values to graph points and consistent variable labeling. Level 2 = logical flow strengthens understanding across representations.

### 3. Guided Practice: "Spot the Proportion" (10 minutes)
Hand out four scenarios:
1. A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 in 3 hours, 240 in 4 hours.
2. A taxi charges a $3 flat fee plus $2 per mile.
3. 5 apples cost $2.50; 10 apples cost $5.00.
4. A plant grows 2 cm week 1, 5 cm week 2, 9 cm week 3.

In pairs, students determine which are proportional and justify using **at least two representations**.

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** Students cross-check answers by verifying the table against the equation or graph.  
**Assessment:** Ask probing questions: *"How did you confirm scenario 1 is proportional? Did you check more than one way?"* Strong responses verify with both ratio comparison and the origin test.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Students decide which representation provides the strongest evidence of proportionality and recognize that a single point is insufficient.  
**Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Level 2 = *"The graph is most convincing because it shows a straight line through the origin"*; Level 1 = surface-level picks without justification.

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Best Deal (12 minutes)
Present: *"You need notebooks for a club. Store A: 4 notebooks for $10. Store B: 6 for $14. Store C: $3 per notebook plus a $1 membership fee."*

Students must:
- Determine which deals are proportional
- Build a table and graph to compare
- Recommend the best option for buying 12 notebooks AND 30 notebooks

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Students compare three options across different quantities, noticing the "best" choice may shift.  
**Assessment:** Review recommendations for trade-off language: *"Store B is cheaper for small amounts, but Store A becomes better at higher quantities because..."* Level 2 = systematic trade-off analysis; Level 1 = superficial pick.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students justify their choice mathematically using k and unit rates.  
**Assessment:** Check that reasoning uses the constant of proportionality accurately. Strong evidence: *"Store C is not proportional because the $1 fee means the graph doesn't pass through (0,0), so it cannot be written as y = kx."* Flag faulty logic such as comparing totals without per-unit analysis.

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion (4 minutes)
Address the key misconception: a relationship can increase by the same amount but still not be proportional (constant difference ≠ constant ratio). Ask: *"What's the difference between scenario 4 (plant) and scenario 1 (car)?"*

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (10 minutes)
1. A taxi charges $5 to start and $3 per mile. Is total cost proportional to miles? Explain.
2. Describe one mistake you almost made today and how you corrected it.
3. Complete: *"At first I thought ___, but now I know ___."*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** Questions 2 and 3 require metacognition.  
**Assessment:** Review exit tickets. Level 2 = strategy shifts articulated, e.g., *"I first thought scenario 4 was proportional because the numbers grew, but I checked the ratios and they weren't equal, so I changed my answer."* Level 1 = surface mention without explanation.
