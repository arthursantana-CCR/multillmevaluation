## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to justify their reasoning.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout with 4 scenarios (smoothie recipes, car speeds, phone plans, paint mixing)
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Clarifying the Concept (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a*
Display two tables on the board: one showing a proportional relationship (cups of juice to servings: 2/4, 3/6, 5/10) and one that is *not* proportional (ages of siblings over years). Ask: **"Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?"**

Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide students to clarify the definition: a proportional relationship has a constant ratio (unit rate, *k*) where y = kx, and its graph passes through the origin.

**Assessing CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students who can precisely articulate *why* a relationship is proportional (e.g., "every ratio simplifies to the same number"). Record student definitions on the board, refining vague statements into precise ones. Level 2 evidence: students distinguish "constant difference" from "constant ratio."

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b*
Introduce a single scenario: **"A smoothie recipe uses 3 cups of strawberries for every 2 cups of yogurt."** Together, represent it five ways:
- **Verbal:** "For every 2 cups yogurt, use 3 cups strawberries."
- **Table:** Yogurt: 2, 4, 6, 8 | Strawberries: 3, 6, 9, 12
- **Equation:** s = 1.5y
- **Graph:** Plot points; note line through origin.
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 3:2 ratio blocks.

Have students copy this organized chart in their notebooks, with each representation in its own quadrant.

**Assessing CRI1b (Organizing information):** Circulate to check notebooks. Are representations clearly labeled and logically connected? Level 2 evidence: students draw arrows or annotations showing how *k* = 1.5 appears in each representation.

---

### 3. Group Investigation: Evaluating Phone Plan Claims (15 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a, CRI2b, CRI3a*
Distribute a handout with three phone plan ads:
- **Plan A:** "$15 for 3 GB — best deal!"
- **Plan B:** Table showing 2 GB/$12, 5 GB/$30, 8 GB/$48
- **Plan C:** Graph showing a line with slope $4/GB but a $10 starting fee

Groups of 4 must: (a) determine which plans are truly proportional, (b) calculate unit rates, (c) decide which is the best value for someone using 6 GB/month, and (d) identify which claim is misleading.

**Assessing CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Listen for groups questioning Plan A's "best deal" claim by cross-checking with the unit rates from B and C. Level 2 evidence: students verify by computing *k* across multiple data points.

**Assessing CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Look for groups who recognize Plan C is *not* proportional (y-intercept ≠ 0) despite looking linear. Collect group worksheets; rate based on whether they used all relevant data points or relied on a single number.

**Assessing CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must write 2–3 sentences justifying their pick for the 6 GB user. Level 2 evidence: students consider trade-offs ("Plan C is cheaper per GB but the flat fee makes it worse for low users").

---

### 4. Whole-Class Reasoning Discussion (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI4a*
Each group presents their best-value pick. Pose a counter-question: **"If a customer might use anywhere from 2 to 10 GB, does your answer change? Defend your reasoning with a graph or equation."**

Push students to use mathematical evidence (equations, points on a graph) rather than intuition.

**Assessing CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Score oral arguments. Level 2 evidence: students anticipate edge cases ("At low usage Plan B wins; at high usage Plan C wins—the lines cross at 5 GB"). Level -1: students assert a winner without computation.

---

### 5. Exit Ticket & Metacognitive Reflection (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a*
Students complete two tasks individually:

**Part A (Math):** Given the verbal description "A car travels 150 miles in 3 hours at constant speed," create a table, equation, and graph. State the unit rate.

**Part B (Reflection):** Answer in 3–4 sentences:
- *What strategy did I use first to check if a relationship was proportional? Did it work?*
- *What mistake did I almost make today, and how did I catch it?*
- *Next time I see a proportional relationship problem, what will I do differently?*

**Assessing CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Read exit tickets. Level 2 evidence: students name a specific strategy shift (e.g., "I first divided y/x for one pair, but realized I needed to check multiple pairs to confirm constancy"). Level 1: vague comments like "I learned a lot." Level -1: no reflection or repetition of unaddressed errors.

---

## Homework
Find one real-world example of a proportional relationship (recipe, sports stat, shopping). Represent it in all five forms and write one sentence explaining how you verified it is truly proportional.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-labeled table templates and sentence starters for reflection.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to construct a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain the trap.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).
**Objective:** Students will analyze real-world data from multiple sources to determine if a proportional relationship exists, represent that relationship in multiple formats, and use critical thinking to evaluate their mathematical models and decision-making processes.

---

### **Part 1: Introduction and Scenario Clarification (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: "You are helping your family choose a new cell phone data plan. You have gathered information from three different places: a promotional flyer from the store, a printed billing statement from a neighbor, and a verbal description from a customer service representative." 

The teacher hands out the "Data Packets" containing these three sources. Before doing any math, students must read the verbal descriptions and define the variables they are working with.

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students read the customer service representative's verbal description of the data plan. They must precisely clarify and define the independent variable (gigabytes of data used) and the dependent variable (total cost in dollars) to support a deeper understanding of the relationship before calculating.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and listen to group discussions, assessing whether students can precisely articulate the variables and the concept of a unit rate, rather than just guessing at numbers. 

---

### **Part 2: Organizing and Verifying Data (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students extract the raw numbers from their three sources. They will notice that the data is scattered and includes a promotional "first month free" anomaly in the store flyer. Students must organize this data and verify its accuracy to see if the cost is truly proportional to the data used.

*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students take the scattered data points from the three different texts and arrange them into a logical, sequential T-table (e.g., 1 GB, 2 GB, 3 GB). 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' whiteboards to ensure the T-table is arranged sequentially. This demonstrates the ability to arrange information to strengthen logical flow, making it easier to identify the constant of proportionality ($y/x$).

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must cross-check the pricing claims made in the promotional flyer against the actual historical data shown in the neighbor's billing statement. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students calculating the ratio of $y/x$ for both sources. Students achieve this competency by rigorously cross-checking the math across multiple authoritative sources to validate whether the company's claim of a "flat proportional rate" is actually true.

*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** The store flyer includes a data point for a "free trial month" (0 GB = $0, but 5 GB = $0 for the first month). Students must decide which data points are reliable for building a long-term mathematical model.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by intentionally excluding the "free trial" outlier. Students should recognize that this data point is a promotional anomaly and select only the high-quality, consistent data points to represent the true proportional relationship.

---

### **Part 3: Representation Showdown and Decision Making (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Now that the data is organized and verified, students must represent the proportional relationship using a graph, an equation (e.g., $y = 15x$), and a diagram. Once they have created all representations, they must prepare a recommendation for their family on whether to choose this plan, and decide which mathematical representation is best to show their parents.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students evaluate the trade-offs of presenting their findings to their family using a graph versus an equation. They must write down the pros and cons of each alternative.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the students' written trade-off analyses. A high-level demonstration will systematically evaluate implications, such as noting: "A graph is visually easier for my parents to understand quickly, but an equation is better for calculating exact long-term costs if we use a highly specific amount of data, like 12.5 GB."

*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students write a final recommendation on whether their family should choose this data plan over a non-proportional unlimited plan (e.g., a flat $80/month). 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the final written recommendation. Students should demonstrate sophisticated reasoning that anticipates complexities—for example, concluding that "Because the relationship is proportional at $15 per GB, this plan is only the logical choice if our family consistently uses less than 5.3 GB a month; otherwise, the non-proportional flat-rate plan is more cost-effective."

---

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an exit ticket focused entirely on how their understanding of the data and the mathematical relationships evolved during the lesson. They are asked to reflect on their initial assumptions upon first reading the promotional flyer versus their conclusions after graphing the data and writing the equation.

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students write a reflection detailing how the process of organizing, cross-checking, and representing the data changed their initial perspective on the cell phone plan.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets looking for deep metacognitive awareness. A successful assessment will feature statements where students strategically analyze their own thinking, such as, "I initially assumed the plan was a great deal because I only looked at the flyer's free trial. By forcing myself to cross-check the billing statement and graph the equation, I realized my initial thinking was flawed and short-sighted regarding long-term proportional costs."
