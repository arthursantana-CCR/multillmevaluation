## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with four scenarios, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Proportional?" (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*

Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "3 apples cost $1.50; 6 apples cost $3.00; 9 apples cost $4.50."
- **Scenario B:** "A taxi charges $3 to start, plus $2 per mile."

Ask students to write a one-sentence explanation of what makes one proportional and the other not. Have 3 students share.

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "starts at zero," "no flat fee"). Students at Level 2 clarify *why* the constant rate matters; Level 1 students identify ratios but miss the zero-intercept idea. Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*

Introduce the relationship: "A bike travels 12 miles per hour." Walk through all five representations side-by-side on the board:

| Representation | Example |
|---|---|
| Verbal | "12 miles every hour" |
| Table | (1,12), (2,24), (3,36) |
| Equation | y = 12x |
| Graph | Line through origin, slope 12 |
| Diagram | Tape diagram showing equal segments |

Have students copy this organizer into their notebooks, emphasizing how each form connects.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks at end of class. Level 2 students arrange representations with clear labels and arrows showing connections; Level 1 students copy without showing relationships.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Source Comparison (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information*

Distribute a handout with three "student claims":
- **Claim 1:** "y = 5x + 2 is proportional because it has an x and y."
- **Claim 2:** "The table {(2,6),(4,12),(6,18)} shows a proportional relationship because each y is 3 times x."
- **Claim 3:** "Any line on a graph represents a proportional relationship."

In pairs, students identify which claims are valid and which contain errors, citing evidence (constant of proportionality, graph passing through origin).

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Circulate and ask, "How do you know that's true? What rule are you checking against?" Level 2 students reference multiple criteria (table ratios AND graph behavior AND equation form). Level 1 students rely on a single check.

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Review written justifications. Level 2 responses cite authoritative criteria (definition of proportionality, k = y/x). Level 1 responses use vague reasoning ("it looks right").

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Phone Plan (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons*

Present two plans:
- **Plan A:** $0.10 per text (proportional)
- **Plan B:** $5 monthly fee + $0.05 per text (non-proportional)

In groups of 3, students create a table, graph both relationships on the same axes, write equations, and decide which plan is better for a light user (20 texts/month) versus a heavy user (200 texts/month).

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Level 2 groups systematically evaluate trade-offs at multiple usage levels and identify the break-even point. Level 1 groups pick one plan without comparing scenarios.

---

### 5. Independent Reasoning Task (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning*

On a half-sheet, students answer:
> "A recipe says 2 cups of flour makes 12 cookies. Maya claims she needs 5 cups for 30 cookies. Is she correct? Justify using TWO different representations."

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Score responses. Level 2 students use two representations correctly AND anticipate edge cases (e.g., "this works because the ratio 2:12 simplifies to 1:6, and 5:30 also simplifies to 1:6"). Level 1 students show partial logic with one representation. Level -1 responses contain unsupported claims.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes*

Students complete a two-part exit ticket:

**Part 1 (Math):** Determine whether y = 4x and y = 4x + 1 are proportional. Explain.

**Part 2 (Reflection):**
- What strategy did you use first to decide?
- Did you change your approach at any point? Why?
- What would you do differently next time you see this type of problem?

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read reflections. Level 2 students describe shifting strategies ("I first tried plugging in numbers, then realized checking the y-intercept was faster"). Level 1 students mention what they did but not why or what they'd change. Level -1 responses show no awareness of process.

---

## Closing (immediate)
Quickly review: "A relationship is proportional when y = kx — it passes through the origin with a constant ratio." Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying the constant of proportionality (k) in real-world contexts.

## Differentiation
Provide sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___") for ELL students and extension problems (non-linear ratios) for advanced learners.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).
**Objective:** Students will analyze real-world data from multiple sources to determine if a proportional relationship exists, represent that relationship in multiple formats, and use critical thinking to evaluate their mathematical models and decision-making processes.

---

### **Part 1: Introduction and Scenario Clarification (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: "You are helping your family choose a new cell phone data plan. You have gathered information from three different places: a promotional flyer from the store, a printed billing statement from a neighbor, and a verbal description from a customer service representative." 

The teacher hands out the "Data Packets" containing these three sources. Before doing any math, students must read the verbal descriptions and define the variables they are working with.

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students read the customer service representative's verbal description of the data plan. They must precisely clarify and define the independent variable (gigabytes of data used) and the dependent variable (total cost in dollars) to support a deeper understanding of the relationship before calculating.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and listen to group discussions, assessing whether students can precisely articulate the variables and the concept of a unit rate, rather than just guessing at numbers. 

---

### **Part 2: Organizing and Verifying Data (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students extract the raw numbers from their three sources. They will notice that the data is scattered and includes a promotional "first month free" anomaly in the store flyer. Students must organize this data and verify its accuracy to see if the cost is truly proportional to the data used.

*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students take the scattered data points from the three different texts and arrange them into a logical, sequential T-table (e.g., 1 GB, 2 GB, 3 GB). 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' whiteboards to ensure the T-table is arranged sequentially. This demonstrates the ability to arrange information to strengthen logical flow, making it easier to identify the constant of proportionality ($y/x$).

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must cross-check the pricing claims made in the promotional flyer against the actual historical data shown in the neighbor's billing statement. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students calculating the ratio of $y/x$ for both sources. Students achieve this competency by rigorously cross-checking the math across multiple authoritative sources to validate whether the company's claim of a "flat proportional rate" is actually true.

*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** The store flyer includes a data point for a "free trial month" (0 GB = $0, but 5 GB = $0 for the first month). Students must decide which data points are reliable for building a long-term mathematical model.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by intentionally excluding the "free trial" outlier. Students should recognize that this data point is a promotional anomaly and select only the high-quality, consistent data points to represent the true proportional relationship.

---

### **Part 3: Representation Showdown and Decision Making (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Now that the data is organized and verified, students must represent the proportional relationship using a graph, an equation (e.g., $y = 15x$), and a diagram. Once they have created all representations, they must prepare a recommendation for their family on whether to choose this plan, and decide which mathematical representation is best to show their parents.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students evaluate the trade-offs of presenting their findings to their family using a graph versus an equation. They must write down the pros and cons of each alternative.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the students' written trade-off analyses. A high-level demonstration will systematically evaluate implications, such as noting: "A graph is visually easier for my parents to understand quickly, but an equation is better for calculating exact long-term costs if we use a highly specific amount of data, like 12.5 GB."

*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students write a final recommendation on whether their family should choose this data plan over a non-proportional unlimited plan (e.g., a flat $80/month). 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the final written recommendation. Students should demonstrate sophisticated reasoning that anticipates complexities—for example, concluding that "Because the relationship is proportional at $15 per GB, this plan is only the logical choice if our family consistently uses less than 5.3 GB a month; otherwise, the non-proportional flat-rate plan is more cost-effective."

---

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an exit ticket focused entirely on how their understanding of the data and the mathematical relationships evolved during the lesson. They are asked to reflect on their initial assumptions upon first reading the promotional flyer versus their conclusions after graphing the data and writing the equation.

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students write a reflection detailing how the process of organizing, cross-checking, and representing the data changed their initial perspective on the cell phone plan.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets looking for deep metacognitive awareness. A successful assessment will feature statements where students strategically analyze their own thinking, such as, "I initially assumed the plan was a great deal because I only looked at the flyer's free trial. By forcing myself to cross-check the billing statement and graph the equation, I realized my initial thinking was flawed and short-sighted regarding long-term proportional costs."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships in multiple ways.

### Learning Targets

Students will be able to:

1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain how they know a relationship is or is not proportional.
4. Compare representations and choose the most useful one for a given situation.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- Handout with tables, graphs, diagrams, and word problems
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 8 | 16 | 24 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 10 | 18 | 26 | 34 |

Ask students to silently decide which table shows a proportional relationship and explain why.

Students share with a partner, then the class discusses. Guide students toward the idea that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio, such as dollars per hour.

**Key teacher language:**  
“In a proportional relationship, one quantity is always multiplied by the same constant to get the other quantity. That constant is called the constant of proportionality.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes

Explain that proportional relationships can be recognized in different representations.

Use the example: A store sells apples for $3 per pound.

Represent it in five ways:

**Verbal description:** Apples cost $3 for each pound.

**Table:**

| Pounds | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Equation:**  
\( y = 3x \), where \( x \) is pounds and \( y \) is cost.

**Graph:**  
Plot points \((0,0), (1,3), (2,6), (3,9)\). Emphasize that the graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Diagram:**  
Draw repeated groups: 1 pound → $3, 2 pounds → $6, 3 pounds → $9.

Discuss the main indicators:

- The ratio \( \frac{y}{x} \) is constant.
- The equation can be written as \( y = kx \).
- The graph is a straight line through \((0,0)\).
- The situation has a “for each” or “per” rate.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours at a constant speed.”

As a class, students create:

1. A unit rate  
   \( 12 \div 2 = 6 \), so the cyclist travels 6 miles per hour.

2. A table

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Miles | 0 | 6 | 12 | 18 | 24 |

3. An equation  
   \( y = 6x \)

4. A graph  
   Students plot points and draw a straight line through the origin.

5. A verbal description  
   “The cyclist travels 6 miles for every 1 hour.”

Ask:  
“How can we check that all five representations match?”

Expected student responses:

- The table increases by 6 miles each hour.
- The equation uses 6 as the constant of proportionality.
- The graph includes the same points and goes through the origin.
- The words describe the same rate.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Match and Justify — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs. Give each pair a handout with mixed representations: tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Some are proportional and some are not.

Example items:

- \( y = 4x \)
- \( y = x + 4 \)
- A graph that is a straight line through the origin
- A graph that is straight but starts at \((0,3)\)
- A table with ratios \( \frac{y}{x} = 5 \)
- A table with ratios changing from 4 to 5 to 6
- A diagram showing equal groups
- A verbal description: “A taxi charges $5 plus $2 per mile.”

Students must:

1. Sort each item as proportional or not proportional.
2. Match equivalent representations.
3. Write one sentence of justification for each match.

Circulate and ask probing questions:

- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Are the ratios the same?”
- “How do you know this equation matches that table?”
- “Which representation helped you decide fastest?”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion: Choosing Representations — 5 minutes

Ask students:

“Which representation is most useful for deciding if a relationship is proportional: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words?”

Have students discuss pros and cons.

Possible conclusions:

- Tables make it easy to check ratios.
- Graphs show whether the line goes through the origin.
- Equations make the constant of proportionality clear.
- Verbal descriptions help understand the context.
- Diagrams can help visualize equal groups but may be slower for large numbers.

Emphasize that strong mathematical thinkers choose representations strategically.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete individually:

A printer prints 18 pages in 3 minutes.

1. Find the constant of proportionality.
2. Write an equation.
3. Complete a table for 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 minutes.
4. Explain how you know the relationship is proportional.
5. Name one representation that helped you most and explain why.

Collect as formative assessment.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks. Level 2 students arrange representations with clear labels and arrows showing connections; Level 1 students copy without showing relationships.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Evaluating Student Claims (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information*

Distribute a handout with three "student claims":
- **Claim 1:** "y = 5x + 2 is proportional because it has an x and y."
- **Claim 2:** "The table {(2,6),(4,12),(6,18)} shows a proportional relationship because each y is 3 times x."
- **Claim 3:** "Any straight line on a graph represents a proportional relationship."

In pairs, students identify which claims are valid and which contain errors, citing evidence from multiple representations (constant of proportionality, graph passing through origin, equation form).

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Circulate and ask, "How do you know? What rule are you checking against?" Level 2 students cross-check using multiple criteria (table ratios AND graph behavior AND equation form). Level 1 students rely on a single check.

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Review written justifications. Level 2 responses cite authoritative criteria (definition of proportionality, k = y/x, line through origin). Level 1 responses use vague reasoning ("it looks right").

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Phone Plan (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*

Present two plans:
- **Plan A:** $0.10 per text (proportional)
- **Plan B:** $5 monthly fee + $0.05 per text (non-proportional)

In groups of 3, students build a table, graph both relationships on the same axes, write equations, and recommend a plan for a light user (20 texts/month) versus a heavy user (200 texts/month).

**Assessment of CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Each group submits a written recommendation. Level 2 groups systematically evaluate trade-offs at multiple usage levels and identify the break-even point. Level 1 groups pick one plan without comparing scenarios.

**Assessment of CRI4a (Sound Reasoning):** Evaluate the written justification. Level 2 reasoning anticipates complexity—e.g., "Plan A is the logical choice only if usage stays below 100 texts; beyond that, Plan B is cheaper per text even with the monthly fee." Level 1 responses show partial logic; Level -1 responses contain unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Independent Application (8 minutes) — *Reinforces CRI4a*

On a half-sheet: *"A recipe says 2 cups of flour makes 12 cookies. Maya claims she needs 5 cups for 30 cookies. Is she correct? Justify using TWO different representations."*

Level 2 students use two representations correctly and explain the ratio 2:12 simplifies to 1:6, matching 5:30.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes*

**Part 1 (Math):** Determine whether y = 4x and y = 4x + 1 are proportional. Explain.

**Part 2 (Reflection):**
- What strategy did you use first to decide?
- Did your thinking change during the lesson? What changed it?
- What would you do differently next time?

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read reflections. Level 2 students describe shifting strategies ("I first tried plugging in numbers, then realized checking whether the line passes through the origin was faster"). Level 1 students mention what they did but not why or what they'd change. Level -1 responses show no awareness of process.
