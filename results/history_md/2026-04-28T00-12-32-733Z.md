## Candidate 1
# The Curious Argument: Why Questions Make Us Better Writers

### 7th Grade ELA | 55 Minutes | CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Lesson Overview

Students will discover that the best arguments are born from genuine curiosity. By learning to ask "What if I'm wrong?" and "What does the other side believe — and *why*?" students develop both intellectual humility and stronger persuasive writing skills.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Students will construct an argument with a clear claim, reasons, and evidence
- Students will acknowledge and respond to an opposing claim
- Students will practice curiosity as a thinking tool — not just a feeling

**Materials:** Whiteboard, index cards (2 per student), pencils, printed "Curiosity Starter" handout (optional)

---

## WARM-UP — *The Locked Door* (8 minutes)

As students settle in, write this sentence on the board:

> *"I already know I'm right."*

Ask students: **"Is this a good place to start when you're writing an argument?"**

Let a few hands go up. Take 2–3 quick responses without judgment.

Then say: *"Here's what's interesting — when you think you already know everything, your brain stops looking. And when your brain stops looking, your argument gets weak. Today we're going to find out how curiosity actually makes your writing stronger."*

**Quick Activity:** Give each student two index cards. On Card 1, they write a topic they feel strongly about (example: *"Students should have less homework"* or *"School lunches should be free"*). On Card 2, they write: *"Someone who disagrees with me might think..."* and leave the rest blank for now.

Tell them: *"Don't fill in Card 2 yet. That's the card we're going to earn by the end of the lesson."*

---

## DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *What Is a Curious Argument?* (12 minutes)

**Introduce the concept:**

Say: *"A curious person doesn't just collect facts that prove them right. A curious person asks — what am I missing? What does someone who disagrees actually believe, and why does it make sense to them?"*

Write on the board:

> **Curiosity = Asking honest questions, even uncomfortable ones**

Explain that in argumentative writing, curiosity shows up in three ways:

1. **You wonder about your own claim** — *Is this really true? What's the evidence?*
2. **You wonder about the other side** — *Why would a reasonable person disagree with me?*
3. **You wonder about your audience** — *What does this person already believe, and what might actually change their mind?*

**Model with a class-friendly example:**

Write on the board: *"Claim: Middle schoolers should be allowed to use their phones during lunch."*

Think aloud: *"Okay, I believe this. Now let me get curious. Why do I believe it? What's my actual evidence — not just 'because it would be nice'? And now the harder question — why might a teacher or principal genuinely disagree? What are they actually worried about?"*

Walk through building a mini-argument on the board together:
- **Claim:** Students should be allowed to use phones during lunch
- **Reason + Evidence:** Students use lunch as a mental reset; research shows short breaks with enjoyable activities reduce afternoon stress and improve focus
- **Opposing Claim (the curious part):** Some argue phones distract students from social interaction and contribute to isolation
- **Response:** While this concern is valid, structured guidelines — such as phone-free Mondays — could address this without a full ban

Point to the opposing claim section: *"This is where curiosity lives in your writing. You had to genuinely ask — what does the other side believe, and is any part of it true?"*

---

## GUIDED PRACTICE — *Getting Curious Together* (15 minutes)

**Activity: The Devil's Advocate Circle**

Arrange students in pairs or groups of three. Each group picks ONE of the following topics (or uses the topic from their Card 1 if appropriate):

- Should students grade their own work?
- Is it ever okay to lie to protect someone's feelings?
- Should competitive sports be required in school?

**Round 1 (4 minutes):** Each student argues their genuine opinion. Write down: claim, one reason, one piece of evidence.

**Round 2 (5 minutes):** Switch. Each student now argues *the opposite position* — as convincingly as possible. The rule: you must be genuinely curious about why someone might hold this view. No sarcasm, no strawmanning.

**Round 3 (3 minutes):** Come back to your original position. Ask yourself: *Did anything the other side said change how you'd write your argument? What would you add or adjust?*

**Debrief (3 minutes):** Ask the class — *"Did anyone find something in the opposing view that was actually kind of... reasonable?"* Let 2–3 students share. Validate that noticing the strength in an opposing argument is a sign of intellectual maturity, not weakness.

---

## INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — *Write the Argument* (15 minutes)

Students now return to Card 1 (their original topic). Using what they've learned, they write a short argument paragraph — roughly 8–12 sentences — that includes:

✅ A clear claim
✅ At least two reasons with evidence or examples
✅ An acknowledgment of one opposing claim (*"Some people argue that..."*)
✅ A response to that opposing claim (*"However..." or *"While this may be true, ..."*)

Students also complete Card 2 now: *"Someone who disagrees with me might think..."* — but this time, they write it with genuine curiosity, trying to represent the opposing view fairly.

**Circulate the room** during this time. Look for students who are simply dismissing the opposing view rather than engaging with it. Prompt them with: *"Why might a really smart person believe the opposite of you? What's the best version of their argument?"*

---

## CLOSING — *The Curiosity Payoff* (5 minutes)

Bring the class back together. Ask:

- *"Did acknowledging the other side make your argument weaker or stronger?"*
- *"What's one question you asked yourself today that you wouldn't have asked before?"*

Close with this idea, written on the board:

> **"The strongest arguments aren't built by people who stopped being curious. They're built by people who stayed curious — and kept asking harder questions."**

Tell students: *"Every great scientist, lawyer, journalist, and writer has one thing in common — they didn't just look for proof they were right. They looked for what they might be missing. That's the habit we practiced today."*

**Exit Ticket:** On the back of Card 2, students write one sentence:

*"One question I still have about my topic is..."*

Collect both cards as formative assessment.

---

## Teacher Notes

**Differentiation:** Struggling writers can use a provided sentence frame scaffold. Advanced students can be challenged to include two opposing claims and rebuttals.

**Extension:** Students revise their paragraph into a full multi-paragraph argument essay over the following days, using today's work as a foundation.

**Curiosity Connection:** Emphasize throughout the lesson that curiosity is not a personality trait — it is a *choice* and a *skill* that students can practice deliberately, in writing and in life.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity Through Argument Writing**  
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:  
By the end of this lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity as a mindset and skill, and will be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph supporting the claim that curiosity is essential for learning and personal growth. They will include reasons, evidence, and acknowledge opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Projector or chart paper (optional)  
- Handout: “Curiosity Argument Organizer” (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (about curiosity)  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Engage students with a question:**  
  “What does curiosity mean to you? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new or solve a problem?”  
- Allow 2-3 students to share briefly.  
- **Define curiosity:**  
  Write on the board: *Curiosity is a strong desire to learn or know something.*  
- Explain that curiosity drives us to ask questions, explore new ideas, and discover new things. It’s a key part of learning and growing.  
- **Connect to writing:**  
  Tell students that today they will practice writing an argument about why curiosity is important, using reasons and evidence to support their claim.

---

#### 2. **Mini-Lesson: Argument Writing Basics (10 minutes)**  
- Review the components of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - **Claim:** The main point or opinion.  
  - **Reasons:** Why you believe the claim.  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support the reasons.  
  - **Counterclaim:** An opposing viewpoint.  
  - **Rebuttal:** Explanation of why the counterclaim is less convincing or incorrect.  
- Show a simple example on the board or projector:  
  *Claim:* Curiosity is essential for learning.  
  *Reason:* Curious people ask questions that help them understand new topics.  
  *Evidence:* For example, scientists ask questions to make discoveries.  
  *Counterclaim:* Some people say curiosity can be distracting.  
  *Rebuttal:* However, curiosity focused on learning leads to deeper understanding, not distraction.  
- Emphasize logical organization: claim first, then reasons and evidence, followed by counterclaim and rebuttal.

---

#### 3. **Class Discussion: Why is Curiosity Important? (10 minutes)**  
- Brainstorm reasons why curiosity matters. Write student ideas on the board. Examples might include:  
  - Helps us learn new things  
  - Encourages creativity and problem-solving  
  - Leads to discoveries and inventions  
  - Makes learning more fun and engaging  
- Ask students to think of evidence or examples for each reason. For instance:  
  - Learning about space because astronauts were curious  
  - Inventors like Thomas Edison who were curious about how things work  
- Discuss possible counterclaims, such as:  
  - Curiosity can lead to distractions or trouble  
  - Sometimes curiosity wastes time  
- Talk about how to respond to these counterclaims with a rebuttal.

---

#### 4. **Guided Practice: Planning an Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Distribute the “Curiosity Argument Organizer” handout.  
- As a class, fill out the organizer together using one reason and evidence, and one counterclaim with a rebuttal. For example:  
  - Claim: Curiosity is essential for learning.  
  - Reason: Curiosity motivates people to ask questions.  
  - Evidence: Scientists ask questions to make discoveries, like Marie Curie’s research on radioactivity.  
  - Counterclaim: Some say curiosity can distract students from their work.  
  - Rebuttal: But curiosity focused on learning helps students understand better and stay engaged.  
- Encourage students to take notes in their notebooks.

---

#### 5. **Independent Writing: Compose an Argumentative Paragraph (12 minutes)**  
- Prompt: *Write a paragraph arguing that curiosity is important for learning and personal growth. Use at least one reason and piece of evidence, and include a counterclaim with a rebuttal.*  
- Remind students to organize their writing clearly: start with the claim, then reasons and evidence, then counterclaim and rebuttal.  
- Circulate the room to support students as they write.

---

#### 6. **Sharing and Reflection (8 minutes)**  
- Invite 3-4 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each reading, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, and rebuttal.  
- Discuss how curiosity helped them think about their writing topic.  
- End with a quick reflection question:  
  “How can being curious help you in school and in life?”  
- Allow a few students to share their thoughts.

---

### Homework/Extension (Optional):  
- Ask students to keep a “Curiosity Journal” for one week, writing down questions they have each day and any new things they learn by exploring those questions.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson combines teaching the value of curiosity with practicing argumentative writing skills aligned to CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1. Students learn what curiosity is, why it matters, and how to support a claim with reasons, evidence, and counterclaims. The lesson encourages critical thinking, clear writing, and appreciation for curiosity as a lifelong skill.
