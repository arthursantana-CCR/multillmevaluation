## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Framework:** CCR Critical Thinking

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to clarify and organize arguments about homework, evaluate the reliability of sources supporting different positions, weigh competing perspectives, apply reasoned judgment to form a defensible stance, and reflect on how their thinking evolved throughout the lesson.

---

## Materials

- Two short reading excerpts (one pro-homework, one anti-homework, each from a credible educational source)
- T-chart graphic organizer
- Exit ticket cards
- Whiteboard or projected timer

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up — Hook and Activation (5 minutes)

Begin with a quick show-of-hands poll: *"Do you think schools should assign homework? Yes, No, or Unsure?"* Record the distribution on the board. Tell students they will revisit this poll at the end of the lesson, and their final answer must be supported with reasons. This creates immediate personal investment and sets up the metacognitive thread that runs through the entire lesson.

---

### Phase 1: Clarifying Information — CRI1a (10 minutes)

Distribute the two short reading excerpts. One excerpt might come from a synthesis of research by Harris Cooper suggesting moderate homework has benefits for older students, while the other draws from Alfie Kohn's arguments that homework produces stress without meaningful academic gains.

Students read both excerpts individually, then complete a **"Define It" task**: they must write, in their own words, one clear claim from each side and identify any terms or ideas they found confusing. Partners briefly compare their definitions.

**How to observe/assess CRI1a:** Circulate and look at student paraphrases. A student reaching Level 2 will restate each argument with precision — for example, distinguishing between *correlation* and *causation* when describing homework research findings. A student at Level 1 might capture the general idea but blur important distinctions. Listen during partner talk for clarifying questions that show students are actively resolving confusion rather than glossing over it.

---

### Phase 2: Organizing Information — CRI1b (8 minutes)

Students transfer their clarified ideas onto a **T-chart graphic organizer** divided into "Arguments For Homework" and "Arguments Against Homework." They must group related points together — for example, clustering all social/emotional arguments on one side rather than mixing them randomly with academic performance data.

**How to observe/assess CRI1b:** Review T-charts for logical grouping. A Level 2 student arranges points so that each cluster builds toward a coherent position — academic evidence together, wellbeing evidence together. A Level 1 student lists points but mixes categories or leaves connections implicit.

---

### Phase 3: Evaluating Sources — CRI2a and CRI2b (10 minutes)

Project a slide showing four sources: a peer-reviewed journal article, a parenting blog post, a government education report, and a social media thread. Ask students to rank these from most to least reliable and justify their ranking in writing before discussing as a class.

Then, return to the two excerpts from Phase 1. Ask: *"How do we know these sources are trustworthy? What would make them stronger or weaker?"* Guide students to notice sample sizes, publication dates, author credentials, and whether claims are supported with data.

**How to observe/assess CRI2a and CRI2b:** Collect written rankings. A Level 2 response will articulate specific criteria — methodology, peer review, potential bias — and apply them consistently across all four sources. A Level 1 response might correctly identify the journal article as most reliable without explaining why. Watch for students who default to familiarity (e.g., trusting the blog because it "sounds nice"), which signals Level 1 or below.

---

### Phase 4: Weighing Pros and Cons and Reasoning to a Decision — CRI3a and CRI4a (12 minutes)

Students now engage in a **structured "Deliberation Protocol."** Each student independently writes a two-paragraph response to the question: *Should schools have homework?* The first paragraph must acknowledge the strongest argument from the opposing side before the second paragraph defends their own position.

This forced acknowledgment is the engine of CRI3a, while the quality of logical construction in the defense drives CRI4a.

**How to observe/assess CRI3a and CRI4a:** Read the two-paragraph responses. A Level 2 student for CRI3a will name a genuinely strong counterargument — not a strawman — and explain why it matters before qualifying it. A Level 2 student for CRI4a will connect evidence directly to claims, avoid logical leaps, and anticipate objections. Flag responses that dismiss the opposing view in one dismissive sentence as Level 1 or potentially Level -1.

---

### Phase 5: Reflecting on Thinking — CRI5a (7 minutes)

Students complete an **Exit Ticket** with three prompts:
1. *Did your opinion change from the opening poll? Why or why not?*
2. *What was the hardest part of thinking through this issue?*
3. *What is one assumption you held at the start that you now question?*

**How to observe/assess CRI5a:** A Level 2 response names a specific assumption, traces how evidence challenged it, and describes a deliberate adjustment in thinking. A Level 1 response acknowledges change vaguely ("I now see both sides") without identifying what shifted or why. Collect exit tickets to inform future lessons.

---

### Closing (3 minutes)

Revisit the opening poll. Invite two or three volunteers to share whether their position changed and what drove that shift. Close by framing critical thinking as a *process*, not a destination — the goal was never to find the "right" answer about homework, but to practice thinking rigorously about hard questions.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Homework Huddle - To Assign or Not to Assign?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Should schools have homework?

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify different arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support various perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of alternative choices regarding homework policies.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form and justify their own position on homework.
*   Reflect on their thinking process and how new information influenced their perspective.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (contains 3-4 short, diverse texts/quotes: e.g., a snippet from a research study on homework effectiveness, a quote from a parent advocating for less homework, a quote from a teacher explaining its benefits, a short news article summary on a school that banned homework). Each text will have a clear (or intentionally less clear) source citation.
*   Handout 2: T-Chart graphic organizer ("Arguments For Homework" / "Arguments Against Homework")
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, 7th graders! Let's start with a quick poll. Raise your hand if you think homework is a good idea. Now, raise your hand if you think schools should get rid of homework. Interesting! Today, we're going to explore this very question: 'Should schools have homework?' It's a topic that affects all of us, and there are many different opinions and reasons behind them."
*   **Teacher:** "Our goal today isn't just to share our opinions, but to think critically about *why* we hold those opinions, and to understand the different sides of this complex issue using evidence and careful thought."

**II. Exploring Perspectives & Clarifying Information (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will work in pairs. Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
*   **Teacher:** "In your pairs, read through the different perspectives on homework provided in Handout 1. As you read, your first task is to understand what each person or source is saying. Underline or highlight the main idea or key argument from each perspective."

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** After reading, students will paraphrase each perspective in their own words on their T-chart (Handout 2). They should aim for precision, capturing the core argument without adding their own bias. For example, if a text states, "Research indicates excessive homework can lead to student burnout," students should paraphrase it as "Some studies suggest that too much homework might cause students to feel exhausted," rather than "Homework is bad because it makes kids tired."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate among pairs. Listen to student discussions and review their paraphrases on the T-chart. Look for accurate and precise restatements of the original ideas. Are they capturing the essence of the argument? Are they avoiding misinterpretation or adding personal opinions?

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** As students paraphrase, they will also categorize each argument onto their T-chart (Handout 2) under "Arguments For Homework" or "Arguments Against Homework."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe how students group the information. Are arguments consistently placed on the correct side of the T-chart? Is their organization logical and easy to follow? Do they struggle with arguments that might have nuances, indicating a need for further clarification?

**III. Assessing Information: Validity and Quality (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Bring the class back together for a brief whole-group discussion.
*   **Teacher:** "Now that you've clarified and organized the arguments, let's look at where these arguments come from. Each perspective on your handout has a source listed. Let's discuss these sources."

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Look at the sources for each perspective. For example, one might be 'Dr. Anya Sharma, Educational Researcher,' another 'Parent Blog Post,' and another 'Local News Report.' In your pairs, discuss: Which of these sources seems most reliable or trustworthy? Why? Which might have a particular bias or limited perspective? Why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen to student discussions. Can they differentiate between a research study and a blog post? Do they articulate reasons for why one source might be more valid than another (e.g., "A researcher uses data," "A parent might only speak from their own experience")?

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, thinking about the *information itself*, not just the source: Which arguments or pieces of evidence do you think are the strongest or most convincing for either side? Why? Which ones might be less impactful or lack depth, even if they come from a seemingly valid source?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe which arguments students identify as "strongest" and their justifications. Do they prioritize arguments backed by data or expert opinion over anecdotal evidence? Do they recognize when information is too general or lacks specific supporting details?

**IV. Weighing Choices & Applying Reasoning (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will now prepare to form their own reasoned opinion.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Before you decide on your own stance, I want each pair to briefly discuss *both* sides of the argument, even if you already have a strong opinion. Imagine you are presenting to a school board. What are the strongest 'pros' for homework? What are the strongest 'cons'? Make sure you can articulate both sides fairly, considering the potential trade-offs of each choice."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen. Do students genuinely engage with both sides, or do they quickly dismiss the opposing view? Can they articulate the trade-offs involved in having or not having homework, considering different impacts on students, teachers, and families?

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, based on all the information you've clarified, organized, and assessed, and after weighing the pros and cons, I want each of you individually to write a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) stating your position on whether schools should have homework. You must support your position with at least two pieces of evidence or arguments from our discussion today. Make sure your reasoning clearly connects your evidence to your conclusion."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and review the paragraphs. Look for clear claims, relevant evidence, and logical connections between the evidence and the student's conclusion. Are there any logical fallacies or unsupported claims? Is the reasoning coherent and easy to follow?

**V. Reflection & Conclusion (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Distribute Exit Ticket slips.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, please answer these two questions:
        1.  Did your initial opinion about homework change or become stronger after today's lesson? Explain why or why not, referencing specific information or discussions.
        2.  What part of our critical thinking process today (clarifying, organizing, assessing sources, weighing pros/cons) helped you the most in forming your final opinion, and why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and read exit tickets. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness. Do students acknowledge shifts in their thinking or a deeper understanding? Can they identify specific strategies that aided their critical thinking process and explain *how* it helped them?

*   **Teacher:** "Thank you for engaging in such thoughtful discussion today. Critical thinking isn't just about having an opinion, but about understanding different perspectives, evaluating information, and forming well-reasoned conclusions. Keep practicing these skills in all your subjects!"

### Lesson Outline and Activities

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a) – 10 minutes**  
- Begin with a brief class discussion: “What is homework? Why do schools assign it?”  
- Present a short, clear definition of homework and common reasons schools assign it.  
- Activity: In pairs, students list what they know about homework and any questions they have.  
- Teacher circulates, prompting students to clarify vague ideas (e.g., “What do you mean by ‘too much homework’?”).  

*Assessment:*  
Observe students’ ability to restate ideas clearly and ask precise questions. Look for students who move beyond vague statements to specific clarifications (e.g., “Homework helps practice skills but can reduce free time”).  

---

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b) – 10 minutes**  
- Provide students with a graphic organizer (T-chart) labeled “Pros of Homework” and “Cons of Homework.”  
- Students work individually or in small groups to organize information from a brief text or video clip about homework’s benefits and drawbacks.  
- Encourage students to group similar ideas and arrange points logically (e.g., group all academic benefits together).  

*Assessment:*  
Review students’ completed organizers for logical grouping and clear structure. Assess whether information is arranged to support understanding rather than scattered or contradictory.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) – 8 minutes**  
- Present two short excerpts: one from a reputable educational research article and one from an opinion blog with questionable claims about homework.  
- Activity: In pairs, students identify the source of each excerpt and discuss which is more reliable and why.  
- Teacher guides students to consider author credentials, evidence cited, and publication type.  

*Assessment:*  
Listen for students’ ability to distinguish authoritative sources and articulate reasons for their judgments (e.g., “The research article uses data from many schools, but the blog just shares one person’s opinion”).  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b) – 5 minutes**  
- Show students examples of high-quality and low-quality information about homework (e.g., statistics from a government education site vs. an unverified social media post).  
- Quick class discussion: What makes information trustworthy and useful?  

*Assessment:*  
Ask students to explain what criteria they use to judge quality (accuracy, relevance, depth). Note if students recognize the importance of evidence and expert consensus.  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a) – 10 minutes**  
- Students use their T-charts to write a short paragraph weighing the pros and cons of homework.  
- Prompt: “If you were a school principal, would you keep, reduce, or eliminate homework? Explain your choice considering both sides.”  
- Encourage students to consider long-term effects on learning, stress, and free time.  

*Assessment:*  
Evaluate paragraphs for balanced consideration of alternatives and thoughtful evaluation of trade-offs (e.g., “Reducing homework might lower stress but could also reduce practice time”).  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a) – 7 minutes**  
- Students share their paragraphs in small groups and discuss reasoning behind their decisions.  
- Teacher prompts students to explain how they reached their conclusions and to consider counterarguments.  
- Highlight examples of clear, logical reasoning and gently challenge any faulty logic.  

*Assessment:*  
Listen for reasoning that anticipates complexities (e.g., “Although homework can be stressful, it helps reinforce learning, so maybe a balanced amount is best”). Note if students justify opinions with evidence rather than unsupported claims.  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a) – 5 minutes**  
- End with a reflective writing prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you understand the issue better?”  
- Encourage students to consider how clarifying, organizing, and evaluating information influenced their opinion.  

*Assessment:*  
Review reflections for metacognitive awareness, such as recognition of initial assumptions, adjustments in thinking, or strategies used to understand the topic more deeply.
