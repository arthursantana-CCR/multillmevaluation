## Candidate 1
# 7th Grade Math Lesson: Recognizing Proportional Relationships
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2
**Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

---

## Lesson Overview
Students will identify, represent, and reason about proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions — while developing critical thinking skills throughout each phase.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Proportional or Not?" card sets (one per pair)
- Graph paper
- Calculators
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### HOOK — Real-World Launch *(5 minutes)*

**Activity:** Display two scenarios on the board:
- *"A store charges $3 per pound of apples."*
- *"A taxi charges $2.50 plus $1.75 per mile."*

Ask: *"Are both of these proportional? How do you know — or how would you find out?"*

Allow 60 seconds of think time, then take 3–4 quick responses. Do **not** confirm answers yet. Tell students: *"By the end of class, you'll be able to prove it."*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Building the Concept *(12 minutes)*

**Activity:** Walk students through the definition of a proportional relationship using the apples scenario.

Build a table together on the board:

| Pounds | Cost |
|--------|------|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $12 |

Guide students to:
1. **Calculate the ratio** (Cost ÷ Pounds) for each row → always 3
2. **Graph the points** → straight line through the origin
3. **Write the equation** → y = 3x
4. **State it verbally** → "For every pound, the cost increases by exactly $3"

Then revisit the taxi scenario and build its table. Students discover the ratio is **not constant** and the graph does **not** pass through the origin.

> *"A proportional relationship always has a constant ratio, passes through the origin on a graph, and can be written as y = kx."*

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — "Proportional or Not?" Card Sort *(15 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI1b + CRI2a):** Student pairs receive 8 cards — each showing one representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description). Pairs must sort cards into two categories: **Proportional** and **Not Proportional**, then record their reasoning in writing.

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- *"What evidence from the table made you decide that?"*
- *"Could you represent this same relationship as an equation? What would it look like?"*

**Observation:** Listen for students articulating the constant ratio. Collect written reasoning to assess **CRI1b** (organized logic) and **CRI2a** (evaluating whether the information in each card is valid evidence of proportionality).

---

### CRITICAL THINKING SPOTLIGHT — Structured Debate *(10 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI3a + CRI4a):** Present this scenario:

> *"Two students disagree. Maya says y = 5x is proportional. Jordan says it's not because 'x changes every time.' Who is correct, and why?"*

Students write a 3-sentence argument independently, then share with a partner who must respond with either agreement or a counter-argument. Debrief whole-class.

**Observation:** Assess **CRI3a** by checking whether students acknowledge Jordan's reasoning before refuting it (weighing alternatives). Assess **CRI4a** through the quality of their written justification — strong responses cite the constant ratio, the equation form y = kx, and the graph passing through the origin as converging evidence.

---

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Multi-Representation Task *(10 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI1a + CRI2b):** Each student receives one new proportional relationship described **verbally** (e.g., *"A faucet drips 4 liters every 2 hours"*) and must represent it in **all four other forms**: table, graph, equation, and diagram.

**Observation:** Assess **CRI1a** by checking whether labels, units, and axis titles are precise and correctly placed. Assess **CRI2b** by whether students select the correct starting value and slope — using the verbal description accurately without distortion.

---

### METACOGNITIVE CLOSURE — Exit Ticket + Reflection *(3 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI5a):** Students complete a 3-part exit slip:

1. *Give one example of a proportional relationship and state how you know.*
2. *Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal) is easiest for you? Hardest? Why?*
3. *What confused you today, and what question do you still have?*

**Observation:** Assess **CRI5a** through Part 2 and 3. Students demonstrating Level 2 will name a specific strategy they used to navigate confusion (e.g., *"When the table didn't look proportional, I recalculated the ratios to double-check"*) — showing genuine metacognitive adjustment.

---

## Critical Thinking Subcompetency Summary

| ID | Lesson Moment | Assessment Method |
|----|--------------|-------------------|
| CRI1a | Multi-Representation Task | Precision of labels, units, axis titles |
| CRI1b | Card Sort written reasoning | Logical, organized written explanations |
| CRI2a | Card Sort sorting decisions | Correct use of card evidence to judge proportionality |
| CRI2b | Multi-Representation Task | Accurate translation from verbal to other forms |
| CRI3a | Maya/Jordan Debate | Written acknowledgment of opposing view before refuting |
| CRI4a | Maya/Jordan Debate | Multi-evidence justification in 3-sentence argument |
| CRI5a | Exit Ticket Parts 2–3 | Named strategies and honest identification of confusion |

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide ratio calculation scaffolds on the card sort
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional scenario and explain why it fails each test

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

---

**2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)**  
- Define proportional relationships: Two quantities that increase or decrease at the same rate.  
- Show examples of proportional relationships in:  
  - Tables (e.g., number of apples vs. total cost)  
  - Graphs (straight line through the origin)  
  - Equations (y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality)  
  - Diagrams (scaling figures)  
  - Verbal descriptions (e.g., “For every 1 apple, you pay $2.”)  
- Emphasize the constant ratio or unit rate.

---

**3. Guided Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with mixed representations of proportional relationships.  
- Work through 2-3 examples as a class:  
  - Identify if the relationship is proportional.  
  - Represent it in another form (e.g., from table to graph, from verbal to equation).  
- Encourage students to explain their reasoning aloud.

---

**4. Collaborative Activity (15 minutes)**  
- Divide students into small groups.  
- Each group receives a different proportional relationship scenario (e.g., speed and time, price and quantity, recipe ingredients).  
- Tasks:  
  - Create a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description for their scenario.  
  - Discuss and clarify any confusing parts within the group.  
- Teacher circulates, asking probing questions to deepen understanding.

---

**5. Independent Practice and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Students individually complete a quick problem converting a verbal description into a table and graph.  
- Prompt reflection: “How did you decide the relationship was proportional? What clues helped you?”  
- Collect exit tickets with their answers and reflections.

---

# 7th Grade Math Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2
**Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

---

## Lesson Overview
Students will identify, represent, and reason about proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions — while developing critical thinking skills throughout each phase.

---

## Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:
1. Define a proportional relationship and identify its key characteristics.
2. Recognize proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
3. Represent a proportional relationship in multiple forms.
4. Justify whether a relationship is proportional using evidence from multiple representations.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Proportional or Not?" card sets (one per pair)
- Graph paper and rulers
- Calculators
- Exit ticket slips

## Lesson Sequence

---

### HOOK — Real-World Launch *(5 minutes)*

**Activity:** Display two scenarios on the board:
- *"A store charges $3 per pound of apples."*
- *"A taxi charges $2.50 plus $1.75 per mile."*

Ask: *"Are both of these proportional? How do you know — or how would you find out?"*

Allow 60 seconds of think time, then take 3–4 quick responses. Do **not** confirm answers yet. Tell students: *"By the end of class, you'll be able to prove it."*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Building the Concept *(12 minutes)*

**Activity:** Walk students through the definition of a proportional relationship using the apples scenario. Build a table together on the board:

| Pounds | Cost |
|--------|------|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $12 |

Guide students to:
1. **Calculate the ratio** (Cost ÷ Pounds) for each row → always 3 (the constant of proportionality)
2. **Graph the points** → straight line through the origin
3. **Write the equation** → y = 3x (general form: y = kx)
4. **State it verbally** → "For every pound, the cost increases by exactly $3"

Then revisit the taxi scenario and build its table. Students discover the ratio is **not constant** and the graph does **not** pass through the origin.

> *"A proportional relationship always has a constant ratio, passes through the origin on a graph, and can be written as y = kx."*

Emphasize: both conditions — straight line **and** passing through the origin — are required.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — "Proportional or Not?" Card Sort *(15 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI1b + CRI2a):** Student pairs receive 8 cards — each showing one representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description). One card intentionally contains a **mismatched graph** (the graph does not accurately match the data in its paired table). Pairs must:
1. Sort all cards into two categories: **Proportional** and **Not Proportional**
2. Record their reasoning in writing for each card
3. Identify which card contains an inconsistency and explain why

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- *"What evidence from the table made you decide that?"*
- *"Could you represent this same relationship as an equation? What would it look like?"*
- *"The points on this graph — do they match the pairs in the table?"*

**Observation for CRI1b:** Assess whether students' written reasoning is logically organized — do they state the criterion first, then apply it to the evidence, then reach a conclusion?

**Observation for CRI2a:** Look for students who identify the mismatched graph by cross-checking both representations (e.g., *"The table shows a constant ratio of 2, but the graph doesn't pass through the origin, so the graph is wrong"*). This demonstrates rigorous cross-checking using multiple sources of information.

---

### CRITICAL THINKING SPOTLIGHT — Structured Debate *(10 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI3a + CRI4a):** Present this scenario on the board:

> *"Two students disagree. Maya says y = 5x is proportional. Jordan says it's not because 'x changes every time.' Who is correct, and why?"*

Students write a 3-sentence argument independently, then share with a partner who must respond with either agreement or a counter-argument. Debrief whole-class.

**Observation for CRI3a:** Assess whether students acknowledge Jordan's reasoning before refuting it — strong responses will note what Jordan might be thinking, then explain why that reasoning is flawed. Simply dismissing Jordan without engaging his view indicates Level 1 or below.

**Observation for CRI4a:** Evaluate the quality of written justifications. Sophisticated responses cite converging evidence: the equation is in the form y = kx, the ratio y/x is constant, and the graph would be a straight line through the origin. Responses that rely on only one criterion indicate partial reasoning.

---

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Multi-Representation Task *(10 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI1a + CRI2b):** Each student receives one proportional relationship described **verbally** (e.g., *"A faucet drips 4 liters every 2 hours"* or *"A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs — he also bakes cakes on Tuesdays and his oven is set to 350°F"*) and must represent it in **all four other forms**: table, graph, equation, and diagram.

Note: The second example intentionally includes irrelevant details. Students must identify and discard extraneous information before representing the relationship.

**Observation for CRI1a:** Check whether labels, units, and axis titles are precise and correctly placed. A student demonstrating Level 2 will label axes with units (e.g., "Hours" and "Liters"), title the graph, and define variables in the equation.

**Observation for CRI2b:** Assess whether students correctly filter relevant quantities from irrelevant ones, and whether their representations accurately reflect the verbal description without distortion or omission.

---

### METACOGNITIVE CLOSURE — Exit Ticket + Reflection *(3 minutes)*

**Activity (CRI5a):** Students complete a 3-part exit slip:

1. *"A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this proportional? Explain why or why not using at least two forms of evidence."*
2. *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal) is easiest for you? Hardest? Why?"*
3. *"What confused you today, and what is one specific thing you would do differently next time?"*

**Observation for CRI5a:** Read student responses to Parts 2 and 3. Students demonstrating Level 2 will name a specific strategy they used to navigate confusion (e.g., *"When the table didn't look proportional, I recalculated each ratio to double-check before deciding"*) — showing genuine metacognitive adjustment rather than a generic statement like "I'll try harder next time."
