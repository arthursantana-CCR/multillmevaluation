## Candidate 1
# Should Schools Have Homework?

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** English Language Arts / Critical Thinking

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

- **Analyze** claims about homework by identifying relevant evidence and distinguishing reliable sources from unreliable ones
- **Evaluate** the pros and cons of assigning homework using structured reasoning
- **Apply** logical decision-making to construct a position supported by evidence
- **Reflect** on how their own assumptions and thinking process shaped their conclusions

---

## Materials

- Two printed source cards per student (one pro-homework excerpt, one anti-homework excerpt — teacher-prepared, drawn from credible research summaries)
- "Claim Organizer" graphic organizer (one per student)
- Whiteboard or projector for class anchor chart
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- Exit ticket slips (one per student)
- Timer visible to students

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — 5 Minutes

**What Happens:** The teacher posts the question *"Should schools assign homework?"* on the board. Students write a one-sentence gut-reaction answer in their notebooks without discussing it with anyone. The teacher then asks three or four volunteers to share aloud and records responses on the board without commenting on correctness.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a — Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** This activity surfaces students' prior assumptions before any evidence is introduced. Students are explicitly told: *"This is your starting opinion. We will return to it at the end of class to see if — and why — it changed."*

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, students write a personal preference without any stated reason. At Level 2, students briefly name what is shaping their opinion (e.g., personal experience, something heard from a parent). The teacher collects nothing here — this is a baseline anchor for the closing reflection.

---

### Source Analysis — 12 Minutes

**What Happens:** Students receive two source cards. Card A summarizes findings from a peer-reviewed education study supporting homework benefits. Card B summarizes a researcher's argument against homework, citing stress and equity concerns. Students read both independently, then annotate each card using two prompts: *"Who produced this information?"* and *"What evidence does the author use?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI2a — Assessing validity; CRI2b — Assessing quality):** Students evaluate whether each source makes a claim backed by data, expert authorship, or observable evidence rather than personal opinion alone.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student notes the author's name or the word "study" without further analysis. At Level 2, a student distinguishes between a data-backed claim and an anecdotal one and flags which source they trust more and why. The teacher circulates and listens for these distinctions.

---

### Claim Organizer — 10 Minutes

**What Happens:** Using the Claim Organizer graphic organizer, students individually sort information from both source cards into two columns: *"Evidence that supports homework"* and *"Evidence that challenges homework."* A third row asks: *"Which piece of evidence is strongest, and why?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI1a — Clarifying information; CRI1b — Organizing information):** Students must paraphrase evidence in their own words (clarifying) and place it in a logical structure that separates support from challenge (organizing).

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, students copy phrases directly from source cards with inconsistent placement. At Level 2, students paraphrase accurately and arrange entries so that the strongest claim appears with a clear written justification. The teacher collects organizers at the end of class as a formative artifact.

---

### Pros and Cons Debate — 15 Minutes

**What Happens:** The class divides into two groups. Group A argues in favor of school homework; Group B argues against it. Each group has three minutes to huddle and select their two strongest points from their organizers. The teacher facilitates a structured four-round exchange: each side states a point, then the other side responds directly. The teacher enforces a rule: *"You must address what the other side actually said."*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons; CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning):** Students must evaluate trade-offs in real time and construct logical responses rather than simply restating their own position. The teacher explicitly names any logical fallacy aloud if one appears (e.g., *"That's a generalization — can you add evidence?"*).

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student repeats a point from their organizer without engaging the opposing argument. At Level 2, a student directly addresses a counterpoint and explains why their evidence outweighs it. The teacher notes participant names and response quality on a clipboard checklist.

---

### Position Writing — 8 Minutes

**What Happens:** Students write a structured paragraph answering the question: *"Should schools assign homework? State your position, give two pieces of evidence, and acknowledge one counterargument."* This is completed individually and silently.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning):** The three-part structure (position + evidence + counterargument) requires students to demonstrate logical reasoning in writing, not just in speech.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student states a position and lists evidence without acknowledging the other side. At Level 2, a student names a counterargument and explains why their evidence still leads them to their conclusion. Paragraphs are collected as the primary summative artifact.

---

### Metacognitive Reflection — 5 Minutes

**What Happens:** Students return to their opening gut-reaction sentence from the warm-up. On an exit ticket, they answer two questions: *"Did your opinion change? If yes, what evidence changed it. If no, what evidence confirmed it?"* and *"What was the hardest part of thinking through this issue fairly?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a — Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students explicitly compare their before-and-after thinking, naming the reasoning or evidence that caused a shift or held their position firm.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student writes *"Yes, I changed my mind"* or *"No"* without explanation. At Level 2, a student names a specific piece of evidence or debate moment that influenced their thinking and describes what made fair reasoning difficult. Exit tickets are collected and reviewed before the next class.

## Learning Objectives

- Evaluate the quality and validity of information from multiple sources.  
- Organize and clarify information to support reasoned arguments.

## Materials

- Printed or digital articles presenting arguments for and against homework (2–3 short texts)  
- Whiteboard or chart paper and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Exit ticket slips or digital form for reflection  
- Timer or clock  

---

## Lesson Outline

### 1. Warm-Up: Quick Write and Share — 7 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write a brief response to the prompt: “What do you think about homework? Is it helpful or harmful? Why?” After 3 minutes, students share their ideas in pairs.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) by encouraging students to identify and express their initial ideas clearly.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies relevant ideas but with some vagueness (e.g., “Homework is good because it helps”).  
Level 2: Student clarifies ideas precisely, explaining reasons or examples (e.g., “Homework helps me practice skills I learned in class, which makes me feel more confident”).  

---

### 2. Source Reading and Highlighting — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students read two short articles: one supporting homework and one opposing it. They highlight key points and underline any unclear or confusing information.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI2b (Assessing quality of information) by having students identify important ideas and judge the relevance and clarity of the content.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student highlights some relevant points but misses key ideas or highlights irrelevant details.  
Level 2: Student highlights main arguments clearly and notes any questionable or unclear information.  

---

### 3. Group Discussion: Organizing Arguments — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
In small groups, students organize the highlighted points into two columns: Pros and Cons of homework. They discuss how to arrange the points logically (e.g., strongest arguments first).  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1b (Organizing information) by arranging ideas to strengthen logical flow. Also supports CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons) as students compare alternative viewpoints.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Group creates a basic list with some organization but inconsistent logic.  
Level 2: Group arranges points clearly, showing a logical progression and grouping related ideas.  

---

### 4. Source Evaluation: Cross-Checking Information — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Teacher provides a third brief source or fact-checking resource related to homework research. Students compare this new information with the previous articles to check for accuracy and reliability.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information) and reinforces CRI2b (Assessing quality) by encouraging students to cross-check and judge source credibility.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies source but does not consistently evaluate its reliability.  
Level 2: Student explains differences or confirms information using multiple sources, noting which are more trustworthy and why.  

---

### 5. Structured Debate Preparation — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Pairs prepare short arguments either supporting or opposing homework, using organized information and evaluated sources. They plan to present their points clearly and anticipate counterarguments.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) by requiring systematic evaluation of trade-offs and logical reasoning in argument construction.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student presents simple arguments with limited reasoning or consideration of counterpoints.  
Level 2: Student develops well-reasoned arguments, anticipating complexities and addressing opposing views.  

---

### 6. Debate and Reflection — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Pairs present their arguments to the class or another pair. After presentations, students individually write a brief reflection on how their thinking changed or stayed the same after hearing others’ views.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) during debate and CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes) during reflection.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student’s reasoning is somewhat logical but may lack clarity; reflection mentions personal opinion without deep insight.  
Level 2: Student uses sophisticated reasoning in debate and demonstrates metacognitive awareness by explaining how new information influenced their thinking or led to adjustments.  

---

### 7. Exit Ticket: Final Position and Reasoning — 2 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write their final position on homework (for or against) and provide two clear reasons supporting their view, citing evidence from the lesson.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) by requiring precise expression and logical justification.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student states a position with general reasons.  
Level 2: Student clearly articulates a position with specific, well-supported reasons referencing credible sources.
