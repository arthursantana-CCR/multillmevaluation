# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
### Grade 7 | 55 Minutes | CCR Critical Thinking Framework

---

## Lesson Overview

Students will examine the debate around whether schools should assign homework. Through structured activities, they will practice clarifying and organizing information, evaluating sources, weighing competing arguments, applying logical reasoning, and reflecting on their own thinking. By the end of the lesson, students will be able to construct a well-reasoned position supported by evidence and acknowledge the complexity of the issue.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Clearly define key concepts related to the homework debate
- Organize evidence logically to support an argument
- Evaluate the credibility and quality of different information sources
- Systematically compare the pros and cons of homework
- Make a reasoned decision and reflect on how their thinking evolved

**Materials:** Mini source cards (4 prepared sources with varying reliability), T-chart worksheet, sticky notes, reflection exit slip, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### PHASE 1 — Hook and Concept Clarification (0–10 minutes)
**Targeting: CRI1a — Clarifying Information**

Begin by asking students: *"What do you think 'homework' actually means? Is all homework the same?"* Take brief responses and write them on the board. Students often conflate rote practice, creative projects, and reading logs under one label, which creates confusion in the debate.

Guide a short whole-class discussion to sharpen definitions. Introduce three distinct types of homework: **practice homework** (reinforcing skills), **preparatory homework** (previewing new content), and **extension homework** (applying learning creatively). Ask students: *"Does knowing these categories change how you think about the question?"*

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who move from vague statements ("homework is just busywork") toward more precise distinctions ("it depends on what kind of homework"). A Level 2 response precisely names and distinguishes types before forming an opinion. A Level 1 response acknowledges there are differences but cannot articulate them clearly.

---

### PHASE 2 — Organizing What We Know (10–20 minutes)
**Targeting: CRI1b — Organizing Information**

Distribute a T-chart worksheet with the headers: *"Arguments FOR homework"* and *"Arguments AGAINST homework."* Give students five minutes to independently brainstorm and fill in both sides based on their own knowledge and experience.

Then, in pairs, students compare charts and add any arguments they missed. Prompt them to number their points in order of what they consider most to least important. Introduce the idea of **categories**: academic benefits, equity and fairness, student well-being, family dynamics.

Ask pairs: *"Can you reorganize your chart so that related points are grouped together?"*

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Observe whether students list points randomly or begin to cluster them by theme. A Level 2 response shows a deliberately structured chart with grouped, prioritized arguments that build logically. A Level 1 response lists points but without intentional organization or hierarchy.

---

### PHASE 3 — Source Evaluation Gallery (20–32 minutes)
**Targeting: CRI2a and CRI2b — Assessing Validity and Quality of Information**

Place four "source cards" around the room. Students rotate in small groups, reading each card and placing a sticky note with their credibility rating (1–3 stars) and a one-sentence reason.

**Source Cards include:**
1. A 2019 peer-reviewed study from *Educational Psychology* (controlled sample, clear methodology)
2. A parent blog post titled "Homework Ruined Our Family" (personal anecdote, no data)
3. A national teacher union statement with cited statistics but a clear advocacy agenda
4. A news article summarizing multiple studies, published by a reputable outlet

After the rotation, regroup and debrief: *"Which source would you trust most to support an argument? Why? Can a biased source still contain useful information?"*

**Assessment of CRI2a/CRI2b:** A Level 2 response cross-references what the source says with who produced it and why, recognizing that even credible sources may have limitations. A Level 1 response identifies the peer-reviewed study as "best" without explaining what makes it more valid than the others.

---

### PHASE 4 — Structured Argument and Decision-Making (32–47 minutes)
**Targeting: CRI3a and CRI4a — Weighing Pros/Cons and Sound Reasoning**

Students now take a position: *"Schools should significantly reduce or eliminate homework"* or *"Schools should keep or increase homework."* They have eight minutes to prepare a short argument (3–4 sentences) using only evidence from sources they rated as credible.

Conduct a **structured academic controversy**: pairs argue one side, then switch sides and argue the opposite, then collaborate to find a **nuanced position** that acknowledges trade-offs. Prompt: *"Under what conditions might homework be beneficial? For whom might it be harmful?"*

**Assessment of CRI3a:** A Level 2 response identifies specific trade-offs (e.g., "homework may help students without tutors but disadvantages those without quiet study spaces"). A Level 1 response acknowledges both sides exist but without depth. **Assessment of CRI4a:** Listen for logical connectors ("because," "therefore," "however") and for students who anticipate counterarguments rather than ignore them.

---

### PHASE 5 — Metacognitive Reflection Exit Slip (47–55 minutes)
**Targeting: CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking**

Students complete a three-prompt exit slip independently:

1. *"What did you think about homework before this lesson?"*
2. *"What changed in your thinking, and what caused that change?"*
3. *"What question are you still unsure about?"*

**Assessment of CRI5a:** A Level 2 response identifies a specific moment in the lesson that shifted their thinking and names *why* (e.g., "I realized I hadn't considered students who don't have internet at home — that changed how I weighed fairness"). A Level 1 response notes their opinion changed but cannot explain the reasoning process behind it. Collect exit slips to inform future lessons.

---

## Closing Note for Teachers

This lesson treats homework not merely as its topic but as a **mirror**: students practice the same critical thinking skills they are debating. Noticing this connection — that how we think about homework *is itself a thinking exercise* — can be a powerful closing moment if time allows.
