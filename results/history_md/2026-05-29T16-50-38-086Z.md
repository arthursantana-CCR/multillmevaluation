## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout (4 recipe scenarios), exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Proportional?" (8 minutes)
Display three scenarios on the board:
- (A) 2 apples cost $1; 6 apples cost $3
- (B) A baby is 20 inches at birth, 30 inches at age 1
- (C) A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 180 miles in 3 hours

Students discuss with elbow partners: Which are proportional? Why? Students must **clarify what "proportional" means in their own words** before sharing.

> **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students who restate "proportional means a constant ratio" precisely vs. those who say "they go up together." Assess via cold-call responses and partner whispers. Score Level 2 if students identify the constant of proportionality concept; Level 1 if they only note "both increase."

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce proportional relationships through the equation **y = kx**, where k is the constant of proportionality. Using the example "1 smoothie requires 3 strawberries," demonstrate all five representations:
- **Table:** smoothies (1,2,3,4) → strawberries (3,6,9,12)
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** bar model showing 3:1 ratio
- **Verbal:** "For every smoothie, you need 3 strawberries"

Emphasize how each representation must be **organized** to reveal the constant ratio. Show a non-example: a graph that doesn't pass through origin.

> **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students complete a graphic organizer linking all five representations. Assess by reviewing whether tables are arranged with consistent input/output columns and whether their equations match table values. Level 2: organized so the constant k is visible across all forms.

### 3. Guided Practice: "Smoothie Shop Investigation" (15 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Shop handout with four recipes:
- Recipe 1: 2 cups yogurt, 6 berries
- Recipe 2: 3 cups yogurt, 9 berries
- Recipe 3: 2 cups yogurt, 5 berries
- Recipe 4: 4 cups yogurt, 12 berries

In groups of 3, students determine which recipes are proportional to each other and **must justify using at least two different representations**.

> **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Groups must verify their conclusion by cross-checking the ratio in a table AND testing it on a graph. Circulate and ask: "How do you know your answer is correct? What's a second way to check?" Level 2 students confirm using multiple representations; Level 1 students rely on one method.

> **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some recipes are "close but not proportional" (Recipe 3). Assess whether students select precise mathematical evidence (exact ratios) rather than estimates ("looks about right"). Level 2 students cite k = 3 explicitly.

### 4. Decision-Making Task: "Which Recipe Should the Shop Use?" (10 minutes)
Pose the dilemma: "The shop wants ONE proportional recipe to scale up for large orders. Which would you choose and why? Consider cost, taste consistency, and ease of scaling."

Students individually write a 4-sentence argument, then share with their group. Groups present one consensus choice.

> **Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Assess written arguments for explicit comparison of recipes. Level 2: student systematically evaluates each option's trade-offs (e.g., "Recipe 4 scales easily but uses more berries, increasing cost; Recipe 1 is cheapest but yields less"). Level 1: picks one without comparison.

> **Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Look for logical chains: "Because k = 3 in Recipe 1 and Recipe 4, both are proportional, but Recipe 4 produces more, so for large orders…" Level 2 anticipates complications (running out of ingredients). Flag faulty logic like "Recipe 3 works because the numbers are bigger."

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

**Part A (5 min):** Students answer in their math journals:
- *"What was the trickiest part of identifying proportional relationships today? What strategy worked best for you, and what would you do differently next time?"*

> **Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Read journal entries. Level 2: student names a specific strategy shift ("At first I just looked at the numbers going up, but then I realized I needed to divide y by x to check k"). Level 1: vague reflection ("It was hard but I got it"). Level -1: no reflection or repeated errors.

**Part B (5 min):** Exit Ticket — Given the table (x: 1,2,3,4; y: 4,8,12,16), students:
1. Write the equation
2. State the constant of proportionality
3. Describe in words

---

## Assessment Summary
- **Formative:** Warm-up discussion, guided practice observations, group presentations
- **Summative checkpoint:** Exit ticket scored on accuracy across representations
- **Critical thinking evidence:** Journal reflections and group justification quality, scored using the CRI rubric levels (-1 to 2)

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-formatted tables and sentence stems ("This is proportional because…")
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional scenario that "tricks" classmates

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**

*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**

    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** Middle School / Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships in multiple ways.

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
3. Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional using mathematical evidence.
4. Compare different representations and choose the most useful one for a situation.

### Materials

- Projector or board
- Student handout with tables, graphs, equations, and word problems
- Graph paper
- Colored pencils
- Exit ticket
- Calculators, optional

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Which One Belongs?” — 7 minutes

Display four relationships:

A. 2 pencils cost $1, 4 pencils cost $2, 6 pencils cost $3  
B. A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile  
C. y = 5x  
D. Points: (1, 4), (2, 8), (3, 12)

Ask students: **“Which relationships appear proportional? How do you know?”**

Students first write individually, then share with a partner. Bring the class together and record reasoning.

Guide students toward key ideas:

- A proportional relationship has a constant ratio.
- Its equation can be written as **y = kx**.
- Its graph is a straight line through the origin.
- A table should show the same unit rate each time.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Activity: Students identify what information matters: ratios, ordered pairs, starting values, unit rates.  
Assessment: Listen for students clarifying ideas precisely, such as: “The taxi is not proportional because it starts at $3 before any miles are driven.” Student written responses can be rated from -1 to 2 based on clarity and accuracy.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Teach the definition:

A relationship is proportional if two quantities have equivalent ratios. It can be represented by the equation:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Model with this example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask: “What is the ratio of money to hours?”

Students calculate:

8 ÷ 1 = 8  
16 ÷ 2 = 8  
24 ÷ 3 = 8  
32 ÷ 4 = 8

So, the relationship is proportional and the equation is:

**y = 8x**

Then graph the points and show that the line passes through the origin.

Contrast with:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 26 |
| 4 | 34 |

Students notice the amount increases by 8 each time but the ratios are not equal. Explain that a constant difference does not always mean proportional.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Activity: Students organize information in a table, equation, and graph.  
Assessment: Check whether students arrange work logically: table ratios, unit rate, equation, graph. A level 2 response should show a clear sequence that supports the conclusion.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students three situations:

1. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
2. A gym charges a $15 sign-up fee plus $10 per month.  
3. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, and 180 miles in 3 hours.

For each situation, students complete:

- Table
- Constant of proportionality, if it exists
- Equation, if proportional
- Graph sketch
- Verbal explanation

Work through the first one together. Students then work with partners on the second and third.

During discussion, ask:

- “How did you check whether the relationship was proportional?”
- “Which representation made the relationship easiest to understand?”
- “What evidence supports your answer?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Activity: Students cross-check a relationship using more than one representation: table, equation, graph, and verbal description.  
Assessment: Observe whether students rely on only one clue or verify with multiple forms. A strong response might state: “The gym is not proportional because the equation is y = 10x + 15, and the graph would not pass through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Activity: Students decide which information is useful: unit rate, initial fee, ratio, graph through origin.  
Assessment: Look for students selecting relevant information rather than distracting details. For example, recognizing that the $15 sign-up fee is important because it prevents the relationship from being proportional.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Representation Sort — 15 minutes

Give each pair a set of cards. Cards include:

- Tables
- Graphs
- Equations
- Verbal descriptions
- Diagrams such as double number lines or ratio diagrams

Students sort cards into two categories:

1. Proportional  
2. Not proportional

Then students choose two proportional cards and match them to equivalent representations. For example:

- Table: 1 → 4, 2 → 8, 3 → 12  
- Equation: y = 4x  
- Graph: line through origin with slope 4  
- Verbal: “Each ticket costs $4.”

Students must write a justification for at least three cards.

Teacher circulates and asks probing questions:

- “What makes you sure?”
- “Can you prove that using another representation?”
- “Is there another way to organize this information?”
- “What mistake might someone make here?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Activity: Students compare representations and decide which best supports their conclusion.  
Assessment: Look for students discussing trade-offs, such as: “The graph is quick because it shows the origin, but the table helps us calculate the constant ratio.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Activity: Students decide whether each relationship is proportional and justify their decision with evidence.  
Assessment: Evaluate whether reasoning is mathematically sound. A level 2 response should connect evidence to conclusion, such as: “This is proportional because every y-value divided by x-value equals 6, so the equation is y = 6x.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Debrief — 6 minutes

Select two or three cards that caused disagreement. Invite students to explain different viewpoints.

Example discussion:

A graph is a straight line but does not pass through the origin. Ask:  
“Is it proportional? Why or why not?”

Emphasize that proportional graphs must be straight lines **and** pass through the origin.

Create an anchor chart:

**A proportional relationship has:**

- Constant ratio/unit rate
- Equation y = kx
- Graph through the origin
- Equivalent ratios in table
- Verbal description showing “for every”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students answer:

1. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 7 |
| 4 | 14 |
| 6 | 21 |
| 8 | 28 |

2. Write an equation for the relationship if it is proportional.

3. Reflection: “What representation helped you most today, and why?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Activity: Students reflect on how they made their decision and which representation helped them.  
Assessment: Look for metacognitive statements such as: “At first I looked at the differences, but then I remembered to check y ÷ x. That helped me see the ratio stayed the same.”

---

## Assessment Plan

Use student handouts, partner justifications, discussion responses, and exit tickets to assess both math understanding and critical thinking.

For each critical thinking subcompetency, assign a level from -1 to 2 using evidence from student work or spoken responses:

- **CRI1a:** Did the student clarify relevant information accurately?
- **CRI1b:** Did the student organize work logically?
- **CRI2a:** Did the student check validity using multiple representations?
- **CRI2b:** Did the student choose relevant, high-quality mathematical evidence?
- **CRI3a:** Did the student compare the usefulness of different representations?
- **CRI4a:** Did the student use sound reasoning to justify decisions?
- **CRI5a:** Did the student reflect on and adjust their thinking?

## Differentiation

**Support:** Provide partially completed tables, sentence frames, and graph axes.  
Sentence frame: “This relationship is/is not proportional because ______.”

**Challenge:** Ask students to create their own proportional and non-proportional relationship and represent each in three ways.

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Proportional?" (8 minutes)
Display three scenarios:
- (A) An official store receipt: 2 apples cost $1; 6 apples cost $3
- (B) A social media post: "A baby is 20 inches at birth, 30 inches at age 1"
- (C) A driving log: a car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 180 miles in 3 hours

Students discuss with elbow partners: Which are proportional? Which data sources are trustworthy enough to use? Students must **clarify what "proportional" means in their own words** before sharing.

> **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students who restate "proportional means a constant ratio (y/x = k)" precisely vs. those who vaguely say "they go up together." Assess via cold-call responses. Score Level 2 if students identify the constant of proportionality concept; Level 1 if they only note "both increase."

> **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** As students evaluate the three sources, assess whether they demonstrate sophisticated judgment — recognizing that the receipt provides authoritative, precise data, while the social-media baby-growth claim lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate. Level 2 students explicitly select high-quality sources; Level 1 students use information without questioning it.

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce **y = kx**, where k is the constant of proportionality. Using "1 smoothie requires 3 strawberries," demonstrate all five representations:
- **Table:** smoothies (1,2,3,4) → strawberries (3,6,9,12)
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** bar model showing 3:1 ratio
- **Verbal:** "For every smoothie, you need 3 strawberries"

Show a non-example: a table where values increase by a constant difference (+8 each time) but ratios are NOT equal. Emphasize that a constant difference does not always mean proportional, and a proportional graph must pass through the origin.

> **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students complete a graphic organizer linking all five representations. Assess by reviewing whether tables are arranged with consistent input/output columns, axes are labeled, and equations match table values. Level 2: organized so the constant k is visible across all forms; Level 1: basic organization with inconsistencies.

### 3. Guided Practice: "Smoothie Shop Investigation" (15 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Shop handout with four recipes:
- Recipe 1: 2 cups yogurt, 6 berries
- Recipe 2: 3 cups yogurt, 9 berries
- Recipe 3: 2 cups yogurt, 5 berries
- Recipe 4: 4 cups yogurt, 12 berries

In groups of 3, students determine which recipes are proportional and **must justify using at least two different representations**.

> **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Groups must verify their conclusion by cross-checking the ratio in a table AND testing it on a graph or equation. Circulate and ask: "How do you know? What's a second way to check?" Level 2 students rigorously cross-check across multiple representations; Level 1 students rely on one method.

### 4. Decision-Making Task: "Which Recipe Should the Shop Use?" (10 minutes)
Pose the dilemma: "The shop wants ONE proportional recipe to scale up for a 450-smoothie large order. Which would you choose? Consider cost, taste consistency, and ease of scaling."

**Twist:** The supplier adds a one-time $20 delivery fee. Is the cost relationship still proportional? Students individually write a 4-sentence argument, then share with their group.

> **Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Assess written arguments for explicit comparison of recipes and representations. Level 2: student systematically evaluates trade-offs (e.g., "Recipe 4 scales easily but uses more berries, increasing cost; extending a table 450 times is inefficient, but the equation gives instant accuracy"). Level 1: picks one without comparison.

> **Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Look for logical chains anticipating the $20 fee complication: "Adding $20 makes the equation y = 3x + 20, so the line no longer passes through the origin — it's no longer proportional." Level 2 anticipates complexity; Level -1 flags faulty logic like "Recipe 3 works because the numbers are bigger."

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

**Part A (5 min) — Journal Reflection:**
*"At the start of class, how did you decide if something was proportional? How did your strategy change? What's the trickiest part, and what will you do differently next time?"*

> **Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Level 2: student names a specific strategy shift ("I used to just look at differences, but now I check y ÷ x AND verify the line passes through the origin"). Level 1: vague reflection ("It was hard but I got it"). Level -1: no reflection.

**Part B (5 min) — Exit Ticket:** Given the table (x: 1,2,3,4; y: 4,8,12,16), students:
1. Write the equation
2. State the constant of proportionality
3. Describe the relationship in words

## Anchor Chart (posted during debrief)
**A proportional relationship has:**
- A constant ratio / unit rate (k = y/x)
- Equation in the form y = kx
- A straight-line graph through the origin
- Equivalent ratios across a table
- A verbal description using "for every"

## Assessment Summary
- **Formative:** Warm-up discussion, guided practice observations, group justifications
- **Summative checkpoint:** Exit ticket scored on accuracy across representations
- **Critical thinking evidence:** Journal reflections and group work, scored using the CRI rubric (-1 to 2)

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Pre-formatted tables, labeled axes, and sentence frames ("This is proportional because ______").
- **Challenge:** Students create a non-proportional scenario that "tricks" classmates by appearing proportional at first glance.
