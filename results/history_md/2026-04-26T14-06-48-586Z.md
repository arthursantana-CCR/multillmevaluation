## Generator
# The Curious Argumentalist: Why Questions Make Better Arguments

## 55-Minute Lesson for 7th Grade ELA

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Central Idea:** The best arguments don't start with answers — they start with genuine curiosity. Writers who ask deep questions find stronger evidence, acknowledge opposing views more honestly, and persuade more effectively.

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Practice deliberate curiosity as a thinking strategy before and during writing
2. Write an argument with a clear claim, logically organized reasoning, and relevant evidence
3. Identify and acknowledge an opposing claim within their argument
4. Explain how curiosity improves the quality of an argument

**Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Curiosity Cards" (one per student — template described below)
- Printed "Argument Builder" graphic organizer (one per student)
- Pencils
- A single, surprising physical object to open the lesson (suggestions: a snow globe, a compass, a cracked geode, a very old coin, a small bird feather)
- Optional: sticky notes

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### OPENING HOOK — "The Object" *(5 minutes)*

**What the teacher does:**

Walk in silently. Place the mystery object in the center of the front desk or table without saying a word. Let students look at it for 30 seconds in silence.

Then ask, **without context:**

> *"Write down every question that comes to your mind when you look at this. Don't answer any of them. Just ask."*

Give students 90 seconds to write questions privately. Then invite 8–10 students to share their questions aloud while you record them rapidly on the board. Do not answer any of them. Do not evaluate any of them. Just write them down.

Step back and look at the board with the class.

**Say:**

> *"Look at what just happened. You didn't know anything about this object — and your brain immediately started working. It started arguing: 'This could be from somewhere old.' 'This might have been someone's.' 'I wonder if this is valuable.' That thing happening in your head right now — that itch to know — that's curiosity. And today we're going to discover that curiosity is actually a writer's most powerful tool, especially when writing arguments."*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — "Why Curious People Win Arguments" *(8 minutes)*

**Transition:** Erase or set aside the object. Write on the board in large letters:

> **CURIOUS → QUESTIONS → EVIDENCE → STRONGER ARGUMENT**

**Explain the connection using three short points:**

**Point 1: Curious people find better evidence.**

> *"When you assume you already know everything about a topic, you stop looking. But when you stay genuinely curious — when you wonder 'Wait, is that really true?' — you dig deeper. You find facts, examples, and details that make your argument impossible to ignore."*

**Point 2: Curious people understand the other side.**

> *"Here's something strange: the best arguers are also the best listeners. They're genuinely curious about why someone might disagree with them. If you can walk into someone else's thinking — really understand it — you can address it directly. That's called acknowledging an opposing claim, and it makes your argument dramatically more convincing."*

**Point 3: Curious people ask 'Why?' one more time than everyone else.**

> *"Anyone can state an opinion. Curious writers ask 'Why is this true?' and 'How do I know?' one more time than they think they need to. That extra 'Why?' is usually where the real reason lives."*

Write this sentence on the board and leave it visible:

> **"Strong arguments are built by curious minds."**

---

### VOCABULARY ANCHOR *(3 minutes)*

Quickly review the key argument terms students will use today. Write each term and its brief definition on the board.

| Term | Student-Friendly Definition |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | Your main argument — what you believe is true |
| **Reason** | Why your claim is true |
| **Evidence** | Facts, examples, or data that prove your reason |
| **Opposing Claim** | What someone who disagrees with you would say |
| **Rebuttal** | Your response to the opposing claim |

Ask students to give a thumbs up if they've seen these words before. Confirm that today they'll use all five in real writing.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — "The Curiosity Card" *(10 minutes)*

**Distribute Curiosity Cards.** Each card has the following printed on it:

---

*CURIOSITY CARD*

**Today's Topic:** Should middle schools eliminate traditional letter grades and replace them with written feedback only?

**Step 1 — Before you decide anything, ask 5 genuine questions about this topic:**
1. _______________________________________________
2. _______________________________________________
3. _______________________________________________
4. _______________________________________________
5. _______________________________________________

**Step 2 — Circle the one question you are MOST curious about.**

**Step 3 — What would someone who DISAGREES with you wonder about?**
They might ask: _______________________________________________

---

**Walk students through Step 1 together.** Model aloud what genuine curiosity sounds like versus lazy assumptions.

*Model for students:*

> *"A lazy question sounds like: 'Isn't this a bad idea?' — because it already has the answer baked in. A curious question sounds like: 'How do students actually feel about their grades — do grades motivate them or stress them out more?' That question opens a door. It doesn't close one."*

Give students 3 minutes to write their five questions independently. Then invite students to share. Record 6–8 student questions on the board under the label **"Our Class's Curiosity Wall."**

**Lead a 60-second discussion:**

> *"Look at how different our questions are. Some of you are curious about students' mental health. Some of you are curious about what colleges would think. Some of you are curious about teachers. Each of those curiosities will lead to a totally different argument — and that's the point. Your curiosity shapes your argument before you write a single word."*

Complete Steps 2 and 3 briefly — students circle one question and try to imagine what a person who disagrees might wonder.

---

### MODELED WRITING — "Watch Me Build an Argument" *(7 minutes)*

**Tell students:**

> *"I'm going to write the skeleton of an argument in front of you, and I want you to watch how I use curiosity at every step. Call me out if I stop being curious."*

Write the following on the board step by step, narrating your thinking aloud:

**Claim:** Middle schools should replace letter grades with written feedback because written feedback gives students more useful information about how to actually improve.

*Say:* "Okay. I believe that. But now — why? And how do I know? Let me get curious."

**Curious Question I asked myself:** *"Do students actually use their grades to get better, or do they just... look at them and move on?"*

**Reason:** When students receive only a letter grade, research shows they often focus on the number rather than the learning itself, which can reduce their motivation to grow.

*Say:* "Good. Now evidence. I need to get even more curious — 'What do I actually know? What have I read or experienced?'"

**Evidence:** A 2018 study from Stanford found that students who received written feedback on assignments made significantly more improvement than students who received only grades.

*Say:* "Now — what would a curious, honest writer do next? They'd ask: 'Who would disagree with me, and why?'"

**Opposing Claim:** Some people argue that grades are necessary because colleges and employers need a clear, standardized way to evaluate students.

*Say:* "And now I stay curious: 'Is that completely true? Can I disagree with it without just dismissing it?'"

**Rebuttal:** While standardized evaluation is important, many schools that use written feedback have found ways to produce transcripts that colleges can still interpret, meaning both goals can be achieved simultaneously.

**Point to the board and say:**

> *"That argument was built entirely by asking questions. Every single sentence came from curiosity. Now it's your turn."*

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — "Build Your Own Argument" *(15 minutes)*

**Distribute the Argument Builder graphic organizer.** It contains the following sections:

---

*ARGUMENT BUILDER*

**Topic:** Should middle schools eliminate traditional letter grades and replace them with written feedback only?

**My Position (circle one):** YES / NO

**Curious Question I Asked Myself:** _______________________________________________

**CLAIM** — What do I believe?
_______________________________________________

**REASON 1** — Why is my claim true?
_______________________________________________

**EVIDENCE** — What specific fact, example, or detail proves Reason 1?
_______________________________________________

**REASON 2** — A second reason my claim is true:
_______________________________________________

**EVIDENCE** — What specific fact, example, or detail proves Reason 2?
_______________________________________________

**OPPOSING CLAIM** — What would someone who disagrees say?
_______________________________________________

**REBUTTAL** — How do I respond? (Hint: stay curious. Don't just dismiss them — understand them first.)
_______________________________________________

**CONCLUDING THOUGHT** — Why does this matter?
_______________________________________________

---

**Instructions to give students:**

> *"You have 15 minutes to fill in your Argument Builder. Before you write your claim, you must write your 'Curious Question I Asked Myself.' This is not optional — it is the engine of your argument. If you get stuck anywhere, ask yourself: 'What am I genuinely curious about here?' That question will unlock the next sentence."*

**Teacher circulates** during this time. Specific prompts to use when conferencing with individual students who are stuck:

- *"What do you actually wonder about this topic?"*
- *"What would change your mind?"*
- *"What would someone smarter than you who disagrees think?"*
- *"What do you not know yet that would help you?"*

---

### SHARING & PEER RESPONSE *(5 minutes)*

**Pair students** and have them share one thing with a partner:

> *"Read your partner your Opposing Claim and your Rebuttal only. Then ask your partner: 'Did I take the other side seriously, or did I dismiss them too quickly?'"*

Partners respond honestly. This is a quick, targeted exchange — no more than 2–2.5 minutes per person.

**Bring the class back together.** Ask two volunteers to share their opposing claim and rebuttal with the whole class.

After each one, ask the class:

> *"Did they stay curious about the other side, or did they dismiss it? How could curiosity make that rebuttal even stronger?"*

---

### CLOSING REFLECTION — "The Curiosity Connection" *(2 minutes)*

Ask students to write a 2–3 sentence response to this prompt on the back of their graphic organizer:

> *"How did being curious — asking genuine questions — make your argument stronger today? Give one specific example."*

**Read 2–3 responses aloud** (with permission) to close the lesson with student voices.

**Final teacher statement:**

> *"The best thinkers in history — scientists, lawyers, journalists, leaders — were not people who had all the answers. They were people who could not stop asking questions. Curiosity is not a personality trait you either have or don't have. It is a skill. It is a choice. And as you saw today, it makes everything you write sharper, more honest, and more convincing. Next time you sit down to write an argument, the first thing you should reach for is not your pen — it's a question."*

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a partially filled-in Argument Builder with the claim already written, so students can focus on reasons, evidence, and the opposing claim
- Provide a sentence starter bank: *"I wonder whether…" / "One reason is…" / "Someone might disagree because…" / "However, …"*
- Allow verbal responses recorded by a peer or teacher during independent writing

**For students who need extension:**
- Challenge them to write a second, shorter opposing argument — take the opposite position and build it equally well
- Ask: *"Which argument was stronger? How do you know? What would it take to settle this question — what evidence would be decisive?"*
- Encourage them to identify a logical fallacy that a weak version of their argument might contain

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the vocabulary anchor chart translated or in bilingual format
- Allow students to draft their curiosity questions in their home language first, then translate
- Partner with a bilingual peer during the sharing activity

---

## ASSESSMENT

**Formative:** Teacher observes quality of curiosity questions on Curiosity Card and graphic organizer during circulation. Listens to peer sharing exchanges for evidence of genuine engagement with opposing views.

**Exit Ticket:** Completed Argument Builder graphic organizer, evaluated on:
- Presence of a clear claim *(0–1 point)*
- At least one reason supported by specific evidence *(0–2 points)*
- An opposing claim that is genuinely stated (not strawmanned) *(0–1 point)*
- A rebuttal that responds directly to the opposing claim *(0–1 point)*
- Curiosity question that is open-ended and authentic *(0–1 point)*

**Total: 6 points** — used as a quick formative check, not a grade

---

## EXTENSION CONNECTIONS

**For homework or the following class:**
Students take their completed graphic organizer and write it into a full argumentative paragraph or multi-paragraph essay, following the structure they built today.

**Cross-curricular connection:**
This same curiosity framework applies to science hypothesis formation, historical analysis, and mathematical reasoning — teachers in partner classrooms can reinforce the *Curious Question* step in their own disciplines.

**Long-term curiosity habit:**
Introduce a **Classroom Curiosity Wall** — a section of the whiteboard or a bulletin board where students can post genuine open questions they encounter during the week. Once per week, use one of those questions as a brief argument warm-up.

> *\"When you assume you already know everything about a topic, you stop looking. But when you stay genuinely curious — when you wonder 'Wait, is that really true?' — you dig deeper. You find facts, examples, and details that make your argument impossible to ignore.\"*

**Point 2: Curious people understand the other side.**

> *\"Here's something strange: the best arguers are also the best listeners. They're genuinely curious about why someone might disagree with them. If you can walk into someone else's thinking — really understand it — you can address it directly. That's called acknowledging an opposing claim, and it makes your argument dramatically more convincing.\"*

**Point 3: Curious people ask 'Why?' one more time than everyone else.**

> *\"Anyone can state an opinion. Curious writers ask 'Why is this true?' and 'How do I know?' one more time than they think they need to. That extra 'Why?' is usually where the real reason lives.\"*

Write this sentence on the board and leave it visible:

> **\"Strong arguments are built by curious minds.\"**

### VOCABULARY ANCHOR *(3 minutes)*

Quickly review the key argument terms students will use today. Write each term and its brief definition on the board.

| Term | Student-Friendly Definition |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | Your main argument — what you believe is true |
| **Reason** | Why your claim is true |
| **Evidence** | Facts, examples, or data that prove your reason |
| **Opposing Claim** | What someone who disagrees with you would say |
| **Rebuttal** | Your response to the opposing claim |

Ask students to give a thumbs up if they've seen these words before. Confirm that today they'll use all five in real writing.

### GUIDED PRACTICE — \"The Curiosity Card\" *(10 minutes)*

**Distribute Curiosity Cards.** Each card has the following printed on it:

---

*CURIOSITY CARD*

**Today's Topic:** Should middle schools eliminate traditional letter grades and replace them with written feedback only?

**Step 1 — Before you decide anything, ask 5 genuine questions about this topic:**
1. _______________________________________________
2. _______________________________________________
3. _______________________________________________
4. _______________________________________________
5. _______________________________________________

**Step 2 — Circle the one question you are MOST curious about.**

**Step 3 — What would someone who DISAGREES with you wonder about?**
They might ask: _______________________________________________

---

**Walk students through Step 1 together.** Model aloud what genuine curiosity sounds like versus lazy assumptions.

*Model for students:*

> *\"A lazy question sounds like: 'Isn't this a bad idea?' — because it already has the answer baked in. A curious question sounds like: 'How do students actually feel about their grades — do grades motivate them or stress them out more?' That question opens a door. It doesn't close one.\"*

Give students 3 minutes to write their five questions independently. Then invite students to share. Record 6–8 student questions on the board under the label **\"Our Class's Curiosity Wall.\"**

**Lead a 60-second discussion:**

> *\"Look at how different our questions are. Some of you are curious about students' mental health. Some of you are curious about what colleges would think. Some of you are curious about teachers. Each of those curiosities will lead to a totally different argument — and that's the point. Your curiosity shapes your argument before you write a single word.\"*

Complete Steps 2 and 3 briefly — students circle one question and try to imagine what a person who disagrees might wonder.

### MODELED WRITING — \"Watch Me Build an Argument\" *(7 minutes)*

**Tell students:**

> *\"I'm going to write the skeleton of an argument in front of you, and I want you to watch how I use curiosity at every step. Call me out if I stop being curious.\"*

Write the following on the board step by step, narrating your thinking aloud:

**Claim:** Middle schools should replace letter grades with written feedback because written feedback gives students more useful information about how to actually improve.

*Say:* \"Okay. I believe that. But now — why? And how do I know? Let me get curious.\"

**Curious Question I asked myself:** *\"Do students actually use their grades to get better, or do they just... look at them and move on?\"*

**Reason:** When students receive only a letter grade, research shows they often focus on the number rather than the learning itself, which can reduce their motivation to grow.

*Say:* \"Good. Now evidence. I need to get even more curious — 'What do I actually know? What have I read or experienced?'\"

**Evidence:** Research and observations suggest that written feedback can encourage deeper learning and improvement compared to letter grades alone.

*Say:* \"Now — what would a curious, honest writer do next? They'd ask: 'Who would disagree with me, and why?'\"

**Opposing Claim:** Some people argue that grades are necessary because colleges and employers need a clear, standardized way to evaluate students.

*Say:* \"And now I stay curious: 'Is that completely true? Can I disagree with it without just dismissing it?'\"

**Rebuttal:** While standardized evaluation is important, many schools that use written feedback have found ways to produce transcripts that colleges can still interpret, meaning both goals can be achieved simultaneously.

**Point to the board and say:**

> *\"That argument was built entirely by asking questions. Every single sentence came from curiosity. Now it's your turn.\"*

_______________________________________________

**EVIDENCE** — What specific fact, example, or detail proves Reason 2?
_______________________________________________

**OPPOSING CLAIM** — What would someone who disagrees say?
_______________________________________________

_______________________________________________

**CONCLUDING THOUGHT** — Why does this matter?

**Instructions to give students:**

> *\"You have 15 minutes to fill in your Argument Builder. Before you write your claim, you must write your 'Curious Question I Asked Myself.' This is not optional — it is the engine of your argument. If you get stuck anywhere, ask yourself: 'What am I genuinely curious about here?' That question will unlock the next sentence.\"*

**Teacher circulates** during this time. Specific prompts to use when conferencing with individual students who are stuck:

- *\"What do you actually wonder about this topic?\"*
- *\"What would change your mind?\"*
- *\"What would someone smarter than you who disagrees think?\"*
- *\"What do you not know yet that would help you?\"*

### SHARING & PEER RESPONSE *(5 minutes)*

**Pair students** and have them share one thing with a partner:

> *\"Read your partner your Opposing Claim and your Rebuttal only. Then ask your partner: 'Did I take the other side seriously, or did I dismiss them too quickly?'\"*

Partners respond honestly. This is a quick, targeted exchange — no more than 2–2.5 minutes per person.

**Bring the class back together.** Ask two volunteers to share their opposing claim and rebuttal with the whole class.

After each one, ask the class:

> *\"Did they stay curious about the other side, or did they dismiss it? How could curiosity make that rebuttal even stronger?\"*
