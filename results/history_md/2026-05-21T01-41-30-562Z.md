## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with mixed scenarios, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A taxi charges $3 to start plus $2 per mile."
- **Scenario B:** "Apples cost $2 per pound."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students think-pair-share for 3 minutes, then share aloud. Guide them to articulate that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and pass through (0,0).

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying information:** Students must precisely clarify what makes a relationship proportional vs. non-proportional.
**Assessment:** Listen for clarity during pair-share; circulate and note whether students distinguish "constant rate" from "starts at zero." Use cold-call follow-ups: *"Can you restate that more precisely?"*

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the **constant of proportionality (k)** using y = kx. Walk through one scenario ("3 bracelets cost $12") shown five ways:

| Representation | Example |
|---|---|
| **Table** | (1,4), (2,8), (3,12) |
| **Graph** | Straight line through origin |
| **Equation** | y = 4x |
| **Diagram** | Bar model showing 4:1 ratio |
| **Verbal** | "Each bracelet costs $4" |

Emphasize how each form reveals "k = 4." Organize notes on the board in a labeled 5-column anchor chart.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing information:** Students copy the 5-representation chart into notebooks, arranging the forms in a logical, comparable structure.
**Assessment:** Spot-check notebooks during transition; look for parallel structure and clear labeling that supports cross-form comparison.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Source Analysis (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout with **four "student claims"** about whether given relationships are proportional. Two claims are correct, two contain errors (e.g., "Since the table shows equal differences, it's proportional"—a confusion of arithmetic vs. proportional growth).

Students work in pairs to verify each claim using at least two representations (e.g., check the table AND graph it).

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing validity of information:** Students cross-check claims using multiple representations as "authoritative sources."
**Assessment:** Collect handouts; check whether students verified using *more than one* method rather than accepting a claim at face value.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing quality of information:** When multiple representations are available, do students select the most revealing one (e.g., graphing to check if line passes through origin)?
**Assessment:** During circulation, ask: *"Which representation gave you the strongest evidence? Why?"* Note depth of justification.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Plan (12 minutes)
Present a real-world problem:
> *"You're buying snacks for a class party. Vendor A: 'Chips cost $1.50 per bag.' Vendor B: '$5 fee plus $1 per bag.' Vendor C: 'Buy 10 bags for $14.'"*

Groups of 4 must:
1. Represent each vendor as an equation and table.
2. Identify which are proportional.
3. **Recommend a vendor for buying 8 bags AND for buying 30 bags.**

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing pros and cons:** Students systematically compare vendors, considering different purchase quantities (short-term vs. long-term cost implications).
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation listing trade-offs. Look for evidence of evaluating *both* small and large purchase scenarios, not just one.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying sound reasoning:** Recommendations must be justified with calculations and proportional reasoning.
**Assessment:** Check that conclusions follow logically from the math (e.g., "Vendor B is cheaper after 10 bags because the fixed fee gets distributed"). Flag any unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Independent Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students complete an exit ticket with three parts:
1. Given a new table, determine if it's proportional and write the equation.
2. Sketch its graph.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"Describe one moment today when you changed your thinking. What clue (table, graph, equation, etc.) caused the change, and what would you do differently next time?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on thinking processes:** Students articulate metacognitive shifts during the lesson.
**Assessment:** Score reflection on a 0–2 scale: 0 = no reflection, 1 = surface mention ("I changed my mind"), 2 = specific, strategic reflection ("I assumed equal differences meant proportional, but graphing showed it didn't pass through origin, so now I'll always check the origin first").

---

### 6. Closing (3 minutes)
Quick whole-class share: two volunteers read their reflections. Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying the constant of proportionality from graphs.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence stems for reflection.
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price, etc.) and represent it in all five forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportionality in the Real World: Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will recognize, represent, and analyze proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions. Students will critically evaluate data sources, organize mathematical evidence, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook & Source Evaluation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: "We are planning a school pancake breakfast. We need to know the proportional relationship between cups of pancake mix and the number of pancakes produced to scale our recipe." 
The teacher provides two information sources: 
1. A screenshot of an anonymous social media comment claiming "1 cup makes 5 pancakes, 2 cups make 12 pancakes, 3 cups make 20 pancakes."
2. An official printout from a culinary institute’s nutritional database stating a strict ratio: "1 cup yields exactly 8 pancakes."

Students work in pairs to evaluate these sources before doing any math. They must cross-check the social media claim against the culinary database to see if the social media numbers form a proportional relationship.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students discuss and select the culinary database over the social media comment, demonstrating sophisticated judgment in choosing a high-quality, authoritative source to build their mathematical model.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher listens to pair discussions and assesses whether students explicitly identify the culinary database as more reliable for establishing a constant mathematical ratio, noting this on a formative assessment checklist.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students rigorously cross-check the social media claim by testing its internal validity. They divide the pancakes by cups (5/1, 12/2, 20/3) to prove the source is mathematically invalid because it lacks a constant of proportionality.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reviews students' scratchpad calculations to ensure they used division to cross-check the data points, successfully proving the first source's invalidity.

---

### **Part 2: Organizing and Clarifying Data (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Using the valid data (1 cup = 8 pancakes), students are given a scrambled list of bulk ingredient purchases (e.g., "400 pancakes," "10 cups," "24 pancakes," "50 cups"). They must translate this verbal and scattered data into a clear, structured format. 

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students clarify the verbal descriptions by precisely translating them into an algebraic equation ($y = 8x$, where $y$ is pancakes and $x$ is cups). This clarifies the core mathematical idea to support deeper understanding of the relationship.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses the accuracy of the equations written on students' whiteboards, ensuring they have correctly identified the independent and dependent variables.

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students arrange the scattered data points into a logical, ascending T-table, and then plot these points on a coordinate plane, drawing a straight line through the origin.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher collects the graph paper to observe if the table is structured sequentially and if the graph strengthens the logical flow of the data (straight line, passing through 0,0).

---

### **Part 3: The Bulk Discount Dilemma (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a twist: "The grocery store sells syrup. 1 bottle is $3. 2 bottles are $6. But if you buy a case of 10 bottles, it costs $25." Students must decide if the pricing is proportional and choose the best way to present this information to the school principal to justify their budget.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students must determine that the bulk pricing breaks the proportional relationship. They demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by explaining that while $3/1$ and $6/2$ share a unit rate of $3$, $25/10$ has a unit rate of $2.50$. They conclude the relationship is non-proportional.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher evaluates the students' written justifications, looking for explicit use of unit rate comparisons to support their final decision.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students debate whether to show the principal a table, a graph, or an equation to prove the syrup pricing is not proportional. They systematically evaluate trade-offs (e.g., "A graph visually shows the line bending at 10 bottles, which is easy to see, but a table shows the exact dollar amounts we will save").
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher facilitates a whole-class discussion, tracking which students successfully articulate the long-term implications and trade-offs of using visual (graph) versus numerical (table) representations for a specific audience.

---

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an Exit Ticket titled "Thinking About My Thinking." They must write a short paragraph answering: "How did your understanding of what makes a relationship proportional change from the beginning of the lesson to now? Describe a moment today where you caught a mistake in your own thinking or had to adjust your strategy."

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students reflect on their initial assumptions (e.g., assuming all store pricing is proportional) and demonstrate deep metacognitive awareness by explaining how graphing the data or finding the unit rate forced them to strategically adjust their mathematical thinking.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reads the Exit Tickets, scoring them based on the depth of self-awareness. High scores are given to students who explicitly name the strategy (like cross-checking or graphing) that helped them correct a misconception about proportionality.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Grade:** 7  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student handout with tables, graph grids, and scenarios  
- Rulers  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 5 minutes  
Display two situations:

1. **Store A:** 3 notebooks cost $6.  
2. **Store B:** 5 notebooks cost $12.

Ask: “Which store has the better deal? How do you know?” Students solve independently, then share with a partner.

Guide students toward finding the unit rate:  
- Store A: $6 ÷ 3 = $2 per notebook  
- Store B: $12 ÷ 5 = $2.40 per notebook  

Explain that unit rate helps us compare quantities and is connected to proportional relationships.

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify the relevant quantities: number of notebooks and total cost.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for students clearly stating what each number represents and writing labels such as “dollars per notebook,” not just “2” or “2.40.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes  
Introduce the definition:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities stays constant. In a proportional relationship, one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other quantity.

Show the equation form:

\[
y = kx
\]

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Use the notebook example:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |
| 4 | 8 |

Ask students: “What is the constant of proportionality?”  
Students should identify **2**, because each notebook costs $2.

Then graph the points: (1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8). Emphasize:

A proportional relationship has a graph that is a straight line through the origin, (0,0).

Show a non-example:

| Hours | Total Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 7 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 17 |

Explain that this has a starting fee of $2 plus $5 per hour, so it follows:

\[
y = 5x + 2
\]

It is linear but **not proportional** because it does not pass through the origin and cannot be written as \(y = kx\).

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students organize the same relationship as a table, graph, equation, and sentence.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether students arrange information logically and consistently, such as matching table values to graph points and equation values.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes  
Give students this scenario:

“A smoothie recipe uses 2 cups of fruit for every 3 cups of yogurt.”

Students work with the teacher to create:

1. **Ratio statement:** 2 cups fruit for every 3 cups yogurt  
2. **Table:**

| Fruit | Yogurt |
|---|---|
| 2 | 3 |
| 4 | 6 |
| 6 | 9 |
| 8 | 12 |

3. **Equation:** If \(x\) = cups of fruit and \(y\) = cups of yogurt, then  
\[
y = \frac{3}{2}x
\]

4. **Graph:** Plot the points and draw a line through the origin.  
5. **Verbal description:** “The amount of yogurt is always 1.5 times the amount of fruit.”

Ask: “How can each representation prove the relationship is proportional?”

Students should explain that the ratio stays constant, the graph passes through the origin, and the equation is in the form \(y = kx\).

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students verify that the table, graph, equation, and verbal description all match the original recipe.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to point to at least two representations that support their conclusion, such as “The table shows 3/2 each time, and the graph goes through (0,0).”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes  
Pairs receive four relationship cards. Each card includes a different representation.

Example cards:

**Card A: Table**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |

**Card B: Equation**  
\[
y = 4x
\]

**Card C: Graph**  
A straight line that crosses the y-axis at 3.

**Card D: Verbal Description**  
“A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.”

Students must sort each card into “Proportional” or “Not Proportional” and justify their choice.

Then students choose one proportional card and represent it in at least two additional ways. For example, if they start with an equation, they create a table and graph.

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students decide which evidence is most useful for determining proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students using high-quality mathematical evidence, such as constant ratios, origin on graph, or equation form \(y = kx\), instead of guesses like “it looks even.”

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students discuss which representation is easiest or most reliable for deciding proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for comparisons such as “The table makes ratios easy to check, but the graph quickly shows whether it goes through the origin.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes  
Bring the class together. Ask:

- “Can a graph be a straight line but not proportional?”  
- “Why does a proportional relationship have to go through the origin?”  
- “Which representation helped you most today, and why?”

Clarify that not all linear relationships are proportional. A proportional relationship must have a constant ratio and no starting amount other than zero.

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students justify whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical reasoning.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Students should connect their decisions to evidence: constant ratio, equation \(y = kx\), or graph through the origin.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 7 minutes  
Students complete independently:

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 3 | 12 |
| 5 | 20 |
| 8 | 32 |

Is it proportional? Explain.

2. Write an equation for the relationship.

3. Describe how you know your answer is correct.

Expected answer: Yes, it is proportional because \(y/x = 4\) for each pair. Equation:  
\[
y = 4x
\]

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students explain how they checked their work.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for reflection such as “At first I looked only at the numbers, but then I divided y by x for each row to make sure the ratio stayed the same.”

| **Equation** | y = 8x |
| **Diagram** | Bar model showing 8:1 ratio |
| **Verbal** | "Each cup yields 8 pancakes" |

Then show a **non-example**: a taxi that charges $3 plus $2/mile (y = 2x + 3). Emphasize: proportional graphs must pass through (0,0).

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying information:** Students articulate precisely what distinguishes proportional from non-proportional (constant ratio AND passes through origin).
**Assessment:** Cold-call follow-ups: *"Can you restate that more precisely?"* Listen for both criteria.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing information:** Students copy the 5-representation chart into notebooks with parallel structure.
**Assessment:** Spot-check notebooks for labeled columns and matched values across representations.

---

### 3. Collaborative Task: The Vendor Decision (15 minutes)
Present:
> *"You're buying snacks for a class party. Vendor A: 'Chips cost $1.50 per bag.' Vendor B: '$5 fee plus $1 per bag.' Vendor C: 'Buy 10 bags for $14.'"*

Groups of 4 must:
1. Represent each vendor as a table and equation.
2. Identify which are proportional (graph if helpful).
3. **Recommend a vendor for buying 8 bags AND for buying 30 bags**, with written justification.

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing pros and cons:** Groups systematically compare vendors across small and large purchase scenarios.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation listing trade-offs. Look for evaluation of *both* quantities, not just one (e.g., "Vendor B is worse for 8 bags but best for 30 because the fixed fee distributes").

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying sound reasoning:** Recommendations must be grounded in unit-rate or equation reasoning.
**Assessment:** Check that conclusions follow logically from the math. Flag any unsupported claims; ask groups to defend with calculations.

---

### 4. Independent Practice & Reflection (15 minutes)
**Part 1 (Exit Ticket — 10 min):** Given a new table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 3 | 12 |
| 5 | 20 |
| 8 | 32 |

a) Is it proportional? Justify using two representations.
b) Write its equation and sketch its graph.

**Part 2 (Reflection — 5 min):**
*"Describe one moment today when you changed your thinking. What clue (table, graph, equation, source) caused the change, and what would you do differently next time?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on thinking processes:** Students articulate metacognitive shifts.
**Assessment:** Score reflection 0–2:
- **0** = no reflection
- **1** = surface mention ("I changed my mind")
- **2** = specific, strategic ("I assumed equal differences meant proportional, but graphing showed it didn't pass through origin—now I'll check the origin first").

---

### 5. Closing (5 minutes)
Two volunteers read reflections aloud. Reinforce: *Not every linear relationship is proportional—it must have constant ratio AND pass through (0,0).* Preview tomorrow's focus on identifying k from graphs.
