## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Time:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Materials Needed
- Whiteboard and markers
- Sticky notes (2 per student)
- Lined paper or notebooks
- A single, strange object (a compass, an hourglass, an old key — anything unusual)

---

## Lesson Flow

### HOOK — The Mysterious Object (5 minutes)

Walk in silently. Place the strange object on the front desk. Say nothing about it.

Let students stare. Let them squirm. After thirty seconds, simply ask: *"What do you want to know?"*

Take every question they offer and write them on the board without answering a single one. Fill the board. When a student says, "Just tell us what it is!" respond: *"That feeling you have right now — that need to know — that's curiosity. And today, it's your most important writing tool."*

Reveal the object. Briefly explain it. Then pivot: curiosity doesn't just help you wonder. It helps you **argue**.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Arguments Start With Questions (12 minutes)

Explain to students that the best arguments in history didn't start with someone having all the answers. They started with someone asking a better question.

Write this on the board:

> **Curious writers ask: "What if I'm wrong? What does the other side think? What evidence actually exists?"**

Walk through the anatomy of a strong argument:
- **Claim** — your position (what you believe)
- **Reasons** — why you believe it
- **Evidence** — facts, quotes, data that support your reasons
- **Counterclaim** — what someone who disagrees might say
- **Rebuttal** — why your argument still holds despite that opposition

Emphasize the counterclaim. Tell students: *"Weak arguers ignore the other side because they're afraid of it. Curious arguers seek it out, because understanding it makes them stronger."*

Ask: *"Would you rather debate someone who has never heard your opinion, or someone who has studied it?"* Let two or three students respond briefly.

---

### MODELING — Think Aloud (8 minutes)

Write this prompt on the board:

> **"Should students have homework over school breaks?"**

Model your own thinking aloud. Start with your instinct — maybe "no" — then immediately ask yourself: *"But what would someone who disagrees say? What do they know that I might be ignoring?"*

List the counterclaim on the board: *Some students benefit from routine and continued practice.*

Then model the rebuttal using curiosity: *"That's interesting. Is there evidence for that? Or does rest and reduced stress actually improve learning more?"*

Show students that curiosity forces you to search for real evidence instead of just defending what you already feel.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — Sticky Note Challenge (10 minutes)

Give each student two sticky notes.

On the **first sticky note**, they write their immediate opinion on this question:
*"Should middle schoolers be allowed to choose their own classes?"*

On the **second sticky note**, they write the strongest possible argument **against** their own opinion — not a weak version of it, the strongest version they can imagine.

Have students pair up and share both sticky notes. Partners give feedback: *"Is that counterclaim actually strong, or did you make it easy to defeat?"*

Circulate the room. Push students who write weak counterclaims. Say things like: *"Play devil's advocate harder. If you disagree with yourself, what's the most convincing thing you could say?"*

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — Draft an Argument (15 minutes)

Students write a short structured argument (one page, five paragraphs or close to it) using the homework-break prompt or the class-choice prompt — their choice.

Requirement: They **must** include a counterclaim paragraph and a rebuttal. Before they start, they must write one genuine question they're curious about at the top of their paper — something they don't fully know the answer to yet about their topic.

This question becomes their compass while drafting. Encourage them to write toward answering it.

---

### CLOSING — Curiosity Is a Habit (5 minutes)

Bring the class back together. Ask two or three volunteers to share the question they wrote at the top of their paper.

Close with this: *"The best writers, scientists, leaders — they all share one habit. They stay genuinely curious about what they don't know. When you write an argument and you're willing to ask hard questions — even ones that challenge your own opinion — you stop guessing and start discovering. That's when your writing gets powerful."*

Return to the mysterious object from the beginning. Ask: *"Are you more curious about it now, or less?"* 

Usually, more. End there.

---

**Exit Ticket:** One sentence — *"The most curious question I still have about my topic is..."*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Compelling Argument

**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade

**Learning Objectives:**
*   Students will understand that curiosity is a driving force behind effective argumentative writing.
*   Students will practice generating curiosity-driven questions about a given topic.
*   Students will begin to formulate claims based on their curiosity and identify potential evidence and opposing viewpoints.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers or pens
*   Handout: "The Great Debate: Should [Topic]?" (e.g., "Should students be allowed to use cell phones during lunch?")
*   Small slips of paper for exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Hook: Sparking Curiosity (7 minutes)**

1.  **Display a Mystery (3 minutes):** Project an intriguing image or a short, silent video clip of something mysterious or unexplained (e.g., an optical illusion, a strange historical artifact, a puzzling news headline, or a "What is this?" object).
2.  **"What Do You Notice? What Do You Wonder?" (4 minutes):** Ask students to silently observe for 30 seconds. Then, have them quickly jot down 2-3 things they *notice* and 2-3 *questions* they have about the image/video. Invite a few students to share their questions. Emphasize the natural human tendency to ask "why?" and "how?" when faced with something new or unclear. Introduce this feeling as "curiosity."

**II. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (8 minutes)**

1.  **Define Curiosity (3 minutes):** Ask students: "What does it mean to be curious?" Guide them to understand it as a strong desire to know or learn something. Explain that curiosity isn't just for mysteries; it's essential for understanding the world around us.
2.  **The Argumentative Link (5 minutes):** Explain that strong arguments don't just appear; they are born from curiosity.
    *   "To write an argument (W.7.1), you need a *claim* – something you believe to be true. How do you figure out what to claim? By being curious enough to investigate!"
    *   "You need *reasons* and *evidence*. How do you find those? By being curious enough to ask questions and research!"
    *   "You need to acknowledge *alternate or opposing claims*. How do you understand other viewpoints? By being curious enough to consider perspectives different from your own!"
    *   Summarize: "Curiosity is the engine that drives a good argument."

**III. Activity: Generating Curiosity Questions (15 minutes)**

1.  **Introduce the Debate Topic (2 minutes):** Distribute the "The Great Debate" handout. The topic should be relevant and debatable for middle schoolers (e.g., "Should students be allowed to use cell phones during lunch?").
2.  **Small Group Brainstorm (10 minutes):** Divide students into small groups (3-4 students). Instruct them to brainstorm and write down as many "curiosity questions" as they can about the topic.
    *   Encourage open-ended questions that start with "Why," "How," "What if," "What are the effects of," "Who benefits/loses," "What do experts say," "What are the different perspectives?"
    *   *Teacher circulates, prompting with questions like:* "What are you curious about regarding the rules?" "What are you curious about regarding student behavior?" "What are you curious about regarding the school's perspective?"
3.  **Share a Few (3 minutes):** Ask each group to share 1-2 of their most interesting curiosity questions with the class. Write a few on the board.

**IV. Activity: From Curiosity to Claim (20 minutes)**

1.  **Modeling: Question to Claim (5 minutes):**
    *   Choose one of the shared curiosity questions from the board (e.g., "How do cell phones impact student social interaction during lunch?").
    *   Model how to turn this question into a *claim* (a statement that can be argued).
        *   *Example Claim:* "Allowing cell phones during lunch negatively impacts student social interaction."
    *   Briefly discuss what kind of *evidence* you'd need to support this claim, driven by the initial curiosity (e.g., "I'd need to look for studies on screen time and social skills, or observe students during lunch.").
2.  **Modeling: Acknowledging Opposing Claims (5 minutes):**
    *   Ask: "If someone disagreed with this claim, what would *they* be curious about? What questions would *they* ask?"
    *   Guide students to think about the opposite perspective.
        *   *Example Opposing Claim:* "Allowing cell phones during lunch provides students with important communication tools and a sense of independence."
    *   Explain that being curious about other viewpoints makes your own argument stronger because you can address them.
3.  **Individual/Pair Practice (10 minutes):**
    *   Instruct students to choose one of their own curiosity questions from Activity III.
    *   On their handout or a separate piece of paper, they should:
        *   Turn their curiosity question into a preliminary *claim*.
        *   List 2-3 types of *evidence* they would need to find to support that claim (e.g., "statistics," "expert opinions," "student surveys," "personal anecdotes").
        *   Formulate one potential *opposing claim* that someone might make.
    *   *Teacher circulates, providing feedback and guidance.*

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**

1.  **Review (2 minutes):** Briefly reiterate how curiosity is the starting point for strong argumentative writing – it helps us ask questions, find evidence, and understand different perspectives.
2.  **Exit Ticket (3 minutes):** Distribute small slips of paper. Ask students to answer one of the following questions:
    *   "How does being curious help you write a better argument?"
    *   "Write one curiosity question about a topic you care about, and then turn it into a potential claim."
    *   Collect slips as students leave.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity Through Argument Writing**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity as a mindset and be able to write a short argumentative paragraph supporting the claim that curiosity is important, using clear reasons, relevant evidence, and acknowledging opposing views.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Projector or chart paper (optional)  
- Handout with a simple argumentative paragraph example (provided by teacher)  
- Sticky notes or index cards  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board. Highlight key words like “asking questions,” “wanting to learn,” “exploring,” etc.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci, or a modern scientist) who used curiosity to discover or invent something important.  
- Explain that curiosity is a powerful tool that helps us learn and grow, and it’s important not only in science or art but in everyday life and learning.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that today, students will practice writing an argument—a type of writing where you state your opinion (claim) and support it with reasons and evidence.  
- Tell them they will write about why curiosity is important.  
- Briefly review the parts of an argument:  
  - Claim (your main idea)  
  - Reasons (why you believe it)  
  - Evidence (facts, examples, or details that support your reasons)  
  - Acknowledging opposing views (showing you understand other opinions)  
  - Conclusion (restating your claim in a strong way)

**3. Modeling an Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Display or read aloud a short example paragraph arguing that curiosity is important. For example:  

*"Curiosity is important because it helps us learn new things and solve problems. When we are curious, we ask questions and explore ideas, which leads to discoveries. For instance, many scientists have made breakthroughs because they were curious about how the world works. Some people might say that curiosity can be distracting or lead to trouble, but when guided properly, curiosity encourages creativity and critical thinking. Therefore, curiosity is a valuable quality that helps us grow and succeed."*  

- Break down the paragraph with the class, identifying the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing view.

**4. Guided Practice: Brainstorming (10 minutes)**  
- Ask students to brainstorm reasons why curiosity is important. Write their ideas on the board. Examples might include:  
  - Helps us learn new things  
  - Encourages creativity  
  - Leads to problem-solving  
  - Makes life more interesting  
- Then, brainstorm possible opposing views, such as:  
  - Curiosity can be distracting  
  - Sometimes curiosity leads to trouble  
- Have students jot down one or two reasons and one opposing view on sticky notes or index cards.

**5. Independent Writing (15 minutes)**  
- Instruct students to write a short argumentative paragraph (5-7 sentences) supporting the claim that curiosity is important.  
- Remind them to:  
  - Clearly state their claim  
  - Include at least two reasons with evidence or examples  
  - Acknowledge an opposing view  
  - Organize their ideas logically  
- Circulate the room to provide support and feedback as needed.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each reading, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing view.  
- End with a brief discussion: *“How can being curious help you in school and in life?”*  
- Encourage students to keep curiosity in mind as they learn and write in the future.

---

### Extension/Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to observe something they are curious about during the week and write a short paragraph explaining why their curiosity about it is important.

---

This lesson combines teaching curiosity as a mindset with practicing argumentative writing skills aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1, helping students develop both critical thinking and writing abilities in an engaging, meaningful way.
