## Generator
# Argument Writing: Taking a Stand with Style and Structure

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Lesson Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Construct an argumentative claim supported by clear reasons and relevant evidence
- Acknowledge an opposing viewpoint within their argument
- Organize their reasoning in a logical, cohesive structure
- Apply creative thinking to develop a distinctive argumentative voice

---

## Materials
- Student journals or lined paper
- Whiteboard or projector
- "Argument Skeleton" graphic organizer (distributed at start)
- Topic card slips (one per student)

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up: The Debate Spark (8 minutes)

Display three controversial-but-age-appropriate prompts on the board:
- *"Homework should be banned on weekends."*
- *"Every student should learn a musical instrument."*
- *"Social media does more harm than good for teenagers."*

Ask students to stand on one side of the room based on their genuine opinion. Then — crucially — tell them: **"Now defend the side you actually disagree with."**

This moment targets **CRE1a (Developing Personal Tastes)** and **CRE3a (Comfort with Risk and Uncertainty)**. Students must articulate personal preferences while simultaneously stepping into uncomfortable intellectual territory. Observe which students lean in eagerly and which hesitate — both responses are valid entry points for discussion. Assess by listening for phrases like *"I personally think..."* versus students who simply echo what peers around them say.

---

### Direct Instruction: The Anatomy of an Argument (10 minutes)

Using the whiteboard, build the argument framework collaboratively:

**Claim → Reason → Evidence → Counterclaim → Rebuttal → Conclusion**

Explain each component with a mentor example about a relatable topic. Emphasize that a strong argument does not ignore opposing views — it engages them. This directly models **CRE4a (Connecting Ideas for Cohesiveness)**, showing students how each structural piece links to the next. Students are not just memorizing parts; they are seeing how ideas feed and reinforce one another.

Ask students: *"Where do you think the counterclaim belongs — and why does placement matter?"* This question activates **CRE4b (Reorganizing Ideas for Cohesiveness)**, prompting students to think about logical flow rather than arbitrary order. Assess through class discussion: can students explain why structure affects persuasion?

---

### Guided Practice: Building the Skeleton Together (10 minutes)

Using the Argument Skeleton graphic organizer, the class drafts a shared argument together on a teacher-chosen topic. Model thinking aloud: *"I could use the obvious example here, but what's a surprising piece of evidence that might actually change someone's mind?"*

This models **CRE2a (Generating New Ideas)** and **CRE2b (Seeking New Ideas)** — pushing students beyond the first idea that comes to mind. Encourage students to suggest evidence from unexpected angles: a personal story, a statistic, a historical parallel. Observe who contributes novel examples versus who repeats the most familiar talking points.

---

### Independent Writing: Your Argument, Your Voice (20 minutes)

Students receive a topic card (each student may get a different topic or choose from the three warm-up prompts). They have 20 minutes to write a structured argument of at least two paragraphs using their graphic organizer.

Key instructions posted on the board:
- Your argument should sound like *you* — not a textbook
- Include one counterclaim and explain why your position still holds
- Your conclusion should leave the reader thinking, not just summarizing

This extended writing block is the heart of the lesson, engaging multiple subcompetencies simultaneously:

**CRE1b (Personal Aesthetics and Style):** Students are explicitly encouraged to write in their own voice. Assess by reading for distinctive word choices, sentence rhythms, or rhetorical moves rather than generic phrasing.

**CRE3b (Comfort with Failure):** Midway through writing, pause and say: *"If your argument feels shaky right now, that's normal — that's the messy middle of good thinking. Keep going."* This normalizes struggle as part of the process. Observe which students push through versus shut down.

**CRE5a (Realizing Ideas):** The finished draft is the tangible product. Assess whether students successfully moved from the organizer framework to flowing written prose — not just filling in blanks but constructing an actual argument.

**CRE5b (Working with Constraints):** The graphic organizer and the 20-minute time limit are deliberate constraints. Watch for students who use the structure as a creative scaffold rather than a cage — who add flair within the form.

---

### Peer Review and Refinement (5 minutes)

Partners exchange drafts and circle one place where the argument feels strongest and one place where a reason needs more support. This brief exchange targets **CRE4c (Refining Ideas for Cohesiveness)**. Students are not rewriting — they are identifying where polish is needed. Assess by asking: did students give specific, actionable feedback, or only general praise?

---

### Closing Reflection (2 minutes)

Students write one sentence in their journal: *"The most interesting part of my argument is _________ because it made me think differently about _________."* This reinforces metacognitive ownership of their creative and argumentative choices.

---

## Assessment Note

Collect graphic organizers and drafts. Evaluate using a simple rubric aligned to W.7.1 components, noting specifically where students demonstrated creative risk-taking, original evidence selection, and structural intentionality alongside standard argumentative requirements.
