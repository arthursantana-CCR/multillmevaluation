## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking habits.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with sample scenarios, exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "3 apples cost $2. How much for 9 apples?"
- **Scenario B:** "A baby weighs 8 lbs at birth and 15 lbs at 6 months. Will she weigh 30 lbs at 12 months?"

Students discuss with shoulder partners: *Which is proportional? Why?*

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must articulate what "proportional" means and clarify why constant growth ≠ proportional growth.
**Assessment:** Listen for precise student language ("constant ratio," "passes through origin"). Cold-call two pairs to share clarifying definitions.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Teach the five representations using the apple scenario:
- **Table:** (3, $2), (6, $4), (9, $6)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = (2/3)x
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing repeated ratio
- **Verbal:** "Cost is two-thirds of the number of apples"

Highlight the **constant of proportionality (k)** across all five.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Model how to organize one relationship across five formats in a graphic organizer.
**Assessment:** Review student notebooks for parallel, logically sequenced representations (e.g., table values match graph points match equation outputs).

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Sort (12 minutes)
Distribute 6 cards per group (mix of tables, graphs, equations, word problems). Groups sort into "Proportional" vs. "Not Proportional" piles and justify each decision.

Sample non-proportional trap: y = 2x + 1 (looks linear but doesn't pass through origin).

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** Groups must verify proportionality by cross-checking *multiple* representations (e.g., test ratios in the table AND check if the graph crosses the origin).
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How do you know for sure?" Students at Level 2 will reference at least two confirming methods.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Some cards include incomplete data (e.g., only two table points). Students must judge whether evidence is sufficient.
**Assessment:** Collect group justification sheets. Look for statements like "Two points isn't enough — we need to check more ratios."

---

### 4. Collaborative Problem (12 minutes)
**Task:** "Your class is planning a pizza party. Pizza Place A charges $12 per pizza. Pizza Place B charges $20 base fee plus $8 per pizza. Which deal is proportional? Which is the better choice for 5 pizzas? For 20 pizzas?"

Students must represent both options as tables, graphs, and equations, then make a recommendation.

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Students compare two pricing models, considering small vs. large orders.
**Assessment:** Recommendation paragraphs should explicitly weigh trade-offs ("Place A is proportional and better for small orders, but Place B becomes cheaper per pizza at higher quantities because the fixed fee spreads out").

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students must justify their final recommendation using mathematical evidence from multiple representations.
**Assessment:** Score reasoning on a 0–2 rubric. Level 2 responses anticipate complications ("If we order exactly X pizzas, both cost the same — so it depends on group size").

---

### 5. Independent Practice (8 minutes)
Three problems on a handout:
1. Given a table, determine proportionality and find k.
2. Given a graph, write the equation.
3. Write a verbal description that matches y = 4x.

---

### 6. Closure & Metacognitive Exit Ticket (6 minutes)
Exit Ticket prompts:
1. What is one rule for recognizing proportional relationships?
2. **Reflection:** "Describe a moment today when you changed your mind or caught an error. What made you rethink it? What strategy will you use next time?"

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The reflection prompt requires students to identify shifts in their reasoning.
**Assessment:** Score exit tickets. Level 2 responses identify a specific error, explain *why* the mistake happened, and propose a future strategy (e.g., "I assumed any straight line was proportional. Now I'll always check if it goes through (0,0)").

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-filled tables; sentence starters for reflections.
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own "proportional trap" problem for classmates.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, gas mileage, etc.) and represent it in all five formats.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Decide whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.
3. Explain reasoning using constant of proportionality and multiple representations.

### Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, whiteboards or notebooks, projector/board, exit ticket.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 0–5 min — Warm-Up: “Same or Not?”
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Students answer: “Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

Briefly review: A proportional relationship has a constant ratio, written as \( y = kx \), and its graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify what information matters: x-values, y-values, ratios, and whether the pattern is multiplicative.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for precise statements such as “In Table A, \( y/x = 3 \) every time, so the constant of proportionality is 3.” Look for students distinguishing additive patterns from proportional ones.

---

### 5–15 min — Direct Instruction: Five Ways to Represent Proportions
Use the context: “A store sells notebooks at $2 each.”

Build five representations together:

1. **Verbal description:** Each notebook costs $2.  
2. **Table:**

| Notebooks, x | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost, y | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |

3. **Equation:** \( y = 2x \)  
4. **Graph:** Plot points \((0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6)\). Emphasize straight line through origin.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing $2 per notebook, such as 1 notebook → $2, 2 notebooks → $4.

Ask: “Where do you see the same idea in each representation?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students organize the same relationship across table, equation, graph, diagram, and words.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether students connect representations logically, such as using the table to create ordered pairs for the graph and using the constant rate to write the equation.

---

### 15–25 min — Guided Practice: Is It Proportional?
Give students three situations:

A. A cyclist travels 12 miles in 1 hour, 24 miles in 2 hours, and 36 miles in 3 hours.  
B. A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.  
C. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

Students work in pairs to decide which are proportional and represent at least one proportional example in two ways.

Discuss:
- A is proportional: \( y = 12x \).
- B is not proportional because it does not start at 0; equation \( y = 2x + 4 \).
- C is proportional because the ratio flour:sugar is constant.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check whether a relationship is proportional using more than one test: constant ratio, equation form, and graph through origin.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask students to justify with multiple forms of evidence: “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph would pass through \((0,0)\).” Note if they rely on only one clue or check consistency across representations.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence. For example, the taxi situation may look linear, but the equation and y-intercept reveal it is not proportional.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students choosing useful evidence, not just surface patterns. Strong responses mention that “linear” does not always mean “proportional.”

---

### 25–38 min — Group Task: Representation Challenge
Divide students into groups of 3–4. Give each group one relationship card:

1. “A dog eats 1.5 cups of food per day.”  
2. “A streaming service charges $10 per month.”  
3. “A plant is already 5 cm tall and grows 2 cm per week.”  
4. “A car travels 60 miles each hour.”

Each group must:
- Decide if the relationship is proportional.
- Create a table with at least four values.
- Write an equation if proportional.
- Sketch a graph.
- Write a one-sentence explanation.

Groups then post their work. Students do a quick gallery walk and leave one sticky note: “I agree because…” or “Check this because…”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Groups decide which representation best proves proportionality. They compare table, graph, equation, and verbal description.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for discussion of trade-offs: “The graph shows the origin clearly, but the table helps us see the constant ratio.” Assess whether students compare representations rather than using only one.

---

### 38–48 min — Whole-Class Discussion: Common Mistakes
Review one proportional and one non-proportional example from the group work.

Ask:
- “What would the graph of every proportional relationship have in common?”
- “Can a relationship be linear but not proportional?”
- “Why is \( y = 2x + 5 \) not proportional?”
- “What does the constant of proportionality mean in the situation?”

Emphasize:
- Proportional equations have the form \( y = kx \).
- The point \((0,0)\) matters.
- Constant additive change alone is not enough; the ratio must be constant.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students defend whether a relationship is proportional by using mathematical reasoning, not guessing.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask students to complete the sentence: “I know this is/is not proportional because…” Strong reasoning includes constant ratio, origin, and equation form.

---

### 48–55 min — Exit Ticket and Reflection
Students complete:

1. A snack mix uses 4 cups of cereal for every 3 cups of pretzels. Make a table for 1, 2, 3, and 4 batches.  
2. Write an equation relating cereal cups \( y \) to batches \( x \).  
3. Would the graph be a straight line through the origin? Explain.  
4. Reflection: “Which representation helped you most today—table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students explain which representation helped them reason and why.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive responses such as “The table helped me because I could check the ratio each time” or “The graph helped me notice whether it went through the origin.”

---

## Assessment
Use student warm-ups, group posters, discussion responses, and exit tickets to assess whether students can:
- Identify proportional relationships.
- Represent them in multiple ways.
- Explain the constant of proportionality.
- Use evidence to justify decisions.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables, graph axes, and sentence starters: “The ratio is constant because…”  
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create a non-proportional relationship that is still linear and explain why it fails the proportional test.

- **Table:** (0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6)
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing $2 per cupcake

Highlight the **constant of proportionality (k = 2)** in each representation.

**🎯 CRI1b — Organizing Information:** Model a graphic organizer that arranges the same relationship across all five formats with axes labeled and x-values in ascending order.
**Assessment:** Review student notebooks for parallel, logically sequenced representations (table values match graph points match equation outputs).

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Card Sort (12 minutes)
Distribute six cards per group (mix of tables, graphs, equations, and word problems). Include traps such as y = 2x + 1 (linear but not proportional) and a taxi that charges "$4 to start plus $2 per mile."

Groups sort into "Proportional" vs. "Not Proportional" and justify each decision in writing.

**🎯 CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information:** Groups must verify proportionality by cross-checking *multiple* representations — testing ratios in the table AND confirming the graph passes through the origin AND checking that the equation has the form y = kx.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How do you know for sure?" Level 2 students reference at least two confirming methods and can explain why "linear" does not always mean "proportional."

---

### 4. Collaborative Problem — Pizza Party (12 minutes)
**Task:** "Pizza Place A charges $12 per pizza. Pizza Place B charges a $20 base fee plus $8 per pizza. Which is proportional? Which is the better choice for 5 pizzas? For 20 pizzas?"

Groups represent both options as tables, graphs, and equations, then write a recommendation.

**🎯 CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons:** Students systematically compare pricing models for small vs. large orders, evaluating trade-offs.
**Assessment:** Recommendation paragraphs should weigh trade-offs explicitly ("Place A is proportional and cheaper for small orders, but Place B becomes cheaper per pizza at large quantities because the fixed fee spreads out").

**🎯 CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence and recognize that Place B is non-proportional because its graph does not pass through the origin.
**Assessment:** Score reasoning on a 0–2 rubric. Level 2 responses anticipate complexities ("There's a break-even point where both cost the same — the best choice depends on group size").

---

### 5. Gallery Walk & Whole-Class Discussion (8 minutes)
Groups post recommendations. Peers leave sticky notes: "I agree because…" or "Check this because…"

Debrief questions:
- "Can a relationship be linear but not proportional?"
- "Why is y = 2x + 5 not proportional?"
- "What does the constant of proportionality mean in our pizza scenario?"

---

### 6. Closure & Metacognitive Exit Ticket (6 minutes)
1. A snack mix uses 4 cups of cereal for every 3 cups of pretzels. Make a table for 1–4 batches, write an equation, and explain whether its graph passes through the origin.
2. **Reflection:** "Describe a moment today when you changed your mind or caught an error. What made you rethink it? What strategy will you use next time?"

**🎯 CRI5a — Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The reflection requires students to identify shifts in their reasoning.
**Assessment:** Level 2 responses identify a specific error, explain *why* it happened, and propose a strategy (e.g., "I assumed any straight line was proportional — now I'll always check whether it passes through (0,0) and whether the equation has a +b term").

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Pre-filled tables, labeled axes, sentence starters ("The ratio is constant because…").
- **Extension:** Create a non-proportional linear relationship and explain why it fails the proportional test.
