## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Clarify and organize arguments about homework using evidence
- Evaluate the reliability of sources supporting each position
- Weigh pros and cons to form a reasoned personal stance
- Reflect on how their thinking evolved throughout the lesson

---

## Materials
- Two short reading excerpts (one pro-homework, one anti-homework) from reputable education journals
- T-chart graphic organizer
- Source credibility checklist
- Exit ticket slips
- Whiteboard / projector

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### **Hook – 0:00 to 0:05 (5 minutes)**

Open with a quick show of hands: *"Who thinks homework helps you learn? Who thinks it doesn't?"* Record the class split on the board without comment. Tell students: *"By the end of this lesson, I want you to defend your position — or change it — using real evidence and solid reasoning."* This creates immediate personal investment and establishes that opinions must be supported.

---

### **Activity 1: Clarifying the Question — CRI1a (0:05 to 0:15 | 10 minutes)**

Distribute both short reading excerpts. Before students read, ask them to underline the **central claim** in each text and write one sentence in their own words explaining what each author actually means.

**Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students must restate complex arguments simply and precisely. Many will initially confuse "homework is ineffective" with "homework is harmful" — teacher circulates to probe: *"What exactly is the author claiming? Is that the same as saying homework should be banned?"*

**Assessment:** Teacher observes written restatements. Level 2 is demonstrated when a student accurately distinguishes nuanced positions (e.g., *"The author says homework helps in high school but not elementary school — that's different from saying all homework is bad"*). Level 1 is demonstrated when a student captures the general idea but conflates details.

---

### **Activity 2: Organizing the Evidence — CRI1b (0:15 to 0:25 | 10 minutes)**

Students use a **T-chart graphic organizer** to sort evidence from both texts into "Supports Homework" and "Opposes Homework" columns. They must also add one sub-column labeled "Type of Evidence" (statistic, expert opinion, anecdote).

**Targeting CRI1b (Organizing Information):** The structured organizer pushes students beyond simple listing toward categorizing evidence logically. Students who struggle will dump all points randomly; stronger students will notice that some evidence supports both sides depending on context.

**Assessment:** Collect T-charts. Level 2 is demonstrated when evidence is sorted accurately and labeled by type with logical groupings. Level 1 is demonstrated when sorting is mostly correct but labels are missing or inconsistent.

---

### **Activity 3: Source Investigation — CRI2a and CRI2b (0:25 to 0:35 | 10 minutes)**

Reveal the sources of both excerpts on the projector — one from a peer-reviewed education journal, one from a parent blog. Give students a **source credibility checklist** (author credentials, publication type, date, potential bias). In pairs, students evaluate which source they trust more and why.

**Targeting CRI2a (Assessing Validity) and CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** Students must move beyond *"it sounds right"* toward structured evaluation. Prompt discussion: *"Does the author's background matter? Could a parent blogger still be right? Could a researcher still be biased?"*

**Assessment:** Listen for reasoning during pair discussion. Level 2 for CRI2a is demonstrated when students cross-reference both the checklist criteria and content accuracy. Level 2 for CRI2b is demonstrated when students articulate *why* peer-reviewed sources carry more evidentiary weight while acknowledging limitations.

---

### **Activity 4: Structured Debate Prep — CRI3a and CRI4a (0:35 to 0:47 | 12 minutes)**

Assign half the class to argue *for* homework and half *against*, regardless of personal opinion. Students have five minutes to build their argument using only evidence from the texts, then participate in a brief structured fishbowl debate (four students inside, rest observing and noting strengths/weaknesses of reasoning).

**Targeting CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Students assigned a position they disagree with must genuinely engage with the opposing view. Prompt observers: *"Did they address long-term effects? Did they ignore any important trade-offs?"*

**Targeting CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Watch for logical fallacies — *"Everyone hates homework, so it must be bad"* signals Level -1. Level 2 is demonstrated when a student anticipates a counterargument and addresses it proactively within their own reasoning.

**Assessment:** Teacher uses a brief observation checklist during the fishbowl, noting whether reasoning is evidence-based, logically structured, and responsive to complexity.

---

### **Exit Ticket — CRI5a (0:47 to 0:55 | 8 minutes)**

Students independently complete a three-prompt exit ticket:
1. *"What did you believe about homework at the start of class?"*
2. *"What do you believe now, and what changed your thinking?"*
3. *"What question are you still uncertain about?"*

**Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):** This metacognitive prompt requires students to track their own reasoning process, not just their conclusion.

**Assessment:** Level 2 is demonstrated when a student identifies a specific moment — an argument, a source evaluation, a peer's comment — that shifted their thinking and explains *why* it was persuasive. Level 1 is demonstrated when a student simply says *"I learned more about homework"* without identifying how their reasoning changed. Level -1 is demonstrated when a student's response shows no awareness of the learning process at all.

---

## Closing Note to Teacher

Return to the opening show of hands. Ask students to vote again and briefly discuss what moved in the room — this closes the metacognitive loop and validates that changing one's mind based on evidence is a strength, not a weakness.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Homework Huddle - To Assign or Not to Assign?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Should schools have homework?

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify different arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support various perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of alternative choices regarding homework policies.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form and justify their own position on homework.
*   Reflect on their thinking process and how new information influenced their perspective.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (contains 3-4 short, diverse texts/quotes: e.g., a snippet from a research study on homework effectiveness, a quote from a parent advocating for less homework, a quote from a teacher explaining its benefits, a short news article summary on a school that banned homework). Each text will have a clear (or intentionally less clear) source citation.
*   Handout 2: T-Chart graphic organizer ("Arguments For Homework" / "Arguments Against Homework")
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, 7th graders! Let's start with a quick poll. Raise your hand if you think homework is a good idea. Now, raise your hand if you think schools should get rid of homework. Interesting! Today, we're going to explore this very question: 'Should schools have homework?' It's a topic that affects all of us, and there are many different opinions and reasons behind them."
*   **Teacher:** "Our goal today isn't just to share our opinions, but to think critically about *why* we hold those opinions, and to understand the different sides of this complex issue using evidence and careful thought."

**II. Exploring Perspectives & Clarifying Information (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will work in pairs. Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
*   **Teacher:** "In your pairs, read through the different perspectives on homework provided in Handout 1. As you read, your first task is to understand what each person or source is saying. Underline or highlight the main idea or key argument from each perspective."

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** After reading, students will paraphrase each perspective in their own words on their T-chart (Handout 2). They should aim for precision, capturing the core argument without adding their own bias. For example, if a text states, "Research indicates excessive homework can lead to student burnout," students should paraphrase it as "Some studies suggest that too much homework might cause students to feel exhausted," rather than "Homework is bad because it makes kids tired."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate among pairs. Listen to student discussions and review their paraphrases on the T-chart. Look for accurate and precise restatements of the original ideas. Are they capturing the essence of the argument? Are they avoiding misinterpretation or adding personal opinions?

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** As students paraphrase, they will also categorize each argument onto their T-chart (Handout 2) under "Arguments For Homework" or "Arguments Against Homework."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe how students group the information. Are arguments consistently placed on the correct side of the T-chart? Is their organization logical and easy to follow? Do they struggle with arguments that might have nuances, indicating a need for further clarification?

**III. Assessing Information: Validity and Quality (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Bring the class back together for a brief whole-group discussion.
*   **Teacher:** "Now that you've clarified and organized the arguments, let's look at where these arguments come from. Each perspective on your handout has a source listed. Let's discuss these sources."

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Look at the sources for each perspective. For example, one might be 'Dr. Anya Sharma, Educational Researcher,' another 'Parent Blog Post,' and another 'Local News Report.' In your pairs, discuss: Which of these sources seems most reliable or trustworthy? Why? Which might have a particular bias or limited perspective? Why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen to student discussions. Can they differentiate between a research study and a blog post? Do they articulate reasons for why one source might be more valid than another (e.g., "A researcher uses data," "A parent might only speak from their own experience")?

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, thinking about the *information itself*, not just the source: Which arguments or pieces of evidence do you think are the strongest or most convincing for either side? Why? Which ones might be less impactful or lack depth, even if they come from a seemingly valid source?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe which arguments students identify as "strongest" and their justifications. Do they prioritize arguments backed by data or expert opinion over anecdotal evidence? Do they recognize when information is too general or lacks specific supporting details?

**IV. Weighing Choices & Applying Reasoning (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will now prepare to form their own reasoned opinion.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Before you decide on your own stance, I want each pair to briefly discuss *both* sides of the argument, even if you already have a strong opinion. Imagine you are presenting to a school board. What are the strongest 'pros' for homework? What are the strongest 'cons'? Make sure you can articulate both sides fairly, considering the potential trade-offs of each choice."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen. Do students genuinely engage with both sides, or do they quickly dismiss the opposing view? Can they articulate the trade-offs involved in having or not having homework, considering different impacts on students, teachers, and families?

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, based on all the information you've clarified, organized, and assessed, and after weighing the pros and cons, I want each of you individually to write a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) stating your position on whether schools should have homework. You must support your position with at least two pieces of evidence or arguments from our discussion today. Make sure your reasoning clearly connects your evidence to your conclusion."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and review the paragraphs. Look for clear claims, relevant evidence, and logical connections between the evidence and the student's conclusion. Are there any logical fallacies or unsupported claims? Is the reasoning coherent and easy to follow?

**V. Reflection & Conclusion (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Distribute Exit Ticket slips.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, please answer these two questions:
        1.  Did your initial opinion about homework change or become stronger after today's lesson? Explain why or why not, referencing specific information or discussions.
        2.  What part of our critical thinking process today (clarifying, organizing, assessing sources, weighing pros/cons) helped you the most in forming your final opinion, and why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and read exit tickets. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness. Do students acknowledge shifts in their thinking or a deeper understanding? Can they identify specific strategies that aided their critical thinking process and explain *how* it helped them?

*   **Teacher:** "Thank you for engaging in such thoughtful discussion today. Critical thinking isn't just about having an opinion, but about understanding different perspectives, evaluating information, and forming well-reasoned conclusions. Keep practicing these skills in all your subjects!"

### Lesson Outline

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Briefly introduce the topic: “Today we will explore whether schools should have homework.”  
- Explain that we will practice critical thinking skills to understand different viewpoints and make a reasoned decision.

---

**2. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Present a short, clear definition of “homework” and its common purposes (e.g., practice, preparation, extension).  
- Provide two brief student quotes or statements about homework (one positive, one negative).  
- Ask students to work in pairs to clarify what each statement means and identify any unclear terms or ideas.  
*Assessment:*  
- Observe students’ ability to ask clarifying questions or restate ideas in their own words.  
- Listen for precise explanations that deepen understanding rather than vague or confusing comments.

---

**3. Organizing Information (CRI1b) – 7 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Distribute a graphic organizer with two columns labeled “Pros of Homework” and “Cons of Homework.”  
- In small groups, students list reasons supporting and opposing homework based on the initial statements and their own ideas.  
*Assessment:*  
- Check if students arrange their points logically under each column.  
- Look for consistency and coherence in how they group related ideas.

---

**4. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Provide students with two short excerpts from different sources: one from a teacher’s blog and one from a research article on homework effects.  
- Guide students to identify the source of each excerpt and discuss which might be more reliable and why.  
*Assessment:*  
- Listen for students’ ability to recognize source types and evaluate credibility.  
- Note if they question the authority or potential bias of each source.

---

**5. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b) – 7 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Present a third excerpt that contains some questionable or exaggerated claims about homework.  
- Ask students to compare this with the previous excerpts and decide which information is higher quality and more trustworthy.  
*Assessment:*  
- Observe students’ judgment in selecting authoritative, relevant information.  
- Assess their ability to explain why some information is less reliable or relevant.

---

**6. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Facilitate a class discussion where students systematically evaluate the benefits and drawbacks of homework, considering short-term and long-term effects on learning, stress, and time management.  
- Encourage students to consider multiple perspectives, including those of students, teachers, and parents.  
*Assessment:*  
- Note if students consider trade-offs and implications beyond surface-level points.  
- Look for balanced analysis rather than one-sided arguments.

---

**7. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a) – 7 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Ask students to write a short paragraph stating their position on homework (for or against) and to support it with clear reasons based on the discussion and evidence.  
*Assessment:*  
- Evaluate the clarity and logic of their reasoning.  
- Check if they anticipate complexities or counterarguments in their justification.

---

**8. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a) – 5 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Have students complete a brief reflection prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you understand the issue better?”  
*Assessment:*  
- Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing assumptions, adjusting opinions, or identifying strategies that helped their thinking.
