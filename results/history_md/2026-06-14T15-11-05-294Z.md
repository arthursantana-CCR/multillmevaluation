## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with mixed scenarios, exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $3 per visit."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Record student reasoning on the board without confirming answers yet.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate what makes a relationship "proportional." 
- *Assessment:* Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "passes through origin"). Note whether students clarify ideas precisely (Level 2) or only identify surface features (Level 1).

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: *y = kx*, where k is the constant of proportionality. Demonstrate four representations using Scenario A:

| Smoothies | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |

- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 3 times the number of smoothies."

Contrast with Scenario B (y = 3x + 20) — graph does NOT pass through origin.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to arrange the same relationship across multiple representations in a logically connected way.
- *Assessment:* During guided notes, check whether students structure their notes so representations connect (Level 2) versus listing them in isolation (Level 1).

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Gallery (12 minutes)
Post 5 stations around the room, each showing a different representation (table, graph, equation, diagram of mixing paint, verbal description). Students rotate in pairs, deciding if each is proportional and citing evidence.

Sample station: A table showing hours worked (1, 2, 4, 5) and pay ($12, $24, $50, $60). Is k constant?

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must verify claims by cross-checking the ratio across multiple data points, not just one.
- *Assessment:* Review their station recording sheets. Did they check multiple ratios (Level 2) or only one (Level 1)? Did they identify the non-proportional pay table?

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some stations contain "almost proportional" data designed to look correct at a glance.
- *Assessment:* Students who select only the strongest evidence (e.g., test ALL ratios, check origin) demonstrate Level 2. Those who accept surface patterns demonstrate Level 1.

### 4. Collaborative Problem — Decision-Making Task (12 minutes)
**Problem:** "Maya needs to buy juice for a class party. Brand X sells 2 liters for $5. Brand Y sells 5 liters for $12. Brand Z sells 8 liters for $19. Which is the best deal? Justify with proportional reasoning."

Students work in groups of 3 to:
1. Find unit rate (constant of proportionality) for each brand.
2. Compare options.
3. Consider: *Does cheapest always mean best? What if Maya only needs 3 liters?*

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must compare three options and consider context (quantity needed, waste, cost).
- *Assessment:* Group write-ups. Level 2 students discuss trade-offs (e.g., "Brand Z has the lowest unit rate but we'd waste juice"). Level 1 students simply pick the lowest price.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must use proportional reasoning (not estimation) to justify their choice.
- *Assessment:* Check whether justifications use mathematical evidence (unit rates, equations) coherently (Level 2) versus vague claims like "it looks cheaper" (Level 1).

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (11 minutes)

**Whole-class debrief (5 min):** Groups share answers. Highlight differing strategies. Ask: *"Did anyone change their mind during the task? What convinced you?"*

**Exit Ticket (6 min):**
1. Create a table, equation, and graph for: "A car travels 60 miles per hour."
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"What's one mistake you almost made today, and how did you catch it? What strategy will you use next time when checking if a relationship is proportional?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking):** The reflection prompt requires metacognition about errors and strategy adjustment.
- *Assessment:* Read exit tickets. Level 2 responses identify a specific reasoning shift (e.g., "I assumed any straight line was proportional, but now I check for the origin"). Level 1 responses mention assumptions without depth ("I was confused but figured it out"). Level 0 responses skip the reflection.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables; sentence starters for reflection.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "tricks" classmates.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, distances). Represent it in all four forms and write a sentence explaining how you verified it is proportional.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Many Faces of Proportionality
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to recognize, represent, and translate proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, using critical thinking to evaluate the validity and utility of each representation.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Introduction (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson begins with a messy, real-world verbal description projected on the board: *"At the local market, I bought 3 apples for $1.50. Later, my friend bought 5 apples for $2.50. Another person paid $4.00 for 8 apples."* Students are asked to translate this wordy paragraph into a clear mathematical format. 

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students extract the relevant mathematical data from the paragraph, filtering out unnecessary words, and precisely define the two variables (Cost and Number of Apples) to find the unit rate ($0.50 per apple).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and observe student whiteboards, assessing whether students have precisely clarified the unit rate and defined the variables without introducing confusion.
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students take their clarified data and arrange it into a logical, sequential T-table (x = apples, y = cost), ensuring the x-values increase in a basic, consistent structure.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the students' tables to ensure the information is arranged sequentially to strengthen the logical flow, rather than presenting scattered or randomized data points.

### **Part 2: Guided Practice – Cross-Checking Representations (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher introduces a new scenario: A car traveling at a constant speed. The teacher provides three "sources" of information for this scenario: a data table, a coordinate graph, and an equation ($y = 60x$). The teacher presents a fictional student's claim: *"This car travels 150 miles in 2 hours."*

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must act as "fact-checkers." They cannot just guess; they must rigorously cross-check the fictional student's claim by testing it against all three mathematical sources (the table, the graph, and the equation) to prove it is invalid (the car actually travels 120 miles in 2 hours).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess student responses during a whole-class share-out, listening for students who explicitly reference multiple sources (e.g., "I checked the graph at x=2, and I also plugged 2 into the equation $y=60x$") to validate their conclusion.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity:** The teacher asks, *"If I want to know exactly how far the car travels in 34.5 hours, which source of information is the best to use and why?"* Students must demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting the equation over the graph or table.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess this by having students vote with their fingers (1 for table, 2 for graph, 3 for equation) and calling on students to justify that the equation is the most authoritative, high-quality tool for precise, large-scale calculations, whereas extending a hand-drawn graph could introduce inaccuracies.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Group Task – The Trade-Offs of Proportionality (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups. Each group is given a set of cards featuring different proportional and non-proportional relationships (some with flat fees, like a $5 taxi base fare plus $2 per mile). Groups must sort them into "Proportional" and "Not Proportional" and then choose one proportional relationship to represent in all four ways (verbal, table, graph, equation).

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Groups complete a "Trade-Off Matrix" graphic organizer. They must systematically evaluate the pros and cons of using a graph versus an equation to represent their proportional relationship to a specific audience (e.g., a visual presentation vs. a computer program). 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect the matrices to assess if students systematically evaluated trade-offs (e.g., noting that graphs provide instant visual trends but lack exact precision for decimals, while equations offer precision but require calculation).
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity:** When sorting the taxi cab scenario ($y = 2x + 5$), groups must decide if it is proportional. They must apply logical reasoning to anticipate complexities, specifically noting that while the rate of change is constant, the presence of a starting fee means it does not pass through the origin (0,0).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group deliberations and read their final sorting rationales to ensure they use sound, sophisticated mathematical reasoning (constant ratio $y/x$, passing through the origin) rather than faulty logic (e.g., "it's a straight line, so it must be proportional").

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students independently complete a "Thinking About My Thinking" exit ticket before leaving the classroom. 

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students respond to the prompt: *"How has your understanding of what makes a relationship 'proportional' changed since the beginning of class? What specific strategy will you use next time you are asked to prove a relationship is proportional?"*
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. High-level responses will demonstrate deep reflection and strategic adjustment (e.g., "I used to think any pattern was proportional. Now I realize I must strategically check if the $y/x$ ratio is identical across the table and if the graph starts exactly at zero.").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Representing Proportional Relationships

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade Math  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships in multiple ways.

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio/unit rate and can be represented by an equation of the form **y = kx**.
3. Compare different representations and justify whether they show proportional relationships.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- “Snack Mix” task handout
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display the following table:

| Bags of Snack Mix | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $12 |

Ask students:

- “What do you notice?”
- “What do you wonder?”
- “How could we describe the relationship between bags and cost?”

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite 2–3 students to share aloud.

Guide students toward recognizing that each bag costs $3 and the cost increases at a constant rate.

**Teacher move:** Emphasize precise language: “Instead of saying ‘it goes up by 3,’ we can say the cost is $3 per bag, which is the unit rate.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when two quantities have a constant ratio. In a proportional relationship:

- The table has the same ratio between corresponding values.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin, point **(0, 0)**.
- The equation can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- The verbal description includes a constant “per 1” rate.

Use the snack mix example:

- Table: Cost ÷ bags = 3 each time.
- Equation: **y = 3x**
- Graph: Points are (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12), and the line passes through (0, 0).
- Diagram: 1 bag → $3, 2 bags → $6, etc.
- Verbal description: “Each bag costs $3.”

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $25 |

Ask: “Is this proportional? Why or why not?”

Students should identify that the ratio is not constant because 8/1 = 8, 16/2 = 8, but 25/3 is not 8.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A cyclist rides at a constant speed of 12 miles per hour.”

As a class, create five representations:

1. **Verbal:** The cyclist travels 12 miles every hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |
| 4 | 48 |

3. **Equation:** **y = 12x**
4. **Graph:** Plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.
5. **Diagram:** Use arrows or ratio boxes: 1 hour → 12 miles, 2 hours → 24 miles.

Ask students:

- “Where do we see the unit rate?”
- “How does the table connect to the graph?”
- “How does the equation connect to the verbal description?”

Students discuss briefly with partners before answering.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Is It Proportional? — 18 minutes

Distribute a handout with four scenarios. Students work in pairs to decide whether each relationship is proportional and represent proportional ones in at least two different ways.

#### Scenario A
A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cups of oats.

#### Scenario B
A taxi charges $4 to start the ride plus $2 per mile.

#### Scenario C
A printer prints 15 pages every 3 minutes.

#### Scenario D
The table shows the relationship between tickets and cost:

| Tickets | Cost |
|---|---|
| 2 | $10 |
| 4 | $20 |
| 6 | $30 |
| 8 | $40 |

Students must:

1. Decide: proportional or not proportional?
2. Explain their reasoning.
3. If proportional, identify the constant of proportionality.
4. Represent the relationship using at least two forms: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description.
5. Compare two representation choices and decide which one best shows the proportional relationship.

Circulate and ask probing questions:

- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “Does the relationship start at zero?”
- “Can you write this as y = kx?”
- “Which representation makes the constant of proportionality easiest to see?”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 7 minutes

Review each scenario.

Expected conclusions:

- **A:** Proportional. Ratio is 2 cups flour to 5 cups oats, or flour = 2/5 oats depending on variables.
- **B:** Not proportional. The $4 starting fee means the graph does not pass through the origin and the equation would be y = 2x + 4, not y = kx.
- **C:** Proportional. 15 pages every 3 minutes means 5 pages per minute, so y = 5x.
- **D:** Proportional. Cost per ticket is $5, so y = 5x.

Highlight that proportional relationships always have a constant multiplicative relationship.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 3 minutes

Students answer independently:

A water faucet fills 6 gallons every 2 minutes.

1. Is the relationship between minutes and gallons proportional?
2. What is the constant of proportionality?
3. Write an equation.
4. Name one other way you could represent the relationship.

Expected answer: Yes; 3 gallons per minute; y = 3x; table, graph, diagram, or verbal description.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display the following table:

| Bags of Snack Mix | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $12 |

Ask students:

- “What do you notice?”
- “What do you wonder?”
- “How could we describe the relationship between bags and cost?”

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite 2–3 students to share aloud.

Guide students toward recognizing that each bag costs $3 and the cost increases at a constant rate.

**Teacher move:** Emphasize precise language: “Instead of saying ‘it goes up by 3,’ we can say the cost is $3 per bag, which is the unit rate.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when two quantities have a constant ratio. In a proportional relationship:

- The table has the same ratio between corresponding values.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin, point **(0, 0)**.
- The equation can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.
- The verbal description includes a constant “per 1” rate.

Use the snack mix example:

- Table: Cost ÷ bags = 3 each time.
- Equation: **y = 3x**
- Graph: Points are (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12), and the line passes through (0, 0).
- Diagram: 1 bag → $3, 2 bags → $6, etc.
- Verbal description: “Each bag costs $3.”

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $25 |

Ask: “Is this proportional? Why or why not?”

Students should identify that the ratio is not constant because 8/1 = 8, 16/2 = 8, but 25/3 is not 8.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A cyclist rides at a constant speed of 12 miles per hour.”

As a class, create five representations:

1. **Verbal:** The cyclist travels 12 miles every hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |
| 4 | 48 |

3. **Equation:** **y = 12x**
4. **Graph:** Plot the points and draw a straight line through the origin.
5. **Diagram:** Use arrows or ratio boxes: 1 hour → 12 miles, 2 hours → 24 miles.

Ask students:

- “Where do we see the unit rate?”
- “How does the table connect to the graph?”
- “How does the equation connect to the verbal description?”

Students discuss briefly with partners before answering.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Is It Proportional? — 18 minutes

Distribute a handout with four scenarios. Students work in pairs to decide whether each relationship is proportional and represent proportional ones in at least two different ways.

#### Scenario A
A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cups of oats.

#### Scenario B
A taxi charges $4 to start the ride plus $2 per mile.

#### Scenario C
A printer prints 15 pages every 3 minutes.

#### Scenario D
The table shows the relationship between tickets and cost:

| Tickets | Cost |
|---|---|
| 2 | $10 |
| 4 | $20 |
| 6 | $30 |
| 8 | $40 |

Students must:

1. Decide: proportional or not proportional?
2. Explain their reasoning.
3. If proportional, identify the constant of proportionality.
4. Represent the relationship using at least two forms: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description.
5. Compare two representation choices and decide which one best shows the proportional relationship.

Circulate and ask probing questions:

- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “Does the relationship start at zero?”
- “Can you write this as y = kx?”
- “Which representation makes the constant of proportionality easiest to see?”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 7 minutes

Review each scenario.

Expected conclusions:

- **A:** Proportional. Ratio is 2 cups flour to 5 cups oats, or flour = 2/5 oats depending on variables.
- **B:** Not proportional. The $4 starting fee means the graph does not pass through the origin and the equation would be y = 2x + 4, not y = kx.
- **C:** Proportional. 15 pages every 3 minutes means 5 pages per minute, so y = 5x.
- **D:** Proportional. Cost per ticket is $5, so y = 5x.

Highlight that proportional relationships always have a constant multiplicative relationship.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 3 minutes

Students answer independently:

A water faucet fills 6 gallons every 2 minutes.

1. Is the relationship between minutes and gallons proportional?
2. What is the constant of proportionality?
3. Write an equation.
4. Name one other way you could represent the relationship.

Expected answer: Yes; 3 gallons per minute; y = 3x; table, graph, diagram, or verbal description.

## Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:
1. Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and can be written as **y = kx**.
3. Compare representations and justify whether they show proportionality.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Is It Proportional?" handout, Trade-Off Matrix organizer, exit ticket.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $3 per visit."

Ask: *"Which is proportional? How do you know?"* Partners discuss (2 min), then share. Record reasoning without confirming answers.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students articulate what makes a relationship "proportional."
- *Assessment:* Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "passes through origin," "unit rate"). Level 2 students define variables and rate precisely; Level 1 students name surface features only.

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships as **y = kx**. Using Scenario A, model four representations:

| Smoothies | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $6 |
| 3 | $9 |

- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Ratio boxes — 1 smoothie → $3, 2 → $6.
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 3 times the number of smoothies."

Contrast with Scenario B (y = 3x + 20) — does NOT pass through origin. Then show a non-example table (1→$8, 2→$16, 3→$25) and ask why it fails.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model arranging the same relationship across representations with logical connections.
- *Assessment:* Check guided notes — Level 2 students show how the table feeds the equation and graph; Level 1 students list representations in isolation.

### 3. Cross-Checking Gallery — "Fact-Checkers" (12 minutes)
Post 5 stations (table, graph, equation, paint-mixing diagram, verbal). Pairs rotate, deciding if each is proportional and citing evidence. One station includes a fictional student claim: *"This car travels 150 miles in 2 hours"* with a table, graph, and y = 60x. Students must cross-check all three sources to verify or refute.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students verify by cross-checking ratios across multiple data points and multiple representations.
- *Assessment:* Recording sheets — Level 2 students reference multiple sources ("I checked the graph at x=2 and plugged 2 into y=60x"); Level 1 students test only one ratio.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Ask: *"To find distance at 34.5 hours, which representation is best?"* Students vote (1=table, 2=graph, 3=equation) and justify.
- *Assessment:* Level 2 students argue the equation is most authoritative for precise/large values; Level 1 students pick based on familiarity.

### 4. Collaborative Decision Task (13 minutes)
**Problem:** "Maya needs juice for a party. Brand X: 2L for $5. Brand Y: 5L for $12. Brand Z: 8L for $19. Which is the best deal?"

Groups of 3:
1. Find unit rate (k) for each brand.
2. Complete a **Trade-Off Matrix** comparing options.
3. Consider: *Does cheapest mean best if Maya only needs 3 liters? What if leftover juice is wasted?*

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups systematically evaluate trade-offs (cost, quantity needed, waste).
- *Assessment:* Matrices — Level 2 groups note trade-offs ("Z has the lowest unit rate but wastes juice"); Level 1 groups simply pick the lowest price.

**Targets CRI4a (Sound reasoning):** Justifications must use proportional reasoning, not estimation. Students also confront a taxi-style trap card (y = 2x + 5) — constant rate but not proportional.
- *Assessment:* Level 2 students cite k = y/x AND origin condition ("constant rate isn't enough — must pass through (0,0)"); Level 1 students rely on "it looks like a line."

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (12 minutes)

**Debrief (5 min):** Groups share. Ask: *"Did anyone change their mind? What convinced you?"*

**Exit Ticket (7 min):**
1. Create a table, equation, and graph for: "A car travels 60 miles per hour."
2. **Reflection:** *"How has your understanding of 'proportional' changed today? What's one mistake you almost made, and what strategy will you use next time to check proportionality?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Prompt forces metacognition about errors and strategy adjustment.
- *Assessment:* Level 2 responses identify a specific reasoning shift ("I assumed any straight line was proportional; now I strategically check that y/x is constant AND the graph hits (0,0)"). Level 1 responses mention assumptions vaguely. Level 0 responses skip reflection.

- **Extension:** Advanced students design a non-proportional scenario meant to "trick" classmates.
