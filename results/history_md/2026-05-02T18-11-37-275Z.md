## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Framework:** CCR Critical Thinking

---

## Lesson Overview

Students will investigate the debate around homework in schools, practicing critical thinking by clarifying arguments, organizing evidence, evaluating sources, weighing opposing viewpoints, applying logical reasoning, and reflecting on how their thinking evolved throughout the lesson.

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Clarify and organize arguments for and against homework
- Evaluate the reliability of sources supporting each side
- Weigh trade-offs and apply sound reasoning to form a defensible position
- Reflect on how their thinking shifted during the lesson

**Materials:** Printed source cards (4 sources of varying quality), T-chart worksheets, sticky notes, debate position cards, reflection journals

---

## Lesson Structure

### Warm-Up — *Do Now* (5 minutes)

Students respond silently in their journals to the prompt:
*"Do you think homework helps you learn? Write your honest opinion and one reason behind it."*

This activates prior beliefs and establishes a baseline for the metacognitive reflection that closes the lesson.

---

### Phase 1: Clarifying the Question — *CRI1a* (10 minutes)

**Activity:** The teacher displays three versions of the debate question on the board:
- "Is homework good?"
- "Should all students have homework every night?"
- "Should schools assign homework as a regular academic practice?"

In pairs, students discuss: *What is the difference between these questions? Which one is fairest to debate and why?*

The class agrees on a precise working question before moving forward.

**Targeting CRI1a — Clarifying Information:**
This moment directly targets the skill of clarifying ideas precisely. Students must recognize that vague questions produce vague thinking. By selecting and refining the question together, they practice moving from Level 1 (partial clarity) toward Level 2 (precise clarification that supports deeper understanding).

**Assessment:** Listen for students who can articulate *why* one version of the question is more useful. Ask: *"What assumptions does each version make?"*

---

### Phase 2: Organizing the Evidence — *CRI1b* (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Each student receives four source cards summarizing real research findings — two suggesting homework benefits learning, two questioning its effectiveness. Students must arrange these on a T-chart ("Supports Homework" / "Challenges Homework") and then rank each source from most to least convincing within its column.

**Targeting CRI1b — Organizing Information:**
Students must move beyond simply categorizing information and instead arrange it to build logical, coherent thinking. Ranking within columns pushes them toward arranging information to strengthen logical flow (Level 2), rather than a basic binary sort (Level 1).

**Assessment:** Review T-charts for whether students can explain their ranking order, not just their placement. Ask: *"Why did you put this piece of evidence first?"*

---

### Phase 3: Evaluating Sources — *CRI2a & CRI2b* (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Students examine the four source cards more carefully. Each card includes author credentials, publication type, date, and sample size. Using a simple "SIFT" checklist (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims), students label each source as Strong, Moderate, or Weak and explain why.

**Targeting CRI2a — Assessing Validity & CRI2b — Assessing Quality:**
Students cross-check sources rather than accepting them at face value. One source card is intentionally a blog post with no cited data — students should flag this. Another is a peer-reviewed study from 2022. Distinguishing between them targets both the validity and quality dimensions simultaneously.

**Assessment:** Cold-call two or three students to justify a source rating. Watch for whether students use specific criteria (sample size, credentials, publication type) or rely on gut feeling alone.

---

### Phase 4: Structured Debate — *CRI3a & CRI4a* (15 minutes)

**Activity:** Students are randomly assigned a position card (Pro-Homework or Anti-Homework), regardless of personal opinion. In groups of four (two per side), they conduct a structured micro-debate using only evidence from their vetted sources. Each speaker gets 90 seconds, followed by a 2-minute rebuttal round.

**Targeting CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons:**
By arguing an assigned position, students must genuinely engage with trade-offs rather than simply defending what they already believe. They are required to acknowledge one valid point from the opposing side during their rebuttal, pushing toward systematic evaluation of trade-offs (Level 2).

**Targeting CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning:**
Students must connect evidence to claims logically. The teacher listens for unsupported generalizations (e.g., *"Everyone hates homework"*) and prompts students to revise their reasoning: *"What evidence supports that claim?"*

**Assessment:** Use a simple 1–3 observation checklist noting whether students used evidence, acknowledged counterarguments, and avoided logical fallacies.

---

### Phase 5: Reflection — *CRI5a* (5 minutes)

**Activity:** Students return to their opening journal entry and respond to three prompts:
1. *Has your opinion changed? Why or why not?*
2. *What was the strongest argument on the side you disagree with?*
3. *What would you need to know to feel more confident in your position?*

**Targeting CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes:**
This closing metacognitive routine asks students to compare their beginning thinking with their end thinking — a hallmark of deep metacognitive awareness. Students who simply restate their original opinion without engaging with what challenged them remain at Level 1. Students who name a specific moment that shifted their thinking, or acknowledge a gap in their reasoning, demonstrate Level 2 metacognitive depth.

**Assessment:** Collect journals. Look specifically for evidence that students engaged with contrary evidence, not just confirmation of their original view.

---

## Closing (Optional — 2 minutes)

Ask the class: *"Did the quality of the sources change which side seemed stronger? Should it?"* Leave this question open — it sets up future lessons on media literacy and epistemic humility.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Debating the role of homework in schools

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify key arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support different perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of various information sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of different approaches to homework.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form a preliminary stance on the topic.
*   Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the discussion.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (short articles/summaries presenting arguments for and against homework, with varied source types – e.g., excerpt from an educational research summary, a school district's rationale, a student blog post, a parent's opinion piece).
*   Handout 2: T-chart graphic organizer
*   Exit Tickets
*   Markers/pens

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, everyone! Today, we're going to tackle a topic that affects all of us: homework. How many of you love homework? How many of you wish it didn't exist? (Allow for a quick show of hands and brief, informal comments). It's clear there are strong feelings on both sides. Today, we're going to explore this question: 'Should schools have homework?' and use our critical thinking skills to understand the different perspectives."

**II. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) & Initial Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1a: Clarifying information):** Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
    *   **Teacher:** "You'll find several short texts here, each presenting different viewpoints or facts about homework. Your first task is to read through these texts individually. As you read, I want you to do two things:
        1.  **Highlight** the main arguments or key pieces of information presented in each text.
        2.  **Circle any words or phrases** you don't understand, and try to define them using context or by asking a classmate."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1a):** Circulate around the room. Observe students' highlighting – are they identifying the core claims? Listen to their discussions about unfamiliar vocabulary. Are they attempting to clarify ideas precisely? I will look for evidence that students are accurately identifying and defining key terms and arguments, rather than just random words or superficial details. For example, if a student highlights "Homework reinforces classroom learning" and can explain what "reinforces" means in this context, they are clarifying information precisely.

**III. Organizing Information (CRI1b) & Assessing Validity/Quality (CRI2a, CRI2b) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1b: Organizing information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've read and highlighted, let's organize this information. In your small groups (3-4 students), use Handout 2, the T-chart, to list the 'Pros of Homework' on one side and the 'Cons of Homework' on the other. Make sure to draw your points directly from the texts."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1b):** Review students' T-charts. Are the points clearly categorized? Is the information presented in a structured and consistent manner? For example, are all "pros" on one side and "cons" on the other, without mixing?
*   **Activity (CRI2a: Assessing validity of information & CRI2b: Assessing quality of information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "As you're filling out your T-chart, I want you to also think critically about *where* this information is coming from. Look at the source listed for each text on Handout 1. Discuss in your groups:
        *   'How reliable do you think this source is?' (CRI2a)
        *   'Does this source provide high-quality information, or is it more opinion-based?' (CRI2b)
        *   'Which pieces of evidence from the texts do you think are the *strongest* to support either side, and why?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI2a & CRI2b):** Listen to group discussions. Do students question the authority of a blog post versus a research summary? Do they identify potential biases? Are they able to articulate *why* certain information is more credible or relevant than others? I will look for students who can explain, for instance, "The study from the Department of Education is high-quality because it's based on research data, not just one person's feelings," demonstrating sophisticated judgment.

**IV. Weighing Pros & Cons & Applying Reasoning (CRI3a, CRI4a) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've organized and evaluated the information, let's delve deeper. In your groups, discuss the following:
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools completely eliminated homework?'
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools assigned a lot more homework?'
        *   'Consider the perspectives of different stakeholders: students, teachers, parents. How might each group weigh these pros and cons differently?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI3a):** Circulate and listen to group discussions. Are students moving beyond simple listing to actively compare and evaluate the trade-offs? Do they consider the broader impact and different viewpoints? I'll note if students are discussing consequences like "If there's no homework, students might forget what they learned faster" or "Too much homework could lead to burnout and less family time," showing they are systematically evaluating implications.
*   **Activity (CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):**
    *   **Teacher:** "After your group discussion, I want each of you to individually write a short paragraph (3-4 sentences) stating your preliminary stance on the question: 'Should schools have homework?' You can say 'yes,' 'no,' or 'it depends/modified approach.' Crucially, you must provide at least two clear reasons to support your stance, drawing directly from the information we've discussed and evaluated today."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI4a):** Collect and read the individual paragraphs. Is the reasoning logical and coherent? Do their conclusions align with the evidence they present? Are there any unsupported claims or faulty logic? I will look for clear connections between their stance and the evidence provided, e.g., "I believe schools should have *some* homework because it helps reinforce learning (Pro 1) and teaches responsibility (Pro 2), as mentioned in the research summary."

**V. Reflecting on Thinking Processes (CRI5a) & Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes):**
    *   **Teacher:** "To conclude our lesson, I want you to reflect on your own thinking today. Please complete the exit ticket."
    *   **Exit Ticket Prompt:**
        *   "My initial thought on homework before this lesson was..."
        *   "After today's lesson, my thinking has [changed / stayed the same / become more nuanced] because..."
        *   "One thing I found challenging about evaluating the information or forming my opinion was..., and I tried to overcome it by..."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI5a):** Collect and review exit tickets. Do students demonstrate metacognitive awareness? Can they articulate how their understanding evolved or deepened? Do they identify specific challenges in their thinking and describe strategies they used to address them? I will look for responses that show genuine self-awareness, such as "Initially, I just hated homework, but after seeing the arguments about responsibility, I now think a little bit is okay. It was hard to let go of my bias, but I tried to focus only on the facts from the research."

### Lesson Outline and Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration

---

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Begin with a class brainstorming session. Ask students: “What do you know about homework? What are some reasons for and against it?” Record their ideas on the board.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Guide students to clarify their ideas by asking probing questions: “Can you explain what you mean by that?” “How does that affect students or teachers?”  
- **Assessment:** Observe students’ ability to express ideas clearly and ask clarifying questions to peers. Look for precise explanations rather than vague statements. For example, a student might say, “Homework helps us practice what we learn in class,” which shows clarity.  

---

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Provide students with a graphic organizer (e.g., a T-chart) to categorize arguments for and against homework based on the brainstormed ideas and additional short texts provided by the teacher.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Model how to group similar ideas and arrange them logically (e.g., benefits on one side, drawbacks on the other).  
- **Assessment:** Review students’ completed organizers to see if information is arranged coherently and logically, supporting a clear flow of ideas. Check for consistent categorization and avoidance of scattered or contradictory points.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Present students with two short excerpts: one from a reputable educational research article and one from an opinion blog with questionable credibility. In pairs, students discuss which source seems more reliable and why.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Teach students to look for author credentials, publication source, and evidence cited. Encourage them to cross-check facts if possible.  
- **Assessment:** Listen to student discussions and review their written justifications for source reliability. Look for evidence of critical evaluation rather than accepting information at face value.  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Have students identify which arguments in their T-chart are supported by strong evidence and which are based on opinions or weak evidence.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Explain criteria for high-quality information (e.g., data-backed, expert opinions, recent studies).  
- **Assessment:** Check students’ ability to distinguish between high-quality and less reliable information, noting their reasoning in annotations or brief written reflections.  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** In small groups, students discuss the trade-offs of having homework versus no homework, considering short-term and long-term effects on learning, stress, family time, and responsibility. Groups then share their conclusions with the class.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Prompt students to consider multiple perspectives and consequences, asking questions like, “What might happen if homework is completely removed?” or “How could homework be improved to reduce stress?”  
- **Assessment:** Evaluate group discussions and presentations for depth of analysis, noting if students systematically consider multiple factors and implications rather than superficial comparisons.  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Individually, students write a short paragraph stating their position on homework and supporting it with reasons and evidence from the lesson.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Encourage students to anticipate counterarguments and address them logically.  
- **Assessment:** Review paragraphs for clear, logical reasoning that connects evidence to conclusions, and for anticipation of complexities rather than oversimplified claims.  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** End with a metacognitive reflection prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you understand the issue better?” Students write brief responses.  
- **Teacher’s Role:** Model reflection by sharing your own thought process and how new information influenced your views.  
- **Assessment:** Assess students’ reflections for evidence of self-awareness and strategic adjustment of their thinking, such as recognizing initial assumptions and how they revised them.
