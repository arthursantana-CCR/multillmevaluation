## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate and justify their reasoning.

---

## Materials
- Graph paper or printed coordinate grids
- Markers/colored pencils
- "Scenario Cards" (teacher-prepared)
- Mini whiteboards or scratch paper
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Hook – Launch Activity (8 minutes)

**Activity:** Display two scenarios on the board:
- *Scenario A:* A car travels 60 miles every hour.
- *Scenario B:* A runner completes 3 miles in the first hour, 5 miles in the second hour, and 6 miles in the third hour.

Ask: *"Are either of these proportional? How do you know?"*

Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**CRI1a – Clarifying Information**
The teacher explicitly defines "proportional relationship" — a relationship where two quantities increase at a **constant rate**, their ratio remains the same, and the graph passes through the origin. Write the definition on the board with precise language. Contrast it directly with non-proportional relationships using Scenario B.

*Assessment:* Listen for students who can articulate the difference. Ask targeted questions: *"What exactly makes Scenario A proportional and Scenario B not?"* Clarity of verbal explanation is the observable evidence.

---

### Direct Instruction – Building Representations (12 minutes)

Using Scenario A (60 mph), model four representations together as a class:

1. **Table** — Fill in distance at 1, 2, 3, 4 hours
2. **Graph** — Plot points; identify that it passes through the origin
3. **Equation** — Derive *d = 60h*; identify the constant of proportionality *k = 60*
4. **Verbal Description** — "For every 1 hour, the distance increases by exactly 60 miles."

**CRI1b – Organizing Information**
Structure the board deliberately: place all four representations side by side with labeled headers. Emphasize to students how each representation tells the **same story** in a different format, and that organizing information across multiple formats deepens understanding.

*Assessment:* Observe whether students' notes mirror the organized structure. Ask: *"Which representation makes it easiest to spot the pattern? Why?"* Students who articulate a logical reason demonstrate organized thinking.

---

### Guided Practice – Small Group Investigation (15 minutes)

Each group receives a different Scenario Card (e.g., cost per pound of apples, pages read per day, miles per gallon). Groups must:
1. Build a table with at least 5 values
2. Create a graph
3. Write an equation
4. Write a verbal description

Then groups receive a **second card** — one proportional, one non-proportional — and must decide which is which and defend their choice.

**CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information**
Groups must verify their conclusion by cross-checking: Does the ratio stay constant in the table? Does the graph pass through the origin? Does the equation fit the form *y = kx*? Students must confirm using **at least two representations** before declaring a relationship proportional.

*Assessment:* Circulate and ask: *"How do you know you're right? Can you prove it a second way?"* Students who check multiple representations are demonstrating validity assessment.

**CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information**
When building their table, students must choose values that are meaningful and reveal the pattern clearly (e.g., avoid only using 0 and 1). Prompt: *"Are your chosen values giving you useful information, or are they hiding the pattern?"*

*Assessment:* Look for tables with varied, thoughtful values. Students who choose only trivial values (0, 1) are prompted to reconsider.

---

### Class Discussion – Weighing Representations (10 minutes)

Whole-class debrief: *"Which representation is most useful for identifying a proportional relationship? Which is most useful for making predictions?"*

**CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
Facilitate a structured debate. Acknowledge that tables are great for spotting constant ratios, graphs are powerful for visual confirmation, and equations are best for prediction. Students must argue for one representation while acknowledging the strengths of others.

*Assessment:* Students who say *"Tables are best because… but graphs also help because…"* are demonstrating balanced trade-off reasoning.

**CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
Ask: *"If I only gave you three data points, could you be sure the relationship is proportional? What if all three happened to align by coincidence?"* Students must reason about the **limits** of their evidence.

*Assessment:* Students who acknowledge that more data strengthens confidence — and that no finite set of points guarantees proportionality — demonstrate sophisticated reasoning.

---

### Exit Ticket – Metacognitive Reflection (10 minutes)

Students independently complete a three-part exit ticket:
1. Represent the relationship *y = 3.5x* in a table and graph.
2. Explain in two sentences how you know it is proportional.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"What was the hardest part of today's lesson for you? What strategy helped you the most, and why?"*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
The written reflection explicitly asks students to examine their own reasoning process, not just the answer. Prompts encourage metacognition: identifying confusion, naming strategies, and evaluating what worked.

*Assessment:* Responses that say *"I was confused about the graph until I checked the table first"* or *"I kept forgetting the line must go through the origin"* demonstrate genuine metacognitive awareness. Collect exit tickets to identify misconceptions for the next lesson.

---

## Closure (Verbal – 2 minutes)
Cold-call two students: *"Name one thing proportional relationships always have in common across ALL representations."* Reinforce: constant ratio, passes through origin, form *y = kx*.

**55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)**

### Lesson Objective:
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

---

### Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Graph paper
- Handouts with proportional relationship problems (tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, verbal descriptions)
- Calculators (optional)
- Exit tickets

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a brief review: “What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”  
- Show a simple example: If 1 apple costs $2, then 3 apples cost $6. Ask students: “How can we show this relationship in different ways?”  
- Write on the board: Table, Graph, Equation, Diagram, Verbal Description.

**Targeting CCR Critical Thinking: Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
- Activity: Ask students to explain in their own words what “proportional relationship” means.  
- Observation: Listen for students who clarify the concept precisely (e.g., “The ratio between quantities stays the same”) versus those who give vague or incorrect explanations.

---

**2. Guided Practice: Representing Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Present a proportional relationship scenario: “A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour. How far does it travel in 3 hours? 5 hours?”  
- Create a table together: Hours vs. Miles.  
- Plot points on a graph as a class.  
- Write the equation: y = 60x.  
- Draw a diagram showing the relationship (e.g., arrows or scaling).  
- Write a verbal description: “The distance traveled is 60 times the number of hours.”  

**Targeting CCR Critical Thinking: Organizing Information (CRI1b)**  
- Activity: Students work in pairs to organize the information from the scenario into all five representations.  
- Observation: Assess how well students arrange the data logically and consistently across formats, noting if they connect the representations clearly.

---

**3. Independent Practice: Multiple Representations (15 minutes)**  
- Hand out worksheets with different proportional scenarios (e.g., recipe ingredients, speed and time, cost and quantity).  
- Students identify and create tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions for each.  
- Encourage students to check their work by comparing the different forms to ensure consistency.

**Targeting CCR Critical Thinking: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information (CRI2a & CRI2b)**  
- Activity: Students cross-check their representations for accuracy and consistency.  
- Observation: Look for students who verify their answers by comparing multiple forms (e.g., does the graph match the table?).  
- Note students who use reasoning to confirm the quality of their information (e.g., “If the ratio changes, it’s not proportional”).

---

**4. Group Discussion: Comparing Representations (10 minutes)**  
- Groups share one example from their worksheet.  
- Discuss: “What are the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? When might one be easier to use than another?”  
- Facilitate weighing pros and cons of alternative choices in representing proportional relationships.

**Targeting CCR Critical Thinking: Weighing Pros and Cons (CRI3a)**  
- Activity: Students articulate reasons for choosing one representation over another in different contexts.  
- Observation: Evaluate the depth of their analysis—do they consider long-term usefulness, clarity, or ease of understanding?

---

**5. Reasoning and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Pose a challenge: “If a relationship is not proportional, how would the table, graph, or equation look different?”  
- Ask students to explain their reasoning.  
- Then, have students write a brief reflection on how they decided which representations to use and how their thinking changed during the lesson.

**Targeting CCR Critical Thinking: Applying Sound Reasoning and Reflecting on Thinking (CRI4a & CRI5a)**  
- Activity: Students provide reasoning for identifying proportional vs. non-proportional relationships and reflect on their learning process.  
- Observation: Assess clarity and sophistication of reasoning, and look for evidence of metacognitive awareness in reflections (e.g., “I realized I needed to check the ratio in the table to be sure”).

---

**6. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Quick problem: Given a table, graph, or verbal description, students identify if the relationship is proportional and represent it in two other forms.  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding.
