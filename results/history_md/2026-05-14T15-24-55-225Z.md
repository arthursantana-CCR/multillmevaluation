## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Explain *why* a relationship is or is not proportional using precise mathematical language
- Reflect on their own reasoning strategies and adjust when needed

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Proportional or Not?" task cards (4 scenarios each)
- Graph paper
- Student journals
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Structure

---

### Warm-Up – "Which One Doesn't Belong?" (8 minutes)
**Activity:** Display four representations on the board:
1. A table showing (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9)
2. The equation y = 3x
3. A graph passing through the origin with a constant slope
4. A verbal description: *"A taxi charges $5 plus $2 per mile"*

Ask students: *"Which one doesn't belong with the others, and why?"*

**CRI1a – Clarifying Information:**
Students must articulate *precisely* what makes a relationship proportional — constant ratio, passes through the origin, unit rate visible. Push students beyond vague answers: *"How do you know it's not proportional? What exactly is the difference?"*
**Assessment:** Listen for students who use terms like "constant rate of change," "y = kx," or "passes through the origin" versus those who describe it loosely. Ask 2–3 students to restate a peer's answer in their own words to sharpen clarity.

---

### Direct Instruction – Defining Proportionality (10 minutes)
**Activity:** Using the whiteboard, build a class anchor chart with four columns: Table | Graph | Equation | Verbal Description. Walk through one clear proportional example (cost of apples at $1.50/lb) across all four representations.

**CRI1b – Organizing Information:**
Students copy the anchor chart into their journals, then add their own labels and arrows connecting the representations (e.g., drawing an arrow from the equation *y = 1.5x* to the table column showing the ratio 1.5).
**Assessment:** Circulate and check whether student journals show logical connections between representations or isolated, disconnected notes. Students who organize with clear labels and arrows demonstrate Level 2; students with only copied text demonstrate Level 1.

---

### Guided Practice – "Is It Proportional?" Task Cards (12 minutes)
**Activity:** Pairs receive four task cards. Each card presents a different scenario (e.g., a table with an inconsistent ratio, a graph not passing through the origin). Students must decide: proportional or not — and *prove it* using at least two representations.

**CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:**
Students cross-check their conclusion by verifying the ratio in the table *and* checking the graph. Prompt: *"If your table says yes but your graph says no, which do you trust and why? How do you resolve the conflict?"*
**Assessment:** Observe whether students catch contradictions between representations and reconcile them logically. Students demonstrating Level 2 will recalculate ratios and re-examine graphs before committing to an answer.

**CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:**
Within the task cards, one scenario includes a partially completed table with a missing value. Students must decide if they have *enough* information to conclude proportionality.
**Assessment:** Listen for students who say, *"We can't be sure yet — we need more data points"* versus those who jump to conclusions. Probe: *"What would make you more confident in your answer?"*

---

### Whole-Class Discussion – Comparing Strategies (10 minutes)
**Activity:** Two groups share their reasoning for the same task card but reached different conclusions. Facilitate a structured discussion: *"Walk us through your thinking. Where did you agree? Where did you split?"*

**CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices:**
Ask the class: *"Group A used the equation to check. Group B used the graph. What are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach? When might one method fail you?"*
**Assessment:** Students who identify that graphs can be misleading at small scales, or that equations require knowing the rule already, demonstrate sophisticated trade-off thinking (Level 2).

**CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making:**
Students must choose *which* representation gives the strongest proof of proportionality and justify it logically.
**Assessment:** Look for students who acknowledge that *no single representation alone is sufficient* and that cross-checking strengthens confidence — this reflects anticipating complexity (Level 2).

---

### Independent Practice & Metacognitive Reflection (10 minutes)
**Activity:** Students independently solve one novel problem, then respond in their journal to two prompts:
1. *"What strategy did you use first, and did you change it? Why?"*
2. *"What would trip someone else up on this problem?"*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes:**
**Assessment:** Students demonstrating Level 2 will describe a moment they caught their own error or deliberately switched strategies. Students at Level 1 will mention steps taken but not *why* they made those choices. Collect journals for formative review.

---

### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
Students answer: *"Give one example of a proportional relationship and represent it TWO ways. Explain how you know it's proportional."*

---

## Closure
Reconnect to the warm-up: *"Could you now explain why the taxi problem didn't belong? What has changed in your thinking?"*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations.
4.  Determine the constant of proportionality from various representations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with tables and graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Calculators (optional, for efficiency)
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore a really important concept in math: proportional relationships. You've already worked with ratios and rates. Can anyone remind us what a ratio is? What about a unit rate?"
*   *(Briefly review ratios and unit rates, connecting them to real-world examples like miles per hour or cost per item. For instance, 'If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?')*
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll see how these ideas connect to something called a 'proportional relationship' and how we can spot them in different ways."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice: Defining Proportionality (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "A **proportional relationship** exists between two quantities when one quantity is a constant multiple of the other. This constant multiple is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by 'k'. In simpler terms, if you divide the 'y' value by the 'x' value, you always get the same number, 'k'."
*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** "Let's try to put that in our own words. Turn to your elbow partner and explain what 'constant multiple' means in the context of a proportional relationship. What are the two key things we need to look for when deciding if a relationship is proportional?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen to student discussions. I will observe if students are accurately rephrasing the definition, identifying "constant ratio" and "relationship between two quantities" as key ideas. I'll ask a few pairs to share their rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in their language.
*   **Teacher:** "We can see proportional relationships in different forms. Let's start with tables."
    *   *Display a table on the board:*
        | x (hours) | y (miles) |
        | :-------- | :-------- |
        | 1         | 50        |
        | 2         | 100       |
        | 3         | 150       |
    *   **Teacher:** "For each pair (x, y), let's calculate y/x. What do you notice?" (Students should find y/x = 50 for all pairs). "Since y/x is constant, this is a proportional relationship, and k = 50. The equation would be y = 50x."
    *   *Display a non-proportional table:*
        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
        | :---------- | :------- |
        | 1           | $1.50    |
        | 2           | $2.50    |
        | 3           | $3.50    |
    *   **Teacher:** "Now, let's check this one. What do you find for y/x?" (Students should find different ratios). "Is this proportional? Why not?" (No constant ratio).
*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships also have a special look on a graph. They always form a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0)."
    *   *Quickly sketch the graph of y=50x, showing it's a straight line through the origin.*

**III. Activity 1: Table & Graph Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn! In pairs, you'll get a handout with several tables. Your task is to determine if each relationship is proportional. If it is, you'll find the constant of proportionality (k), write the equation, and then graph it on the provided graph paper."
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Remember, to be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers. You need to check *every* pair in the table. If even one ratio is different, it's not proportional. Be rigorous in your checks!"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and observe pairs. I will specifically look for students systematically calculating y/x for *all* rows in the tables, not just the first one. I'll ask groups, "How did you confirm this table is proportional? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why is checking all of them important?"
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** "When you're working with your tables and graphs, make sure your work is clear and organized. Label your axes on the graph, use a consistent scale, and show your calculations for 'k' clearly next to each table."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will check student handouts for clear organization. Are tables filled out neatly? Are calculations for 'k' shown consistently for each row? Are graphs clearly labeled with axes, units, and a consistent scale? Do the plotted points accurately reflect the table data in an orderly fashion?
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** As students work, they will constantly make decisions: "Is this proportional?", "What's the constant?", "How do I write the equation?". They must use the definition and their calculations to justify their answers.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** During group work, I will listen to their discussions. Are they using the definition (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to justify their decisions? For example, if a group incorrectly identifies a non-proportional relationship, I'll ask, "What evidence from your calculations or graph led you to that conclusion? Does it meet *all* the criteria for proportionality, including passing through (0,0)?"

**IV. Activity 2: Equation & Diagram Connection (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Great work with tables and graphs! Now let's connect to equations and verbal descriptions."
*   *Provide a new scenario:* "A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."
*   **Teacher:** "Is this a proportional relationship? How do you know? What's the constant of proportionality if we consider flour to milk?" (k = 2/3). "What if we consider milk to flour?" (k = 3/2).
*   **Teacher:** "How would we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)?" (F = (2/3)M). "And milk (M) in terms of flour (F)?" (M = (3/2)F).
*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Imagine you're explaining this recipe to someone who's never cooked before. Which representation – the verbal description, a table, a graph, or an equation – do you think would be most helpful for them to understand the relationship between flour and milk? Why? Are there situations where another representation might be better or worse?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will listen to student responses during a brief class discussion. Do they articulate the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? For example, "The verbal description is easy to start with, but an equation is better if you need to scale the recipe up a lot because you can just plug in numbers." or "A graph gives a quick visual, but a table shows exact values for specific amounts."
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, create your own short verbal description of a proportional relationship. Then, write its equation and sketch a simple diagram (like a ratio table or a simple picture showing groups) that represents it. Make sure your example is clear and accurate."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will review their created examples. Do their verbal descriptions clearly imply proportionality? Is the equation correctly derived from the description? Does the diagram accurately represent the relationship? I'll look for examples that are clear, concise, and mathematically sound, avoiding vague or incorrect relationships.

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Let's quickly review. What are the key characteristics of a proportional relationship in a table? (Constant ratio y/x) In a graph? (Straight line through the origin) In an equation? (y = kx)"
*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, I want you to answer two questions:
        1.  What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today?
        2.  What strategy did you use, or what 'aha!' moment did you have, that helped you overcome that challenge?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will collect exit tickets. I'll look for responses that demonstrate metacognitive awareness. Do students identify specific difficulties (e.g., "remembering to check (0,0) on a graph," "calculating k consistently when numbers were fractions") and describe a strategy they used (e.g., "I made sure to divide y by x for every single point," "I drew a quick sketch to see if it went through the origin before plotting all points")? This shows they are reflecting on their learning process and adjusting their thinking.

---
**Differentiation:**
*   **Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality.
*   **Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but isn't. Have them explore inverse proportionality or multi-step proportional problems.

**Assessment:**
*   Informal observation during activities and discussions.
*   Review of student handouts (tables, graphs, equations, created examples).
*   Exit ticket responses.

## Lesson Structure

---

### Warm-Up – "Which One Doesn't Belong?" (8 minutes)

**Activity:** Display four representations on the board:
1. A table showing (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9)
2. The equation y = 3x
3. A graph passing through the origin with a constant slope
4. A verbal description: *"A taxi charges $5 plus $2 per mile"*

Ask students: *"Which one doesn't belong with the others, and why?"*

**CRI1a – Clarifying Information:**
Students must articulate *precisely* what makes a relationship proportional — constant ratio, passes through the origin, unit rate visible. Push students beyond vague answers: *"How do you know it's not proportional? What exactly is the difference?"* Ask 2–3 students to restate a peer's reasoning in their own words to sharpen clarity.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who use terms like "constant rate of change," "y = kx," or "passes through the origin" versus those who describe it loosely. Note which students can distinguish between a proportional and a non-proportional relationship with precision.

---

### Direct Instruction – Defining Proportionality (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Using the whiteboard, build a class anchor chart with four columns: Table | Graph | Equation | Verbal Description. Walk through one clear proportional example — cost of apples at $1.50/lb — across all four representations. Explicitly show that y ÷ x = 1.5 for every pair in the table, the graph passes through the origin, and the equation takes the form y = kx.

Then display a non-proportional table (e.g., a cookie shop with a base fee: 1 cookie = $1.50, 2 cookies = $2.50, 3 cookies = $3.50). Ask: *"What do you find for y/x? Is this proportional? Why not?"*

**CRI1b – Organizing Information:**
Students copy the anchor chart into their journals, then add their own labels and arrows connecting the representations (e.g., drawing an arrow from the equation *y = 1.5x* to the table column showing the constant ratio of 1.5).

**Assessment:** Circulate and check whether student journals show logical connections between representations or isolated, disconnected notes. Students who organize with clear labels and arrows connecting across columns demonstrate Level 2; students with only copied text demonstrate Level 1.

---

### Guided Practice – "Is It Proportional?" Task Cards (12 minutes)

**Activity:** Pairs receive four task cards. Each card presents a different scenario (e.g., a table with an inconsistent ratio, a graph not passing through the origin, a recipe, a speed-time scenario). Students must decide: proportional or not — and *prove it* using at least two representations.

**CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:**
Students cross-check their conclusion by verifying the ratio in the table *and* checking the graph. Prompt: *"If your table says yes but your graph says no, which do you trust and why? How do you resolve the conflict?"* Remind students: to be sure a relationship is proportional, they must check *every* pair of values — one consistent ratio is not enough.

**Assessment:** Observe whether students catch contradictions between representations and reconcile them logically. Students demonstrating Level 2 will recalculate ratios and re-examine graphs before committing to an answer. Ask groups: *"Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why does that matter?"*

**CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:**
Within the task cards, one scenario includes a partially completed table with missing values. Students must decide if they have *enough* information to conclude proportionality.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who say, *"We can't be sure yet — we need more data points"* versus those who jump to conclusions. Probe: *"What would make you more confident in your answer?"*

---

### Whole-Class Discussion – Comparing Strategies (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Two groups share their reasoning for the same task card but reached different conclusions. Facilitate a structured discussion: *"Walk us through your thinking. Where did you agree? Where did you split?"*

**CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices:**
Ask the class: *"Group A used the equation to check. Group B used the graph. What are the advantages and disadvantages of each approach? When might one method fail you?"* Extend to verbal descriptions and tables: *"Which representation would be most useful if you needed to scale a recipe? Which helps you see the relationship most quickly?"*

**Assessment:** Students who identify that graphs can be misleading at small scales, or that equations require knowing the rule in advance, demonstrate sophisticated trade-off thinking (Level 2). Students who say only "graphs are easier" without further analysis demonstrate Level 1.

**CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making:**
Students must choose *which* representation gives the strongest proof of proportionality for a given scenario and justify it logically.

**Assessment:** Look for students who acknowledge that *no single representation alone is sufficient* and that cross-checking across representations strengthens confidence — this reflects anticipating complexity (Level 2). Challenge students: *"Could someone be fooled by just one representation? How?"*

---

### Independent Practice & Metacognitive Reflection (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Students independently solve one novel problem (given a verbal description, create a table and graph; given a graph, write the equation and verbal description; identify whether a relationship is proportional and explain why). Students then respond in their journals to two prompts:
1. *"What strategy did you use first, and did you change it? Why?"*
2. *"What would trip someone else up on this problem?"*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes:**
**Assessment:** Students demonstrating Level 2 will describe a moment they caught their own error or deliberately switched strategies (e.g., "I thought it was proportional just from the table, but then I graphed it and it didn't go through the origin, so I rechecked my ratios"). Students at Level 1 will mention steps taken but not *why* they made those choices. Collect journals for formative review.

---

### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students answer:
1. *"Give one example of a proportional relationship and represent it TWO ways. Explain how you know it's proportional."*
2. *"What was the most challenging part of today's lesson, and what strategy helped you work through it?"*
