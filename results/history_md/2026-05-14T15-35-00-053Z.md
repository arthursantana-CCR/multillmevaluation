## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objectives
Students will identify and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills through comparison, reflection, and reasoned decision-making.

---

## Materials
- Printed "Rate Comparison" task cards (two scenarios per pair)
- Graph paper
- Whiteboard markers
- Exit ticket slips
- Anchor chart: "What Makes a Relationship Proportional?"

---

## Lesson Sequence

### **Hook – Launch (0–7 min)**
**Activity:** Display two real-world scenarios on the board:
- *Scenario A:* A car travels 60 miles per hour.
- *Scenario B:* A delivery driver charges $5 plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: *"Are both of these proportional relationships? How do you know?"*

Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):**
Students must articulate what they already believe about proportional relationships before receiving formal instruction. The teacher listens for precise language (e.g., "constant ratio," "starts at zero") versus vague language (e.g., "they both go up").
**Assessment:** Teacher circulates and notes which pairs use clear, mathematically grounded language. Misconceptions are recorded on the board for revisiting later.

---

### **Direct Instruction – Anchor Chart Build (7–17 min)**
**Activity:** Teacher and students co-construct an anchor chart listing the four representations of proportional relationships: tables, graphs, equations (y = kx), and verbal descriptions. For each representation, the teacher models using the car scenario (Scenario A).

Key questions asked aloud:
- *"How do we check a table for proportionality?"*
- *"What must be true about the graph?"*
- *"Why does the equation always pass through the origin?"*

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b (Organizing Information):**
Students copy the anchor chart into a structured notes template that arranges the four representations in a 2×2 grid with "What to look for" and "Example" columns.
**Assessment:** Teacher collects notes at the end of class. Look for logical sequencing — does the student connect the constant ratio in the table to the slope in the graph and the *k* value in the equation?

---

### **Guided Practice – Comparing Two Scenarios (17–32 min)**
**Activity:** Students work in pairs using task cards comparing Scenario A (proportional) and Scenario B (non-proportional). They complete a table, graph both relationships on the same axes, write an equation if possible, and write one sentence describing each relationship.

Pairs are then asked: *"Which scenario is proportional, and how did you decide? What evidence from each representation supports your answer?"*

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a (Assessing Validity of Information):**
Students must cross-check their conclusion across all four representations — they cannot rely on just the graph or just the table. If representations conflict, they must resolve the discrepancy.
**Assessment:** Teacher observes pairs and asks probing questions: *"Your table says proportional, but does your graph pass through the origin? Let's check."* Students who reconcile conflicts across representations demonstrate Level 2.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b (Assessing Quality of Information):**
Students evaluate whether their completed table has enough data points to make a valid claim. Pairs with only two data points are prompted: *"Is two points enough to be sure? What if the pattern changed at mile 10?"*
**Assessment:** Listen for students who voluntarily add data points or question the sufficiency of their evidence.

---

### **Structured Debate – Pros and Cons (32–42 min)**
**Activity:** Present this scenario: *"You're planning a road trip. Driver A charges by the proportional rate (Scenario A). Driver B uses Scenario B's pricing. Which is the better deal for a 20-mile trip? A 5-mile trip?"*

Students must argue a position and anticipate a counterargument in writing before whole-class discussion.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):**
Students explicitly compare both options at two different distances, recognizing that the better choice depends on context.
**Assessment:** Written responses scored for whether students acknowledge the trade-off (Scenario B is cheaper for short trips, Scenario A for long ones) rather than declaring one universally better.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making):**
Students must use their equation and graph as evidence, not just intuition.
**Assessment:** Teacher looks for students citing *y = 2x* versus *y = 2x + 5* with substituted values as justification for their choice.

---

### **Exit Ticket – Metacognitive Reflection (42–55 min)**
**Activity:** Students independently complete a new proportional relationship task, then respond to two reflection prompts:
1. *"What strategy did you use first, and why?"*
2. *"Was there a moment you doubted your answer? What did you do next?"*

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes):**
Students describe their own reasoning process, not just the answer.
**Assessment:** Exit tickets are reviewed for depth of reflection. Level 2 responses name a specific strategy shift (e.g., *"I checked the graph after the table confused me"*). Level 1 responses mention thinking without explaining adjustment.

---

## Closing
Teacher revisits the opening Hook scenarios. Ask: *"Would you change your original answer now? What changed your thinking?"* This closes the metacognitive loop opened at the start of class.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a brief review: “What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”  
- Show a simple example: If 1 apple costs $2, then 3 apples cost $6. Ask students: “How can we show this relationship in different ways?”  
- Write on the board: Table, Graph, Equation, Diagram, Verbal description.

*CCR Critical Thinking Target:*  
- **Clarifying information (CRI1a):** Students clarify what proportional means by discussing the example.  
- **Observation:** Listen for students’ ability to restate or explain proportionality clearly.

---

**2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)**  
- Define proportional relationships formally: Two quantities x and y are proportional if y = kx for some constant k (constant of proportionality).  
- Show how to represent this in:  
  - **Table:** List pairs (x, y) where y/x = k  
  - **Graph:** Plot points that form a straight line through the origin  
  - **Equation:** y = kx  
  - **Diagram:** Use a double number line or ratio tape  
  - **Verbal description:** “For every 1 unit of x, y is k times that amount.”  
- Use the apple example to demonstrate each representation.

*CCR Critical Thinking Target:*  
- **Organizing information (CRI1b):** Present information in a logical sequence from definition to multiple representations.  
- **Observation:** Check students’ notes or responses for clear, logically ordered information.

---

**3. Guided Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with a proportional relationship scenario (e.g., a recipe: 2 cups of flour for 3 cups of sugar).  
- In pairs, students:  
  - Complete a table of values  
  - Plot the points on graph paper  
  - Write the equation representing the relationship  
  - Draw a diagram (double number line)  
  - Write a verbal description of the relationship  
- Circulate to support and ask probing questions.

*CCR Critical Thinking Targets:*  
- **Assessing validity of information (CRI2a):** Students check if their table values maintain the same ratio.  
- **Assessing quality of information (CRI2b):** Students decide which representation best shows the relationship clearly.  
- **Observation:** Listen to student discussions and check if they question or verify their results.

---

**4. Class Discussion and Comparison (10 minutes)**  
- Invite pairs to share their work.  
- Discuss:  
  - How do the different representations show the same proportional relationship?  
  - Which representation is easiest to understand and why?  
  - Are there any mistakes or inconsistencies?  
- Highlight the importance of cross-checking between representations.

*CCR Critical Thinking Targets:*  
- **Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices (CRI3a):** Students compare different representations and discuss advantages/disadvantages.  
- **Applying sound reasoning to decision-making (CRI4a):** Students justify their choice of representation with logical reasoning.  
- **Observation:** Note students’ ability to articulate pros and cons and provide reasoned arguments.

---

**5. Independent Practice (10 minutes)**  
- Provide a new problem: “A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours. Represent this proportional relationship in all five ways.”  
- Students work individually to:  
  - Create a table of values  
  - Graph the points  
  - Write the equation  
  - Draw a diagram  
  - Write a verbal description  
- Encourage students to check their work for consistency.

*CCR Critical Thinking Target:*  
- **Reflecting on one’s thinking processes (CRI5a):** Students self-assess their work and reasoning, adjusting if inconsistencies appear.  
- **Observation:** Collect work and look for evidence of self-correction or notes reflecting on their process.

---

**6. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Prompt: “Explain in your own words what it means for two quantities to be proportional and give an example using one representation.”  
- Collect exit tickets to assess understanding and clarity.

*CCR Critical Thinking Target:*  
- **Clarifying information (CRI1a):** Students articulate their understanding clearly.  
- **Observation:** Evaluate clarity and precision in students’ explanations.
