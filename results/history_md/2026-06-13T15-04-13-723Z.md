## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with mixed scenarios, exit tickets, chart paper

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop sells 2 smoothies for $6, 4 for $12, and 6 for $18."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join, plus $5 per class."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with shoulder partners for 2 minutes, then share.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must identify the relevant defining feature of proportionality (constant ratio, passes through origin).
- **Assessment:** Listen for precise language like "the ratio stays the same" vs. vague answers. Note students who can clearly articulate what makes A proportional.

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities where y/x = k (constant of proportionality). Demonstrate the five representations using Scenario A:
- **Table:** smoothies vs. cost
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing equal groupings
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $3 per smoothie"

Emphasize the two tests: (1) constant ratio, (2) graph passes through origin.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Representation Stations" (15 minutes)
Divide class into 5 groups. Each group receives a different starting representation of the same proportional relationship (e.g., a bike rental costs $4/hour). Groups must produce the other four representations on chart paper.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students must arrange data logically across multiple formats.
- **Assessment:** Check whether the table is ordered, axes are labeled correctly, and the equation matches the table. Look for clear, consistent structure.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** As groups finish, they peer-review another group's poster using a checklist: Does the table show constant ratio? Does the line pass through origin? Is the equation in y=kx form?
- **Assessment:** Collect peer-review checklists; evaluate whether students selected meaningful, accurate critiques versus surface-level comments.

### 4. Critical Analysis Activity — "Spot the Imposter" (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout with 6 relationships (mix of proportional and non-proportional). For each, students must:
1. Decide proportional or not
2. Justify using at least two representations
3. Identify any misleading features

Include one "trick" example: a table that appears proportional for the first two entries but breaks the pattern later.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students must cross-check by testing multiple data points and using more than one representation to verify.
- **Assessment:** Review written justifications for evidence of multi-point verification, not single-pair conclusions.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students construct logical arguments connecting representations to the definition.
- **Assessment:** Score reasoning on the handout: Does the conclusion follow from the evidence? Are claims supported?

### 5. Decision-Making Discussion (7 minutes)
Pose a real-world choice: *"You need to print 100 flyers. Print Shop A charges $0.10 per flyer. Print Shop B charges a $5 setup fee plus $0.05 per flyer. Which is proportional? Which is the better deal — and does proportionality always mean 'better'?"*

Students discuss in pairs, then share out. Push students to consider trade-offs (volume, predictability, fairness).

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must compare options across multiple dimensions, not just price.
- **Assessment:** During share-out, listen for responses that acknowledge trade-offs (e.g., "Shop B is cheaper for large orders but not small ones"). Mark a participation tally for students offering multi-sided reasoning.

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (5 minutes)
Students complete a two-part exit ticket:

**Part 1 (Content):** Given the equation y = 7x, create a table with 3 values and describe the situation in words.

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What helped you see the problem differently? What strategy will you use next time you encounter a tricky proportional relationship?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** The metacognitive prompt requires students to articulate how their reasoning evolved.
- **Assessment:** Score reflections on a 0–2 scale. Level 2 responses identify a specific shift in thinking and name a strategy (e.g., "I assumed it was proportional until I checked a third point — next time I'll always test more than two pairs"). Level 1 responses mention reasoning without depth.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence starters for reflection.
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional relationship that "tricks" classmates and explain why it fails the proportionality test.

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship in your home or community. Represent each in two ways and explain your reasoning.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Connecting Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

### **Lesson Objective**
Students will be able to recognize, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations) while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, weigh problem-solving options, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook & Organizing Data (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world verbal description: *“A local bakery uses a specific recipe for their famous chocolate chip cookies. According to the head baker, 2 cups of sugar are needed for every 3 batches of cookies.”* Students are asked to break down this verbal description and translate it into a mathematical table. 

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must underline the key quantities in the verbal description and rewrite the core relationship in their own words to clarify the exact ratio (cups of sugar to batches of cookies). They must precisely define the independent ($x$) and dependent ($y$) variables to support deeper understanding.
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher circulates and reviews students' written definitions of the variables. A successful student will explicitly clarify that $x$ represents batches of cookies and $y$ represents cups of sugar, avoiding the common confusion of mixing up the inputs and outputs.
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students arrange their clarified data into a basic T-chart (table), scaling the recipe up to 12 batches. They must structure the table logically, ensuring the constant rate of change is clearly visible.
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher assesses the students' tables to ensure the information is arranged with a logical flow (e.g., sequentially ordering batches as 3, 6, 9, 12 rather than a scattered, random order like 9, 3, 12, 6), demonstrating a clear organizational structure.

### **Part 2: Cross-Checking and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces two external sources of data regarding a car's fuel efficiency: a printed data table from a government fuel economy website and a line graph published in a popular car magazine. Students must determine if the car's fuel consumption is proportional to the miles driven.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students rigorously cross-check the information by comparing the government data table with the magazine's graph. They must verify if both authoritative sources show the same constant of proportionality ($k = y/x$) and confirm that the graph forms a straight line passing through the origin $(0,0)$.
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher listens to group discussions and checks student worksheets to see if they are actively calculating the unit rate from both sources to validate the claim, rather than just assuming the magazine graph is correct at first glance.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students are asked to write a brief justification on which source (the exact data table or the visual graph) provides higher-quality information for writing the precise equation ($y = kx$) of the relationship. 
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher evaluates the written justifications. High-level performance is observed when a student demonstrates sophisticated judgment, noting that while the graph is good for a quick visual check of proportionality (straight line through origin), the government data table is a higher-quality, more authoritative source for extracting exact numerical values to calculate the constant $k$.

### **Part 3: Application and Decision-Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a new scenario: *“You need to hire a moving company. Company A charges $50 per hour. Company B charges a $30 flat fee plus $40 per hour.”* Students must represent both companies using equations and graphs, then decide which company to hire for a 5-hour move.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using a graph versus an equation to predict the cost of a 50-hour cross-country move. They list the pros and cons of each mathematical representation.
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher reviews the pros/cons lists. Students should note that a graph is excellent for quickly visualizing when Company B becomes more expensive than Company A, but an equation is far superior (pro) for calculating long-term implications (like a 50-hour move) because extending a graph that far is impractical (con).
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students must decide which company represents a true proportional relationship and justify their conclusion using mathematical evidence.
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher assesses the students' final written decisions. A student demonstrating sophisticated reasoning will correctly identify Company A as proportional because it has a constant ratio and passes through the origin $(0,0)$, while logically explaining that Company B’s initial $30 fee makes it non-proportional, anticipating the complexity of the $y$-intercept.

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket where they reflect on how their understanding of proportionality has evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** The Exit Ticket asks: *"At the beginning of class, how did you decide if something was proportional? How did cross-checking tables, graphs, and equations change your thinking process? What errors did you catch yourself making today?"*
    *   **How to Observe/Assess:** The teacher reads the exit tickets to gauge metacognitive awareness. A strong response will show a student strategically adjusting their thinking—for example, writing, "I used to think any straight line meant a proportional relationship, but after checking the moving company graph, I realized I have to actively check if it starts at zero. I adjusted my thinking to always look for the origin first."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Materials:** Board/projector, graph paper, rulers, calculators optional, handout, exit ticket

### Learning Targets

Students will be able to:

1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Identify the constant of proportionality.
3. Represent a proportional relationship using an equation in the form **y = kx**.
4. Explain their reasoning clearly and check whether their conclusion is valid.

---

## Lesson Agenda

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Cups of Juice | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $2 |
| 2 | $4 |
| 3 | $6 |
| 4 | $8 |

**Table B**

| Cups of Juice | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $5 |
| 3 | $7 |
| 4 | $9 |

Ask students to write individually:

- Which table shows a proportional relationship?
- How do you know?
- What pattern do you notice?

Then students briefly share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students must identify the relevant quantities and clarify what makes a relationship proportional: a constant ratio and, when graphed, a line through the origin.

**Assessment/Observation:** Listen for students saying or writing statements such as, “The cost divided by cups is always 2,” or “Table B adds 2 each time, but the ratio is not the same.” Use a -1 to 2 rubric during review:  
- **2:** Clearly identifies relevant quantities and explains constant ratio.  
- **1:** Notices a pattern but explanation is incomplete.  
- **0:** Not enough evidence.  
- **-1:** Gives misleading reasoning, such as “all increasing patterns are proportional.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A proportional relationship means two quantities have a constant ratio. If **y** is proportional to **x**, then:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Model with Table A:

Cost ÷ Cups = 2 ÷ 1 = 2, 4 ÷ 2 = 2, 6 ÷ 3 = 2, 8 ÷ 4 = 2.

So the constant of proportionality is **2**, and the equation is:

**y = 2x**

where **x** = cups of juice and **y** = cost in dollars.

Show how this relationship appears in different forms:

- **Table:** constant ratio
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = kx
- **Diagram:** equal groups or repeated scaling
- **Verbal description:** “Each cup costs $2.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students learn to organize the same relationship across multiple representations.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask students to label the table, graph, equation, and verbal description for the same relationship on their handout. Look for organized work that connects each representation logically.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 15 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A bike rental costs $6 per hour.”

Students complete:

1. Make a table for 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours.
2. Write an equation.
3. Graph the relationship.
4. Draw or describe a diagram.
5. Write a sentence explaining the relationship.

Expected results:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

Equation: **y = 6x**

Graph: straight line through points such as (0,0), (1,6), (2,12), etc.

Verbal description: “The cost is $6 for each hour.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students check whether their representations agree with one another. For example, if the table says 2 hours costs $12, the graph should include the point (2,12), and the equation y = 6x should also produce 12 when x = 2.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask students to circle one point on their graph and prove it matches both the table and equation. Look for written evidence such as, “When x = 3, y = 6(3) = 18, so the point should be (3,18).”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide which pieces of information are most useful for proving proportionality: constant ratio, origin, and equation form.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask: “What is the strongest evidence that this relationship is proportional?” Students should select high-quality evidence, not just say “the numbers go up.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Compare Two Plans — 13 minutes

Give students two rental plans:

**Plan A:** $5 per hour  
**Plan B:** $3 sign-up fee plus $4 per hour

Students work in pairs to complete a table and decide:

- Which plan is proportional?
- Which plan is cheaper for 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours?
- Which representation helped you decide: table, graph, equation, or verbal description?

Plan A equation: **y = 5x**, proportional.  
Plan B equation: **y = 4x + 3**, not proportional because it does not start at 0 and has an added fee.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare two options and consider when one plan is cheaper than the other.

**Assessment/Observation:** Listen for students comparing trade-offs: “Plan B is cheaper for some hours, but it is not proportional because of the sign-up fee.” Students should not confuse “cheaper” with “proportional.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students justify their decision using mathematical evidence.

**Assessment/Observation:** Require pairs to write a claim-evidence-reasoning response:  
“Plan ___ is proportional because ___.”  
“I know Plan ___ is not proportional because ___.”  
Look for accurate use of ratios, origin, and equations.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Invite two pairs to share. One pair explains Plan A; another explains Plan B. Emphasize:

- A relationship can increase at a steady rate and still not be proportional if it does not pass through the origin.
- A proportional relationship must have the form **y = kx**.
- The constant of proportionality tells how much **y** changes for each 1 unit of **x**.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete independently:

1. A table shows:  
   x: 2, 4, 6, 8  
   y: 5, 10, 15, 20  
   Is it proportional? Explain.
2. Write the equation.
3. One reflection sentence: “At first I thought ___, but now I know ___ because ___.”

Expected answer: Yes, proportional. Constant of proportionality is 2.5, so **y = 2.5x**.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on how their reasoning changed or became clearer.

**Assessment/Observation:** Review exit tickets for metacognitive evidence such as, “At first I only checked if the numbers increased, but now I check y divided by x.” Score reflection using the -1 to 2 scale:  
- **2:** Shows clear awareness of thinking and adjustment.  
- **1:** Mentions thinking but with limited detail.  
- **0:** No reflection evidence.  
- **-1:** Repeats an error without correction.

---

## Lesson Closure

End by restating: “To recognize a proportional relationship, check for a constant ratio, a graph through the origin, and an equation in the form **y = kx**. Strong mathematicians do not rely on only one clue—they check their reasoning across representations.”

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Materials:** Board/projector, graph paper, rulers, calculators optional, handout, exit ticket

### Learning Targets

Students will be able to:

1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Identify the constant of proportionality.
3. Represent a proportional relationship using an equation in the form **y = kx**.
4. Explain their reasoning clearly and check whether their conclusion is valid.

---

## Lesson Agenda

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Cups of Juice | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $2 |
| 2 | $4 |
| 3 | $6 |
| 4 | $8 |

**Table B**

| Cups of Juice | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $3 |
| 2 | $5 |
| 3 | $7 |
| 4 | $9 |

Ask students to write individually:

- Which table shows a proportional relationship?
- How do you know?
- What pattern do you notice?

Then students briefly share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students must identify the relevant quantities and clarify what makes a relationship proportional: a constant ratio and, when graphed, a line through the origin.

**Assessment/Observation:** Listen for students saying or writing statements such as, “The cost divided by cups is always 2,” or “Table B adds 2 each time, but the ratio is not the same.” Use a -1 to 2 rubric during review:  
- **2:** Clearly identifies relevant quantities and explains constant ratio.  
- **1:** Notices a pattern but explanation is incomplete.  
- **0:** Not enough evidence.  
- **-1:** Gives misleading reasoning, such as “all increasing patterns are proportional.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A proportional relationship means two quantities have a constant ratio. If **y** is proportional to **x**, then:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Model with Table A:

Cost ÷ Cups = 2 ÷ 1 = 2, 4 ÷ 2 = 2, 6 ÷ 3 = 2, 8 ÷ 4 = 2.

So the constant of proportionality is **2**, and the equation is:

**y = 2x**

where **x** = cups of juice and **y** = cost in dollars.

Show how this relationship appears in different forms:

- **Table:** constant ratio
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = kx
- **Diagram:** equal groups or repeated scaling
- **Verbal description:** “Each cup costs $2.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students learn to organize the same relationship across multiple representations.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask students to label the table, graph, equation, and verbal description for the same relationship on their handout. Look for organized work that connects each representation logically.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 15 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A bike rental costs $6 per hour.”

Students complete:

1. Make a table for 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours.
2. Write an equation.
3. Graph the relationship.
4. Draw or describe a diagram.
5. Write a sentence explaining the relationship.

Expected results:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

Equation: **y = 6x**

Graph: straight line through points such as (0,0), (1,6), (2,12), etc.

Verbal description: “The cost is $6 for each hour.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students check whether their representations agree with one another. For example, if the table says 2 hours costs $12, the graph should include the point (2,12), and the equation y = 6x should also produce 12 when x = 2.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask students to circle one point on their graph and prove it matches both the table and equation. Look for written evidence such as, “When x = 3, y = 6(3) = 18, so the point should be (3,18).”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide which pieces of information are most useful for proving proportionality: constant ratio, origin, and equation form.

**Assessment/Observation:** Ask: “What is the strongest evidence that this relationship is proportional?” Students should select high-quality evidence, not just say “the numbers go up.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Compare Two Plans — 13 minutes

Give students two rental plans:

**Plan A:** $5 per hour  
**Plan B:** $3 sign-up fee plus $4 per hour

Students work in pairs to complete a table and decide:

- Which plan is proportional?
- Which plan is cheaper for 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours?
- Which representation helped you decide: table, graph, equation, or verbal description?

Plan A equation: **y = 5x**, proportional.  
Plan B equation: **y = 4x + 3**, not proportional because it does not start at 0 and has an added fee.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare two options and consider when one plan is cheaper than the other.

**Assessment/Observation:** Listen for students comparing trade-offs: “Plan B is cheaper for some hours, but it is not proportional because of the sign-up fee.” Students should not confuse “cheaper” with “proportional.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students justify their decision using mathematical evidence.

**Assessment/Observation:** Require pairs to write a claim-evidence-reasoning response:  
“Plan ___ is proportional because ___.”  
“I know Plan ___ is not proportional because ___.”  
Look for accurate use of ratios, origin, and equations.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Invite two pairs to share. One pair explains Plan A; another explains Plan B. Emphasize:

- A relationship can increase at a steady rate and still not be proportional if it does not pass through the origin.
- A proportional relationship must have the form **y = kx**.
- The constant of proportionality tells how much **y** changes for each 1 unit of **x**.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete independently:

1. A table shows:  
   x: 2, 4, 6, 8  
   y: 5, 10, 15, 20  
   Is it proportional? Explain.
2. Write the equation.
3. One reflection sentence: “At first I thought ___, but now I know ___ because ___.”

Expected answer: Yes, proportional. Constant of proportionality is 2.5, so **y = 2.5x**.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on how their reasoning changed or became clearer.

**Assessment/Observation:** Review exit tickets for metacognitive evidence such as, “At first I only checked if the numbers increased, but now I check y divided by x.” Score reflection using the -1 to 2 scale:  
- **2:** Shows clear awareness of thinking and adjustment.  
- **1:** Mentions thinking but with limited detail.  
- **0:** No reflection evidence.  
- **-1:** Repeats an error without correction.

---

## Lesson Closure

End by restating: “To recognize a proportional relationship, check for a constant ratio, a graph through the origin, and an equation in the form **y = kx**. Strong mathematicians do not rely on only one clue—they check their reasoning across representations.”

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills through clarifying, organizing, validating, weighing, reasoning, and reflecting.

## Materials

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop sells 2 smoothies for $6, 4 for $12, and 6 for $18."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join, plus $5 per class."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with shoulder partners for 2 minutes, then share.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must identify the defining features of proportionality (constant ratio, passes through origin) and clearly define independent (x) and dependent (y) variables.
- **Assessment (-1 to 2 scale):** Listen for precise language like "the ratio stays the same." **2:** Clearly identifies relevant quantities and explains constant ratio. **1:** Notices a pattern but explanation is incomplete. **-1:** Misleading reasoning such as "all increasing patterns are proportional."

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities where y/x = k (constant of proportionality), expressed as **y = kx**. Demonstrate the five representations using Scenario A:
- **Table:** smoothies vs. cost (constant ratio of 3)
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing equal groupings
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $3 per smoothie"

Emphasize the two tests: (1) constant ratio, (2) graph passes through origin.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Representation Stations" (12 minutes)
Divide class into 5 groups. Each group receives a different starting representation of the same proportional relationship (a bike rental at $4/hour). Groups produce the other four representations on chart paper.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students must arrange data logically across multiple formats (e.g., ordering the table sequentially as 1, 2, 3, 4 hours rather than scattered).
- **Assessment:** Check whether the table is ordered, axes are labeled correctly, and the equation matches the table. Look for clear, consistent structure across all four representations.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Groups peer-review another group's poster using a checklist: Does the table show constant ratio? Does the line pass through origin? Is the equation in y=kx form? Which representation provides the strongest evidence of proportionality?
- **Assessment:** Collect peer-review checklists. **2-level** students demonstrate sophisticated judgment (e.g., noting the exact data table is more authoritative for calculating k than a visual graph alone). **1-level** students give surface-level feedback like "looks right."

### 4. Critical Analysis Activity — "Spot the Imposter" (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout with 6 relationships (mix of proportional and non-proportional). For each, students must:
1. Decide proportional or not
2. Justify using at least two representations
3. Identify any misleading features

Include one "trick" example: a table that appears proportional for the first two entries but breaks the pattern later.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check by testing multiple data points and using more than one representation to verify, rather than assuming after one pair.
- **Assessment:** Review justifications for evidence of multi-point verification. Look for written work like "When x = 3, y = 6(3) = 18, so the point should be (3,18) — confirmed in the table."

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students construct logical arguments using a claim-evidence-reasoning frame: *"Relationship ___ is proportional because ___. I know ___ is not proportional because ___."*
- **Assessment:** Score reasoning on the handout. **2-level** students anticipate complexities (e.g., recognizing that a steady rate of increase still isn't proportional if it doesn't pass through the origin). **-1:** Faulty logic such as "it's a straight line so it must be proportional."

### 5. Decision-Making Discussion (10 minutes)
Pose: *"You need to print 100 flyers. Print Shop A charges $0.10 per flyer. Print Shop B charges a $5 setup fee plus $0.05 per flyer. Which is proportional? Which is the better deal — and does proportionality always mean 'better'?"*

Students work in pairs to write equations (A: y = 0.10x; B: y = 0.05x + 5), then discuss trade-offs.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare options across multiple dimensions — cost at different volumes, predictability, and whether proportionality itself is desirable.
- **Assessment:** Listen for responses acknowledging trade-offs (e.g., "Shop B is cheaper for large orders because the setup fee gets spread out, but Shop A is more predictable and proportional"). Mark participation for multi-sided reasoning. **2-level** students systematically evaluate long-term implications (what happens at 500 flyers?).

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (5 minutes)
Students complete a two-part exit ticket:

**Part 1 (Content):** Given the equation y = 7x, create a table with 3 values, graph one point, and describe the situation in words.

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"At first I thought ___, but now I know ___ because ___. One strategy I will use next time I encounter a tricky proportional relationship is ___."*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** The metacognitive prompt requires students to articulate how their reasoning evolved.
- **Assessment (-1 to 2 scale):** **2:** Identifies a specific shift in thinking and names a strategy (e.g., "I used to think any straight line meant proportional, but now I check if it passes through the origin first — next time I'll always test more than two pairs"). **1:** Mentions reasoning without depth. **-1:** Repeats an error without correction.

- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional relationship that "tricks" classmates and explain why it fails the proportionality test.

"To recognize a proportional relationship, check for a constant ratio, a graph through the origin, and an equation in the form **y = kx**. Strong mathematicians don't rely on only one clue — they check their reasoning across representations."
