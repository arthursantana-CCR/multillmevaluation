## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging critical thinking skills to evaluate and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Recipe Card" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two smoothie recipes on the board:
- Recipe A: 2 cups strawberries → 3 cups yogurt
- Recipe B: 4 cups strawberries → 6 cups yogurt; 6 cups strawberries → 9 cups yogurt

Ask: *"If we doubled Recipe A, would it taste the same? Why?"* Students Think-Pair-Share.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "tastes the same" means mathematically (same ratio). Teacher prompts: *"Can you explain that more precisely?"*
**Assessment:** Listen for students articulating the ratio 2:3 clearly during pair discussion; circulate with a checklist marking precision of explanations.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities where the ratio y/x is constant (k, the constant of proportionality). Model representing the smoothie recipe across **five forms**:
- **Table:** strawberries vs. yogurt
- **Graph:** plot points; show straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = (3/2)x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing ratio bars
- **Verbal:** "For every 2 cups of strawberries, use 3 cups of yogurt."

Explicitly walk through how each representation connects.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a graphic organizer with five quadrants linking each representation.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers; check whether students arranged representations so connections (e.g., slope = k = unit rate) flow logically.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Is It Proportional?" (10 minutes)
Present three scenarios:
1. Tickets cost $4 each (proportional)
2. Cell phone plan: $20 flat fee + $0.10/min (NOT proportional)
3. Distance table showing 60 mph constant speed (proportional)

For each, students determine if proportional, justify using two representations, and identify k if applicable.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must verify claims by cross-checking: does the table show constant ratio? Does the graph pass through the origin? Does the equation fit y = kx form?
**Assessment:** Cold-call students to explain how they validated their answer using multiple representations. Record responses showing two or more verification methods.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Ask: *"Which representation gave you the strongest evidence that Scenario 2 is NOT proportional?"* Students must judge which evidence is most authoritative.
**Assessment:** Listen for reasoning that prioritizes the y-intercept ($20 fee) or non-zero starting point on the graph as the highest-quality indicator.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task — "Choose Your Representation" (15 minutes)
In pairs, students receive this problem: *"A printer prints 8 pages in 20 seconds. Represent this relationship using ANY TWO of the five forms. Then justify which two you chose."*

Pairs must list pros and cons of at least three representations before committing.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students complete a T-chart weighing trade-offs: tables show patterns but are bulky; equations are concise but abstract; graphs show trends visually but require precision.
**Assessment:** Collect T-charts; look for systematic comparison considering audience, precision, and ease — not just one-sided preference.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Pairs present their two chosen representations and defend the unit rate (0.4 pages/second or 2.5 sec/page).
**Assessment:** Use a rubric scoring logical flow: Did they correctly derive k? Did they anticipate that the inverse rate is also valid? Did they handle unit conversions soundly?

---

### 5. Independent Check & Reflection (8 minutes)
**Exit Ticket (5 min):**
Part A: Given the table {(3, 12), (5, 20), (8, 32)}, write the equation and graph the relationship.
Part B (Reflection): *"Describe one moment during today's lesson where your thinking changed. What made you reconsider? What strategy will you use next time you see a proportional relationship problem?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking):** Part B explicitly asks for metacognitive reflection.
**Assessment:** Score reflections on a 0–2 scale: 2 = identifies a specific shift in thinking AND names a future strategy; 1 = mentions reasoning without depth; 0 = no reflection. Use responses to plan tomorrow's mini-lesson.

**Closing (3 min):** Whole-class share — two volunteers read reflections. Teacher emphasizes that recognizing **y = kx** and **graphs through the origin** are the two most reliable "tests" for proportionality.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables/organizers; sentence starters for justifications ("This is proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance and explain the trap.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price per item, etc.). Represent it in three of the five forms and write a one-sentence reflection on which form best communicates the relationship.

---

This lesson cycles students through identifying, organizing, validating, evaluating, reasoning about, and reflecting on proportional relationships — embedding all six critical thinking subcompetencies into authentic mathematical practice.

**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to identify, translate, and represent proportional relationships across five modalities (verbal, table, graph, equation, diagram) while applying critical thinking skills to validate data, weigh the utility of different representations, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Clarifying the Problem (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Decoding the Deal"**
The lesson begins with a messy, real-world verbal description projected on the board: *"At the local market, a vendor says you can buy 3 pounds of apples for $4.50, but my friend said she paid $7.50 for 5 pounds. Another guy bought 2 pounds and a candy bar for $5.00."* Students must extract the relevant mathematical information, define their variables ($x$ = pounds of apples, $y$ = total cost), and clarify the core proportional question: Is the price of apples consistent? 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students work in pairs to filter out irrelevant information (the candy bar) and clarify the exact unit rate ($1.50 per pound) to support a deeper understanding of the relationship.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and observe students' whiteboards, assessing whether they have precisely isolated the relevant data points (3 lbs for $4.50; 5 lbs for $7.50) and clearly defined their $x$ and $y$ variables without being confused by the misleading "candy bar" data.

### **Part 2: Guided Practice & Organization (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: "The Proportion Web"**
The teacher guides students in taking the clarified apple data and organizing it into a structured "Proportion Web." This graphic organizer requires students to place the verbal description in the center and branch out to create a sequential table, a properly scaled graph, a tape diagram, and an algebraic equation ($y = 1.50x$). 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students arrange the raw data into a logical, sequential input-output table and translate that table into a coordinate plane, ensuring the logical flow of information from one representation to the next.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the students' graphic organizers to ensure the table is ordered sequentially by $x$-values and that the graph features a straight line passing through the origin, demonstrating a strong, consistent logical structure.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Verification (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Fact-Check the Math"**
Students are handed a "Vendor Report" containing data from a competing apple vendor. The report includes a mix of high-quality data (exact digital scale receipts) and low-quality data (a customer's rough estimate). It also includes a graph that looks straight but actually starts at (0, 1). Students must determine if this new vendor's pricing is truly proportional.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must rigorously cross-check the provided graph against the provided table and the standard definition of proportionality (must pass through the origin, $y/x$ must be constant). 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group discussions, assessing whether students successfully identify the contradiction: "The table shows a constant rate, but the graph starts at (0,1), so the graph is invalid and this is not a proportional relationship."

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** When calculating the constant of proportionality ($k$), students must choose which data points from the text to use—the exact receipt data or the customer's rough estimate.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review student worksheets to ensure they demonstrate sophisticated judgment by explicitly discarding the estimated data points and exclusively using the authoritative receipt data to calculate their equations.

### **Part 4: Trade-offs and Decision Making (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: "The Better Buy Decision"**
Students are tasked with purchasing 150 pounds of apples for a school pie-baking event. They must choose which representation (table, graph, diagram, or equation) is the most efficient tool for calculating the cost of this massive order, and then use it to make a final purchasing decision between two valid vendors.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students write a short paragraph comparing the options. They must evaluate the trade-offs of extending a table or graph to 150 (time-consuming, requires huge paper) versus using the equation $y = kx$ (abstract but highly efficient for large numbers).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read these paragraphs to assess if students systematically evaluate the trade-offs, recognizing that while graphs are great for quick visual comparisons, equations are superior for calculating long-term or large-scale implications.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students make their final vendor choice for the 150-pound order and justify it using their chosen mathematical representation.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the final written decision to ensure students use sound, sophisticated logic (e.g., "Using the equation $y = 1.25x$ for Vendor A and $y = 1.50x$ for Vendor B, Vendor A is cheaper, saving the school $37.50, which justifies my choice").

### **Part 5: Closure & Metacognition (5 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Metacognitive Minute Exit Ticket"**
Before leaving, students complete an exit ticket focused entirely on how their brain processed the different representations today. 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students answer the prompt: *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram) was the hardest for you to understand today? What specific mistake did you make, and how did you adjust your thinking to fix it?"*
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect and read the exit tickets, looking for deep metacognitive awareness. A level-2 response would look like: *"I initially struggled with the equation because I kept mixing up $x$ and $y$. I realized my error when my graph didn't match my equation, so I adjusted my thinking by always labeling $y$ as the total cost before writing $y=kx$."*
