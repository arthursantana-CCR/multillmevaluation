## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Clarify and organize arguments around a real-world debate
- Evaluate the reliability and quality of evidence supporting each side
- Weigh pros and cons to reach a reasoned personal position
- Reflect on how their thinking shifted during the lesson

---

## Materials
- Printed "Evidence Cards" (4 cards per student: 2 supporting homework, 2 opposing it — some from credible sources, some deliberately vague or unsourced)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Debate planning worksheet
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — Hook (5 minutes)

Write on the board: *"Last night's homework took me 3 hours. Was that time well spent?"*

Ask students to do a 90-second silent think, then share with a partner. Take 3–4 responses aloud. Do **not** validate or challenge answers yet. Tell students: *"By the end of class, you'll be able to answer that with real evidence and solid reasoning — not just feelings."*

---

### Part 1 — Clarifying the Question (8 minutes)
**Targets: CRI1a – Clarifying Information**

Write the debate question: *"Should schools assign homework?"* Ask students: *"What does this question actually mean? What are we really deciding?"*

Guide students to unpack hidden assumptions: Does "homework" mean daily drills or long-term projects? For all subjects or just some? For all grade levels? Record student clarifications on the board in a shared class definition.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who move beyond surface meaning. A student reaching Level 2 will say something like, *"We need to define what kind of homework before we can even argue this — drill work and creative projects aren't the same thing."* A student at Level 1 gives a general restatement of the question.

---

### Part 2 — Organizing the Argument (10 minutes)
**Targets: CRI1b – Organizing Information**

Distribute the Debate Planning Worksheet. Students create a two-column chart: **Arguments FOR homework** and **Arguments AGAINST homework**. They brainstorm at least three points per side independently, then share with a partner to fill gaps.

Debrief as a class by building a shared chart on the board. Ask: *"Which arguments are strongest? How should we order them — most obvious first, or most powerful first?"*

**Assessment:** Students at Level 2 will group related ideas (e.g., clustering all "time-related" arguments together) and explain why certain arguments carry more logical weight. Students at Level 1 list points without prioritizing or connecting them.

---

### Part 3 — Evaluating the Evidence (12 minutes)
**Targets: CRI2a – Assessing Validity | CRI2b – Assessing Quality**

Distribute the Evidence Cards. Each card contains a claim about homework (e.g., *"A 2006 Duke University study found little correlation between homework and achievement in elementary school"* vs. *"Most teachers agree homework builds discipline"*).

Students rate each card on a simple 1–3 scale: **1 = Weak evidence, 2 = Okay evidence, 3 = Strong evidence**, and write one sentence explaining their rating.

Discuss as a class: What makes a source credible? Introduce three quick criteria: **Author/institution, date, specificity of data.**

**Assessment:** A Level 2 student will explain, *"This card cites a named researcher and a specific study year, so it's more reliable than one that just says 'most teachers think.'"* A Level 1 student scores cards without being able to articulate why. A Level -1 student treats all cards as equally valid.

---

### Part 4 — Weighing Pros and Cons (10 minutes)
**Targets: CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons | CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning**

Students return to their worksheets and now rank their top three arguments on each side. They must answer in writing: *"Which side has stronger overall evidence, and why? What would have to be true for the other side to win?"*

The second question forces students to consider conditions and trade-offs, not just pick a winner. Bring 2–3 students to share their reasoning aloud. Prompt deeper thinking: *"Is this true for all students equally? What about students with after-school jobs or learning differences?"*

**Assessment:** Level 2 students acknowledge complexity (*"Homework may help high-achieving students but hurt those with fewer resources at home"*). Level 1 students pick a side without nuance. Level -1 students dismiss one side entirely without engaging its strongest arguments.

---

### Part 5 — Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes)
**Targets: CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking**

Students complete a structured reflection prompt on their exit ticket:

1. *What did you believe about homework before this lesson?*
2. *What changed your thinking, and why?*
3. *What would you need to learn more about to be even more confident in your position?*

**Assessment:** Level 2 responses name a specific moment of shift (*"When I realized I was only thinking about my own experience, not the evidence"*). Level 1 responses say thinking changed without explaining how. Level -1 responses show no shift in awareness regardless of the lesson's content.

---

### Closing (3 minutes)

Ask one volunteer to summarize the class's strongest conclusion in one sentence. Reinforce: *"Good critical thinking isn't about being right — it's about knowing WHY you believe what you believe."*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Debating the role of homework in schools

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify key arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support different perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of various information sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of different approaches to homework.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form a preliminary stance on the topic.
*   Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the discussion.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (short articles/summaries presenting arguments for and against homework, with varied source types – e.g., excerpt from an educational research summary, a school district's rationale, a student blog post, a parent's opinion piece).
*   Handout 2: T-chart graphic organizer
*   Exit Tickets
*   Markers/pens

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, everyone! Today, we're going to tackle a topic that affects all of us: homework. How many of you love homework? How many of you wish it didn't exist? (Allow for a quick show of hands and brief, informal comments). It's clear there are strong feelings on both sides. Today, we're going to explore this question: 'Should schools have homework?' and use our critical thinking skills to understand the different perspectives."

**II. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) & Initial Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1a: Clarifying information):** Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
    *   **Teacher:** "You'll find several short texts here, each presenting different viewpoints or facts about homework. Your first task is to read through these texts individually. As you read, I want you to do two things:
        1.  **Highlight** the main arguments or key pieces of information presented in each text.
        2.  **Circle any words or phrases** you don't understand, and try to define them using context or by asking a classmate."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1a):** Circulate around the room. Observe students' highlighting – are they identifying the core claims? Listen to their discussions about unfamiliar vocabulary. Are they attempting to clarify ideas precisely? I will look for evidence that students are accurately identifying and defining key terms and arguments, rather than just random words or superficial details. For example, if a student highlights "Homework reinforces classroom learning" and can explain what "reinforces" means in this context, they are clarifying information precisely.

**III. Organizing Information (CRI1b) & Assessing Validity/Quality (CRI2a, CRI2b) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1b: Organizing information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've read and highlighted, let's organize this information. In your small groups (3-4 students), use Handout 2, the T-chart, to list the 'Pros of Homework' on one side and the 'Cons of Homework' on the other. Make sure to draw your points directly from the texts."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1b):** Review students' T-charts. Are the points clearly categorized? Is the information presented in a structured and consistent manner? For example, are all "pros" on one side and "cons" on the other, without mixing?
*   **Activity (CRI2a: Assessing validity of information & CRI2b: Assessing quality of information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "As you're filling out your T-chart, I want you to also think critically about *where* this information is coming from. Look at the source listed for each text on Handout 1. Discuss in your groups:
        *   'How reliable do you think this source is?' (CRI2a)
        *   'Does this source provide high-quality information, or is it more opinion-based?' (CRI2b)
        *   'Which pieces of evidence from the texts do you think are the *strongest* to support either side, and why?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI2a & CRI2b):** Listen to group discussions. Do students question the authority of a blog post versus a research summary? Do they identify potential biases? Are they able to articulate *why* certain information is more credible or relevant than others? I will look for students who can explain, for instance, "The study from the Department of Education is high-quality because it's based on research data, not just one person's feelings," demonstrating sophisticated judgment.

**IV. Weighing Pros & Cons & Applying Reasoning (CRI3a, CRI4a) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've organized and evaluated the information, let's delve deeper. In your groups, discuss the following:
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools completely eliminated homework?'
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools assigned a lot more homework?'
        *   'Consider the perspectives of different stakeholders: students, teachers, parents. How might each group weigh these pros and cons differently?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI3a):** Circulate and listen to group discussions. Are students moving beyond simple listing to actively compare and evaluate the trade-offs? Do they consider the broader impact and different viewpoints? I'll note if students are discussing consequences like "If there's no homework, students might forget what they learned faster" or "Too much homework could lead to burnout and less family time," showing they are systematically evaluating implications.
*   **Activity (CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):**
    *   **Teacher:** "After your group discussion, I want each of you to individually write a short paragraph (3-4 sentences) stating your preliminary stance on the question: 'Should schools have homework?' You can say 'yes,' 'no,' or 'it depends/modified approach.' Crucially, you must provide at least two clear reasons to support your stance, drawing directly from the information we've discussed and evaluated today."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI4a):** Collect and read the individual paragraphs. Is the reasoning logical and coherent? Do their conclusions align with the evidence they present? Are there any unsupported claims or faulty logic? I will look for clear connections between their stance and the evidence provided, e.g., "I believe schools should have *some* homework because it helps reinforce learning (Pro 1) and teaches responsibility (Pro 2), as mentioned in the research summary."

**V. Reflecting on Thinking Processes (CRI5a) & Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes):**
    *   **Teacher:** "To conclude our lesson, I want you to reflect on your own thinking today. Please complete the exit ticket."
    *   **Exit Ticket Prompt:**
        *   "My initial thought on homework before this lesson was..."
        *   "After today's lesson, my thinking has [changed / stayed the same / become more nuanced] because..."
        *   "One thing I found challenging about evaluating the information or forming my opinion was..., and I tried to overcome it by..."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI5a):** Collect and review exit tickets. Do students demonstrate metacognitive awareness? Can they articulate how their understanding evolved or deepened? Do they identify specific challenges in their thinking and describe strategies they used to address them? I will look for responses that show genuine self-awareness, such as "Initially, I just hated homework, but after seeing the arguments about responsibility, I now think a little bit is okay. It was hard to let go of my bias, but I tried to focus only on the facts from the research."

## Candidate 3
Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?  
Grade: 7th

Topic: Exploring the pros and cons of homework in schools through critical thinking  

---

**Lesson Objectives:**  
- Engage students in clarifying and organizing information about homework.  
- Assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.  
- Weigh pros and cons of homework policies.  
- Apply sound reasoning to form and support a decision.  
- Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the lesson.  

---

### Lesson Outline and Activities

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a) – 10 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Begin with a brief class discussion: “What is homework? Why do schools assign it?”  
- Present a short, clear definition of homework and common reasons for and against it.  
- Students work in pairs to list what they know about homework and any questions they have.  
*Assessment:*  
- Observe students’ ability to identify relevant information about homework.  
- Look for students who clarify ideas precisely by asking thoughtful questions or rephrasing concepts to deepen understanding (e.g., “Homework helps practice skills, but can it cause stress?”).  

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Provide students with a graphic organizer (T-chart) labeled “Pros of Homework” and “Cons of Homework.”  
- In small groups, students gather information from a brief, balanced text about homework and place facts and opinions into the chart.  
*Assessment:*  
- Check if students arrange information logically and consistently in the chart.  
- Note if they group similar ideas together and maintain clear categories without mixing unrelated points.  

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Introduce two short excerpts: one from a teacher’s blog and one from a research study on homework effects.  
- Guide students to identify the source of each excerpt and discuss which might be more reliable and why.  
- Students work in pairs to cross-check one fact from the teacher’s blog with the research study.  
*Assessment:*  
- Listen for students’ ability to identify sources and evaluate their credibility.  
- Look for evidence that students critically compare information rather than accepting it at face value.  

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b) – 5 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Discuss characteristics of high-quality information (e.g., evidence-based, unbiased, current).  
- Students review their notes and decide which pieces of information are most trustworthy and relevant to the homework debate.  
*Assessment:*  
- Observe students’ judgment in selecting authoritative information.  
- Note if they can explain why some sources or facts are more reliable or useful than others.  

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a) – 10 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Groups use their organized information to discuss the advantages and disadvantages of having homework.  
- Each group creates a list of trade-offs, considering short-term and long-term effects on students, teachers, and families.  
- Groups share their evaluations with the class.  
*Assessment:*  
- Assess whether students systematically evaluate both sides and consider multiple perspectives.  
- Look for discussion of implications beyond immediate effects (e.g., “Homework can improve skills but may reduce free time and increase stress”).  

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a) – 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Individually, students write a short paragraph stating their position on homework (for or against) and support it with reasons from the lesson.  
- Encourage them to anticipate counterarguments and address them logically.  
*Assessment:*  
- Review paragraphs for clear, logical reasoning that shows understanding of complexities.  
- Check if students avoid fallacies and unsupported claims, and if they acknowledge alternative views.  

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a) – 6 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- End with a reflective journal prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you decide your position?”  
- Invite volunteers to share reflections.  
*Assessment:*  
- Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing how new information influenced their views or how they adjusted their reasoning.  
- Note if students mention strategies they used to think critically or challenges they faced.
