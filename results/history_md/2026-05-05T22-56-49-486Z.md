## Candidate 1
# Should Schools Have Homework?

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** English Language Arts / Critical Thinking

---

## Learning Objectives

- **Analyze** multiple perspectives on the homework debate by identifying and clarifying key claims from different sources.
- **Evaluate** the validity and quality of evidence used to support or oppose homework policies.
- **Apply** structured reasoning to weigh trade-offs and reach a defensible position on a real-world issue.
- **Reflect** on how personal assumptions and biases shaped their thinking throughout the lesson.

---

## Materials

- Two short reading excerpts (1 page each): one pro-homework article excerpt, one anti-homework research summary
- "Claim & Evidence" graphic organizer (one per student)
- Pros and Cons T-chart worksheet
- Whiteboard or shared digital display
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- Exit ticket slip (printed or digital)
- Timer visible to students

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — 5 Minutes

The teacher displays this prompt on the board: *"Do you have homework tonight? Do you think that's fair? Write 3 sentences explaining your gut reaction."* Students write independently in their notebooks without consulting peers.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** This activates prior assumptions before any new information is introduced. Students surface their instinctive beliefs, creating a baseline they will return to at the end of the lesson.

**Assessment Note:** The teacher circulates and notes whether students simply state a preference (Level 1) or acknowledge uncertainty and competing considerations in their opening sentences (Level 2).

---

### Source Introduction & Clarification — 10 Minutes

The teacher distributes the two reading excerpts. Students read independently, then underline one sentence per excerpt that they consider the *clearest, most important claim*. Pairs then share their chosen sentences and explain in one spoken sentence why that claim matters.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI1a, CRI1b):** Students practice identifying and articulating precise claims (CRI1a) and begin sorting information into a logical structure by distinguishing the central argument of each source (CRI1b).

**Assessment Note — CRI1a:** Level 1 students underline a general statement; Level 2 students underline a specific, evidence-backed claim and can explain why it is more precise than surrounding sentences. **Assessment Note — CRI1b:** Level 1 students list claims loosely; Level 2 students explicitly label which claim is the main argument versus supporting detail.

---

### Source Credibility Analysis — 10 Minutes

The teacher reveals brief source metadata for both excerpts (author credentials, publication type, date, funding source). Students complete two rows of their graphic organizer: *"Why should I trust this source?"* and *"What might make this source biased or limited?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI2a, CRI2b):** Students cross-check source features against credibility criteria (CRI2a) and make judgment calls about the depth and reliability of the information provided (CRI2b).

**Assessment Note — CRI2a:** Level 1 students identify one credibility factor; Level 2 students name at least two factors and explain how those factors interact. **Assessment Note — CRI2b:** Level 1 students say a source "seems reliable"; Level 2 students articulate a specific reason the source may be authoritative *and* one concrete limitation.

---

### Pros & Cons Structured Analysis — 10 Minutes

Students complete a T-chart individually, listing at least three pros and three cons of assigning homework. They then star the single strongest argument on each side and write one sentence explaining *why* it outweighs the others.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI3a):** Students move beyond listing to evaluating trade-offs, considering factors such as equity, learning outcomes, student wellbeing, and family impact.

**Assessment Note:** Level 1 students list surface-level points (e.g., "homework takes too long"). Level 2 students identify a systemic implication (e.g., "homework disadvantages students without quiet study space at home, making it an equity issue, not just a time issue").

---

### Small-Group Structured Debate — 12 Minutes

Groups of four are assigned a position (pro or anti-homework) regardless of personal belief. Each group has 4 minutes to build their argument using only evidence from the sources, then 4 minutes to respond to a counterargument posed by the teacher. The final 4 minutes are open discussion where students may argue their *actual* position.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI4a):** Students must construct logical arguments under constraint, anticipate counterarguments, and distinguish evidence-based reasoning from opinion.

**Assessment Note:** Level 1 students make claims without linking them to source evidence. Level 2 students explicitly cite a source, acknowledge the opposing point, and explain why their reasoning still holds.

---

### Metacognitive Reflection & Exit Ticket — 8 Minutes

Students return to their warm-up writing and respond in writing: *"Has your position changed? Identify one assumption you held at the start that the sources challenged. What would you need to know to feel more confident in your final answer?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** Students directly compare their initial thinking to their current reasoning, demonstrating awareness of how evidence and structured analysis shifted their perspective.

**Assessment Note:** Level 1 students state whether their opinion changed but do not explain why. Level 2 students name a specific assumption, link it to a source or discussion moment, and identify a genuine remaining gap in their understanding.

---

## Materials  
- Printed or digital copies of two short articles presenting opposing views on homework (one pro-homework, one anti-homework)  
- Whiteboard or chart paper and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Exit ticket slips or digital form for reflection  
- Timer or clock  

---

## Lesson Outline  

### 1. Warm-Up: Quick Write and Share — 5 Minutes  
**What happens:** Students write a brief response to the prompt: “What do you think about homework? Is it helpful or harmful?” Then, volunteers share their ideas aloud.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) by encouraging students to identify and express their initial thoughts clearly.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Student states a general opinion with some relevant reasons. Level 2 evidence: Student precisely clarifies their ideas with specific examples or explanations.  

---

### 2. Introducing the Topic and Sources — 7 Minutes  
**What happens:** Teacher introduces the question “Should schools have homework?” and distributes two short articles presenting opposing views. Students skim the texts individually, highlighting key points.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI1b (Organizing information) as students identify relevant information and begin to structure their understanding.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Student highlights some relevant points but with limited clarity or organization. Level 2 evidence: Student highlights key ideas precisely and notes how the information is structured (e.g., pros vs. cons).  

---

### 3. Pair Work: Comparing and Organizing Arguments — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:** In pairs, students discuss the main arguments from each article and create a two-column chart listing pros and cons of homework. They organize points logically under each heading.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI1b (Organizing information) and CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons). Students arrange information to strengthen logical flow and compare alternative perspectives.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Chart includes basic pros and cons with minimal explanation. Level 2 evidence: Chart shows well-organized, logically ordered points with clear comparisons and some analysis of implications.  

---

### 4. Source Evaluation Activity — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:** Teacher guides a class discussion on how to assess the validity and quality of information. Students evaluate the two articles by considering the authors’ credentials, evidence used, and possible biases.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity) and CRI2b (Assessing quality). Students learn to cross-check and judge the reliability and relevance of sources.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Student identifies source type or author but with limited evaluation. Level 2 evidence: Student critically analyzes source credibility and evidence quality, citing specific examples.  

---

### 5. Structured Debate Preparation — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:** Students form small groups and prepare arguments either for or against homework, using their charts and source evaluations. They plan to present their reasoning clearly and anticipate counterarguments.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) and CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons). Students develop sophisticated reasoning and consider complexities in decision-making.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Group presents basic reasoning with some logical gaps. Level 2 evidence: Group articulates nuanced arguments, anticipates opposing views, and supports claims with evidence.  

---

### 6. Group Debate and Discussion — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:** Groups present their arguments in a structured debate format. After presentations, the class discusses the strengths and weaknesses of each side’s reasoning.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Reinforces CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) and CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons) through active reasoning and evaluation of alternatives.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Student contributes simple points without elaboration. Level 2 evidence: Student uses clear, logical reasoning and responds thoughtfully to counterarguments.  

---

### 7. Reflection and Metacognition — 5 Minutes  
**What happens:** Students individually complete an exit ticket reflecting on how their thinking about homework changed during the lesson and what strategies helped them understand different viewpoints.  
**Critical Thinking Connection:** Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes). Students demonstrate metacognitive awareness by evaluating their own reasoning and learning strategies.  
**Assessment Note:** Level 1 evidence: Student mentions personal opinion or reasoning without depth. Level 2 evidence: Student articulates specific changes in thinking and describes how they adjusted their approach.
