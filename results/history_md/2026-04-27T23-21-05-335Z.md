## Generator
# The Curious Arguer: Asking Better Questions to Write Better Arguments

## Lesson Overview

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Subject:** ELA / Writing
**Central Theme:** Curiosity as the engine of strong argumentative writing

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Explain how curiosity strengthens argumentative writing by helping writers anticipate opposing views
2. Generate genuine questions about a controversial topic before forming a claim
3. Draft an argument that includes a clear claim, supporting reasons, relevant evidence, and an acknowledged counterargument
4. Organize their reasoning logically using a structured outline

---

## Materials Needed

- Whiteboard or projector
- Printed "Curiosity Question Starters" handout (one per student)
- Printed "Argument Blueprint" graphic organizer (one per student)
- Sticky notes (2 per student, different colors — e.g., yellow and pink)
- Pencils/pens
- Optional: Timer visible to students

---

## Vocabulary to Preview

- **Claim** — the position or argument you are defending
- **Evidence** — facts, data, examples, or expert opinions that support your claim
- **Counterargument** — a view that opposes your claim
- **Rebuttal** — your response to the counterargument
- **Curiosity** — a genuine desire to understand something more deeply, including ideas you disagree with

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### **OPENING — Hook and Introduction** *(8 minutes)*

**Step 1: The "Wrong Side" Challenge (5 minutes)**

Begin class with this prompt written on the board:

> *"You have 90 seconds. Write down every reason someone might disagree with your favorite opinion — about anything."*

Give students 90 seconds to write silently. Then ask 3–4 volunteers to share.

After sharing, ask the class:

> *"How did it feel to think from the other side? Was it uncomfortable? Interesting? Did it change anything about your own view?"*

Allow brief discussion (about 2 minutes). Guide students toward this insight: **thinking about what you don't believe requires curiosity.** You have to genuinely wonder, *"Why would a reasonable person think differently than me?"*

**Step 2: Introduce the Big Idea (3 minutes)**

Tell students:

> *"Today we're going to talk about one of the most underrated tools in writing — and in life. It's not a fancy vocabulary word or a grammar rule. It's curiosity. The best arguers in history — lawyers, scientists, journalists, debaters — aren't just people with strong opinions. They're people who ask relentless questions, especially about ideas they disagree with. Today, you're going to use curiosity to write a stronger argument than you ever have before."*

Write this equation on the board and leave it up for the whole lesson:

> **Curiosity + Evidence + Logic = Powerful Argument**

---

### **DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Teaching the Skill** *(12 minutes)*

**Step 3: The Curious vs. The Certain (5 minutes)**

Present two fictional student writers to the class. Read these short descriptions aloud or display them:

---

**Writer A — "Certain Carl"**
> *"Homework should be banned. It's stressful and takes too long. Everyone knows it's bad. Anyone who disagrees is wrong."*

**Writer B — "Curious Camille"**
> *"Homework should be significantly reduced for middle school students. I want to understand this topic fully, so I'm asking: What is homework actually supposed to accomplish? Does it work? Who benefits and who doesn't? What would teachers and parents worry about if homework disappeared? After researching these questions, I found..."*

---

Ask students:

- *"Which writer do you trust more? Why?"*
- *"Which writer probably has stronger evidence? Why?"*
- *"What did Camille do before she started writing her argument?"*

Lead students to see that **Camille's curiosity led her to ask questions first, which made her argument richer, more honest, and more persuasive.**

**Step 4: Introduce the Curiosity Question Starters (4 minutes)**

Distribute the **Curiosity Question Starters** handout. Walk through it briefly:

---

**Curiosity Question Starters for Argumentative Writing**

*Use these before you decide your final position:*

- What is the real purpose of ___?
- Who is most affected by ___, and how?
- What evidence exists on both sides of ___?
- What would someone who disagrees with me say, and why might they be right?
- What am I assuming that might not be true?
- What would change my mind about this?
- What are the consequences if I'm wrong?
- What does the research actually say, versus what people assume?

---

Tell students: *"Curious people ask these questions BEFORE they decide what they believe. That's what makes their arguments so hard to argue against — they've already thought of the objections."*

**Step 5: Model the Writing Structure (3 minutes)**

Quickly walk through the **Argument Blueprint** graphic organizer on the board:

| Section | What Goes Here |
|---|---|
| **Hook** | A question, surprising fact, or scenario that grabs attention |
| **Claim** | Your clear position statement |
| **Reason 1 + Evidence** | First supporting point with proof |
| **Reason 2 + Evidence** | Second supporting point with proof |
| **Counterargument** | What opponents say — stated fairly |
| **Rebuttal** | Why your position still stands despite the counterargument |
| **Conclusion** | Reinforce claim, call to action or final thought |

Say: *"Notice that the counterargument isn't optional. A curious writer ALWAYS makes room for the other side. It actually makes your argument stronger, not weaker."*

---

### **GUIDED PRACTICE — Whole Class Activity** *(10 minutes)*

**Step 6: The Sticky Note Debate (10 minutes)**

Write this topic on the board:

> **"Middle school students should be allowed to use their phones during lunch."**

Tell students this is their practice topic. They are NOT writing their full argument yet — they are practicing **curiosity first.**

**Yellow sticky note:** Students write down 2–3 genuine questions they have about this topic using the Curiosity Question Starters. They should write questions they actually don't know the answer to.
*(3 minutes)*

**Pink sticky note:** Students write what they think the strongest argument AGAINST their own gut opinion would be — even if they have to argue against themselves.
*(3 minutes)*

Have students briefly share their yellow sticky note questions with a partner. Then ask 3–4 students to share their pink sticky note counterarguments with the class.

Discuss as a class:

> *"Did anyone change their mind even a little bit after trying to argue the other side? Did anyone find a better way to explain their own position because they understood the opposition better?"*

*(4 minutes)*

Emphasize: *"This is exactly what real debaters, lawyers, and scientists do. They try to break their own arguments before someone else does."*

---

### **INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Student Writing** *(18 minutes)*

**Step 7: Choose a Topic and Ask Before You Write (5 minutes)**

Distribute the **Argument Blueprint** graphic organizer. Present students with the following topic choices (or allow them to propose their own with quick teacher approval):

1. Schools should have a four-day school week.
2. Students should be required to learn a second language starting in elementary school.
3. Athletes should be required to maintain a passing GPA to participate in sports.
4. Schools should eliminate letter grades and use written feedback only.

**Before students fill in the Blueprint**, they must complete the top section:

> **"Curiosity First" Box — Write 3 genuine questions about your topic before stating your claim. These should be questions you'd actually want answered."**

Circulate and ensure students are genuinely questioning, not just restating their opinion as a question. Push students who write "Isn't homework bad?" to go deeper: *"Why do teachers assign homework in the first place? What would students lose if it disappeared?"*

**Step 8: Draft the Argument Blueprint (13 minutes)**

Students work independently to complete their Argument Blueprint graphic organizer. This is a structured outline, not a full essay — they are filling in the key components of their argument.

**Circulate and conference briefly with students. Suggested prompts:**

- *"What's your strongest piece of evidence? How do you know it's reliable?"*
- *"What would a parent or teacher say against your argument? Have you written that down?"*
- *"Does your rebuttal actually respond to the counterargument, or does it ignore it?"*
- *"Read me your claim. Is it specific enough that someone could disagree with it?"*

**If students finish early:** Ask them to add a second counterargument, or challenge them to write a "hook" sentence that opens with a surprising question or fact.

---

### **CLOSING — Reflection and Synthesis** *(7 minutes)*

**Step 9: The Curiosity Debrief (4 minutes)**

Bring the class back together. Ask:

- *"Show of hands — did asking questions first change your claim at all? Did it make it more specific, or did you choose different evidence?"*
- *"What was the hardest part of writing the counterargument honestly?"*
- *"If you didn't know much about your topic, what kinds of questions would you want to research?"*

Affirm students' responses and drive home this key message:

> *"The most dangerous writer — in the best possible way — is one who knows the other side better than the other side knows itself. That's what curiosity does. It makes your argument bulletproof."*

**Step 10: Exit Ticket (3 minutes)**

Students respond to ONE of the following prompts on a half-sheet of paper or the back of their Blueprint:

> **Option A:** In one sentence, state your claim. In another sentence, write the strongest argument against it. In a third sentence, explain why your claim still holds.

> **Option B:** Explain in 3–4 sentences how asking questions before writing helped (or challenged) you today.

Collect exit tickets as students leave. Use them to assess who is ready to draft a full essay and who needs additional scaffolding on claim formation or counterargument acknowledgment.

---

## Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Pre-fill the "Counterargument" section of the Blueprint with a sentence stem: *"Some people believe that ___ because ___."*
- Provide a word bank of evidence transition phrases: *"According to..., Research shows..., For example..., Studies indicate..."*
- Allow partner work during independent practice with teacher monitoring

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to include TWO counterarguments with rebuttals
- Ask them to evaluate the quality of their own evidence: *"Is this a fact, an opinion, or a statistic? How would you find a reliable source to back this up?"*
- Invite them to write a full introductory paragraph using their Blueprint

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the Curiosity Question Starters in their home language if possible
- Allow them to brainstorm in their home language before translating key ideas
- Pair with a supportive partner during the sticky note activity

---

## Teacher Notes

**On facilitating genuine curiosity:**
The most common mistake students make is performing curiosity rather than practicing it — writing fake questions they already know the answer to. Push back gently but consistently: *"That's a great start. Now ask the question that makes you a little uncomfortable."* The goal is to cultivate intellectual humility alongside argumentative skill.

**On the counterargument:**
Many students feel that including the other side "weakens" their argument. Spend a moment reinforcing the opposite: in law, science, journalism, and academic writing, failing to acknowledge opposing views signals weak thinking. Acknowledging the counterargument shows confidence and credibility.

**Pacing note:**
If the class runs behind, the sticky note activity (Step 6) can be shortened to 7 minutes by skipping the partner share and going directly to 2–3 whole-class shares.

---

**Curiosity Question Starters for Argumentative Writing**

*Use these before you decide your final position:*

- What is the real purpose of ___?
- Who is most affected by ___, and how?
- What evidence exists on both sides of ___?
- What would someone who disagrees with me say, and why might they be right?
- What am I assuming that might not be true?
- What would change my mind about this?
- What are the consequences if I'm wrong?
- What does the research actually say, versus what people assume?

---

Tell students: *\"Curious people ask these questions BEFORE they decide what they believe. That's what makes their arguments so hard to argue against — they've already thought of the objections.\"*

**Step 5: Model the Writing Structure (3 minutes)**

Quickly walk through the **Argument Blueprint** graphic organizer on the board:

| Section | What Goes Here |
|---|---|
| **Hook** | A question, surprising fact, or scenario that grabs attention |
| **Claim** | Your clear position statement |
| **Reason 1 + Evidence** | First supporting point with proof |
| **Reason 2 + Evidence** | Second supporting point with proof |
| **Counterargument** | What opponents say — stated fairly |
| **Rebuttal** | Why your position still stands despite the counterargument |
| **Conclusion** | Reinforce claim, call to action or final thought |

Say: *\"Notice that the counterargument isn't optional. A curious writer ALWAYS makes room for the other side. It actually makes your argument stronger, not weaker.\"*

---

### **GUIDED PRACTICE — Whole Class Activity** *(10 minutes)*

**Step 6: The Sticky Note Debate (10 minutes)**

Write this topic on the board:

> **\"Middle school students should be allowed to use their phones during lunch.\"**

Tell students this is their practice topic. They are NOT writing their full argument yet — they are practicing **curiosity first.**

**Yellow sticky note:** Students write down 2–3 genuine questions they have about this topic using the Curiosity Question Starters. They should write questions they actually don't know the answer to.
*(3 minutes)*

**Pink sticky note:** Students write what they think the strongest argument AGAINST their own gut opinion would be — even if they have to argue against themselves.
*(3 minutes)*

Have students briefly share their yellow sticky note questions with a partner. Then ask 3–4 students to share their pink sticky note counterarguments with the class.

Discuss as a class:

> *\"Did anyone change their mind even a little bit after trying to argue the other side? Did anyone find a better way to explain their own position because they understood the opposition better?\"*

*(4 minutes)*

Emphasize: *\"This is exactly what real debaters, lawyers, and scientists do. They try to break their own arguments before someone else does.\"*

---

### **INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Student Writing** *(18 minutes)*

**Step 7: Choose a Topic and Ask Before You Write (5 minutes)**

Distribute the **Argument Blueprint** graphic organizer. Present students with the following topic choices (or allow them to propose their own with quick teacher approval):

1. Schools should have a four-day school week.
2. Students should be required to learn a second language starting in elementary school.
3. Athletes should be required to maintain a passing GPA to participate in sports.
4. Schools should eliminate letter grades and use written feedback only.

**Before students fill in the Blueprint**, they must complete the top section:

> **\"Curiosity First\" Box — Write 3 genuine questions about your topic before stating your claim. These should be questions you'd actually want answered.\"**

Circulate and ensure students are genuinely questioning, not just restating their opinion as a question. Push students who write \"Isn't homework bad?\" to go deeper: *\"Why do teachers assign homework in the first place? What would students lose if it disappeared?\"*

**Step 8: Draft the Argument Blueprint (13 minutes)**

Students work independently to complete their Argument Blueprint graphic organizer. This is a structured outline, not a full essay — they are filling in the key components of their argument.

**Circulate and conference briefly with students. Suggested prompts:**

- *\"What's your strongest piece of evidence? How do you know it's reliable?\"*
- *\"What would a parent or teacher say against your argument? Have you written that down?\"*
- *\"Does your rebuttal actually respond to the counterargument, or does it ignore it?\"*
- *\"Read me your claim. Is it specific enough that someone could disagree with it?\"*

**If students finish early:** Ask them to add a second counterargument, or challenge them to write a \"hook\" sentence that opens with a surprising question or fact.

---

### **CLOSING — Reflection and Synthesis** *(7 minutes)*

**Step 9: The Curiosity Debrief (4 minutes)**

Bring the class back together. Ask:

- *\"Show of hands — did asking questions first change your claim at all? Did it make it more specific, or did you choose different evidence?\"*
- *\"What was the hardest part of writing the counterargument honestly?\"*
- *\"If you didn't know much about your topic, what kinds of questions would you want to research?\"*

Affirm students' responses and drive home this key message:

> *\"The most dangerous writer — in the best possible way — is one who knows the other side better than the other side knows itself. That's what curiosity does. It makes your argument bulletproof.\"*

**Step 10: Exit Ticket (3 minutes)**

Students respond to ONE of the following prompts on a half-sheet of paper or the back of their Blueprint:

> **Option A:** In one sentence, state your claim. In another sentence, write the strongest argument against it. In a third sentence, explain why your claim still holds.

> **Option B:** Explain in 3–4 sentences how asking questions before writing helped (or challenged) you today.

Collect exit tickets as students leave. Use them to assess who is ready to draft a full essay and who needs additional scaffolding on claim formation or counterargument acknowledgment.

---

## Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Pre-fill the \"Counterargument\" section of the Blueprint with a sentence stem: *\"Some people believe that ___ because ___.\"*
- Provide a word bank of evidence transition phrases: *\"According to..., Research shows..., For example..., Studies indicate...\"*
- Allow partner work during independent practice with teacher monitoring

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to include TWO counterarguments with rebuttals
- Ask them to evaluate the quality of their own evidence: *\"Is this a fact, an opinion, or a statistic? How would you find a reliable source to back this up?\"*
- Invite them to write a full introductory paragraph using their Blueprint

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the Curiosity Question
