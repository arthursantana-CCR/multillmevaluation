## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate, compare, and reflect on multiple representations.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Rate Cards" (3 scenarios per pair)
- Graph paper
- Student journals
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Opening Hook — *What's the Deal?* (8 minutes)

Display two offers on the board:

> **Store A:** 3 water bottles for $6 | **Store B:** 5 water bottles for $9

Ask: *"Which store gives you a better deal? How do you know?"*

Allow 2 minutes of quiet thinking, then brief partner discussion. Do not confirm answers yet. Tell students: *"By the end of today, you'll have the tools to answer this confidently and explain exactly why."*

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
Students must identify *what information matters* (price per unit, not total price). Prompt with: *"What exactly are we comparing?"* Observe whether students articulate the relevant quantities clearly or conflate total cost with unit rate. Assess by listening to partner conversations and cold-calling for precise verbal explanations.

---

### Direct Instruction — *What Makes a Relationship Proportional?* (10 minutes)

Using the whiteboard, introduce a table showing hours worked vs. money earned ($10/hour). Build the table together (1 hr = $10, 2 hrs = $20, 3 hrs = $30).

Ask: *"What do you notice about the ratio each time?"* Guide students to the constant ratio (unit rate = constant of proportionality, k).

Introduce the equation: **y = kx**, and plot the values on a graph, emphasizing the line passes through the origin.

**CRI1b — Organizing Information**
Students copy the table, graph, and equation into their journals side by side. Explicitly require them to label each representation and draw arrows connecting equivalent information across forms. Assess whether students structure their notes to show logical connections between representations rather than treating each as isolated.

---

### Guided Practice — *Multiple Representations Challenge* (12 minutes)

Distribute Rate Cards. Each card presents one scenario (e.g., *"A car travels 55 miles per hour"*) in a single format. Student pairs must:
1. Identify the constant of proportionality
2. Create the missing three representations (table, graph, equation, or description)
3. Write one sentence explaining how they verified it is proportional

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
Students must cross-check their representations against each other — if their equation gives $y = 2x$ but their table shows inconsistent ratios, they must reconcile the conflict. Prompt: *"Does your graph agree with your table? How do you know it's correct?"* Assess by reviewing whether students catch and correct internal contradictions before sharing out.

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
During pair work, circulate and ask: *"How confident are you in your constant of proportionality? How did you find it?"* Students who simply guess versus students who systematically divide y/x across multiple rows demonstrate differentiated quality of reasoning. Assess depth of justification in their written sentence.

---

### Collaborative Debate — *Proportional or Not?* (12 minutes)

Post three new scenarios on the board:
- A taxi charges $2.50 base fee + $1.50/mile
- A recipe uses 2 cups of flour per dozen cookies
- A savings account starts with $50 and grows $10/week

Groups of four receive each scenario and must **vote and defend**: *Is this proportional or not?*

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
Students argue both sides before committing to a position. Require groups to name at least one reason *for* and one reason *against* before deciding. The taxi scenario is intentionally ambiguous — the base fee breaks proportionality. Assess whether students systematically test the constant ratio rather than relying on surface features like "it involves money."

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
Students must justify their final answer using *mathematical evidence* — not intuition. Prompt: *"Show me in the table where proportionality breaks down."* Assess quality of reasoning by requiring written justifications that cite specific values.

---

### Metacognitive Journal — *Think About Your Thinking* (8 minutes)

Students respond in journals to three prompts:
1. *Which representation (table, graph, equation) feels most natural to you, and why?*
2. *Where did you make an error today, and how did you catch it?*
3. *What question do you still have about proportional relationships?*

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
Prompt 2 directly targets metacognition — students must identify a specific mistake and their self-correction strategy. Assess depth by distinguishing surface responses (*"I got the wrong number"*) from reflective responses (*"I forgot the line must pass through the origin, so I re-checked my graph"*).

---

### Closing — Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students independently answer:
> *"Is y = 4x proportional? Show it two different ways and explain."*

This checks mastery and mirrors the day's critical thinking arc — represent, verify, and explain.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

---

**2. Guided Practice: Representing Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Present a proportional relationship: “A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour. How far does it travel in 2, 3, 4 hours?”  
- Create a table together: Hours vs. Miles.  
- Plot the points on graph paper and draw the line.  
- Write the equation: y = 60x.  
- Draw a diagram (e.g., a speedometer or a distance-time line).  
- Write a verbal description: “The car travels 60 miles every hour, so the distance is 60 times the number of hours.”  

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Organizing information (CRI1b)  
- *Activity:* Have students copy the table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description in their notebooks, noting how each form relates to the others.  
- *Observation:* Check if students arrange the information logically and consistently, e.g., table first, then graph, then equation, etc., or if their notes are scattered or confusing.

---

**3. Independent Practice: Matching Representations (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with several proportional relationships presented in mixed forms (some as tables, some as graphs, some as equations, some as verbal descriptions).  
- Students work individually or in pairs to match each representation to the others that describe the same proportional relationship.  
- Encourage students to explain their reasoning aloud or in writing.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Assessing validity and quality of information (CRI2a & CRI2b)  
- *Activity:* Students must decide if the representations truly show proportional relationships or if any are incorrect or misleading.  
- *Observation:* Listen for students who question the accuracy of the information, e.g., “This graph doesn’t start at zero, so it might not be proportional,” or “The equation doesn’t have a constant ratio.”  
- Assess if students use sound judgment to select high-quality, accurate representations or if they accept all information without critique.

---

**4. Group Discussion: Weighing Alternatives and Reasoning (10 minutes)**  
- Bring the class together to discuss one or two examples from the handout where representations might be confusing or ambiguous.  
- Ask: “What are the pros and cons of using a table versus a graph to show proportional relationships?”  
- “How does the equation help us understand the relationship better or worse than the diagram?”  
- “What reasoning did you use to decide if these were proportional?”

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Weighing pros and cons (CRI3a) and Applying sound reasoning (CRI4a)  
- *Activity:* Facilitate a discussion where students compare different representations and justify their preferences or critiques.  
- *Observation:* Note students who systematically evaluate trade-offs, considering clarity, ease of use, or long-term understanding.  
- Look for sophisticated reasoning that anticipates complexities, e.g., “Graphs show trends visually but might be misleading if scales aren’t clear.”

---

**5. Reflection and Metacognition (5 minutes)**  
- Ask students to write a short reflection: “How did you decide which representations showed proportional relationships? What strategies helped you understand the concept better?”  
- Encourage them to consider how their thinking changed during the lesson.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Reflecting on one’s thinking processes (CRI5a)  
- *Activity:* Written reflection on their reasoning and learning strategies.  
- *Observation:* Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing assumptions, adjusting strategies, or identifying confusion and how they resolved it.

---

**6. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Quick problem: Given a verbal description of a proportional relationship, students write the corresponding equation and sketch a graph.  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding.

### Final Output
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate, compare, and reflect on multiple representations.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Rate Cards" (3 scenarios per pair)
- Graph paper and rulers
- Student journals
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Opening Hook — *What's the Deal?* (8 minutes)

Display two offers on the board:

> **Store A:** 3 water bottles for $6 | **Store B:** 5 water bottles for $9

Ask: *"Which store gives you a better deal? How do you know?"*

Allow 2 minutes of quiet thinking, then brief partner discussion. Do not confirm answers yet. Tell students: *"By the end of today, you'll have the tools to answer this confidently and explain exactly why."*

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
Students must identify *what information matters* (price per unit, not total price). Prompt with: *"What exactly are we comparing? What information is relevant here?"* Students work in pairs for 1–2 minutes to define "proportional" in their own words, then share with the class to build a precise, collective definition emphasizing constant ratio.

*Observation:* Listen to partner conversations and cold-call for precise verbal explanations. Look for students who articulate relevant quantities clearly (e.g., "We need to find the cost per bottle, not just the total") rather than conflating total cost with unit rate.

---

### Direct Instruction — *What Makes a Relationship Proportional?* (10 minutes)

Using the whiteboard, introduce a table showing hours worked vs. money earned ($10/hour). Build the table together:

| Hours (x) | Money Earned (y) |
|-----------|-----------------|
| 1         | $10             |
| 2         | $20             |
| 3         | $30             |

Ask: *"What do you notice about the ratio y/x each time?"* Guide students to identify the constant ratio — this is the **constant of proportionality, k**.

Introduce the equation **y = kx**, and plot the values on a graph, emphasizing:
- The graph is a straight line
- The line passes through the origin (0, 0)

Connect all three forms explicitly: *"The table, the graph, and the equation all tell the same story — just in different languages."*

**CRI1b — Organizing Information**
Students copy the table, graph, and equation into their journals side by side. Explicitly require them to label each representation and draw arrows connecting equivalent information across forms (e.g., the constant ratio in the table corresponds to k in the equation and the slope of the graph).

*Observation:* Check student notes for correctly structured tables (independent variable first, consistent increments), properly labeled graphs, and visible connections drawn between representations. Assess whether students treat each form as part of a unified whole rather than isolated procedures.

---

### Guided Practice — *Multiple Representations Challenge* (12 minutes)

Distribute Rate Cards. Each card presents one scenario (e.g., *"A car travels 55 miles per hour"*) in a single format. Student pairs must:
1. Identify the constant of proportionality
2. Create the missing three representations (table, graph, equation, or verbal description)
3. Write one sentence explaining how they verified it is proportional

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
One Rate Card intentionally contains a mismatched graph (e.g., the graph does not pass through the origin, or plotted points contradict the table). Students must cross-check their representations against each other and identify the inconsistency.

Prompt: *"Does your graph agree with your table? How do you know it's correct? If they disagree, which one do you trust, and why?"*

*Observation:* Review whether students catch and correct internal contradictions before sharing out. Look for reasoning such as: *"The graph doesn't start at zero, but our table shows a constant ratio, so the graph must be wrong."*

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
During pair work, circulate and ask: *"How confident are you in your constant of proportionality? How did you find it?"* Students who systematically divide y/x across multiple rows demonstrate stronger reasoning than those who check only one pair of values.

*Observation:* Assess depth of justification in the written sentence. Look for students who apply the constant-ratio test rigorously rather than relying on a single data point.

---

### Collaborative Debate — *Proportional or Not?* (12 minutes)

Post three new scenarios on the board:
- A taxi charges $2.50 base fee + $1.50/mile
- A recipe uses 2 cups of flour per dozen cookies
- A savings account starts with $50 and grows $10/week

Groups of four receive each scenario and must **vote and defend**: *Is this proportional or not?*

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
Require groups to name at least one reason *for* and one reason *against* proportionality before committing to a final position. Then ask: *"Which representation — table, graph, or equation — is most useful for this specific scenario? What are the advantages and disadvantages of each?"*

The taxi scenario is intentionally designed to provoke debate — the base fee breaks proportionality, but students may initially assume it qualifies. Groups should test their reasoning using multiple representations before concluding.

*Observation:* Listen for systematic testing of the constant ratio rather than surface-level reasoning. Look for statements such as: *"The graph is good for visualizing the trend, but the equation is better for calculating exact values at large numbers."*

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
Students must justify their final answer using *mathematical evidence* — not intuition. Require written justifications that cite specific values.

Prompt: *"Show me in the table exactly where proportionality breaks down."*

*Observation:* Evaluate whether justifications correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) and link specific evidence to the conclusion. Distinguish between responses that state a conclusion versus those that demonstrate it with data.

---

### Metacognitive Journal — *Think About Your Thinking* (8 minutes)

Students respond in journals to three prompts:
1. *Which representation (table, graph, equation) feels most natural to you, and why?*
2. *Where did you make an error today, and how did you catch it? What did you do to fix it?*
3. *What question do you still have about proportional relationships?*

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
Prompt 2 directly targets metacognition — students must identify a specific mistake and articulate their self-correction strategy.

*Observation:* Distinguish surface responses (*"I got the wrong number"*) from genuinely reflective responses (*"I forgot the line must pass through the origin, so I re-checked my graph and found the error"*). Strong responses name a specific conceptual hurdle and propose a concrete strategy for future problems.

---

### Closing — Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students independently answer:

> *"Is y = 4x a proportional relationship? Show it in two different ways and explain how you know."*

> *"What was the most challenging part of today's lesson? What strategy helped you, or what would you do differently next time?"*

The first question checks mastery across representations; the second extends the metacognitive reflection from the journal, providing written evidence of self-awareness that can be reviewed individually.
