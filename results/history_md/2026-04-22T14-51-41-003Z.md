## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Chart paper or projector  
- Handout with a short argumentative writing prompt  
- Graphic organizer for argument writing (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)  
- Sticky notes  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Hook:** Ask students, “What is curiosity? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new or solve a problem?”  
- Write a few student responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain: Curiosity is the desire to learn or know more about something. It drives us to ask questions, explore ideas, and think deeply.  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Albert Einstein) and how their curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science but also in writing and thinking critically.

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us ask questions like “Why do I believe this?” and “What do others think?” which are essential for writing strong arguments.  
- Introduce the day’s writing goal: to write an argument that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, and also considers opposing views.

#### 3. **Mini-Lesson: Elements of an Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Using the whiteboard or projector, review the parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - **Claim:** The main point or opinion.  
  - **Reasons:** Why you believe the claim.  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support the reasons.  
  - **Counterclaim:** An opposing viewpoint.  
  - **Rebuttal:** Your response to the counterclaim.  
- Show a short sample paragraph and identify each part together as a class.

#### 4. **Curiosity-Driven Argument Prompt (5 minutes)**  
- Present the writing prompt:  
  *“Should schools have longer recess periods? Write an argument supporting your position.”*  
- Encourage students to be curious: What questions do they have about recess? What reasons might support or oppose longer recess?  
- Have students jot down questions or ideas on sticky notes.

#### 5. **Planning the Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Distribute the graphic organizer.  
- Guide students to:  
  - Write their claim (their position on longer recess).  
  - List at least two reasons supporting their claim.  
  - Find or think of evidence for each reason.  
  - Identify one possible counterclaim.  
  - Write a rebuttal to that counterclaim.  
- Circulate and assist students as needed.

#### 6. **Writing Time (10 minutes)**  
- Students write a complete argumentative paragraph using their organizer.  
- Remind them to use clear reasons, relevant evidence, and to acknowledge the opposing claim with a rebuttal.

#### 7. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class: How did curiosity help you think about different sides of the issue?  
- Close by reinforcing that curiosity helps us become better thinkers and writers by encouraging us to ask questions and consider multiple perspectives.

---

### Homework/Extension (Optional):  
Write a second paragraph expanding on the rebuttal or researching more evidence to support their claim.

---

**Summary:**  
This lesson combines the development of curiosity with the practice of writing argumentative paragraphs aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1. Students learn to ask questions, consider multiple viewpoints, and organize their reasoning logically to support their claims effectively.
