## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to analyze and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Recipe" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Opening Hook & Warm-Up (8 minutes)
Display two lemonade recipes on the board:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups lemon juice / 6 cups water
- **Recipe B:** 3 cups lemon juice / 8 cups water

Ask: *"Which recipe tastes more lemony? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Guide the class to compare ratios (1:3 vs. 3:8) and introduce the question: *"What makes two quantities proportional?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "more lemony" mathematically means by isolating the relevant ratio rather than comparing raw amounts.
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for students who clearly articulate ratio comparisons vs. those who say "Recipe B has more lemon juice." Record on observation checklist.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Define **proportional relationship**: two quantities where the ratio y/x is constant (the **constant of proportionality, k**). Demonstrate using Recipe A:

| Lemon (x) | Water (y) | y/x |
|-----------|-----------|-----|
| 2 | 6 | 3 |
| 4 | 12 | 3 |
| 6 | 18 | 3 |

Show the four representations:
- **Table:** constant ratio
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Verbal:** "For every 1 cup of lemon juice, use 3 cups of water."

Contrast with Recipe B's table to show it is NOT proportional (ratios differ).

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Modeling how to arrange the same relationship across four organized representations.
**Assessment:** Students copy the four-representation organizer into notes; check notebooks for clear, parallel structure.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Smoothie Detective" (12 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Recipe handout with three scenarios (table, graph, word problem). For each, students must:
1. Determine if it's proportional.
2. Identify k.
3. Translate into the other three representations.

Work the first one together; students complete #2 and #3 in pairs.

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity) & CRI2b (Assessing quality):** One scenario contains a "trap"—a table where ratios *appear* close but aren't equal (e.g., 4/2, 9/4, 14/6). Students must cross-check each ratio rather than assume.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How did you verify this is proportional? Did you check every pair?"* Note students who check all ratios vs. those who check only one.

---

### 4. Critical Thinking Challenge — "Pick the Best Plan" (12 minutes)
Present a real-world dilemma: *A student wants to earn money. Three job offers:*
- **Plan A:** $10 flat fee + $5/hour
- **Plan B:** $8/hour (proportional)
- **Plan C:** $7/hour first 5 hours, then $10/hour

In groups of 3, students:
1. Create tables and graphs for each plan.
2. Identify which is proportional and why.
3. Recommend a plan based on hours worked (2, 5, 10 hours).

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must compare proportional vs. non-proportional options and consider how the "best" choice changes with context.
**Assessment:** Collect group recommendation sheets. Look for explicit trade-off language: "Plan B is better if you work many hours because…" Score 1 (superficial) vs. 2 (systematic trade-off analysis).

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Recommendations must be justified mathematically using the constant of proportionality and computed values.
**Assessment:** Each group presents a 30-second justification. Use a rubric: faulty/no math reasoning (-1), partial (1), reasoning anticipates conditions like "depends on hours" (2).

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (11 minutes)

**Part A — Metacognitive Journal (6 min):** Students write responses to:
- *"What was your first instinct when comparing the plans? Did your thinking change? Why?"*
- *"What's one strategy you used that worked? One mistake you almost made?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Direct prompt to revisit and adjust reasoning.
**Assessment:** Review journals. Score 2 for entries showing strategy adjustment ("I assumed Plan A was best until I graphed it"); score 1 for surface-level reflection ("It was hard but I got it").

**Part B — Exit Ticket (5 min):** Students receive a single table and must:
1. State whether it represents a proportional relationship.
2. If yes, write the equation and describe the graph.
3. If no, explain what would need to change.

**Assessment:** Quick check for mastery of 7.RP.A.2; use to plan tomorrow's reteach groups.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed organizers; pair strategically.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to create their own "trap" non-proportional table for classmates.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price per item, etc.). Represent it in all four forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, create, and translate between five representations of proportional relationships while applying critical thinking skills to validate, organize, and reflect on mathematical data.

---

### **Part 1: The Paint Mixing Dilemma (Warm-Up & Organization) – 10 Minutes**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a verbal description: *"An artist mixes 2 cups of blue paint with 3 cups of yellow paint to make a specific shade of 'Forest Green.' The artist needs to make much larger batches of this exact color for a mural."* 

Students are asked to break down this verbal description and structure the data to find out how much yellow paint is needed if they use 4, 6, or 10 cups of blue paint.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   *Activity:* Students must rewrite the vague verbal description into a precise mathematical rule, clarifying that the ratio of blue to yellow must remain exactly 2:3 to maintain the color. 
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will circulate and observe students translating the word problem into a clear, precise mathematical statement (e.g., "For every 1 cup of blue, I need 1.5 cups of yellow").
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   *Activity:* Students create a T-chart (table) to organize the cups of blue paint versus yellow paint, scaling up the recipe.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will check student T-charts to ensure they have arranged the information with a logical flow, using consistent intervals (e.g., 2, 4, 6, 8) and correctly labeling the independent and dependent variables.

### **Part 2: Cross-Checking the Math (Guided Practice) – 15 Minutes**

**Activity:** 
The teacher projects a graph, a table, and an equation ($y = 1.5x$) created by a "fictional student" based on the paint scenario. The teacher also provides the official textbook definition of a proportional relationship (must have a constant ratio and pass through the origin on a graph). 

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   *Activity:* Students are tasked with rigorously cross-checking the fictional student's equation ($y = 1.5x$) against the textbook definition, their own T-charts, and the projected graph to prove whether the equation is mathematically valid.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will listen to group discussions, assessing whether students are explicitly referencing multiple authoritative sources (the textbook rule, the graph's origin, and the table's unit rate) to validate the equation, rather than just guessing.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   *Activity:* The teacher projects a scatterplot with several data points. Some points fall exactly on the grid intersections (e.g., 2, 3), while others are messy decimals placed between grid lines. Students must select the highest-quality data points to calculate the constant of proportionality.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will observe students demonstrating sophisticated judgment by deliberately selecting exact coordinate pairs (high-quality data) to prove proportionality, rather than using estimated points (low-quality data) that could lead to inaccurate calculations.

### **Part 3: The Representation Station (Collaborative Group Work) – 20 Minutes**

**Activity:** 
Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: *"You earn $15.00 per hour at a summer job."* They must represent this relationship using a diagram (like a tape diagram), a table, a graph, and an equation. 

Once all representations are created, the group must decide which representation is the best tool to figure out how much money they will make after working 1,250 hours, and which representation is best to show their boss to ask for a raise.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   *Activity:* Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using a graph versus an equation for the 1,250-hour question. They must list the pros and cons of each alternative on their whiteboards.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will read the whiteboards to ensure students recognize long-term implications (e.g., "Pro of equation: fast and precise for large numbers. Con of graph: impossible to draw a graph large enough to find 1,250 hours accurately").
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   *Activity:* Groups must choose one representation to present to their "boss" to justify a raise, applying sound reasoning to their decision. 
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will evaluate the groups' final pitches. A high level of competency is shown when students anticipate complexities—for example, choosing a graph because "it provides a clear visual trajectory of earnings over time that is easy for a boss to understand at a glance," demonstrating sophisticated, logical reasoning.

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Exit Ticket (Closure) – 10 Minutes**

**Activity:** 
To close the lesson, students independently complete an exit ticket that asks them to reflect on how their brain processes proportional relationships now compared to the beginning of the class. 

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   *Activity:* Students answer the prompt: *"At the start of class, you only had a verbal description. Now you have five ways to represent proportions. How did your thinking process change when you got stuck today, and which representation do you naturally rely on most? How will you adjust your strategy next time you face a difficult math problem?"*
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will collect and read the exit tickets, looking for deep metacognitive awareness. A strong response will show students recognizing their own assumptions (e.g., "I realized I rely too much on tables, but when I got stuck, I strategically switched to an equation because it made the pattern clearer. Next time, I will start by finding the equation first").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships Across Representations

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Learning Target:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent the relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.  
**Success Criteria:** Students can identify the constant of proportionality, explain what it means, and justify whether a relationship is proportional using multiple representations.

### Materials
- Projector or board  
- Student handout with scenarios, tables, graph grids, and reflection prompts  
- Rulers or straightedges  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Same or Different?” — 7 minutes
Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, and 180 miles in 3 hours.  
**Situation B:** A taxi charges $4 to start plus $2 per mile.

Ask students:  
1. What quantities are being compared?  
2. Which situation seems proportional? Why?

Students first write individually, then share with a partner.

**Teacher move:** Emphasize that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and can be represented by an equation of the form **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify relevant quantities, units, and what each number represents before solving.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for precise clarification, such as: “In Situation A, miles depends on hours, and the ratio is 60 miles per hour.” A student would be at a higher level if they clearly name both quantities, units, and the relationship between them.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Create an anchor chart with students:

A relationship is proportional if:
1. The ratios are equivalent in a table.  
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin.  
3. The equation can be written as **y = kx**.  
4. The verbal description shows a constant rate with no starting fee or added amount.  
5. A diagram shows equal groups or scaled quantities.

Model with Situation A:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

Ask: “What is the constant of proportionality?”  
Students should identify **k = 60**, meaning the car travels 60 miles per hour.

Graph the points and write the equation: **m = 60h**.

Then compare to Situation B: **cost = 2m + 4**, which is not proportional because the graph would not pass through the origin and there is a starting fee.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students verify proportionality by checking more than one representation: table, graph, and equation.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students who cross-check instead of relying on one clue. Strong evidence might sound like: “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph goes through the origin, so it is proportional.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Build All Representations — 13 minutes
Give students this scenario:

**A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.**

Students complete:
1. A table for sugar and flour  
2. A diagram showing batches  
3. A graph  
4. An equation  
5. A verbal description

Expected equation if **x = cups of sugar** and **y = cups of flour**:  
**y = 1.5x**

Circulate and ask:
- “What does the constant of proportionality mean here?”
- “How does your diagram match your table?”
- “Does your graph pass through the origin?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students organize the same relationship into a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether representations are logically connected and labeled. Strong work will show matching values across all forms, correct units, and a clear flow from table to graph to equation.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide whether their representations are complete and useful by checking labels, units, scale, and accuracy.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students improving unclear work, such as changing an unlabeled graph into one with labeled axes and consistent scale. Strong evidence: “This graph is better because the axes are labeled sugar and flour, and the scale lets us see the constant rate clearly.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Proportional or Not? — 12 minutes
Partners receive three cards:

**Card 1:** A gym charges $15 per month.  
**Card 2:** A babysitter earns $10 per hour plus a $5 travel fee.  
**Card 3:** A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |
| 8 | 20 |

For each card, students decide:
- Is it proportional?
- What representation best proves your answer?
- What are the pros and cons of using a table, graph, or equation here?

Example discussion:
- A table is useful for checking ratios quickly.
- A graph is useful for seeing whether the line passes through the origin.
- An equation is useful for showing the rule clearly.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare which representation best supports their claim.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for more than “I like graphs.” Strong evidence includes trade-offs: “A graph shows the origin clearly, but a table helps me check the ratio faster.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students make a decision about proportionality and justify it with mathematical evidence.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for claims supported by valid reasoning. Strong evidence: “The babysitter situation is not proportional because the equation is y = 10x + 5, not y = kx. The $5 fee means when hours are 0, the cost is not 0.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Share and Error Analysis — 6 minutes
Display an incorrect student-style claim:

“The taxi situation is proportional because the cost goes up by the same amount each mile.”

Ask students to respond:
- What is correct about this thinking?
- What is missing or incorrect?
- How could we revise the statement?

Guide students to see that constant increase alone does not guarantee proportionality. A proportional relationship must also include the point **(0, 0)** and have the form **y = kx**.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 7 minutes
Students complete:

1. A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

Is the relationship proportional? Represent it with an equation and a verbal description.

2. Reflection prompt:  
“Which representation helped you most today—table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why? What would you check next time to avoid mistakes?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on which representation supported their thinking and how they will check their reasoning in the future.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for metacognitive responses beyond preference. Strong evidence: “I used the table first because I could check equal ratios, but next time I will also graph it to make sure it goes through the origin.”

---

## Assessment Summary
By the end of the lesson, students should demonstrate that they can:
- Clarify quantities and units in a proportional relationship.
- Organize one relationship across multiple representations.
- Validate proportionality using tables, graphs, and equations.
- Choose high-quality representations with labels, scales, and accurate values.
- Compare the usefulness of different representations.
- Justify proportional or nonproportional decisions with sound reasoning.
- Reflect on their own mathematical thinking and error-checking strategies.

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Create an anchor chart with students:

A relationship is proportional if:
1. The ratios are equivalent in a table.  
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin.  
3. The equation can be written as **y = kx**.  
4. The verbal description shows a constant rate with no starting fee or added amount.  
5. A diagram shows equal groups or scaled quantities.

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

**y = 1.5x**

- “What does the constant of proportionality mean here?”
- “How does your diagram match your table?”
- “Does your graph pass through the origin?”

- **Activity/moment:** Students organize the same relationship into a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.

- **Activity/moment:** Students compare which representation best supports their claim.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for more than “I like graphs.” Strong evidence includes trade-offs: “A graph shows the origin clearly, but a table helps me check the ratio faster.”

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to analyze and justify their reasoning.

## Materials

## 1. Opening Hook & Warm-Up (7 minutes)
Display two lemonade recipes:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups lemon juice / 6 cups water
- **Recipe B:** 3 cups lemon juice / 8 cups water

Ask: *"Which recipe tastes more lemony? How do you know?"* Partners discuss for 2 minutes, then share.

**🎯 CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "more lemony" means mathematically by isolating the relevant ratio rather than comparing raw amounts.
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for students who articulate ratio comparisons (1:3 vs. 3:8) vs. those who say "B has more juice." Record on observation checklist.

---

## 2. Direct Instruction & Anchor Chart (10 minutes)
Co-create an anchor chart. A relationship is proportional if:
1. Ratios in a table are equivalent.
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin.
3. The equation has the form **y = kx**.
4. The verbal description shows a constant rate with no starting fee.
5. A diagram shows equal scaled groups.

Demonstrate with Recipe A (k = 3): table, equation y = 3x, graph through origin, verbal form. Contrast with Recipe B (ratios differ).

**🎯 CRI1b (Organizing information):** Modeling parallel representations of one relationship.
**Assessment:** Students copy the four-representation organizer into notes; check notebooks for clear, parallel structure with labels and units.

---

## 3. Guided Practice — "Smoothie Detective" (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout with three scenarios (a table, a graph, a word problem). For each, students determine if it is proportional, identify k, and translate into the other representations. One table is a "trap": ratios appear close but aren't equal (e.g., 4/2, 9/4, 14/6). The graph scenario includes both exact gridline points and messy estimated points.

**🎯 CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check every ratio against the anchor chart criteria rather than assume.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How did you verify this? Did you check every pair against multiple criteria?"*

**🎯 CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must deliberately select exact coordinate pairs (high-quality data) rather than estimated points to calculate k.
**Assessment:** Note which students choose precise points and can explain why ("This point is exactly on the gridline so my k is accurate").

---

## 4. Critical Thinking Challenge — "Pick the Best Plan" (15 minutes)
Present three summer job offers:
- **Plan A:** $10 flat fee + $5/hour
- **Plan B:** $8/hour (proportional)
- **Plan C:** $7/hour first 5 hours, then $10/hour

In groups of 3, students create tables and graphs for each, identify which is proportional, and recommend a plan for 2, 5, and 10 hours of work.

**🎯 CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare proportional vs. non-proportional options and how the "best" choice shifts with context.
**Assessment:** Collect recommendation sheets. Score 2 for explicit trade-off language ("Plan B wins at high hours because k is constant, but Plan A wins at low hours due to the flat fee"); score 1 for superficial comparisons.

**🎯 CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Each group gives a 30-second justification using mathematical evidence.
**Assessment:** Rubric — faulty/no math (-1), partial (1), reasoning anticipates conditions like "it depends on hours" with computed values (2).

---

## 5. Whole-Class Error Analysis (6 minutes)
Display an incorrect claim: *"Plan A is proportional because the cost goes up by the same amount each hour."*

Ask: What's correct? What's missing? How do we revise? Guide students to see that constant increase alone is not enough — the relationship must also pass through (0, 0) and fit y = kx.

---

## 6. Reflection & Exit Ticket (7 minutes)

**Part A — Metacognitive Journal (3 min):**
*"At the start of class, you only had a verbal description. Now you have five ways to represent proportions. What was your first instinct when comparing the job plans? Did your thinking change? Which representation do you naturally rely on, and how will you adjust your strategy next time?"*

**🎯 CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Direct prompt to revisit and adjust reasoning.
**Assessment:** Score 2 for entries showing strategic adjustment ("I assumed Plan A was best until I graphed it and saw it didn't pass through the origin"); score 1 for surface reflection ("It was hard but I got it").

**Part B — Exit Ticket (4 min):** Students receive a table and must (1) state whether it is proportional, (2) if yes, write the equation and describe the graph, (3) if no, explain what would need to change.

**Assessment:** Quick mastery check for 7.RP.A.2; use to plan tomorrow's reteach groups.

- **Extension:** Advanced students create their own "trap" non-proportional table for classmates.
