## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Overview
Students will identify and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions — building conceptual clarity, logical organization, and reflective mathematical thinking.

---

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, colored markers
- "Lemonade Stand" task cards (one per pair)
- Whiteboard/projector
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up — 7 Minutes
**"Which One Doesn't Belong?"**
Display four representations of relationships (a proportional table, a non-proportional table, a graph through the origin, and a graph with a y-intercept of 3). Ask: *"Which one doesn't belong? Explain your reasoning."*

Students write a 2-sentence justification before sharing with a partner.

> **CRI1a — Clarifying Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students must precisely articulate *why* one representation breaks the proportional pattern, not simply identify it. The teacher prompts: *"What exactly makes it different? Use the word 'ratio' in your explanation."*
> **Assessment:** Listen for students distinguishing constant ratios from non-constant ones. Look for written sentences that go beyond "it looks different" to statements like "the ratio between y and x changes, so it isn't proportional."

---

### Direct Instruction — 10 Minutes
**"The Constant of Proportionality"**
Using a lemonade recipe scenario (2 cups sugar per 5 cups water), teacher models how to:
1. Build a ratio table
2. Plot the relationship on a graph
3. Write the equation y = kx
4. Describe it verbally

Teacher explicitly connects each representation, asking: *"Does the graph pass through the origin? What does that tell us?"*

> **CRI1b — Organizing Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Teacher models organizing the four representations in a structured two-column anchor chart: left side = "What We See," right side = "What It Means." Students copy and annotate.
> **Assessment:** Check student anchor charts for logical sequencing — representations should connect to each other, not exist as isolated facts. Look for arrows or notes linking the equation to the graph and table.

---

### Guided Practice — 12 Minutes
**"Is It Proportional?" Gallery Walk**
Six posters around the room show different data sets (earning $12/hour, a taxi with a $3 base fee, distance traveled at constant speed, etc.). Pairs visit each poster, decide if it's proportional, and justify using at least two representations.

> **CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students must cross-check their proportional claim using *both* the table (constant ratio check) and the graph (through-origin check). If the two representations disagree, they must resolve the conflict.
> **Assessment:** Circulate and ask: *"Your table says proportional — does your graph confirm that? If not, which do you trust and why?"* Students who reconcile discrepancies demonstrate rigorous validation.

> **CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** One poster intentionally contains a rounding error in the table, making a proportional relationship appear non-proportional. Students must judge whether the data is reliable before concluding.
> **Assessment:** Students who flag the inconsistency and question the data quality — rather than accepting it at face value — demonstrate sophisticated judgment. Look for written notes like "this might be a rounding issue."

---

### Collaborative Task — 15 Minutes
**"Choose Your Plan" — Cell Phone Scenario**
Two phone plans are presented:
- **Plan A:** $0.10 per text, no monthly fee
- **Plan B:** $5/month + $0.05 per text

Students must determine which plan is proportional, represent both in tables and graphs, and recommend a plan for three different types of users (light, moderate, heavy texter).

> **CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students complete a structured comparison chart evaluating both plans across different usage levels, considering cost implications over time.
> **Assessment:** Look for recommendations that vary by user type — students who say "Plan A is always better" without qualification are operating at a superficial level. Strong responses acknowledge that Plan B becomes advantageous at higher usage.

> **CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students must write a 3-sentence recommendation that includes a mathematical justification (e.g., identifying the break-even point where both plans cost the same).
> **Assessment:** Evaluate reasoning for logical progression: claim → mathematical evidence → conclusion. Flag responses that recommend a plan without referencing the data.

---

### Closure — 11 Minutes
**Metacognitive Exit Ticket**
Students respond to three prompts:
1. *"Which representation of proportional relationships is easiest for you to use? Why?"*
2. *"Where did your thinking change during today's lesson?"*
3. *"What would you do differently if you solved the phone plan problem again?"*

> **CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Prompt #2 and #3 explicitly require students to identify a moment of cognitive shift and articulate a revised strategy — targeting deep metacognitive awareness.
> **Assessment:** Responses scored on specificity. Surface-level: *"I would check my work."* Deep-level: *"I jumped to a conclusion using only the table. Next time I'd graph it first to see if it passes through the origin before deciding."*

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide sentence frames for justifications and pre-labeled graph axes.
- **Extension:** Students write their own "Is It Proportional?" scenario and swap with a peer to solve.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations.
4.  Determine the constant of proportionality from various representations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with tables and graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Calculators (optional, for efficiency)
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore a really important concept in math: proportional relationships. You've already worked with ratios and rates. Can anyone remind us what a ratio is? What about a unit rate?"
*   *(Briefly review ratios and unit rates, connecting them to real-world examples like miles per hour or cost per item. For instance, 'If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?')*
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll see how these ideas connect to something called a 'proportional relationship' and how we can spot them in different ways."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice: Defining Proportionality (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "A **proportional relationship** exists between two quantities when one quantity is a constant multiple of the other. This constant multiple is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by 'k'. In simpler terms, if you divide the 'y' value by the 'x' value, you always get the same number, 'k'."
*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** "Let's try to put that in our own words. Turn to your elbow partner and explain what 'constant multiple' means in the context of a proportional relationship. What are the two key things we need to look for when deciding if a relationship is proportional?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen to student discussions. I will observe if students are accurately rephrasing the definition, identifying "constant ratio" and "relationship between two quantities" as key ideas. I'll ask a few pairs to share their rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in their language.
*   **Teacher:** "We can see proportional relationships in different forms. Let's start with tables."
    *   *Display a table on the board:*
        | x (hours) | y (miles) |
        | :-------- | :-------- |
        | 1         | 50        |
        | 2         | 100       |
        | 3         | 150       |
    *   **Teacher:** "For each pair (x, y), let's calculate y/x. What do you notice?" (Students should find y/x = 50 for all pairs). "Since y/x is constant, this is a proportional relationship, and k = 50. The equation would be y = 50x."
    *   *Display a non-proportional table:*
        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
        | :---------- | :------- |
        | 1           | $1.50    |
        | 2           | $2.50    |
        | 3           | $3.50    |
    *   **Teacher:** "Now, let's check this one. What do you find for y/x?" (Students should find different ratios). "Is this proportional? Why not?" (No constant ratio).
*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships also have a special look on a graph. They always form a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0)."
    *   *Quickly sketch the graph of y=50x, showing it's a straight line through the origin.*

**III. Activity 1: Table & Graph Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn! In pairs, you'll get a handout with several tables. Your task is to determine if each relationship is proportional. If it is, you'll find the constant of proportionality (k), write the equation, and then graph it on the provided graph paper."
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Remember, to be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers. You need to check *every* pair in the table. If even one ratio is different, it's not proportional. Be rigorous in your checks!"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and observe pairs. I will specifically look for students systematically calculating y/x for *all* rows in the tables, not just the first one. I'll ask groups, "How did you confirm this table is proportional? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why is checking all of them important?"
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** "When you're working with your tables and graphs, make sure your work is clear and organized. Label your axes on the graph, use a consistent scale, and show your calculations for 'k' clearly next to each table."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will check student handouts for clear organization. Are tables filled out neatly? Are calculations for 'k' shown consistently for each row? Are graphs clearly labeled with axes, units, and a consistent scale? Do the plotted points accurately reflect the table data in an orderly fashion?
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** As students work, they will constantly make decisions: "Is this proportional?", "What's the constant?", "How do I write the equation?". They must use the definition and their calculations to justify their answers.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** During group work, I will listen to their discussions. Are they using the definition (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to justify their decisions? For example, if a group incorrectly identifies a non-proportional relationship, I'll ask, "What evidence from your calculations or graph led you to that conclusion? Does it meet *all* the criteria for proportionality, including passing through (0,0)?"

**IV. Activity 2: Equation & Diagram Connection (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Great work with tables and graphs! Now let's connect to equations and verbal descriptions."
*   *Provide a new scenario:* "A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."
*   **Teacher:** "Is this a proportional relationship? How do you know? What's the constant of proportionality if we consider flour to milk?" (k = 2/3). "What if we consider milk to flour?" (k = 3/2).
*   **Teacher:** "How would we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)?" (F = (2/3)M). "And milk (M) in terms of flour (F)?" (M = (3/2)F).
*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Imagine you're explaining this recipe to someone who's never cooked before. Which representation – the verbal description, a table, a graph, or an equation – do you think would be most helpful for them to understand the relationship between flour and milk? Why? Are there situations where another representation might be better or worse?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will listen to student responses during a brief class discussion. Do they articulate the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? For example, "The verbal description is easy to start with, but an equation is better if you need to scale the recipe up a lot because you can just plug in numbers." or "A graph gives a quick visual, but a table shows exact values for specific amounts."
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, create your own short verbal description of a proportional relationship. Then, write its equation and sketch a simple diagram (like a ratio table or a simple picture showing groups) that represents it. Make sure your example is clear and accurate."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will review their created examples. Do their verbal descriptions clearly imply proportionality? Is the equation correctly derived from the description? Does the diagram accurately represent the relationship? I'll look for examples that are clear, concise, and mathematically sound, avoiding vague or incorrect relationships.

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Let's quickly review. What are the key characteristics of a proportional relationship in a table? (Constant ratio y/x) In a graph? (Straight line through the origin) In an equation? (y = kx)"
*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, I want you to answer two questions:
        1.  What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today?
        2.  What strategy did you use, or what 'aha!' moment did you have, that helped you overcome that challenge?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will collect exit tickets. I'll look for responses that demonstrate metacognitive awareness. Do students identify specific difficulties (e.g., "remembering to check (0,0) on a graph," "calculating k consistently when numbers were fractions") and describe a strategy they used (e.g., "I made sure to divide y by x for every single point," "I drew a quick sketch to see if it went through the origin before plotting all points")? This shows they are reflecting on their learning process and adjusting their thinking.

---
**Differentiation:**
*   **Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality.
*   **Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but isn't. Have them explore inverse proportionality or multi-step proportional problems.

**Assessment:**
*   Informal observation during activities and discussions.
*   Review of student handouts (tables, graphs, equations, created examples).
*   Exit ticket responses.

  - Equation: y = 2x

---

**3. Guided Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with mixed representations of proportional relationships.  
- In pairs, students identify whether the relationship is proportional and represent it in at least two other forms (e.g., from a table to an equation and graph).

- Select a few pairs to share their answers and reasoning with the class.

---

**4. Independent Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Students work individually on a set of problems requiring them to:  
  - Recognize proportional relationships from verbal descriptions  
  - Complete tables and graphs for proportional relationships  
  - Write equations representing proportional relationships  
- Encourage students to check their work by verifying the constant ratio.  
- Provide support as needed.

---

**5. Reflection and Discussion (5 minutes)**  
- Ask students to write a brief explanation of how they determine if two quantities are proportional and how they represent that relationship.  
- Invite a few students to share their reflections aloud.  
- Highlight the importance of clear reasoning and multiple representations.

---

**6. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- A quick problem: Given a table or verbal description, students identify if the relationship is proportional and write an equation representing it.  
- Collect exit tickets to assess understanding.

Strong responses acknowledge that Plan B becomes advantageous at higher usage. Listen for students articulating trade-offs across representations (e.g., "A graph shows the crossover point visually, but the equation lets you calculate exact break-even quickly").

> **CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students write a 3-sentence recommendation that includes a mathematical justification (e.g., identifying the break-even point where both plans cost the same).
> **Assessment:** Evaluate reasoning for logical progression: claim → mathematical evidence → conclusion. Flag responses that recommend a plan without referencing data or that use only one representation to justify a decision. Ask groups: *"Does your evidence meet all the criteria? Does it account for both plans across all user types?"*

# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

## Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:
1. Define a proportional relationship and identify its key characteristics.
2. Recognize proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
3. Represent proportional relationships across multiple forms and determine the constant of proportionality.

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up — 7 Minutes
**"Which One Doesn't Belong?"**

Display four representations of relationships: a proportional table, a non-proportional table, a graph through the origin, and a graph with a y-intercept of 3. Ask: *"Which one doesn't belong? Explain your reasoning."*

Students write a 2-sentence justification before sharing with a partner. Briefly review ratios and unit rates to connect prior knowledge (e.g., "If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?").

> **CRI1a — Clarifying Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students must precisely articulate *why* one representation breaks the proportional pattern, not simply identify it. The teacher prompts: *"What exactly makes it different? Use the word 'ratio' in your explanation."*
> **Assessment:** Listen for students distinguishing constant ratios from non-constant ones. Look for written sentences that go beyond "it looks different" to statements like "the ratio between y and x changes, so it isn't proportional." During pair shares, ask a few pairs to share rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in language.

---

### Direct Instruction — 10 Minutes
**"The Constant of Proportionality"**

Using a lemonade recipe scenario (2 cups sugar per 5 cups water), teacher models how to:
1. Build a ratio table
2. Plot the relationship on a graph (confirming it passes through the origin)
3. Write the equation y = kx
4. Describe it verbally

Teacher displays a proportional table and a non-proportional table side by side:

| x (hours) | y (miles) |        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
|-----------|-----------|        |-------------|----------|
| 1         | 50        |        | 1           | $1.50    |
| 2         | 100       |        | 2           | $2.50    |
| 3         | 150       |        | 3           | $3.50    |

Teacher calculates y/x for each, asking: *"Does the graph pass through the origin? What does that tell us?"* Students see that a constant ratio and a line through the origin are the two key markers of proportionality.

> **CRI1b — Organizing Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Teacher models organizing the four representations in a structured two-column anchor chart: left side = "What We See," right side = "What It Means." Students copy and annotate, labeling axes on graphs and showing k calculations consistently.
> **Assessment:** Check student anchor charts for logical sequencing — representations should connect to each other, not exist as isolated facts. Look for arrows or notes linking the equation to the graph and table. Check handouts for neatly organized calculations and clearly labeled graphs with units and consistent scale.

---

### Guided Practice — 12 Minutes
**"Is It Proportional?" Gallery Walk**

Six posters around the room show different data sets (earning $12/hour, a taxi with a $3 base fee, distance traveled at constant speed, etc.). Pairs visit each poster, decide if it's proportional, and justify using at least two representations.

> **CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** Students must cross-check their proportional claim using *both* the table (constant ratio check) and the graph (through-origin check). If the two representations disagree, they must resolve the conflict. The teacher reinforces: *"To be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers — you must check every pair."*
> **Assessment:** Circulate and ask: *"Your table says proportional — does your graph confirm that? If not, which do you trust and why? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few?"* Students who reconcile discrepancies demonstrate rigorous validation. Look for students systematically calculating y/x for all rows, not just the first.

> **CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information (Target Level 2)**
> **Activity:** One poster intentionally contains a rounding error in the table, making a proportional relationship appear non-proportional. Students must judge whether the data is reliable before drawing a conclusion.
> **Assessment:** Students who flag the inconsistency and question the data quality — rather than accepting it at face value — demonstrate sophisticated judgment. Look for written notes such as "this might be a rounding issue" or verbal challenges to the data during discussion.

**"Choose Your Plan" — Cell Phone Scenario**

Two phone plans are presented:

- **Plan B:** $5/month + $0.05 per text

Students determine which plan is proportional, represent both in tables and graphs, and recommend a plan for three different types of users: light, moderate, and heavy texter.
