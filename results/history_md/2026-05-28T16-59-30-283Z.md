## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Snack Shack" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (5 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "3 apples cost $1.50; 6 apples cost $3.00; 9 apples cost $4.50."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask: *"Which situation shows a constant rate? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 90 seconds.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must identify the relevant numerical relationships and articulate what makes a relationship "proportional." 
*Assessment:* Listen for precise language ("the ratio stays the same," "doubling one doubles the other"). Record student responses on the board, pushing for clarity: "Can you say that more precisely?"

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Defining Proportional Relationships (10 minutes)
Introduce the definition: Two quantities are proportional if their ratio (y/x) is constant, called the **constant of proportionality (k)**, expressed as **y = kx**.

Demonstrate using Scenario A:
- **Table:** Show ratios 1.50/3 = 0.50, 3.00/6 = 0.50
- **Equation:** y = 0.50x
- **Graph:** Plot points—show line passing through origin
- **Verbal:** "Each apple costs $0.50"

Emphasize that proportional graphs are **straight lines through the origin (0,0)**.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Model a four-column graphic organizer (Table | Equation | Graph | Verbal) showing how the same relationship is represented.
*Assessment:* Collect student notebooks at lesson end; check whether students replicated and used the organizer to structure their thinking logically.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Is It Proportional?" (10 minutes)
Distribute the "Snack Shack" handout with four scenarios:
1. Lemonade: 2 cups/$1, 4 cups/$2, 6 cups/$3
2. Popcorn: $3 entry fee + $2 per bag
3. Cookies: 12/$4, 18/$6, 24/$8
4. A graph showing a line NOT through the origin

Students work in pairs to determine which are proportional and justify using **two different representations**.

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity:** Students must verify claims by cross-checking ratios across multiple rows of data AND confirming the graph passes through the origin.
*Assessment:* Circulate and ask, "How do you know for sure? What's a second way to check?" Look for students using multiple verification methods rather than just one ratio.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Some scenarios contain extra/distractor information (e.g., the popcorn entry fee). Students must judge which information truly matters.
*Assessment:* Review handouts—did students focus on the rate relationship rather than irrelevant numbers? Did they cite the strongest evidence (e.g., the y-intercept) for their conclusions?

---

### 4. Collaborative Challenge: Choosing the Best Plan (15 minutes)
Present a real-world problem:
*"You're planning a class pizza party. Pizza Place A charges $8 per pizza. Pizza Place B charges $20 delivery + $5 per pizza. Pizza Place C offers 3 pizzas for $21, 6 pizzas for $42."*

In groups of 3-4, students must:
- Represent each plan as a table, equation, and graph
- Identify which plans are proportional
- Recommend the best choice for 10 pizzas and justify

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Students must compare three options, considering both cost and proportionality.
*Assessment:* Each group presents a 60-second recommendation. Score whether they discussed trade-offs (e.g., "Plan B is worse for small orders but...") rather than picking one option without comparison.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students must justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence.
*Assessment:* Listen for reasoning chains: "Since Plan A is proportional with k=8, and 8×10=$80, which is less than Plan B's $70... wait, let me recompute." Look for logical, evidence-based conclusions that anticipate edge cases (e.g., "but if we needed 20 pizzas...").

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students complete an exit ticket with three parts:
1. **Math task:** Given a table, determine if it's proportional and write the equation.
2. **Representation switch:** Convert the equation y = 4x into a verbal description and a graph.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"What was one moment today when you changed your mind or strategy? What made you reconsider?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The reflection prompt requires metacognitive awareness.
*Assessment:* Read exit tickets for evidence of self-monitoring. Strong responses identify a specific shift (e.g., "I first thought Plan B was proportional because the cost went up steadily, but then I realized the $20 fee meant it didn't pass through zero"). Score on a 0-2 scale: vague mention (1), specific strategy adjustment (2).

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Co-create an anchor chart: **"4 Tests for Proportionality"**
1. Constant ratio (table)
2. Equation form y = kx
3. Straight line through origin (graph)
4. "Per" or "for each" language (verbal)

Preview tomorrow: "We'll use these proportional relationships to solve real-world problems involving unit rates."

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables/graphs
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to identify, translate, and represent proportional relationships across five modalities (verbal, table, graph, equation, diagram) while applying critical thinking skills to validate data, weigh the utility of different representations, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Clarifying the Problem (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Decoding the Deal"**
The lesson begins with a messy, real-world verbal description projected on the board: *"At the local market, a vendor says you can buy 3 pounds of apples for $4.50, but my friend said she paid $7.50 for 5 pounds. Another guy bought 2 pounds and a candy bar for $5.00."* Students must extract the relevant mathematical information, define their variables ($x$ = pounds of apples, $y$ = total cost), and clarify the core proportional question: Is the price of apples consistent? 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students work in pairs to filter out irrelevant information (the candy bar) and clarify the exact unit rate ($1.50 per pound) to support a deeper understanding of the relationship.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and observe students' whiteboards, assessing whether they have precisely isolated the relevant data points (3 lbs for $4.50; 5 lbs for $7.50) and clearly defined their $x$ and $y$ variables without being confused by the misleading "candy bar" data.

### **Part 2: Guided Practice & Organization (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: "The Proportion Web"**
The teacher guides students in taking the clarified apple data and organizing it into a structured "Proportion Web." This graphic organizer requires students to place the verbal description in the center and branch out to create a sequential table, a properly scaled graph, a tape diagram, and an algebraic equation ($y = 1.50x$). 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students arrange the raw data into a logical, sequential input-output table and translate that table into a coordinate plane, ensuring the logical flow of information from one representation to the next.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the students' graphic organizers to ensure the table is ordered sequentially by $x$-values and that the graph features a straight line passing through the origin, demonstrating a strong, consistent logical structure.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Verification (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Fact-Check the Math"**
Students are handed a "Vendor Report" containing data from a competing apple vendor. The report includes a mix of high-quality data (exact digital scale receipts) and low-quality data (a customer's rough estimate). It also includes a graph that looks straight but actually starts at (0, 1). Students must determine if this new vendor's pricing is truly proportional.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must rigorously cross-check the provided graph against the provided table and the standard definition of proportionality (must pass through the origin, $y/x$ must be constant). 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group discussions, assessing whether students successfully identify the contradiction: "The table shows a constant rate, but the graph starts at (0,1), so the graph is invalid and this is not a proportional relationship."

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** When calculating the constant of proportionality ($k$), students must choose which data points from the text to use—the exact receipt data or the customer's rough estimate.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review student worksheets to ensure they demonstrate sophisticated judgment by explicitly discarding the estimated data points and exclusively using the authoritative receipt data to calculate their equations.

### **Part 4: Trade-offs and Decision Making (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: "The Better Buy Decision"**
Students are tasked with purchasing 150 pounds of apples for a school pie-baking event. They must choose which representation (table, graph, diagram, or equation) is the most efficient tool for calculating the cost of this massive order, and then use it to make a final purchasing decision between two valid vendors.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students write a short paragraph comparing the options. They must evaluate the trade-offs of extending a table or graph to 150 (time-consuming, requires huge paper) versus using the equation $y = kx$ (abstract but highly efficient for large numbers).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read these paragraphs to assess if students systematically evaluate the trade-offs, recognizing that while graphs are great for quick visual comparisons, equations are superior for calculating long-term or large-scale implications.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students make their final vendor choice for the 150-pound order and justify it using their chosen mathematical representation.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the final written decision to ensure students use sound, sophisticated logic (e.g., "Using the equation $y = 1.25x$ for Vendor A and $y = 1.50x$ for Vendor B, Vendor A is cheaper, saving the school $37.50, which justifies my choice").

### **Part 5: Closure & Metacognition (5 Minutes)**

**Activity: "Metacognitive Minute Exit Ticket"**
Before leaving, students complete an exit ticket focused entirely on how their brain processed the different representations today. 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students answer the prompt: *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram) was the hardest for you to understand today? What specific mistake did you make, and how did you adjust your thinking to fix it?"*
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect and read the exit tickets, looking for deep metacognitive awareness. A level-2 response would look like: *"I initially struggled with the equation because I kept mixing up $x$ and $y$. I realized my error when my graph didn't match my equation, so I adjusted my thinking by always labeling $y$ as the total cost before writing $y=kx$."*

### Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $15 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $31 |

Ask students:  
“Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

Students write a quick explanation, then share with a partner.

**Teacher focus:** Guide students to identify the constant ratio in Table A: money ÷ hours = 8. Table B does not have a constant ratio.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify the important information in each table and clarify what must be checked: whether the ratio stays the same.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for precise statements such as, “I divided money by hours for each row and got 8 every time.” Use the CRI scale:  
  - 2: Clearly explains relevant quantities and ratio.  
  - 1: Identifies some relevant information but explanation is incomplete.  
  - -1: Gives misleading reasoning, such as “It is proportional because the numbers get bigger.”  

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Define a proportional relationship:

A relationship is proportional if the ratio between two quantities is constant. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

Use Table A to model multiple representations:

- **Verbal description:** A student earns $8 per hour.  
- **Table:** For each hour, money increases by $8.  
- **Equation:** \(y = 8x\), where \(x\) is hours and \(y\) is money earned.  
- **Graph:** Points such as \((1,8), (2,16), (3,24)\) lie on a straight line through the origin.  
- **Diagram:** Draw 1 hour matched to 8 dollars, 2 hours matched to 16 dollars, etc.

Emphasize: In a proportional relationship, the graph is a straight line through \((0,0)\).

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students organize one proportional relationship into five representations: verbal, table, equation, graph, and diagram.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether students arrange information logically and consistently across representations. A strong response connects all representations to the same constant, such as \(k = 8\).  

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes
Give students this scenario:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

Ask students to complete the following:

1. Make a table for 2, 4, 6, and 8 cups of sugar.  
2. Write an equation.  
3. Graph the relationship.  
4. Write a verbal description of the constant of proportionality.

Expected reasoning: Flour ÷ sugar = \( \frac{3}{2} \), so \(y = \frac{3}{2}x\), where \(x\) is cups of sugar and \(y\) is cups of flour.

Discuss: “Does it matter which quantity is \(x\) and which is \(y\)?” Explain that the constant depends on how the variables are defined.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check their table, equation, and graph to see whether they all show the same relationship.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to point to evidence in at least two representations. For example: “The table shows 2 cups of sugar gives 3 cups of flour, and the equation \(y = \frac{3}{2}x\) also gives 3 when \(x = 2\).”  
- **Assessment level guidance:** A level 2 response uses multiple representations to verify the relationship.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which information from the scenario is necessary and which representation gives the clearest evidence of proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask: “Which representation best proves this is proportional, and why?” Look for responses such as, “The table is useful because I can check that flour divided by sugar is always \( \frac{3}{2} \).”  

---

### 4. Partner Task: Comparing Two Plans — 13 minutes
Students work in pairs on this problem:

“Two bike rental companies charge the following prices.”

**Company A:** $6 per hour  
**Company B:**

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $5 |
| 2 | $10 |
| 3 | $15 |
| 4 | $20 |

Students must:
1. Represent both plans with equations.  
2. Graph both plans on the same coordinate plane.  
3. Decide which company is cheaper for 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours.  
4. Explain whether both relationships are proportional.

Expected:
- Company A: \(y = 6x\)  
- Company B: \(y = 5x\)  
Both are proportional because each has a constant hourly rate and graphs pass through the origin. Company B is always cheaper.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare two rental options and consider cost differences.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students comparing both options with evidence, not just guessing. A strong response says, “Company B is cheaper for every hour shown because its constant rate is $5 per hour instead of $6 per hour.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students justify their choice using equations, tables, and graphs.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask pairs to explain their decision. Listen for logical reasoning based on the constant of proportionality. Level 2 reasoning connects the decision to multiple representations and avoids unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes
Invite two pairs to share their graphs and equations. Ask:

- “How can you tell from the graph that each relationship is proportional?”  
- “How can you tell from the equation?”  
- “What mistake might someone make when deciding if a relationship is proportional?”

Highlight common misconceptions:
- A relationship is not proportional just because both quantities increase.  
- A straight line is proportional only if it passes through the origin.  
- The constant of proportionality depends on which quantity is compared to which.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 8 minutes
Students complete an exit ticket:

2. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
3. Reflection: “What representation helped you understand the relationship best today? Why?”

Expected equation: \(y = 4x\)

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on which representation helped their thinking and why.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for metacognitive comments such as, “The table helped me first because I could see the pattern, but the graph helped me check that the line went through the origin.”  
- A level 2 response shows the student understands how their thinking changed or how they checked their work.

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Decoding the Deal" handout, "Pizza Party" task sheet, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Decoding the Deal" (5 minutes)
Project a messy scenario: *"At the market, a vendor sells 3 pounds of apples for $4.50. My friend paid $7.50 for 5 pounds. Another customer bought 2 pounds and a candy bar for $5.00."* Students discuss with a partner: Is the apple price proportional? What information matters?

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must filter out the irrelevant candy bar data and isolate the apple ratios.
*Assessment:* Circulate and listen for precise statements ("3 lbs for $4.50 and 5 lbs for $7.50 both give $1.50 per pound"). A level-2 response cleanly defines variables and discards distractors; a level-1 response notes ratios but includes the candy bar; -1 confuses the irrelevant data as meaningful.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Five Representations (10 minutes)
Define: Two quantities are proportional if their ratio y/x is constant. Express as **y = kx**, where k is the constant of proportionality. Model the apple scenario across all five representations required by the standard:
- **Verbal:** "Apples cost $1.50 per pound."
- **Table:** 1 lb→$1.50, 2 lb→$3.00, 3 lb→$4.50
- **Equation:** y = 1.50x
- **Graph:** Straight line through (0,0)
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 1 lb matched to $1.50 repeated

Emphasize: Proportional graphs are **straight lines through the origin**.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Model a five-column graphic organizer connecting all representations to the same k = 1.50.
*Assessment:* Check notebooks for a logically sequenced organizer where each representation reinforces the same constant.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Fact-Check the Math" (12 minutes)
Distribute a "Vendor Report" with four scenarios:
1. Lemonade: 2 cups/$1, 4 cups/$2, 6 cups/$3
2. Popcorn: $3 entry fee + $2 per bag
3. A graph that looks straight but starts at (0, 1)
4. A table with one data point from a "rough customer estimate" and others from exact receipts

Students determine which scenarios are proportional, using **two representations** to verify.

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity:** Students cross-check ratios across rows AND confirm the graph passes through the origin.
*Assessment:* Ask, "How do you know? What's a second way to check?" Listen for students catching the contradiction: "The table looks constant, but the graph starts at (0,1), so it's not proportional."

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** Students must judge which data points are authoritative (receipts) versus unreliable (rough estimates) and which scenario details (entry fees) disqualify proportionality.
*Assessment:* Review handouts—did students explicitly discard low-quality data and identify the y-intercept as decisive evidence?

---

### 4. Collaborative Challenge: Pizza Party Decision (15 minutes)
Present: *"You're planning a class pizza party. Place A charges $8 per pizza. Place B charges $20 delivery + $5 per pizza. Place C offers 3 pizzas for $21, 6 pizzas for $42."*

In groups of 3–4, students:
- Represent each plan as a table, equation, and graph
- Identify which plans are proportional
- Recommend the best choice for 10 pizzas, then reconsider for 20 pizzas

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Groups compare all three options across multiple order sizes, recognizing trade-offs (Plan B is non-proportional but may win at high volume).
*Assessment:* Each group gives a 60-second recommendation. Score whether they discussed trade-offs ("Plan B is worse for small orders but...") rather than picking one option without comparison.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students justify recommendations using mathematical evidence chains.
*Assessment:* Listen for reasoning like, "Since Plan A has k=8, 8×10=$80. Plan B gives 20+5(10)=$70. So Plan B wins at 10 pizzas—but at 4 pizzas, Plan A is cheaper." Look for anticipation of edge cases.

---

### 5. Closure: Anchor Chart (5 minutes)
Co-create **"4 Tests for Proportionality"**:
1. Constant ratio y/x (table)
2. Equation form y = kx
3. Straight line through the origin (graph)
4. "Per" or "for each" language (verbal)

Address misconceptions: A relationship is not proportional just because both quantities increase; a straight line is only proportional if it passes through (0,0).

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (8 minutes)
Students complete:
1. **Math task:** Given a table, determine if it's proportional and write the equation.
2. **Representation switch:** Convert y = 4x into a verbal description and a graph.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"What was one moment today when you changed your mind or strategy? Which representation helped you most, and why?"*

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The reflection requires metacognitive awareness.
*Assessment:* Read exit tickets for self-monitoring. A level-2 response identifies a specific shift: "I first thought Plan B was proportional because cost rose steadily, but then I realized the $20 fee meant the graph didn't pass through zero, so I switched my answer." A level-1 response mentions a preference without explaining adjustment.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and pre-scaled graph axes.
- **Extension:** Students create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance and challenge a partner to spot why.
