## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent them using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain their reasoning using mathematical evidence.

### Materials

- Projector or board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes

Display the following situation:

> A store sells 3 notebooks for $6 and 5 notebooks for $10. Is the price proportional to the number of notebooks?

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Ask students to identify what information matters and what they can ignore.

Bring the class together and ask:

- “What quantities are being compared?”
- “How can we tell whether the relationship is proportional?”
- “What does the unit rate tell us?”

Guide students to see that $6 ÷ 3 = $2 per notebook and $10 ÷ 5 = $2 per notebook, so the relationship is proportional.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the relevant quantities and restate the problem in their own words.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students saying or writing statements such as, “We compare cost to number of notebooks,” or “The important information is the number of notebooks and the total cost.”  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students clarify ideas precisely to support deeper understanding.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship means two quantities have a constant ratio. In 7th grade terms, if one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other, the relationship is proportional.

Write on the board:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 12 |
| 4 | 16 |

Ask students to find the ratio y/x for each row.

Then introduce the equation form:

> y = kx

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

For the table, k = 4, so the equation is:

> y = 4x

Show that the graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line that passes through the origin, point (0, 0).

Emphasize five representations:

1. **Table:** constant ratio  
2. **Graph:** straight line through origin  
3. **Equation:** y = kx  
4. **Diagram:** equal-sized groups or repeated scaling  
5. **Verbal description:** “For every 1 x, there are k y”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students create a five-part organizer in their notebooks labeled table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students arrange information clearly and connect each representation to the same proportional relationship.  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students arrange information to strengthen logical flow.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: One Relationship, Five Ways — 13 minutes

Use the scenario:

> A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours. At the same speed, how far does the cyclist travel in 1, 3, and 4 hours?

As a class, complete the table:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

Ask students to identify the constant of proportionality:

> 6 miles per hour

Then write the equation:

> y = 6x

where x = hours and y = miles.

Students plot the points on graph paper and draw the line through the origin. Then they sketch a simple diagram showing equal groups of 6 miles per hour.

Finally, students write a verbal description:

> “For every 1 hour, the cyclist travels 6 miles.”

Pause to ask:

- “How can we verify that all five representations match?”
- “If the graph did not pass through the origin, would it still be proportional?”
- “Which representation makes the constant of proportionality easiest to see?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check the table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description to confirm they all show the same relationship.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students verifying using more than one representation, such as, “The table shows 6 each hour, and the equation y = 6x gives the same values.”  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students cross-check information using multiple mathematical representations.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation gives the strongest evidence that the relationship is proportional.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify their choice. Strong responses may include, “The table is useful because I can compare y/x in every row,” or “The graph helps because it is a straight line through the origin.”  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students select high-quality mathematical evidence.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give pairs a handout with three relationships:

**A.**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |
| 8 | 20 |

**B.**

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 7 |
| 3 | 11 |
| 4 | 15 |

**C.**  
A taxi charges $4 just to enter the cab, plus $2 per mile.

Students must:

1. Decide whether each relationship is proportional.
2. Explain their reasoning.
3. Represent one proportional relationship in at least three ways.
4. Represent one non-proportional relationship and explain why it fails.

Encourage partners to discuss different strategies: checking ratios, writing equations, or graphing.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare methods: table ratios, graphing, and equations.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students discussing trade-offs, such as, “Graphing helps us see the origin, but checking ratios is faster for a table.”  
- **Target Level:** 1–2 depending on depth of comparison.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify whether each relationship is proportional or not.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check explanations for logical evidence, such as, “A is proportional because y/x is always 2.5,” or “C is not proportional because the starting fee means the graph does not pass through the origin.”  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students use sound reasoning and anticipate exceptions, such as added starting values.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Review answers as a class.

Expected conclusions:

- **A is proportional** because the ratio y/x is always 2.5.
- **B is not proportional** because y/x is not constant.
- **C is not proportional** because there is an initial fee of $4, so the equation would be y = 2x + 4, not y = kx.

Ask:

- “What mistake might someone make with the taxi problem?”
- “Why is ‘increases by the same amount’ not always the same as proportional?”

Clarify that adding the same amount can show a linear pattern, but proportional relationships must multiply by the same constant and pass through the origin.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete:

1. A table shows x = 2, 4, 6 and y = 9, 18, 27. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.
2. Write the equation if it is proportional.
3. Finish this sentence:  
   > “At first I thought _____, but then I checked _____, so now I think _____.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students complete the reflection sentence on the exit ticket.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for metacognitive statements such as, “At first I thought it was not proportional because the numbers were large, but then I checked y/x and saw the ratio was always 4.5.”  
- **Target Level:** 2 — Students show awareness of how their thinking changed or was confirmed.

---

## Assessment

**Formative assessment:** Teacher circulates during partner work, checking tables, graphs, equations, and explanations.  
**Exit ticket:** Used to determine whether students can identify a proportional relationship and explain using constant ratios.

## Differentiation

- **Support:** Provide sentence stems: “The relationship is proportional because…” and “The constant of proportionality is…”  
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own real-world proportional and non-proportional examples and represent each in two ways.
