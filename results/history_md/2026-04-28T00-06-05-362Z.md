## Generator
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Materials:** Whiteboard, markers, student notebooks, pencils, printed "Mystery Scenario" cards (one per group), argument graphic organizer handout

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Generate genuine questions about a topic before forming an argument
- Write a structured argumentative paragraph that includes a claim, reasons, evidence, and an acknowledgment of an opposing viewpoint
- Explain why curiosity strengthens written arguments

---

## Lesson Opening: The Hook (8 minutes)

**Begin with this scenario — read it aloud dramatically:**

*"A new school policy is being considered: students would have no homework for an entire year. The school board wants student opinions. You have 60 seconds. Go — write your argument."*

Give students 60 seconds to jot down their position. Then ask two or three volunteers to share what they wrote.

Now pause and ask the class: **"Before you wrote that, how many of you asked yourself ANY questions about this topic?"**

Most students won't have. That's your entry point.

Tell students: *"Today we're going to discover something surprising — the best arguers in history weren't people who already had all the answers. They were people who couldn't stop asking questions."*

Write this on the board: **Curiosity → Better Questions → Stronger Arguments**

---

## Direct Instruction: What Makes an Argument Weak? (7 minutes)

Write two sample arguments on the board:

**Argument A:**
*"Homework should be banned because it's stressful and kids need a break."*

**Argument B:**
*"While some believe homework reinforces learning, research from Stanford University found that students who spend more than two hours on homework per night show increased anxiety without significant academic gains. This suggests that excessive homework may do more harm than good."*

Ask: *"Which argument sounds more convincing? Why?"*

Guide students to notice that Argument B sounds like it came from someone who was **curious enough to ask real questions** — questions like: *What does research actually say? What do people on the other side believe? What evidence exists?*

Introduce the key writing components on the board:
1. **Claim** — Your position
2. **Reasons** — Why you believe it
3. **Evidence** — Facts, data, examples that support your reasons
4. **Counterclaim** — What someone who disagrees might say
5. **Rebuttal** — Why your argument still holds

Tell students: *"You can't write a strong argument about something you've never been curious about. Curiosity is the engine. The argument is the vehicle."*

---

## Guided Practice: The Curiosity Drill (10 minutes)

Present this statement to the whole class:

**"Middle schoolers should be allowed to choose their own school schedule."**

Before anyone argues FOR or AGAINST it, the class will practice being curious together.

**Round 1 — Ask "What do I actually know?"**
Invite students to share facts or experiences they genuinely know about school schedules. Write responses on the board.

**Round 2 — Ask "What do I NOT know?"**
Now ask students to generate questions they'd need answered before making a solid argument. Prompt them if needed:
- *Who would benefit? Who might be harmed?*
- *Has any school tried this? What happened?*
- *What would teachers say? Parents? Administrators?*

Write their questions on the board. Let the list grow. Celebrate unusual or surprising questions.

**Round 3 — Ask "Who disagrees, and why might they be right?"**
This is the hardest part. Ask students to genuinely try to understand the opposing view — not to defeat it, but to understand it. Spend two minutes having students talk with a partner about the strongest possible argument on the OTHER side from their own instinct.

Debrief: *"Notice how your thinking got more complex? That's curiosity at work. And complex thinking makes for powerful writing."*

---

## Group Activity: Mystery Scenario Cards (15 minutes)

Distribute students into groups of three or four. Each group receives a **Mystery Scenario Card** with a debatable topic:

- *Should schools have cell phone-free zones during lunch?*
- *Should students grade their own tests?*
- *Should physical education be optional in middle school?*
- *Should schools start one hour later in the morning?*

**Instructions:**

**Step 1 (3 min):** Before picking a side, each group writes down at least **five genuine questions** about their topic. No arguing yet — only questioning.

**Step 2 (5 min):** Using their questions as a guide, groups discuss both sides and decide on a group claim.

**Step 3 (7 min):** Groups complete the argument graphic organizer together, filling in: claim, two reasons with evidence (they may use logical reasoning and general knowledge), one counterclaim, and one rebuttal.

Circulate and coach groups. Push them with questions like: *"What's the strongest thing someone who disagrees could say?"* and *"How do you know that? What would make this more convincing?"*

---

## Independent Writing (10 minutes)

Students return to the original homework scenario from the opening.

This time, they write a **full argumentative paragraph** independently using their graphic organizer structure. Remind them to:
- Begin with a clear claim
- Include at least one reason with evidence or logical support
- Acknowledge what someone on the other side might say
- End with a sentence that reinforces their position

Encourage students to let their **curiosity show** in their writing — the questions they considered should make their argument feel richer and more honest.

---

## Closing: The Curiosity Connection (5 minutes)

Bring the class back together. Ask two or three students to share a sentence from their paragraph — specifically their counterclaim sentence.

Then close with this reflection question, asked aloud or written in notebooks:

**"What's one question about this topic you're still curious about — one you couldn't fully answer today?"**

Take a few responses. Celebrate that students still have questions. Tell them: *"The best writers — and the best thinkers — are never fully satisfied with what they know. That hunger to keep asking is called intellectual curiosity, and it will make you a stronger writer, a stronger student, and a stronger person for the rest of your life."*

End with the takeaway on the board:

> **"An argument without curiosity is just an opinion. An argument with curiosity becomes a conversation worth having."**

---

## Assessment

**Informal:** Monitor group discussions and graphic organizers during group activity.
**Exit Ticket:** Collect the independent paragraph. Look for presence of a claim, at least one reason, acknowledgment of a counterclaim, and logical organization.

---

## Extension

Students who finish early may write a second paragraph from the **opposing side**, practicing the skill of arguing for a position they don't personally hold — the ultimate exercise in curious, empathetic thinking.
