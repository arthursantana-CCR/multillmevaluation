## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Writing Arguments That Matter
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Lesson Objective
Students will construct a structured written argument supporting a claim with clear reasons, relevant evidence, and acknowledgment of opposing views — while engaging creativity throughout the process.

---

## Materials
- Sticky notes (two colors)
- Argument graphic organizer (teacher-prepared)
- Sample argument paragraph (projected)
- Writing journals or lined paper

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Hook — "Take a Stand" (5 minutes)
Post three controversial-but-safe prompts on the board:
- *Should school start later?*
- *Is homework necessary?*
- *Should students choose their own reading books?*

Students physically move to corners labeled AGREE, DISAGREE, or UNSURE. They briefly explain their position to a neighbor. This activates prior opinions and warms students up for argumentation.

---

### Direct Instruction — Anatomy of an Argument (10 minutes)
Using a projected sample paragraph, teacher models the components of W.7.1:
- **Claim** (your position)
- **Reasons** (why you believe it)
- **Evidence** (facts, data, examples)
- **Counterclaim** (opposing view)
- **Rebuttal** (why your side still wins)

Teacher thinks aloud: *"I could write what everyone already thinks — or I could find the angle nobody's considered yet."* This signals that originality matters.

---

### Guided Activity — Counterclaim Carousel (10 minutes)
Students receive a sticky note in two colors. On **Color A**, they write their chosen claim. On **Color B**, they write the strongest possible opposing argument — one they genuinely find challenging to answer.

Students post both notes on the board and do a gallery walk, reading three peers' counterclaims. They mark ones that surprised them with a star.

---

### Independent Work — Argument Draft (20 minutes)
Students select one of the three topic prompts (or propose their own with teacher approval) and draft a structured argument paragraph using the graphic organizer. Requirements:
- A clear, specific claim (not vague)
- Two reasons with at least one piece of evidence each
- One acknowledged counterclaim
- A rebuttal that directly addresses the counterclaim

Teacher circulates, conferencing briefly with students who are stuck or taking familiar, surface-level positions. Prompt struggling students: *"What would someone who disagrees say? Can you use that to make your argument stronger?"*

---

### Peer Review — "Challenge and Strengthen" (7 minutes)
Partners exchange drafts. Reviewers underline the counterclaim and write one sentence: *"Your argument would be even stronger if…"* Writers then have two minutes to revise one part of their draft based on feedback.

---

### Closing Reflection (3 minutes)
Students write one sentence in their journal: *"The riskiest or most original choice I made in my argument today was…"*

---

## Creativity Subcompetency Integration

**CRE1a — Developing Personal Tastes**
*Activity:* The "Take a Stand" hook requires students to commit to a genuine personal position rather than a safe or expected one.
*Assessment:* Observe whether students' claim reflects a considered personal stance or a default crowd-pleasing answer. Listen during corner discussions for authentic justification.

**CRE1b — Developing Personal Aesthetics and Style**
*Activity:* During the draft, students choose how to open their argument — with a question, a bold statement, or a surprising fact.
*Assessment:* Note variety in opening strategies. Students who experiment with voice and structure beyond the template show emerging stylistic identity.

**CRE2a — Generating New Ideas**
*Activity:* Students are encouraged to propose their own topic rather than selecting from the provided list.
*Assessment:* Track how many students choose a self-generated topic and whether their claims move beyond the obvious angle (e.g., arguing *why* homework causes anxiety rather than simply that it should be eliminated).

**CRE2b — Seeking New Ideas**
*Activity:* The Counterclaim Carousel gallery walk exposes students to perspectives they hadn't considered.
*Assessment:* Check whether starred counterclaims appear incorporated into students' rebuttals, indicating genuine integration of new perspectives.

**CRE3a — Being Comfortable with Risks and Uncertainty**
*Activity:* Students are explicitly invited to take an unpopular or minority position during the Hook.
*Assessment:* Note students who move to the UNSURE corner and then commit to a specific claim anyway — demonstrating engagement with uncertainty rather than avoidance.

**CRE3b — Being Comfortable with Failure**
*Activity:* The peer review phase asks students to receive critical feedback and immediately revise.
*Assessment:* Observe students' responses to critique. Students who revise substantively rather than superficially demonstrate productive use of failure-adjacent feedback.

**CRE4a — Connecting Ideas for Cohesiveness**
*Activity:* The graphic organizer requires students to link evidence explicitly back to their reason and claim.
*Assessment:* Review organizers for logical chains: Does the evidence actually support the stated reason? Does the reason connect to the claim?

**CRE4b — Reorganizing Ideas for Cohesiveness**
*Activity:* During the two-minute post-feedback revision window, students must decide *where* to insert the improvement.
*Assessment:* Check whether revisions are inserted logically within the argument structure or tacked on at the end without integration.

**CRE4c — Refining Ideas for Cohesiveness**
*Activity:* The closing journal prompt asks students to identify and name their boldest revision choice.
*Assessment:* Journal responses that describe a specific, deliberate change (rather than "I fixed a spelling error") indicate intentional refinement.

**CRE5a — Realizing Ideas**
*Activity:* The 20-minute draft transforms the student's position from a sticky note into a structured written argument.
*Assessment:* Evaluate whether the final draft is complete across all required components. Partial completion signals difficulty translating ideas into form.

**CRE5b — Working with Constraints**
*Activity:* The graphic organizer imposes structural constraints (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal). Students must argue creatively *within* that form.
*Assessment:* Students who produce original, specific arguments despite — or because of — the structure demonstrate strategic use of constraints rather than resistance to them.

**55-Minute Lesson Plan: Writing Arguments to Support Claims with Clear Reasons and Relevant Evidence (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1) with Focus on CCR Creativity Subcompetencies**

### Lesson Objective:
Students will write a well-organized argumentative paragraph supporting a claim with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledgment of opposing claims. Throughout the lesson, students will engage in activities designed to develop critical thinking creativity subcompetencies (CRE1–CRE5).

---

### Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Sample argumentative prompt (e.g., “Should school uniforms be mandatory?”)
- Graphic organizer for argument structure (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim)
- Student notebooks or writing paper
- Sticky notes
- Timer

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction & Warm-up (5 minutes)**
- Briefly explain the lesson goal: writing an argument with clear reasons, evidence, and acknowledging opposing views.
- Introduce the argumentative prompt: “Should school uniforms be mandatory?”
- Quick think-pair-share: Students share their initial opinion with a partner.

**Targets CRE1a (Developing personal tastes):**  
- *Activity:* Students express their personal stance on the prompt.  
- *Observation:* Listen for students’ clear expression of personal opinions vs. parroting others’ views.

---

#### 2. **Mini-Lesson: Structure of an Argument (10 minutes)**
- Present the components of an argument: claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, and rebuttal.
- Show a sample paragraph on the board.
- Highlight how the writer acknowledges an opposing claim and logically organizes reasoning.

**Targets CRE4a (Connecting ideas for cohesiveness):**  
- *Activity:* Analyze the sample paragraph’s flow and how ideas connect.  
- *Observation:* Ask students to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and counterclaim; note their ability to see logical connections.

---

#### 3. **Idea Generation & Brainstorming (10 minutes)**
- Students individually brainstorm reasons and evidence supporting their claim and possible counterclaims.
- Encourage them to think beyond obvious ideas and consider different perspectives.
- Use sticky notes to jot down ideas.

**Targets CRE2a (Generating new ideas) and CRE2b (Seeking new ideas):**  
- *Activity:* Brainstorming session encouraging originality and exploration of diverse perspectives.  
- *Observation:* Review sticky notes for originality and integration of diverse ideas; note if students avoid or embrace new perspectives.

---

#### 4. **Group Discussion: Exploring Opposing Claims (8 minutes)**
- In small groups, students share their claims and brainstorm possible opposing claims.
- Groups discuss how to respectfully acknowledge and respond to these counterclaims.

**Targets CRE3a (Being comfortable with risks and uncertainty) and CRE3b (Being comfortable with failure):**  
- *Activity:* Students take intellectual risks by considering and defending against opposing views.  
- *Observation:* Note students’ willingness to engage with uncertainty and handle disagreement constructively.

---

#### 5. **Writing Draft Argument Paragraph (15 minutes)**
- Students write a draft paragraph including:  
  - A clear claim  
  - At least two reasons with relevant evidence  
  - Acknowledgment of an opposing claim with a rebuttal  
- Encourage students to organize ideas logically and use transitions.

**Targets CRE1b (Developing personal aesthetics and style), CRE4b (Reorganizing ideas for cohesiveness), CRE4c (Refining ideas for cohesiveness), CRE5a (Realizing ideas), and CRE5b (Working with constraints):**  
- *Activity:* Writing draft with attention to personal voice, logical flow, and clarity within time constraints.  
- *Observation:* Assess students’ unique writing style, ability to reorganize ideas for clarity, refine their argument, and produce a coherent paragraph under time limits.

---

#### 6. **Peer Review & Revision (7 minutes)**
- Students exchange drafts with a partner.
- Using a checklist, peers identify strengths and suggest improvements focusing on clarity, evidence, and acknowledgment of counterclaims.
- Students revise their paragraphs accordingly.

**Targets CRE4b (Reorganizing ideas), CRE4c (Refining ideas), CRE5a (Realizing ideas), and CRE5b (Working with constraints):**  
- *Activity:* Peer feedback and revision to improve logical flow and clarity.  
- *Observation:* Monitor how students incorporate feedback and improve their writing cohesiveness and quality.

---

#### 7. **Closing & Reflection (5 minutes)**
- Volunteers share their revised claims and one reason or counterclaim.
- Reflect on the creative process: What was challenging? How did they handle new ideas or opposing views?
- Briefly discuss how creativity helped them write stronger arguments.

**Targets CRE1a (Developing personal tastes), CRE3a (Being comfortable with risks), and CRE3b (Being comfortable with failure):**  
- *Activity:* Reflection on personal growth and creative risk-taking.  
- *Observation:* Listen for students’ awareness of their creative process and comfort with challenges.
