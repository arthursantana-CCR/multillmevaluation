## Candidate 1
# The Case for Curiosity: Building Arguments Around What We Don't Know

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Materials Needed
- Sticky notes (two colors)
- Whiteboard or chart paper
- Argument graphic organizer (hand-drawn or printed)
- Pencils

---

## Lesson Flow

### HOOK — *The Burning Question* (8 minutes)

Walk in and write one sentence on the board before saying a word:

**"Curious people are more successful than smart people."**

Give students 90 seconds to silently decide — agree or disagree. Then do a quick show of hands. Don't reveal your opinion. Just say: *"Interesting. But you can't just have a feeling about something. You have to be able to defend it. That's what we're doing today."*

Ask students: *"What does it even mean to be curious?"* Take 4–5 responses. Write their words on the board. Circle the ones that surprise you. Tell them: *"Notice I'm writing down things I didn't expect. That's what curiosity looks like from the front of a room."*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *What Makes an Argument* (10 minutes)

Tell students that an argument isn't a fight — it's a structured way of saying *I believe this, and here's why you should too.*

Draw this simple framework on the board:

**CLAIM → REASONS → EVIDENCE → COUNTERCLAIM → REBUTTAL**

Walk through each piece briefly:

- **Claim:** Your position. What you believe.
- **Reasons:** Why you believe it.
- **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or research that back up your reasons.
- **Counterclaim:** The other side's strongest point.
- **Rebuttal:** Why your argument still holds up despite that point.

Tell students: *"The counterclaim is where most students get scared. They think admitting another side weakens them. It actually does the opposite — it shows you've thought harder than everyone else."*

Connect this directly to curiosity: *"Curious people are better arguers. Why? Because they actually want to know if they're wrong. They ask questions. They look for the counterclaim themselves."*

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — *Build It Together* (12 minutes)

Use this prompt as a class:

**"Should students be allowed to choose what they learn about for one hour each week?"**

Work through the argument framework together on the board. Invite students to contribute each piece. As they do, push with questions like:

- *"What evidence would make that reason stronger?"*
- *"Who would disagree with that? What would they say?"*
- *"How do we answer that disagreement without dismissing it?"*

When a student says something unexpected or off-topic, don't redirect immediately. Pause. Say: *"That's an interesting angle — where does that idea come from?"* Model curiosity visibly and out loud.

---

### CURIOSITY DETOUR — *The Two-Sticky-Note Activity* (7 minutes)

Hand every student two sticky notes — two different colors.

On **Color 1:** Write one question you have about anything — a topic, a person, a place, an idea. No limits. The stranger the better.

On **Color 2:** Write one question you have about *the other side* of your own opinion on something you care about.

Students post both on the board. Read a handful aloud. Point out that the second sticky note — the one questioning their own belief — is the hardest one to write. Tell them: *"That discomfort? That's where real thinking happens. That's where good arguments are born."*

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — *Your Turn* (15 minutes)

Students choose one of three prompts:

1. Should schools assign homework?
2. Is social media more harmful than helpful for teenagers?
3. Should students grade their own work?

Using their graphic organizer, they write a structured argument paragraph (or two) that includes a claim, at least two reasons with evidence or examples, a counterclaim, and a rebuttal.

Remind them: *"You don't have to know everything about this topic. Curiosity means you start writing and let the questions guide you."*

Circulate. When a student is stuck, ask *"What would someone who disagrees with you say?"* That question almost always unlocks the writing.

---

### CLOSING — *The Curious Arguer* (3 minutes)

Bring the class back. Ask:

- *"What question came up while you were writing that you didn't expect?"*
- *"Did anyone change their mind even a little while writing?"*

End with this: *"The best arguers and the most curious people share one habit — they take their own ideas seriously enough to challenge them. That's not weakness. That's how you get smarter."*

Leave the original sentence on the board. Don't erase it. Let them wonder.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity Through Argument Writing**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
Students will understand the value of curiosity and learn how to express their curiosity-driven claims through well-organized argumentative writing that includes clear reasons, relevant evidence, and acknowledgment of opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short curiosity-themed prompt and example arguments  
- Chart paper or projector for displaying examples  
- Sticky notes  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board. Highlight key ideas such as asking questions, wanting to learn more, exploring new ideas, and being open-minded.  
- Briefly explain: Curiosity is the desire to learn and understand more about the world. It drives discovery, creativity, and problem-solving.  
- Share a quick, relatable example: *“Imagine you see a strange plant in your backyard. Curiosity makes you ask, ‘What kind of plant is this? Is it safe? How does it grow?’”*  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science or exploration but also in writing and thinking critically.

---

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Explain that today, students will practice writing arguments based on curious questions or claims they have about a topic.  
- Introduce the standard: Writing arguments means making a claim and supporting it with reasons and evidence. It also means thinking about other opinions and explaining why your claim still makes sense.  
- Present a simple curiosity-driven prompt on the board or handout, for example:  
  *“Should schools have longer recess to encourage curiosity and learning?”*  
- Discuss briefly: This question invites curiosity about how recess affects learning and creativity.  
- Show a quick example of a claim: *“Yes, schools should have longer recess because it gives students time to explore, ask questions, and think creatively.”*  
- Point out how this claim can be supported with reasons and evidence, and how opposing views might say recess takes away from learning time.

---

**3. Guided Practice: Building an Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).  
- Give each group a curiosity-based question related to school or everyday life, such as:  
  - *“Is it better to learn from books or from hands-on experiences?”*  
  - *“Should students be allowed to choose their own projects in class?”*  
  - *“Does asking more questions help you learn better?”*  
- Each group will:  
  1. Choose a claim (yes or no) related to their question.  
  2. List at least two reasons supporting their claim.  
  3. Think of one opposing claim and how they might respond to it.  
- Circulate and assist groups as needed, encouraging them to be curious about the topic and to think deeply about reasons and evidence.

---

**4. Sharing and Class Discussion (10 minutes)**  
- Have each group share their claim, reasons, and opposing viewpoint with the class.  
- Write key points on the board to highlight how curiosity helped them think of reasons and consider other opinions.  
- Ask the class: *“How did being curious help you come up with your argument?”*  
- Emphasize that curiosity leads to asking questions that make arguments stronger and more interesting.

---

**5. Independent Writing (8 minutes)**  
- Ask students to choose one curiosity question from the group activity or create their own curiosity question.  
- Have them write a short argumentative paragraph that includes:  
  - A clear claim related to their curiosity question  
  - At least two reasons with supporting evidence or examples  
  - Acknowledgment of an opposing claim and a response to it  
- Remind them to organize their ideas logically and clearly.

---

**6. Wrap-Up and Reflection (2 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- End with a quick reflection prompt: *“How can curiosity help you become a better writer and thinker?”*  
- Encourage students to keep asking questions and using curiosity to explore ideas in writing and in life.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Write a longer argumentative essay on a curiosity-driven question of their choice, using the skills practiced in class.

---

**Summary:**  
This lesson helps students understand curiosity as a powerful tool for learning and argument writing. By connecting curiosity to the writing standard CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1, students practice making claims supported by reasons and evidence, while also considering opposing views, all through the lens of their natural desire to ask questions and explore ideas.
