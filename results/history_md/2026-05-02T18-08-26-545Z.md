## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Define and clarify the central question using precise language
- Organize evidence into a logical argument structure
- Evaluate the reliability of sources on both sides of the debate
- Weigh pros and cons of homework policies using reasoned analysis
- Reflect on how their own thinking evolved during the lesson

---

## Materials Needed
- Printed source cards (3–4 short excerpts from researchers, teachers, and students on homework)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Graphic organizer handout (T-chart + reflection box)
- Timer

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### Opening Hook — 5 Minutes

Write on the board: *"Homework: necessary tool or unnecessary burden?"*

Ask students to do a quick silent thumbs up/down/middle to show their initial stance. Tell them not to worry about being right — their opinion may change by the end of class. This primes students to stay open-minded throughout the lesson.

---

### Activity 1: Clarifying the Question — CRI1a — 10 Minutes

**Activity:** Before debating anything, students must first understand what they are actually arguing about. Present three slightly different versions of the question:

- "Should schools assign any homework?"
- "Should homework be graded?"
- "Should homework be limited to 30 minutes per night?"

In pairs, students discuss: *Are these the same question? What exactly does "homework" mean — busywork, review, creative projects?*

As a class, collaboratively write one agreed-upon, precise question on the board that will guide the rest of the lesson.

**How CRI1a is assessed:** Listen for students who distinguish between vague and specific claims. Students operating at Level 2 will push back on imprecise language and insist on a tighter definition before moving forward. Students at Level 1 will accept the question without interrogating its meaning.

---

### Activity 2: Sorting and Organizing Evidence — CRI1b — 8 Minutes

**Activity:** Each student receives a set of six statement cards drawn from research, parent opinions, student testimonials, and teacher perspectives. Students must sort these into two columns — *Supports Homework* and *Opposes Homework* — and then rank them within each column from strongest to weakest evidence.

**How CRI1b is assessed:** Walk the room observing whether students create a consistent logic for their ranking. Level 2 students will explain their organizational reasoning clearly ("I ranked this one higher because it uses data, not just opinion"). Level 1 students will sort correctly but struggle to justify the internal ranking.

---

### Activity 3: Evaluating Sources — CRI2a and CRI2b — 10 Minutes

**Activity:** Reveal the source behind each statement card — one is from a peer-reviewed education journal, one from a parent blog, one from a teacher union statement, one from a student newspaper, and one from a government education report. Students answer three questions in writing:

1. Which sources are most trustworthy and why?
2. Which sources might have a bias? What is it?
3. Would you remove any source from your argument? Why?

**How CRI2a is assessed:** Students who cross-reference the source type with the claim being made — noting, for example, that a union statement may advocate for teachers rather than students — demonstrate Level 2 validity assessment. Students who simply say "it's from the internet so it's bad" remain at Level 1.

**How CRI2b is assessed:** Quality judgment is observed when students select sources based on depth, authority, and relevance rather than convenience. A student who chooses the peer-reviewed journal and explains what makes its methodology stronger shows Level 2 quality assessment.

---

### Activity 4: Structured Pros and Cons Debate Prep — CRI3a and CRI4a — 12 Minutes

**Activity:** Using their graphic organizer, students individually build a mini-argument addressing *both* sides — even if they personally disagree with one. They must list at least two genuine pros and two genuine cons of assigning homework, then write a concluding sentence that justifies their final position with reasoning.

Then, in groups of four, students share their arguments and challenge each other's weakest points.

**How CRI3a is assessed:** Look for students who acknowledge legitimate trade-offs rather than dismissing the opposing side. Level 2 students will note long-term implications, such as homework's unequal impact on students without quiet home environments.

**How CRI4a is assessed:** The concluding sentence is key. Students at Level 2 construct an "if-then" or conditional argument that anticipates objections. Students at Level 1 state a preference without logical scaffolding.

---

### Closing Reflection — CRI5a — 10 Minutes

**Activity:** Students return to their original thumbs up/down/middle position from the opening. They write a short paragraph (4–6 sentences) answering:

- Did your opinion change? Why or why not?
- What assumption did you hold at the start that the lesson challenged?
- What would you need to know more about before making a final decision?

**How CRI5a is assessed:** Students demonstrating Level 2 metacognition will identify a specific belief they held, explain what disrupted it, and articulate remaining uncertainty. Students at Level 1 will note a surface-level change ("I didn't think about teachers before") without deeper self-examination.

---

## Closing — 5 Minutes

Ask two or three volunteers to share one thing that shifted in their thinking. End with this reminder: *"Strong thinkers don't always change their mind — but they always know why they believe what they believe."*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Homework Huddle - To Assign or Not to Assign?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Should schools have homework?

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify different arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support various perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of alternative choices regarding homework policies.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form and justify their own position on homework.
*   Reflect on their thinking process and how new information influenced their perspective.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (contains 3-4 short, diverse texts/quotes: e.g., a snippet from a research study on homework effectiveness, a quote from a parent advocating for less homework, a quote from a teacher explaining its benefits, a short news article summary on a school that banned homework). Each text will have a clear (or intentionally less clear) source citation.
*   Handout 2: T-Chart graphic organizer ("Arguments For Homework" / "Arguments Against Homework")
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, 7th graders! Let's start with a quick poll. Raise your hand if you think homework is a good idea. Now, raise your hand if you think schools should get rid of homework. Interesting! Today, we're going to explore this very question: 'Should schools have homework?' It's a topic that affects all of us, and there are many different opinions and reasons behind them."
*   **Teacher:** "Our goal today isn't just to share our opinions, but to think critically about *why* we hold those opinions, and to understand the different sides of this complex issue using evidence and careful thought."

**II. Exploring Perspectives & Clarifying Information (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will work in pairs. Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
*   **Teacher:** "In your pairs, read through the different perspectives on homework provided in Handout 1. As you read, your first task is to understand what each person or source is saying. Underline or highlight the main idea or key argument from each perspective."

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** After reading, students will paraphrase each perspective in their own words on their T-chart (Handout 2). They should aim for precision, capturing the core argument without adding their own bias. For example, if a text states, "Research indicates excessive homework can lead to student burnout," students should paraphrase it as "Some studies suggest that too much homework might cause students to feel exhausted," rather than "Homework is bad because it makes kids tired."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate among pairs. Listen to student discussions and review their paraphrases on the T-chart. Look for accurate and precise restatements of the original ideas. Are they capturing the essence of the argument? Are they avoiding misinterpretation or adding personal opinions?

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** As students paraphrase, they will also categorize each argument onto their T-chart (Handout 2) under "Arguments For Homework" or "Arguments Against Homework."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe how students group the information. Are arguments consistently placed on the correct side of the T-chart? Is their organization logical and easy to follow? Do they struggle with arguments that might have nuances, indicating a need for further clarification?

**III. Assessing Information: Validity and Quality (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Bring the class back together for a brief whole-group discussion.
*   **Teacher:** "Now that you've clarified and organized the arguments, let's look at where these arguments come from. Each perspective on your handout has a source listed. Let's discuss these sources."

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Look at the sources for each perspective. For example, one might be 'Dr. Anya Sharma, Educational Researcher,' another 'Parent Blog Post,' and another 'Local News Report.' In your pairs, discuss: Which of these sources seems most reliable or trustworthy? Why? Which might have a particular bias or limited perspective? Why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen to student discussions. Can they differentiate between a research study and a blog post? Do they articulate reasons for why one source might be more valid than another (e.g., "A researcher uses data," "A parent might only speak from their own experience")?

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, thinking about the *information itself*, not just the source: Which arguments or pieces of evidence do you think are the strongest or most convincing for either side? Why? Which ones might be less impactful or lack depth, even if they come from a seemingly valid source?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe which arguments students identify as "strongest" and their justifications. Do they prioritize arguments backed by data or expert opinion over anecdotal evidence? Do they recognize when information is too general or lacks specific supporting details?

**IV. Weighing Choices & Applying Reasoning (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will now prepare to form their own reasoned opinion.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Before you decide on your own stance, I want each pair to briefly discuss *both* sides of the argument, even if you already have a strong opinion. Imagine you are presenting to a school board. What are the strongest 'pros' for homework? What are the strongest 'cons'? Make sure you can articulate both sides fairly, considering the potential trade-offs of each choice."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen. Do students genuinely engage with both sides, or do they quickly dismiss the opposing view? Can they articulate the trade-offs involved in having or not having homework, considering different impacts on students, teachers, and families?

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, based on all the information you've clarified, organized, and assessed, and after weighing the pros and cons, I want each of you individually to write a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) stating your position on whether schools should have homework. You must support your position with at least two pieces of evidence or arguments from our discussion today. Make sure your reasoning clearly connects your evidence to your conclusion."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and review the paragraphs. Look for clear claims, relevant evidence, and logical connections between the evidence and the student's conclusion. Are there any logical fallacies or unsupported claims? Is the reasoning coherent and easy to follow?

**V. Reflection & Conclusion (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Distribute Exit Ticket slips.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, please answer these two questions:
        1.  Did your initial opinion about homework change or become stronger after today's lesson? Explain why or why not, referencing specific information or discussions.
        2.  What part of our critical thinking process today (clarifying, organizing, assessing sources, weighing pros/cons) helped you the most in forming your final opinion, and why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and read exit tickets. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness. Do students acknowledge shifts in their thinking or a deeper understanding? Can they identify specific strategies that aided their critical thinking process and explain *how* it helped them?

*   **Teacher:** "Thank you for engaging in such thoughtful discussion today. Critical thinking isn't just about having an opinion, but about understanding different perspectives, evaluating information, and forming well-reasoned conclusions. Keep practicing these skills in all your subjects!"

Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?

---

**Lesson Objectives:**  
- Students will clarify and organize information about homework.  
- Students will assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.

- Students will apply sound reasoning to form and support their own opinions.  
- Students will reflect on their thinking processes throughout the lesson.

### Lesson Outline and Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration

---

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Begin with a class brainstorming session: “What do you know about homework? What are some reasons for and against it?”  
- Teacher records student ideas on the board, prompting students to explain their thoughts clearly.  
- Then, provide a short, clear informational text (about 150 words) summarizing common arguments for and against homework.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Observe students’ ability to identify relevant information during brainstorming.  
- During discussion, note if students clarify ideas precisely (e.g., explaining why homework might help learning or cause stress).  
- Look for students who ask clarifying questions or rephrase ideas to deepen understanding.  

---

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** In pairs, students receive mixed-up statements about homework (some pros, some cons, some facts).  
- Task: Organize these statements into a logical structure (e.g., pros on one side, cons on the other, facts in the middle).  
- Then, pairs create a simple outline or graphic organizer to show their arrangement.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Review students’ organizers for logical flow and coherence.  
- Check if information is grouped meaningfully to support understanding.  
- Note if any students present scattered or contradictory arrangements.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Provide students with two short excerpts about homework: one from a reputable educational research source, one from an opinion blog.  
- In small groups, students discuss which source seems more reliable and why.  
- Guide them to identify clues about source credibility (author expertise, evidence cited, tone).  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Listen for students’ ability to identify source types and evaluate reliability.  
- Look for evidence of cross-checking or questioning information rather than accepting it at face value.  
- Note if students repeat misinformation without critique.  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Teacher models how to judge quality by comparing a well-supported fact with a vague claim about homework.  
- Students then quickly rate a few sample statements as high or low quality based on relevance and depth.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Check students’ judgments for sophistication—do they recognize authoritative, detailed information?  
- Note if students accept irrelevant or shallow information without question.  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Students write a short paragraph weighing the pros and cons of homework, considering both immediate effects (e.g., learning, stress) and long-term implications (e.g., study habits, family time).  
- Encourage them to consider alternative perspectives and trade-offs.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Review paragraphs for balanced analysis and consideration of multiple factors.  
- Look for systematic evaluation rather than superficial listing of pros and cons.  
- Note if any students ignore alternative views or present one-sided arguments.  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Students share their position on homework (for or against) and explain their reasoning aloud or in writing.  
- Prompt them to anticipate counterarguments and address complexities (e.g., “Some say homework helps, but what about students with less support at home?”).  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Listen for logical, well-structured reasoning that acknowledges complexities.  
- Identify any faulty logic or unsupported claims.  
- Note clarity and sophistication in justifying their stance.  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** End with a metacognitive reflection: students write or discuss how their thinking changed during the lesson.  
- Questions to guide reflection: “Did you change your opinion? What new information helped you? How did you decide what to believe?”  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Look for evidence of deep reflection and awareness of their own reasoning process.  
- Note if students mention adjusting their thinking based on new evidence or perspectives.  
- Identify any lack of self-awareness or repeated errors without reflection.

- Mixed-up statements about homework for sorting  
- Excerpts from different sources on homework  
- Sample statements for quality assessment
