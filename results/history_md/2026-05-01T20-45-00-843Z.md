# Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts
**Topic:** Should Schools Have Homework?

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Clarify and organize arguments about the homework debate using precise language
2. Evaluate the validity and quality of sources related to educational research
3. Weigh the pros and cons of assigning homework from multiple perspectives
4. Apply sound reasoning to defend a position with evidence
5. Reflect on how their thinking changed throughout the lesson

---

## Materials Needed

- Printed or projected excerpts from two sources: a study supporting homework (e.g., Cooper et al., 2006) and one questioning its value (e.g., Alfie Kohn's *The Homework Myth*)
- T-chart graphic organizer handout
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- Whiteboard or shared digital space
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### Warm-Up — Hook and Activation (8 Minutes)

**Activity:** "Homework: Love It or Leave It?"

Begin by asking students to silently write for two minutes answering the prompt: *"Do you think homework helps you learn? Why or why not?"* Then invite 3–4 volunteers to share responses. Record key words on the board without judgment.

Tell students: *"Today we are not just sharing opinions — we are going to think like detectives and lawyers. We will examine evidence, challenge assumptions, and build real arguments."*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking):** Students articulate their initial assumptions before instruction begins, creating a baseline for later comparison.

---

### Segment 1 — Clarifying the Question (10 Minutes)

**Activity:** "What Are We Actually Asking?"

**(CRI1a — Clarifying Information)**

Present students with three versions of the question:
- "Is homework good?"
- "Should all schools assign homework every night?"
- "Under what conditions, if any, does homework benefit student learning?"

In pairs, students discuss: *Which question is the most precise? Why does the wording matter?*

Bring the class together to agree on a refined, working question for the lesson. Demonstrate how vague questions produce vague arguments. Guide students toward understanding that precision in framing a question directly affects the quality of answers they can find and defend.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who distinguish between broad and narrow claims. Look for precise language in their verbal responses.

---

### Segment 2 — Organizing What We Know (10 Minutes)

**Activity:** T-Chart Build

**(CRI1b — Organizing Information)**

Distribute the T-chart graphic organizer with two columns: *Arguments FOR Homework* and *Arguments AGAINST Homework.* Students read two short text excerpts (provided by the teacher — approximately 150 words each) summarizing opposing research positions. Students independently fill in their T-charts, pulling direct evidence from the texts.

After individual work, pairs compare their charts and resolve any gaps or inconsistencies before sharing one strong point from each column with the class.

**Assessment:** Collect T-charts at the end of class. Look for logical grouping of ideas rather than random lists. Level 2 students will categorize by theme (academic outcomes, equity, family time, etc.).

---

### Segment 3 — Evaluating Sources (10 Minutes)

**Activity:** "Who Said It and Does It Matter?"

**(CRI2a — Assessing Validity | CRI2b — Assessing Quality)**

Display four "sources" on the board:
1. A peer-reviewed study published in a psychology journal
2. A blog post by a frustrated parent
3. A teacher's anecdotal classroom observation
4. A national education policy report

Students use sticky notes — one color for "high credibility," another for "low credibility" — to rank each source and write a one-sentence justification. Facilitate a whole-class discussion about *why* source quality matters and how bias, expertise, and evidence standards affect trustworthiness.

Ask: *"Could the parent's blog ever be useful? Could the journal study ever be misleading?"* Push students toward nuanced thinking rather than binary judgments.

**Assessment:** Review sticky note justifications. Strong responses will reference specific criteria such as peer review, author expertise, sample size, or potential bias.

---

### Segment 4 — Weighing Trade-Offs (10 Minutes)

**Activity:** Structured Academic Controversy

**(CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons | CRI4a — Sound Reasoning)**

Divide students into groups of four. Two students argue FOR homework, two argue AGAINST. After each side presents for 90 seconds, they switch positions and argue the opposite side. Finally, the group of four must reach a consensus recommendation: *"Under what specific conditions should schools assign homework?"*

Each group writes one consensus sentence on the board.

Encourage students to move beyond surface claims ("homework is stressful") toward systemic reasoning ("homework may reinforce inequality because not all students have quiet study spaces or parental support at home").

**Assessment:** Observe group discussions. Look for students who acknowledge complexity, consider long-term implications, and anticipate counterarguments — markers of Level 2 reasoning for both CRI3a and CRI4a.

---

### Closing — Metacognitive Reflection (7 Minutes)

**Activity:** Exit Ticket + "Thinking Shift" Statement

**(CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking)**

Students complete a structured exit ticket with three prompts:
1. *"My original opinion about homework was..."*
2. *"One thing that challenged or changed my thinking today was..."*
3. *"The strongest argument on the opposing side was... and I would respond by..."*

Invite two or three students to share their "thinking shift" statements aloud. Close by affirming that changing your mind based on evidence is a sign of intellectual strength, not weakness.

**Assessment:** Exit tickets reveal the depth of metacognitive reflection. Level 2 responses will name a specific moment, piece of evidence, or reasoning error that prompted them to reconsider, and will articulate a strategic adjustment to their thinking.
