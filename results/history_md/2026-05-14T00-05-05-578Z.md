## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Everywhere

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify whether a relationship is proportional using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Represent proportional relationships across multiple forms
- Justify their reasoning using mathematical evidence

---

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, markers
- "Is It Proportional?" task cards (one per pair)
- Anchor chart paper
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### WARM-UP — 7 Minutes
#### *CRI1a: Clarifying Information*

**Activity:** Display two tables on the board:

| Hours Worked | Pay ($) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |

| Miles Driven | Gas Used (gal) |
|---|---|
| 10 | 2 |
| 20 | 5 |
| 30 | 9 |

Ask students: *"Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know? Write your reasoning in one precise sentence."*

Cold-call two or three students. Prompt them to replace vague language ("it looks the same") with precise mathematical language ("the ratio of pay to hours is always 12:1"). Record the refined definitions on an anchor chart.

**Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students who use exact ratio language versus imprecise descriptors. Students at Level 2 will state clearly: *"A proportional relationship has a constant ratio between the two quantities."* Students at Level 1 may say the numbers "go up evenly" without naming the ratio.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 10 Minutes
#### *CRI1b: Organizing Information & CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information*

**Activity:** Using the anchor chart, introduce the **four representations** of proportional relationships: tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions. Model one complete example using a real-world context — a smoothie recipe where every 2 cups of fruit require 1 cup of yogurt.

Walk students through all four representations simultaneously, explicitly organizing them in a 2×2 quadrant on the board. Emphasize:
- Tables: constant ratio (k = y/x)
- Graphs: straight line through the origin
- Equations: y = kx form
- Verbal: "for every x, there is a constant y"

Pause and ask: *"If someone showed you a graph that was a straight line but didn't pass through the origin, would that be proportional? Why or why not?"* This question pushes students to assess which features of information actually matter — a high-quality, precise standard.

**Observation/Assessment:** Watch for students who note the origin detail unprompted. Students at Level 2 demonstrate they are selecting the most authoritative mathematical criteria, not just surface patterns.

---

### PARTNER TASK — 15 Minutes
#### *CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons & CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning*

**Activity:** Each pair receives a task card with a scenario:

*"A school store sells notebooks. Option A: 3 notebooks for $4.50. Option B: 5 notebooks for $7.00. Option C: 8 notebooks for $12.80. A student claims all three options are proportional to each other — and that Option B is the best deal. Do you agree? Show your reasoning using at least two representations."*

Partners must:
1. Determine whether the relationship across all options is truly proportional
2. Evaluate which option is the "best deal" and define what "best" means
3. Represent their findings in a table AND a graph

This task deliberately introduces a decision that requires trade-off thinking — is "best deal" the lowest unit price, the most notebooks, or the most convenient quantity?

**Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and ask: *"What does 'best deal' mean to you? Could it mean something different to someone else?"* Students at Level 2 will systematically compare unit rates, acknowledge that "best" depends on context (budget, need), and represent both findings clearly. Students at Level 1 will pick one option without exploring alternatives.

---

### GROUP SHARE & STRUCTURED DEBATE — 10 Minutes
#### *CRI5a: Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes*

**Activity:** Two pairs share their graphs and conclusions. After each share, ask the class: *"Did this group's representation make the proportional relationship easier or harder to see? What would you change about your own work after seeing theirs?"*

Require every student to write one sentence in their notebook: *"Before this activity I thought ___, but now I think ___ because ___."*

**Observation/Assessment:** Students at Level 2 will identify a specific mathematical misconception they held and explain what evidence changed their mind. Students at Level 1 will note a surface-level change ("I drew my graph bigger") without connecting it to mathematical reasoning.

---

### EXIT TICKET — 8 Minutes
#### *CRI2a: Assessing Validity of Information*

**Prompt:** *"A friend says y = 3x + 2 represents a proportional relationship because it has a constant number. Is your friend correct? Use two different representations to support your answer."*

Students must cross-check the equation against the graph and table, identifying that the "+2" breaks proportionality — the line does not pass through the origin.

**Observation/Assessment:** Students who rigorously verify their answer using both a graph AND a table (two representations) demonstrate Level 2 — they are cross-checking, not assuming.

---

### CLOSING — 5 Minutes

Revisit the anchor chart. Ask: *"What is the one mathematical 'test' you trust most to confirm proportionality, and why?"* Allow three student responses, reinforcing precise language.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a graphic organizer with the four-quadrant representation template pre-labeled
- **Extension:** Students write their own proportional vs. non-proportional scenario and challenge a partner to identify which is which

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

### Lesson Outline:

- Begin with a brief review: “What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”  
- Show a simple example: If 1 apple costs $2, then 3 apples cost $6. Ask students: “How can we show this relationship in different ways?”  
- Write on the board: Table, Graph, Equation, Diagram, Verbal description.

**Targeted Critical Thinking: Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
*Activity:* Students discuss in pairs what proportional means and share examples. Teacher clarifies misconceptions by asking guiding questions: “What does ‘proportional’ mean in your own words?”  
*Assessment:* Listen for students who can clearly explain proportionality and identify relevant information (e.g., constant ratio). Note if explanations are precise or vague.

---

**2. Guided Practice: Representing Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Present a proportional relationship: “A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour. How far does it travel in 2, 3, 4 hours?”  
- Fill out a table together: Hours vs. Miles.  
- Plot points on a graph and draw the line.  
- Write the equation: y = 60x.  
- Draw a diagram (e.g., arrows showing distance increasing with time).  
- Write a verbal description: “The distance traveled increases by 60 miles every hour.”  

*Targeted Critical Thinking: Organizing Information (CRI1b)*  
*Activity:* Students organize the same proportional relationship into all five representations on their own handouts.  
*Assessment:* Review student work for logical flow and completeness—do they arrange information coherently from table to graph to equation, etc.?

---

**3. Independent Practice: Identifying Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Provide students with mixed examples (some proportional, some not) in various forms (tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, verbal descriptions).  
- Students work individually or in pairs to:  
  a) Identify if the relationship is proportional.  
  b) Justify their answer using at least two different representations.  

*Targeted Critical Thinking: Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) and Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)*  
*Activity:* Students evaluate whether the given data truly represents proportional relationships, cross-checking between forms (e.g., does the graph match the table?).  
*Assessment:* Observe students’ explanations and justifications. Do they critically check for consistency? Do they recognize when information is misleading or incorrect?

---

**4. Group Discussion: Comparing Representations (10 minutes)**  
- Groups share their findings. Discuss:  
  - How do different representations help us understand proportional relationships?  
  - What are the advantages or limitations of each?  
  - Are there cases where one representation is clearer or more useful?  

*Targeted Critical Thinking: Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)*  
*Activity:* Facilitate a discussion where students compare pros and cons of tables vs. graphs vs. equations, etc.  
*Assessment:* Listen for students who systematically evaluate trade-offs and consider implications (e.g., “Graphs show trends visually, but tables give exact values”).

---

**5. Reasoning and Decision-Making Activity (5 minutes)**  
- Pose a real-world problem: “You want to buy tickets for a concert. Tickets cost $15 each. You have $60. How many tickets can you buy?”  
- Ask students to decide which representation (table, graph, equation, verbal description) best helps solve the problem and explain why.  

*Targeted Critical Thinking: Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)*  
*Activity:* Students justify their choice with clear reasoning, anticipating complexities (e.g., “The equation y=15x is quickest, but a table helps if you want to see multiple options”).  
*Assessment:* Evaluate clarity and sophistication of reasoning in student explanations.

---

**6. Reflection and Metacognition (5 minutes)**  
- Ask students to write a short reflection:  
  - “What strategy helped you understand proportional relationships best today?”  
  - “How did you decide which representation to use?”  
  - “What will you do differently next time you solve a proportional problem?”  

*Targeted Critical Thinking: Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)*  
*Activity:* Students articulate their thinking process and adjustments.  
*Assessment:* Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness and strategic thinking.

---

**7. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Quick problem: Given a proportional relationship in one form, students convert it into another form (e.g., from a verbal description to an equation).  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding.

## Lesson Outline

---

### WARM-UP / HOOK — 7 Minutes
#### *CRI1a: Clarifying Information*

**Activity:** Display two tables on the board:

| Flour (cups) | Eggs |
|---|---|
| 1 | 2 |
| 2 | 4 |
| 3 | 6 |

| Miles Driven | Gas Used (gal) |
|---|---|
| 10 | 2 |
| 20 | 5 |
| 30 | 9 |

Ask students: *"Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know? Write your reasoning in one precise sentence."* Allow one minute of pair discussion, then cold-call two or three students. Prompt them to replace vague language ("the numbers go up evenly") with precise mathematical language ("the ratio of eggs to flour is always 2:1"). Record refined definitions on an anchor chart.

**Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students who use exact ratio language versus imprecise descriptors. Students at Level 2 will state clearly: *"A proportional relationship has a constant ratio between the two quantities."* Students at Level 1 may say the numbers "go up the same way" without naming or calculating the ratio.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 10 Minutes
#### *CRI1b: Organizing Information | CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information*

**Activity:** Using the anchor chart, introduce the **five representations** of proportional relationships: tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Model one complete example — for every 1 cup of flour, a recipe needs 2 eggs — and organize all five representations simultaneously in a structured layout on the board:

- **Table:** constant ratio (k = y/x)
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = kx form
- **Diagram:** arrows or ratio boxes showing the multiplicative relationship
- **Verbal:** "for every x, there is a constant multiple y"

Explicitly guide students to record each representation in an organized four-to-five quadrant template on their handout. Pause and ask: *"If someone showed you a graph that was a straight line but didn't pass through the origin, would that be proportional? Why or why not?"* This pushes students to assess which features of information actually matter.

**Observation/Assessment:** Watch for students who note the origin detail unprompted. Students at Level 2 demonstrate they are selecting the most authoritative mathematical criteria, not just surface patterns. Check handouts for correctly structured tables (independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with accurate plotted points.

---

### COLLABORATIVE ACTIVITY: "PROPORTIONALITY PUZZLES" — 18 Minutes
#### *CRI2a: Assessing Validity | CRI2b: Assessing Quality | CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons | CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning*

**Activity:** Divide students into groups of 3–4. Provide each group with a handout containing three or four scenarios, each beginning with one representation. Students must: (1) determine if the relationship is proportional, (2) if proportional, represent it in two additional forms, and (3) if not proportional, explain why using mathematical evidence.

**Scenario highlights to include on the handout:**

- **Validity check (CRI2a):** One scenario presents a table alongside a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't pass through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio). Students identify the inconsistency and explain which representation is invalid. *Observation:* Listen for: "The points on the graph don't match the (x, y) pairs in the table," or "The graph should pass through the origin if it's truly proportional."

- **Quality/relevance filter (CRI2b):** One scenario includes a vague verbal description with irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He bakes on Tuesdays and his oven runs at 350°F."). Students identify only the relevant quantities. *Observation:* Ask groups to articulate why certain information was ignored: "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio."

- **Trade-off discussion (CRI3a):** After completing representations, ask groups: *"Which representation — table, graph, or equation — is most useful for this scenario, and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of each?"* *Observation:* Listen for comparisons such as: "The graph shows the trend visually, but the equation is faster for calculating large values without extending the table."

- **Justification (CRI4a):** For each scenario, groups must write a decision statement: *"This IS/IS NOT proportional because…"* supported by specific mathematical evidence (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x = 2 for every point," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin"). *Observation:* Evaluate whether justifications directly link evidence to the mathematical definition of proportionality.

---

### GROUP SHARE & STRUCTURED DISCUSSION — 8 Minutes
#### *CRI5a: Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes*

**Activity:** Two groups share their graphs and conclusions with the class. After each share, ask: *"Did this group's representation make the proportional relationship easier or harder to see? What would you change about your own work after seeing theirs?"*

Require every student to write one sentence in their notebook: *"Before this activity I thought ___, but now I think ___ because ___."*

**Observation/Assessment:** Students at Level 2 will identify a specific mathematical misconception they held and explain what evidence changed their mind (e.g., "I thought any straight line on a graph meant proportional, but now I know it must also pass through the origin"). Students at Level 1 will note a surface change ("I drew my graph bigger") without connecting it to mathematical reasoning.

---

### EXIT TICKET — 7 Minutes
#### *CRI2a: Assessing Validity of Information (revisited)*

**Prompt:** *"A friend says y = 3x + 2 represents a proportional relationship because it has a constant multiplier. Is your friend correct? Use two different representations to support your answer."*

Students must cross-check the equation against a graph and a table, identifying that the "+2" breaks proportionality — the line does not pass through the origin, and the ratio y/x is not constant.

**Observation/Assessment:** Students who verify their answer using both a graph AND a table demonstrate Level 2 — they are cross-checking across representations, not relying on a single piece of evidence. Students who only evaluate the equation without checking another form demonstrate Level 1.

---

### CLOSING — 5 Minutes

Revisit the anchor chart. Ask: *"What is the one mathematical 'test' you trust most to confirm proportionality, and why?"* Allow three student responses, reinforcing precise language about constant ratios, the origin, and the y = kx form.
