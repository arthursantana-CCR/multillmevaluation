## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Recipe Cards" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Structure

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two recipes on the board:
- Recipe A: 2 cups strawberries to 3 cups yogurt
- Recipe B: 4 cups strawberries to 6 cups yogurt
- Recipe C: 3 cups strawberries to 5 cups yogurt

**Prompt:** "Which recipes will taste the same? How do you know?"

Students discuss with elbow partners. Teacher records reasoning on the board, pressing students to **clarify** terms like "ratio" and "equivalent."

**→ Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students articulating *why* equivalent ratios produce the same taste. Assess by noting which students move from vague ("they're bigger") to precise ("both have a 2:3 ratio"). Level 2 = students restate peer ideas with added precision.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the concept using one running example: "A bakery sells 3 muffins for $5."

Build all five representations side-by-side on the board:
- **Table:** muffins (3, 6, 9, 12) / cost (5, 10, 15, 20)
- **Graph:** plotted points through origin
- **Equation:** y = (5/3)x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing units
- **Verbal:** "Cost is proportional to muffins; each muffin costs $5/3."

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule: graphs must be straight lines through the origin.

**→ Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Provide students a graphic organizer with five boxes to copy each representation. Walk around and check that students label k consistently across all formats. Level 2 = student's organizer shows arrows/connections between representations (e.g., k circled in table AND equation).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Is It Proportional?" Sort (10 minutes)
Distribute 6 cards, each showing a relationship in different forms (some proportional, some not). Examples:
- Table with constant ratio ✓
- Graph that doesn't pass through origin ✗
- Equation y = 2x + 1 ✗
- Verbal: "Maria runs 4 miles every 30 minutes" ✓

Students work in pairs to sort and **justify** each decision in writing.

**→ Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Require students to verify each "yes" with at least two representations (e.g., "The table shows constant ratio AND I can write y = 4x"). Assess by reviewing written justifications: Level 2 students cross-check using multiple methods rather than relying on a single visual cue.

**→ Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Ask students which evidence is *strongest* for proving proportionality. Listen for responses prioritizing the equation form (k clearly visible) or origin test on a graph. Level 2 = students rank evidence and explain why some tests are more reliable than others.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Phone Plan Decision (12 minutes)
Present three phone plans:
- Plan A: $0.10 per text (proportional)
- Plan B: $5 flat fee + $0.05 per text (not proportional)
- Plan C: $15/month unlimited (not proportional)

In groups of 3, students:
1. Build a table for each plan (0–200 texts)
2. Graph all three on the same axes
3. Recommend a plan for three different users (light, medium, heavy texter)

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must write a paragraph comparing plans for each user type. Assess by checking whether students consider trade-offs (e.g., "Plan A is proportional and fair for light users but costs more long-term for heavy texters"). Level 2 = students consider scenarios beyond the prompt (e.g., variable monthly usage).

**→ Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Each group presents one recommendation. Require evidence from table, graph, AND equation. Assess for logical chains: "Because Plan A passes through origin and has k = 0.10, a user sending under 50 texts pays less than the $5 base fee of Plan B." Level 2 = reasoning anticipates "what if" complications.

---

### 5. Exit Ticket & Reflection (8 minutes)

**Part A (Math):** Given the equation y = 7x, create a table, graph, and verbal description.

**Part B (Reflection prompt):**
> "What was the trickiest part of deciding if a relationship is proportional? Did your thinking change during the lesson? What would you do differently next time?"

**→ Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Read Part B responses. Look for metacognitive language: "At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but then I realized…" Level 2 = students name a specific strategy adjustment (e.g., "Now I always check the origin first"). Level 1 = surface mention ("It was hard"). Use these reflections to plan tomorrow's mini-reteach.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Quick whip-around: each student states one representation and one defining feature of proportional relationships. Preview tomorrow's lesson on solving real-world problems using the constant of proportionality.

---

## Assessment Summary
- **Formative:** Card sort justifications, group presentations, exit ticket Part A
- **Critical Thinking:** Graphic organizers (CRI1a/1b), card sort writing (CRI2a/2b), group paragraph (CRI3a/4a), exit reflection (CRI5a)
- **Differentiation:** Sentence stems for ELLs ("This is proportional because ___"); extension challenge: create a non-proportional relationship that "looks" proportional in a small data window.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Proportional Detectives – Uncovering Relationships in the Real World**

**Course:** 7th Grade Mathematics  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).  

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to extract data from real-world scenarios, represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations, and critically evaluate the quality and implications of their mathematical models to make informed decisions.

---

### **1. Warm-Up: Making Sense of the Mess (10 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The lesson begins with a deliberately confusing, wordy paragraph projected on the board. The text describes a local coffee shop’s pricing: *"Welcome to Joe's! We have cool vintage tables. If you want our house blend, it's going to cost you $3.00 for a 16-ounce cup. Oh, but if you just want a quick sip, an 8-ounce cup is $1.50. We also sell pastries! A massive 32-ounce thermos fill-up will run you $6.00."* Students must work independently to extract the mathematical relationship between ounces and cost.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must filter out the irrelevant details (vintage tables, pastries) and rewrite the confusing verbal description into a clear, precise mathematical statement that defines the unit rate (e.g., "The coffee costs $0.1875 per ounce").
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher circulates the room, observing students' notes. Assessment is based on whether students successfully ignore the distractors and clarify the core mathematical ideas precisely to support a deeper understanding of the relationship.

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Once the information is clarified, students take the scattered data points (16 oz, 8 oz, 32 oz) and arrange them into a logically structured input/output table (x = Ounces, y = Cost). 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews the students' tables to ensure the data is arranged in ascending order (8, 16, 32). This demonstrates the ability to arrange information to strengthen logical flow, making it immediately obvious that the ratio $y/x$ is constant.

### **2. Investigation: The Great Lemonade Debate (20 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students transition into small groups. They are tasked with modeling the cost of lemonade for an upcoming school dance. The teacher provides three different sources of pricing data: a random student's social media post claiming "lemonade is like 50 bucks for a hundred cups," an official wholesale club's pricing catalog, and a local grocery store's verified weekly ad. 

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before creating any graphs or equations, groups must evaluate the three sources and select the most authoritative data to build their mathematical model. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher listens to group deliberations. Students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by discarding the social media post as anecdotal and unreliable, choosing instead to rely on the official catalog and weekly ad for accurate mathematical modeling.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check the pricing from the wholesale catalog against the grocery store ad. They create graphs for both sources on the same coordinate plane to verify if the price-per-ounce remains strictly proportional across different vendors (checking if both lines are straight and pass through the origin).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher collects the graphs and group notes. Assessment focuses on whether students rigorously cross-checked the data using multiple authoritative sources to validate if the proportional relationship holds true universally or if it varies by store.

### **3. Application: Choosing the Best Representation (15 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Now that students have verified the proportional data and found the constant of proportionality ($k$), they must prepare a brief presentation for the "Dance Committee." They must choose only one way to present their data: a table, a graph, or an algebraic equation ($y = kx$).

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each mathematical representation. For example, a table is easy for the committee to read for specific cup amounts, a graph provides a powerful visual proof of the constant rate, and an equation is the most efficient tool for calculating massive, unforeseen quantities.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Students fill out a "Trade-off Matrix" on mini-whiteboards. The teacher assesses their ability to systematically evaluate trade-offs, looking for groups that consider comprehensive long-term implications (e.g., "If ticket sales double at the last minute, the equation is the only tool that lets us adapt instantly").

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups make a final purchasing recommendation to the committee based on their chosen mathematical model. They must anticipate real-world complexities that disrupt perfect proportionality.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher evaluates the groups' final verbal proposals. High-level reasoning is observed when students anticipate complexities—for instance, noting that while their equation $y = 0.20x$ works perfectly in theory, the store only sells lemonade in 100-ounce jugs, meaning the committee must round up their purchase and the final cost won't be perfectly proportional to the exact ounces consumed.

### **4. Closure: Metacognitive Wrap-Up (10 minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students return to their desks to independently complete an exit ticket. They are asked to look back at the entire process, from the confusing coffee shop paragraph to the final dance committee proposal.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students answer the following prompt: *"Think about how you initially viewed the jumbled coffee shop pricing versus how you view the lemonade data now. How did organizing the data into tables, graphs, and equations change your thinking process? What strategies will you use next time you encounter confusing numerical claims?"*
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reads the exit tickets to identify deep metacognitive awareness. A successful response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking—moving from feeling overwhelmed by verbal descriptions to intentionally relying on structured mathematical representations (like finding the unit rate) to confidently prove proportionality.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain how the constant of proportionality appears in different representations.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student handout with table, graph grid, equation space, and reflection prompts  
- Rulers or graph paper  
- Calculators optional  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Plan

### 0–5 Minutes: Warm-Up — “Is It Proportional?”
Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, and 180 miles in 3 hours.  
**Situation B:** A taxi charges $4 to start plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: “Which situation shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

Students write a quick response independently, then share with a partner.

Teacher highlights: A proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`. Its graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify the quantities, units, and what is being compared before deciding if the relationship is proportional.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for students clarifying variables and units. Evidence might include student statements such as, “The two quantities are miles and hours, and the ratio is 60 miles per 1 hour,” or “The taxi problem has a starting fee, so when miles are 0, the cost is $4.”

---

### 5–15 Minutes: Direct Instruction — What Makes a Relationship Proportional?
Teacher introduces key features:

A relationship is proportional if:
- The ratios between quantities are equivalent.
- The equation can be written as `y = kx`.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- The constant of proportionality, `k`, represents the unit rate.

Use the example:

“A store sells notebooks for $3 each.”

Create representations together:

| Notebooks, x | Cost, y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 6 |
| 3 | 9 |
| 4 | 12 |

Equation: `y = 3x`  
Graph: points `(1,3), (2,6), (3,9), (4,12)` on a line through `(0,0)`  
Verbal description: “Each notebook costs $3.”  
Diagram: draw groups of $3 for each notebook.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students see the same relationship organized in five forms: table, graph, equation, diagram, and words.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether students place information in the correct representation and connect them logically. Evidence might include student work such as, “Since each notebook adds $3, the table increases by 3 each row, and the equation is `y = 3x`.”

---

### 15–25 Minutes: Guided Practice — Multiple Representations
Give students this situation:

“A cyclist rides 12 miles every hour.”

Together, students complete:
1. Table for 0, 1, 2, 3, and 4 hours  
2. Equation  
3. Graph  
4. Verbal description  
5. Diagram or number line

Ask guiding questions:
- What is the constant of proportionality?
- How does it appear in the table?
- How does it appear in the equation?
- How can the graph help prove it is proportional?

Expected equation: `y = 12x`, where `x` is hours and `y` is miles.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and verbal description all represent the same relationship.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask students to verify one representation using another. Evidence might include, “The graph point `(3,36)` matches the table because 3 hours times 12 miles per hour equals 36 miles,” or “The line goes through the origin, so it matches `y = 12x`.”

---

### 25–38 Minutes: Partner Task — Which Representation Is Best?
Partners receive a scenario:

“A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 5 cups of sugar.”

They must create a table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description. Then they answer:

“Which representation makes the proportional relationship easiest to understand? Explain why.”

Students may choose different answers, but they must justify their choice.

Possible equation: `y = 2.5x` if `x` is cups of flour and `y` is cups of sugar, or `f = 2/5s` depending on variables. Teacher emphasizes defining variables clearly.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students judge which representation gives the clearest, most useful information for the situation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for reasoning about labels, scale, accuracy, and usefulness. Evidence might include, “The table is best because it shows several equivalent ratios clearly,” or “The graph is useful only if the axes are labeled and scaled correctly.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare the strengths and weaknesses of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for trade-off language. Evidence might include, “The equation is fastest for calculating large amounts, but the diagram is easier for seeing the ratio at first.”

---

### 38–48 Minutes: Independent Practice — Proportional or Not?
Students complete three problems independently.

1. A movie streaming service costs $8 per month.  
2. A gym charges a $20 joining fee plus $10 per month.  
3. A plant grows 3 inches every 2 weeks.

For each, students:
- Decide whether the relationship is proportional.
- Explain their reasoning.
- Create at least two representations.

Teacher circulates and asks: “What is your evidence?” “Does the graph go through the origin?” “Can the equation be written as `y = kx`?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students make and justify decisions about proportionality using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/assess:** Review explanations for logical reasoning. Evidence might include, “The gym membership is not proportional because when months are 0, the cost is still $20, so the graph would not pass through the origin.”

---

### 48–55 Minutes: Exit Ticket and Reflection
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell from a table if a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “How can you tell from a graph?”  
3. “Describe one mistake you almost made today or one strategy that helped you.”

Collect exit tickets.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on their reasoning process and identify how they checked or adjusted their thinking.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive comments. Evidence might include, “At first I thought the gym example was proportional because it increased by 10 each month, but then I checked month 0 and noticed the $20 fee.”

---

## Assessment
Students demonstrate understanding if they can:
- Identify the constant of proportionality.
- Represent the same relationship in multiple ways.
- Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional using evidence from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, or words.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, and identify the constant of proportionality across representations.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Recipe Cards" handout, card sort packet, exit tickets, whiteboard, student handout with five-representation organizer.

---

## Lesson Structure

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display three smoothie recipes:
- Recipe A: 2 cups strawberries to 3 cups yogurt
- Recipe B: 4 cups strawberries to 6 cups yogurt
- Recipe C: 3 cups strawberries to 5 cups yogurt

**Prompt:** "Which recipes will taste the same? How do you know?"

Students discuss with elbow partners. Teacher records reasoning on the board, pressing students to clarify terms like "ratio" and "equivalent."

**→ Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students articulating *why* equivalent ratios produce the same taste. Assess by noting which students move from vague ("they're bigger") to precise ("both have a 2:3 ratio"). Level 2 = students restate peer ideas with added precision, naming units and what is being compared.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes)
Introduce the concept using one running example: "A store sells notebooks for $3 each."

Build all five representations side-by-side on the board:
- **Table:** notebooks (1, 2, 3, 4) / cost (3, 6, 9, 12)
- **Graph:** plotted points on a line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram showing groups of $3 per notebook
- **Verbal:** "Each notebook costs $3; cost is proportional to number of notebooks."

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule: a relationship is proportional if and only if the equation has the form y = kx AND the graph is a straight line through the origin.

**→ Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Provide students a graphic organizer with five boxes to copy each representation. Walk around and check that students label k consistently across all formats. Level 2 = the organizer shows arrows/connections between representations (e.g., k circled in table AND equation), demonstrating logical flow.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Is It Proportional?" Sort with Source Evaluation (10 minutes)
Distribute 6 cards, each showing a relationship in different forms (some proportional, some not). Examples:
- Table with constant ratio ✓
- Graph that doesn't pass through origin ✗
- Equation y = 2x + 1 ✗
- Verbal: "Maria runs 4 miles every 30 minutes" ✓
- Gym membership: $20 join fee + $10/month ✗
- "A cyclist rides 12 miles every hour" ✓

Students work in pairs to sort and **justify** each decision in writing. For each "yes," they must verify using at least two representations (e.g., "The table shows a constant ratio AND I can write y = 4x").

Additionally, present students with a quick scenario: three "sources" claiming the price of lemonade — a social media post ("lemonade is like 50 bucks for 100 cups"), a wholesale catalog, and a grocery store weekly ad. Ask which sources they would trust to build a proportional model and why.

**→ Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Assess by reviewing written justifications: Level 2 students cross-check using multiple representations rather than relying on a single visual cue, and they cross-check pricing across authoritative sources before modeling.

**→ Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Ask which evidence is *strongest* for proving proportionality. Listen for responses prioritizing the equation form (k clearly visible) or the origin test on the graph, and for students discarding the social media post as anecdotal. Level 2 = students rank evidence and explain why some tests/sources are more reliable than others.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Phone Plan Decision (13 minutes)
Present three phone plans:
- Plan A: $0.10 per text (proportional)
- Plan B: $5 flat fee + $0.05 per text (not proportional)
- Plan C: $15/month unlimited (not proportional)

In groups of 3, students:
1. Build a table for each plan (0–200 texts)
2. Graph all three on the same axes
3. Recommend a plan for three different users (light, medium, heavy texter)

Groups complete a "Trade-off Matrix" comparing tables, graphs, and equations as decision tools, then write a short paragraph defending their recommendation.

**→ Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Assess whether students consider trade-offs (e.g., "Plan A is fair for light users but expensive long-term for heavy texters"; "the equation is fastest for large numbers, but the graph instantly shows which plan is cheapest at any point"). Level 2 = students consider scenarios beyond the prompt (e.g., variable monthly usage, last-minute changes).

**→ Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Each group presents one recommendation citing evidence from table, graph, AND equation. Assess for logical chains: "Because Plan A passes through the origin and has k = 0.10, a user sending fewer than 50 texts pays less than Plan B's $5 base fee." Level 2 = reasoning anticipates real-world complications (e.g., usage spikes, rounding to whole texts).

---

### 5. Exit Ticket & Reflection (10 minutes)

**Part A (Math):** Given the equation y = 7x, create a table, graph, and verbal description. Then identify which of these is NOT proportional and explain why: (a) y = 5x, (b) y = 2x + 3, (c) a line through (0,0) and (4,8).

**Part B (Reflection prompt):**
> "What was the trickiest part of deciding if a relationship is proportional? Did your thinking change during the lesson? Describe one mistake you almost made or one strategy that helped you, and what you would do differently next time."

**→ Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Read Part B responses. Look for metacognitive language: "At first I thought the gym example was proportional because it increased by $10 each month, but then I checked month 0 and noticed the $20 fee." Level 2 = students name a specific strategy adjustment (e.g., "Now I always check whether the line passes through the origin first"). Level 1 = surface mention ("It was hard"). Use these reflections to plan tomorrow's mini-reteach.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Quick whip-around: each student states one representation and one defining feature of proportional relationships (constant ratio, y = kx, line through origin). Preview tomorrow's lesson on solving real-world problems using the constant of proportionality.
