## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two recipes on the board:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 smoothies; 4 cups → 6 smoothies; 8 cups → 12 smoothies
- **Recipe B:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 smoothies; 4 cups → 7 smoothies; 6 cups → 10 smoothies

Ask: "Which recipe is 'fair' — meaning every smoothie has the same amount of strawberries?" Students discuss with a partner.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "fair" means mathematically (constant ratio). Observe by listening for students who say things like "the ratio has to stay the same" versus vague answers. Assess via cold-call responses.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Defining Proportional Relationships (10 minutes)
Define a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x is constant (the constant of proportionality, k). Demonstrate the five representations:
- **Table:** Show Recipe A; calculate y/x for each row → always 1.5
- **Equation:** y = 1.5x
- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 2:3 ratio scaled up
- **Verbal:** "For every 2 cups of strawberries, you make 3 smoothies."

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Have students copy a four-column organizer (Table | Equation | Graph | Verbal) into their notebooks. Assess by reviewing notebooks for completeness and logical arrangement during circulation.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Is It Proportional?" (12 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Shop handout with **four scenarios** (two proportional, two not). For each, students must:
1. Test if the ratio is constant
2. Identify the constant of proportionality (if applicable)
3. Sketch a graph
4. Justify their answer in writing

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students cross-check their answers using *multiple* representations — does the table confirm what the graph shows? Does a line through the origin match a constant k? Assess by asking, "How do you *know* it's proportional? Show me two ways." Look for students using more than one representation to verify.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some scenarios include "distractor" data (e.g., an outlier point). Students must judge which data points are reliable indicators. Assess by reviewing written justifications for whether students identified strong evidence versus misleading details.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Plan (15 minutes)
Present a real-world problem: "A phone company offers two data plans:
- **Plan X:** $15 flat fee + $5 per GB
- **Plan Y:** $7 per GB (no flat fee)

Which plan is proportional? Which is the better deal? Justify using a table, graph, and equation."

Students work in pairs to analyze both plans and recommend one based on usage levels.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must systematically compare both plans, considering low-usage vs. high-usage customers. Assess by collecting written recommendations — look for trade-off analysis (e.g., "Plan Y is better under 7 GB, but Plan X is cheaper for heavy users").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must defend their reasoning with mathematical evidence (the equations, intersection point on the graph). Assess written explanations for logical chains: "Because Plan Y's line passes through the origin and has slope 7, it's proportional. At 7 GB, both cost $50, so above that, Plan X saves money." Watch for fallacies like assuming a flat fee makes a plan "proportional."

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Distribute exit tickets with three prompts:
1. Give an example of a proportional relationship from your life.
2. What was one mistake you almost made today (or did make), and how did you catch it?
3. Which representation (table, graph, equation, verbal) helps YOU most, and why?

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Question 2 directly demands metacognition. Assess for depth — a "2" response describes a specific strategy shift ("I assumed it was proportional because the numbers went up evenly, but I forgot to check if it went through the origin — now I always check that first"). A "1" response only mentions an error without strategy adjustment.

---

### 6. Closing (3 minutes)
Review key takeaway: A proportional relationship has a **constant ratio** AND **passes through the origin**. Preview tomorrow's lesson on solving real-world problems using the constant of proportionality.

---

## Assessment Summary
- **Formative:** Smoothie Shop handout, partner discussions, exit ticket
- **Critical Thinking Evidence Collection:** Teacher uses a clipboard checklist during pair work, noting student quotes that demonstrate each subcompetency level (-1 to 2) based on observed reasoning, written justifications, and reflection responses.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables; sentence stems for justifications
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportionality in Action: Connecting Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade 
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will identify, represent, and compare proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate mathematical information and make data-driven decisions.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Information Processing (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: The Messy Recipe**
The teacher presents a verbal description of a local bakery’s cookie recipe, but the information is presented as a disorganized paragraph with extra, irrelevant details (e.g., the baker's favorite color, scattered ingredient amounts for different batch sizes). Students must extract the relevant mathematical data regarding the ratio of cups of flour to batches of cookies.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students read the complex verbal description, filter out irrelevant details, and precisely define the two quantities being compared (flour and batches) to clarify the unit rate. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher circulates to observe students highlighting relevant text and writing down a clear, precise ratio (e.g., "3 cups of flour per 2 batches"). Mastery is assessed by their ability to state the unit rate without confusion.

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Once the ratio is clarified, students must arrange the scattered batch data into a logically structured, ascending input/output (x/y) table, and then translate that table into a coordinate graph.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews the students' tables and graphs. Students demonstrate this skill by organizing the $x$-values (batches) in sequential order and plotting the points to reveal a straight line through the origin, strengthening the logical flow of the data.

---

### **Part 2: Collaborative Investigation and Cross-Checking (20 Minutes)**

**Activity: The Catering Dilemma**
Students are placed in small groups and given a scenario: The school is hiring a catering company for a dance. 
- **Company A** provides a pricing graph.
- **Company B** provides a pricing table.
- **Company C** provides a promotional flyer with a verbal claim: "We charge $15 per student, the best proportional rate in town!"

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students cannot simply trust Company C’s promotional flyer. They must rigorously cross-check Company C's verbal claim by generating their own table and equation ($y = 15x$) and comparing it against the mathematical models (sources) provided by Companies A and B to see if the claim holds true.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher listens to group dialogue and checks their scratchpad work. Assessment is based on whether students actively verify the flyer's claim using multiple mathematical representations rather than accepting the advertisement at face value.

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** To find the constant of proportionality ($k$) for Company A’s graph, students must select a data point to calculate $y/x$. The graph has some points that fall exactly on grid intersections and others that float between lines. Students must demonstrate judgment by selecting the highest-quality, most precise data points (exact integer coordinates) to find the equation.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher asks groups, "Which point did you use to find your equation and why?" Mastery is observed when students articulate that they selected clear, unambiguous coordinate pairs because estimating fractions on a graph leads to inaccurate equations.

---

### **Part 3: Decision Making and Trade-Offs (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: The Final Recommendation**
Groups must write a brief proposal to the principal recommending which catering company to hire for 250 students, using equations to justify their choice. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Before calculating the final cost, groups discuss the pros and cons of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost for 250 students. They must evaluate the trade-offs of each representation.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses this through a quick class share-out. Students should systematically evaluate the options (e.g., "A graph is great for a quick visual comparison of prices, but extending a graph to 250 students is impractical. The equation $y = kx$ is the most efficient choice for large, long-term calculations").

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** Students finalize their recommendation. They must use their proportional equations to calculate the exact costs and write a justification that anticipates complexities (e.g., recognizing that while one company has a cheaper unit rate, another might be better if the number of students drops).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher collects the written proposals. Assessment focuses on the sophistication of their mathematical reasoning. A high-level response will use the calculated constant of proportionality to logically justify the financial decision without relying on unsupported claims.

---

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: Metacognitive Exit Ticket**
Students independently complete an exit ticket that asks them to solve one final proportional equation and answer a reflection question about their learning process during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity/Moment:** The exit ticket asks: *"At the beginning of class, how did you try to find the best deal, and how did your strategy change after learning to use the equation $y = kx$? What errors did you catch in your own thinking today?"*
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reads the exit tickets after class. Mastery is demonstrated when a student shows deep metacognitive awareness—for example, writing, "Initially, I just looked at the graph and guessed which line looked cheaper. I realized that was inaccurate, so I adjusted my thinking to always find the unit rate ($k$) first so I could rely on exact math instead of guessing."

## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Materials
- Whiteboard/projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Exit tickets  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and word problems  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 26 |
| 4 | 34 |

Ask students:  
1. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. How do you know?

Students first answer independently, then share with a partner.

**Key idea:** A proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify what information matters: the x-values, y-values, and ratios.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for students clarifying precisely, such as, “In Table A, the ratio of money to hours is always 8 dollars per hour.” Collect notes for evidence of whether they identify relevant quantities clearly.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a relationship is proportional when:

1. The ratios between corresponding quantities are equivalent.  
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin, `(0,0)`.  
3. The equation can be written in the form `y = kx`, where `k` is the constant of proportionality.  
4. In a diagram or verbal description, one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other quantity.

Model using Table A:

| Hours, x | Money, y | y ÷ x |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 8 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 | 8 |
| 3 | 24 | 8 |
| 4 | 32 | 8 |

Since `y ÷ x = 8` every time, the constant of proportionality is 8. The equation is:

`y = 8x`

Then quickly graph the points `(1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)` and show that the line passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students organize the same relationship in a table, equation, graph, and sentence.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether students arrange information logically, for example moving from table → ratio → equation → graph → conclusion.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this scenario:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours, 18 miles in 3 hours, and 30 miles in 5 hours.”

Students complete:

1. Table of values  
2. Ratios: miles ÷ hours  
3. Equation  
4. Graph  
5. Verbal description

Guide the class:

| Hours, x | Miles, y | y ÷ x |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | 12 | 6 |
| 3 | 18 | 6 |
| 5 | 30 | 6 |

The constant of proportionality is 6, so the equation is:

`y = 6x`

Students write: “The cyclist travels 6 miles per hour.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students verify whether the relationship is proportional by checking the table, equation, and graph.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students cross-checking representations, such as saying, “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph would pass through the origin, so it is proportional.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which information best proves proportionality: one ordered pair, all ratios, the graph, or the equation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask: “Is checking only one point enough? Why or why not?” Strong responses should explain that multiple points or ratios give better evidence than one point alone.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Choose the Best Representation — 13 minutes

Give each pair three problem cards.

**Card 1:**  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for 2 batches and 6 cups for 4 batches.

**Card 2:**  
A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.

**Card 3:**  
A student reads 15 pages in 10 minutes, 30 pages in 20 minutes, and 45 pages in 30 minutes.

For each card, partners decide:

1. Is the relationship proportional?  
2. Which representation best shows your reasoning: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal explanation?  
3. Why did you choose that representation?

Expected conclusions:
- Card 1 is proportional: `y = 1.5x`, where y is cups of flour and x is batches.  
- Card 2 is not proportional because it has a starting fee; the graph would not pass through the origin.  
- Card 3 is proportional: `y = 1.5x`, where y is pages and x is minutes.

**Critical Thinking: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare representations and choose the clearest one for each situation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for trade-off language: “A graph is useful because it shows the origin, but a table makes the constant rate easier to see.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students justify whether each relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for reasoning based on constant ratios, origin, or equation form, not guesses. Example evidence: “The taxi situation is not proportional because the equation would be `y = 2x + 4`, not `y = kx`.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Bring the class together. Ask selected pairs to share one card and their reasoning.

Discussion questions:
- What is the fastest way to tell if a table is proportional?  
- What does the origin tell us on a graph?  
- Why is `y = kx` proportional but `y = mx + b` with `b ≠ 0` not proportional?  
- Can two different situations have the same constant of proportionality?

Emphasize that proportional relationships can look different depending on representation, but they always show the same constant relationship between quantities.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 7 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 9 |
| 4 | 18 |
| 6 | 27 |
| 8 | 36 |

Is it proportional? Explain using at least two representations.

2. Write one sentence beginning with:  
“Today I changed or confirmed my thinking when…”

3. Create an equation for the relationship if it is proportional.

Expected answer: The relationship is proportional because `y ÷ x = 4.5` each time. Equation: `y = 4.5x`.

**Critical Thinking: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on how their thinking changed or how they verified their answer.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive statements such as, “At first I only looked at the numbers going up, but then I checked the ratios and realized that was stronger evidence.”

---

## Assessment

Students demonstrate mastery if they can:
- Identify proportional and non-proportional relationships.
- Find the constant of proportionality.
- Represent a proportional relationship with a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
- Justify their answer using clear mathematical reasoning.

For critical thinking, assess student written and spoken responses using evidence from their work, such as explanations, ratio checks, representation choices, and reflection statements.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 26 |
| 4 | 34 |

Ask students:  
1. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. How do you know?

Students first answer independently, then share with a partner.

**Key idea:** A proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify what information matters: the x-values, y-values, and ratios.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for students clarifying precisely, such as, “In Table A, the ratio of money to hours is always 8 dollars per hour.” Collect notes for evidence of whether they identify relevant quantities clearly.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a relationship is proportional when:

1. The ratios between corresponding quantities are equivalent.  
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin, `(0,0)`.  
3. The equation can be written in the form `y = kx`, where `k` is the constant of proportionality.  
4. In a diagram or verbal description, one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other quantity.

Model using Table A:

| Hours, x | Money, y | y ÷ x |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 8 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 | 8 |
| 3 | 24 | 8 |
| 4 | 32 | 8 |

Since `y ÷ x = 8` every time, the constant of proportionality is 8. The equation is:

`y = 8x`

Then quickly graph the points `(1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)` and show that the line passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students organize the same relationship in a table, equation, graph, and sentence.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether students arrange information logically, for example moving from table → ratio → equation → graph → conclusion.

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this scenario:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours, 18 miles in 3 hours, and 30 miles in 5 hours.”

Students complete:

1. Table of values  
2. Ratios: miles ÷ hours  
3. Equation  
4. Graph  
5. Verbal description

Guide the class:

| Hours, x | Miles, y | y ÷ x |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | 12 | 6 |
| 3 | 18 | 6 |
| 5 | 30 | 6 |

The constant of proportionality is 6, so the equation is:

`y = 6x`

Students write: “The cyclist travels 6 miles per hour.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students verify whether the relationship is proportional by checking the table, equation, and graph.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students cross-checking representations, such as saying, “The table has a constant ratio, and the graph would pass through the origin, so it is proportional.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which information best proves proportionality: one ordered pair, all ratios, the graph, or the equation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask: “Is checking only one point enough? Why or why not?” Strong responses should explain that multiple points or ratios give better evidence than one point alone.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Choose the Best Representation — 13 minutes

Give each pair three problem cards.

**Card 1:**  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for 2 batches and 6 cups for 4 batches.

**Card 2:**  
A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.

**Card 3:**  
A student reads 15 pages in 10 minutes, 30 pages in 20 minutes, and 45 pages in 30 minutes.

For each card, partners decide:

1. Is the relationship proportional?  
2. Which representation best shows your reasoning: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal explanation?  
3. Why did you choose that representation?

Expected conclusions:
- Card 1 is proportional: `y = 1.5x`, where y is cups of flour and x is batches.  
- Card 2 is not proportional because it has a starting fee; the graph would not pass through the origin.  
- Card 3 is proportional: `y = 1.5x`, where y is pages and x is minutes.

**Critical Thinking: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare representations and choose the clearest one for each situation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for trade-off language: “A graph is useful because it shows the origin, but a table makes the constant rate easier to see.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students justify whether each relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for reasoning based on constant ratios, origin, or equation form, not guesses. Example evidence: “The taxi situation is not proportional because the equation would be `y = 2x + 4`, not `y = kx`.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Bring the class together. Ask selected pairs to share one card and their reasoning.

Discussion questions:
- What is the fastest way to tell if a table is proportional?  
- What does the origin tell us on a graph?  
- Why is `y = kx` proportional but `y = mx + b` with `b ≠ 0` not proportional?  
- Can two different situations have the same constant of proportionality?

Emphasize that proportional relationships can look different depending on representation, but they always show the same constant relationship between quantities.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 7 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 9 |
| 4 | 18 |
| 6 | 27 |
| 8 | 36 |

Is it proportional? Explain using at least two representations.

2. Write one sentence beginning with:  
“Today I changed or confirmed my thinking when…”

3. Create an equation for the relationship if it is proportional.

Expected answer: The relationship is proportional because `y ÷ x = 4.5` each time. Equation: `y = 4.5x`.

**Critical Thinking: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on how their thinking changed or how they verified their answer.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive statements such as, “At first I only looked at the numbers going up, but then I checked the ratios and realized that was stronger evidence.”

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two recipes as a slightly messy paragraph containing extra details (the baker's favorite color, the shop's hours):
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 smoothies; 4 → 6; 8 → 12
- **Recipe B:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 smoothies; 4 → 7; 6 → 10

Ask: "Which recipe is 'fair' — meaning every smoothie has the same amount of strawberries?" Students filter irrelevant details and discuss with a partner.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "fair" means mathematically (constant ratio) and extract only the relevant quantities. Observe by listening for precise statements like "the ratio y/x has to stay the same" versus vague answers. Assess via cold-call responses and highlighted handouts.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Defining Proportional Relationships (10 minutes)
Define a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x is constant (the constant of proportionality, k). State the three tests:
1. Ratios between corresponding quantities are equivalent.
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin (0,0).
3. The equation has the form y = kx.

Demonstrate the five representations using Recipe A:
- **Table:** y/x = 1.5 for every row
- **Equation:** y = 1.5x
- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 2:3 ratio scaled up
- **Verbal:** "For every 2 cups of strawberries, you make 3 smoothies."

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a four-column organizer (Table | Equation | Graph | Verbal) into notebooks, moving logically from table → ratio → equation → graph. Assess by reviewing notebooks for completeness and logical flow during circulation.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Is It Proportional?" (12 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Shop handout with **four scenarios** (two proportional, two not). For each, students must:
1. Test if the ratio is constant
2. Identify the constant of proportionality (if applicable)
3. Sketch a graph
4. Justify their answer in writing

One scenario provides a graph where some points fall exactly on grid intersections and others float between lines. Students must choose which points to use when computing k.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students cross-check answers using *multiple* representations — does the table confirm what the graph shows? Does a line through the origin match a constant k? Assess by asking, "How do you *know* it's proportional? Show me two ways." Look for students using more than one representation to verify.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students must judge which data points are high-quality evidence (exact integer coordinates) versus low-quality (estimated/floating points or single ordered pairs). Assess by asking groups, "Which point did you use and why?" Mastery: students articulate that ambiguous coordinates yield inaccurate equations.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Plan (15 minutes)
Present: "A phone company offers two data plans:
- **Plan X:** $15 flat fee + $5 per GB
- **Plan Y:** $7 per GB (no flat fee)

Which plan is proportional? Which is the better deal? Justify using a table, graph, and equation."

Pairs analyze both plans and recommend one based on usage levels.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students systematically compare both plans across low-usage vs. high-usage scenarios. Assess written recommendations for trade-off analysis (e.g., "Plan Y is cheaper under 7 GB, but Plan X wins for heavy users").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students defend reasoning with mathematical evidence (equations, intersection point on graph). Assess for logical chains: "Plan Y's line passes through the origin with slope 7, so it's proportional. At 7 GB, both cost $50; above that, Plan X saves money." Watch for fallacies like assuming a flat fee makes a plan proportional.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Distribute exit tickets:
1. Given the table (2,9), (4,18), (6,27), (8,36) — is it proportional? Write the equation if so.
2. What was one mistake you almost made today, and how did you catch it?
3. Which representation helps YOU most, and why?

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Question 2 demands metacognition. A "2" response describes a specific strategy shift ("I assumed it was proportional because numbers went up evenly, but I forgot to check the origin — now I always check that first"). A "1" response only names an error without strategy adjustment.

---

### 6. Closing (3 minutes)
Reinforce: A proportional relationship has a **constant ratio** AND **passes through the origin**. Preview tomorrow's work on applying k to real-world problems.
