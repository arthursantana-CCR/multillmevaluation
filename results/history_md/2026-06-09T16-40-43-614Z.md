## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (Grade 7)

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, verbal descriptions).
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Materials:** Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios, sticky notes, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional, and how do you know?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner for 2 minutes, then share. Record student reasoning on the board without confirming correctness yet.

> **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate what "proportional" means and identify the key feature (constant ratio, passes through origin). 
> **Assessment:** Listen for clarity in partner talk; collect at least 3 student definitions on the board and ask the class to refine them into one precise statement.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the smoothie scenario across five representations:
1. **Verbal:** "$3 per smoothie"
2. **Table:** (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9)…
3. **Equation:** y = 3x
4. **Graph:** straight line through origin
5. **Diagram:** tape diagram showing repeated units of 3

Explicitly highlight the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule: *proportional graphs pass through (0,0)*.

> **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to arrange the same relationship across five formats in a structured graphic organizer. 
> **Assessment:** Students complete a parallel organizer for a new scenario ("$4 per taco"). Check for logical alignment across columns.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sort & Justify (10 minutes)
Hand out 6 cards showing mixed scenarios (tables, graphs, equations). In pairs, students sort them into **Proportional** vs. **Not Proportional** piles and write one sentence justifying each.

Examples include:
- Table: (2,5), (4,10), (6,15) → Proportional, k = 2.5
- Graph: line with y-intercept of 2 → Not proportional
- Equation: y = 7x → Proportional

> **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students must check evidence (does the ratio stay constant? does it pass through origin?) against multiple representations to validate their claim. 
> **Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "What evidence proves this is proportional? Could you confirm using a second representation?" Look for students cross-checking table values AND testing the equation form.

> **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some cards contain near-misses (e.g., y = 2x + 0.1). Students must judge whether information is sufficient and high-quality enough to decide. 
> **Assessment:** Exit conversation — did students dismiss superficial features (straight line ≠ always proportional) and rely on rigorous criteria?

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Choose the Best Deal (12 minutes)
Present three phone plans:
- **Plan A:** $0.10 per minute
- **Plan B:** $5 flat fee + $0.05 per minute
- **Plan C:** $15 unlimited

Students work in small groups to:
1. Identify which plan(s) represent a proportional relationship.
2. Recommend the best plan for a light user (30 min/month), medium user (100 min), and heavy user (500 min).
3. Graph all three plans on the same axes.

> **Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students systematically compare three options across multiple usage levels, considering trade-offs (cost certainty vs. low usage savings). 
> **Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Look for explicit trade-off language: "Plan A is cheaper for low use, but Plan C is better long-term if usage exceeds 150 minutes."

> **Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must justify recommendations using equations, graph intersections, and proportional reasoning. 
> **Assessment:** Check that group recommendations cite the **break-even point** mathematically, not just "it feels cheaper." Look for sound logic linking k-values to long-term cost.

---

### 5. Metacognitive Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Distribute exit tickets with three prompts:
1. Solve: *Is y = 4x + 1 proportional? Justify with two representations.*
2. **Reflection prompt:** *What did you initially think made a relationship proportional? How did your thinking change today? What strategy will you use next time you see a new scenario?*
3. Rate your confidence (1–4) and explain why.

> **Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking):** The reflection prompt forces students to articulate prior assumptions, identify shifts in understanding, and name a strategy adjustment. 
> **Assessment:** Score reflections on whether students (a) name a specific prior misconception (e.g., "I thought any straight line was proportional"), (b) describe how their thinking changed, and (c) commit to a future strategy. Strong responses demonstrate metacognitive awareness; weak responses simply restate the lesson.

---

### Closing (1 minute)
Preview tomorrow's lesson: identifying the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts (unit rates, recipes, currency).

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed organizers and sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___").
- **Challenge:** Ask students to construct a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Students find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship in their home (recipes, prices, distances) and represent each in three of the five forms.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Lesson Objective  
Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Students will justify their reasoning using the constant of proportionality.

### Materials  
Whiteboard/projector, graph paper, rulers, calculators if appropriate, student handout, exit ticket.

### Key Vocabulary  
Proportional relationship, ratio, rate, constant of proportionality, origin, equation, graph, table.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes  
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 8 | 16 | 24 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 10 | 18 | 26 | 34 |

Students answer independently:  
1. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. How do you know?  
3. What equation could represent the proportional table?

Invite students to share. Guide them to notice that Table A has a constant ratio, or unit rate, of 8 dollars per hour. Table B increases by the same amount, but it is not proportional because the ratio dollars/hours is not constant.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify what information matters: hours, dollars, ratios, and whether the ratios stay constant.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for students saying things like, “I compared dollars divided by hours each time,” rather than only saying, “It goes up by 8.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes  
Explain that a proportional relationship has these features:

1. **Table:** The ratio between the two quantities is constant.  
2. **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin, or point \((0,0)\).  
3. **Equation:** It can be written as \(y = kx\), where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.  
4. **Verbal description:** One quantity is always a constant multiple of the other.  
5. **Diagram:** Equal groups or scaled quantities show the same ratio.

Use Table A to model all representations:

- Verbal: A worker earns $8 per hour.  
- Equation: \(y = 8x\)  
- Graph: Plot \((1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)\); line goes through \((0,0)\).  
- Diagram: One hour matches $8, two hours match $16, etc.

Emphasize: “A pattern that adds the same amount is not always proportional. We must check the ratio and the origin.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students record the five representations in an organized chart.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether students arrange information logically, such as matching table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description for the same situation.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes  
Give students this situation:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

As a class, complete:

1. Table:

| Sugar cups, \(x\) | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Flour cups, \(y\) | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

2. Equation: \(y = \frac{3}{2}x\)  
3. Graph: Plot points and draw a line through the origin.  
4. Verbal statement: Flour is always \(1.5\) times the amount of sugar.  
5. Diagram: For every 2 sugar units, draw 3 flour units.

Ask: “How can we verify this relationship is proportional in more than one way?”

Students should explain that the table has a constant ratio, the graph is a line through the origin, and the equation has the form \(y = kx\).

**Critical Thinking: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students cross-check the same relationship using table, graph, and equation.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students using multiple representations to confirm their answer, not relying on only one clue.

**Critical Thinking: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students decide which evidence best proves proportionality.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask, “Which is stronger evidence: the graph looking straight, or proving the ratios are equal? Why?” Listen for students recognizing that exact ratios or an equation are more reliable than appearance alone.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Compare Two Plans — 15 minutes  
Students work in pairs on this scenario:

“Two bike rental shops charge different prices.”

**Shop A:** $5 per hour.  
**Shop B:** $4 per hour plus a $6 helmet fee.

Students complete a table for 0–5 hours, graph both plans, write equations, and answer:

1. Which relationship is proportional?  
2. Which is cheaper for 1 hour? 3 hours? 5 hours?  
3. If you wanted a proportional pricing plan, which shop fits?  
4. If you wanted the cheaper option for a long ride, how would your choice change?

Expected equations:  
- Shop A: \(y = 5x\), proportional.  
- Shop B: \(y = 4x + 6\), not proportional because it does not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students compare Shop A and Shop B for different ride lengths.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students explaining trade-offs: “Shop B starts more expensive because of the fee, but it may become cheaper later.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students justify which shop is proportional and which shop is cheaper under different conditions.  
- **Observe/assess:** Require students to support claims with tables, equations, or graph evidence. Check that conclusions match their calculations.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes  
Invite pairs to share. Ask:

- “Can a relationship increase at a constant rate and still not be proportional?”  
- “Why does the graph need to pass through \((0,0)\)?”  
- “How does the equation show proportionality?”

Clarify misconception: \(y = mx + b\) is not proportional when \(b 
eq 0\). A proportional relationship must be \(y = kx\).

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes  
Students complete independently:

A taxi charges are shown below.

| Miles | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 7 | 10 | 13 | 16 |

1. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
2. Write a sentence describing how you checked.  
3. What representation helped you most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?

Expected answer: Not proportional. The cost increases by 3, but the ratios are not equal, and there appears to be a starting fee.

**Critical Thinking: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Targeted moment:** Exit ticket reflection asks students to name the strategy that helped them and why.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive statements such as, “At first I thought it was proportional because it added 3 each time, but then I checked the ratios.”

---

## Formative Assessment  
During the lesson, assess whether students can:

- Identify the constant of proportionality.  
- Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional.  
- Move between tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
- Justify conclusions with evidence.

## Differentiation  
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and graph axes; use color coding for corresponding values.  
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own proportional and non-proportional scenarios and trade with a partner to solve.  
- **Language support:** Provide sentence frames: “This is proportional because ___,” “The constant of proportionality is ___,” and “I checked by ___.”

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (Grade 7)

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, verbal descriptions).
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Materials:** Whiteboard/projector, graph paper, rulers, calculators, student handout with scenarios, sticky notes, exit tickets.
**Key Vocabulary:** Proportional relationship, ratio, unit rate, constant of proportionality (k), origin, equation y = kx.

### Lesson Objective
Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, justifying their reasoning using the constant of proportionality.

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Proportional?" (7 minutes)
Display two tables:

**Table A:** Hours 1,2,3,4 → Dollars 8,16,24,32
**Table B:** Hours 1,2,3,4 → Dollars 10,18,26,34

Ask: *"Which table is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner for 2 minutes, then share. Record student reasoning on the board. Guide students to notice Table A has a constant ratio (8 dollars/hour), while Table B increases by a constant amount but has no constant ratio.

> **Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate what "proportional" means and identify the key feature (constant ratio, passes through origin).
> **Assessment:** Listen for clarity such as, "I divided dollars by hours each time," rather than only "It goes up by 8." Collect 3 student definitions and refine into one precise statement.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes)
Introduce a smoothie scenario ($3 per smoothie) across five representations:
1. **Verbal:** "$3 per smoothie"
2. **Table:** (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9)…
3. **Equation:** y = 3x
4. **Graph:** straight line through origin (0,0)
5. **Diagram:** tape diagram showing repeated units of 3

Explicitly highlight the **constant of proportionality (k)** and the rule: *proportional graphs pass through (0,0) and have the form y = kx*. Emphasize: "A pattern that adds the same amount is not always proportional. We must check the ratio and the origin."

> **Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to arrange the same relationship across five formats in a structured graphic organizer.
> **Assessment:** Students complete a parallel organizer for "$4 per taco." Check for logical alignment across columns.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sort & Justify (10 minutes)
Hand out 6 cards showing mixed scenarios (tables, graphs, equations). In pairs, students sort them into **Proportional** vs. **Not Proportional** piles and write one sentence justifying each.

Examples:
- Table: (2,5), (4,10), (6,15) → Proportional, k = 2.5
- Graph: line with y-intercept of 2 → Not proportional
- Equation: y = 7x → Proportional
- Recipe: "3 cups flour for every 2 cups sugar" → Proportional, y = (3/2)x

> **Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students must cross-check evidence (does the ratio stay constant? does it pass through origin?) using multiple representations.
> **Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "What evidence proves this is proportional? Could you confirm using a second representation?" Look for students cross-checking table values AND testing the equation form.

> **Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Some cards contain near-misses (e.g., y = 2x + 0.1). Ask, "Which is stronger evidence: the graph looking straight, or proving ratios are equal?"
> **Assessment:** Listen for students recognizing that exact ratios or an equation in y = kx form are more reliable than visual appearance alone.
