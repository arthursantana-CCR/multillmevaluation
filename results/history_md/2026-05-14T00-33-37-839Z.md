## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Explain *why* a relationship is or is not proportional using mathematical reasoning
- Reflect on their own thinking and adjust their approach when errors arise

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Proportional or Not?" card sets (one per pair)
- Graph paper
- Student journals
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — *The Lemonade Stand* (8 minutes)

Display this scenario on the board:

> *"Maya sells lemonade. For every 2 cups of lemon juice, she makes 8 cups of lemonade. For every 5 cups of lemon juice, she makes 20 cups. For every 3 cups of lemon juice, she makes 10 cups."*

Ask students: **"Which of these is the odd one out? Why?"**

Students write their answer in journals before sharing with a partner. Cold-call two or three students to share their reasoning aloud.

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information:** Students must identify which data point breaks the proportional pattern (3:10 ≠ constant ratio). Observe whether students can articulate *precisely* what "constant ratio" means, not just which one looks different. Listen for language like *"the ratio of lemon juice to lemonade must always equal 1:4."* Students who say only *"that one is wrong"* without explaining the ratio are demonstrating Level 1; those who explicitly define and apply the constant ratio reach Level 2.

---

### Direct Instruction — *What Makes a Relationship Proportional?* (10 minutes)

Using the board, introduce the four representations side by side for the relationship y = 3x:

- **Table:** (1,3), (2,6), (3,9)
- **Graph:** Plotted points forming a straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x (unit rate as the coefficient)
- **Verbal:** *"Every ticket costs $3, so the total cost is always 3 times the number of tickets."*

Explicitly name the two conditions for proportionality: **(1) constant ratio (k = y/x)** and **(2) the graph passes through the origin.**

**CRI1b — Organizing Information:** The side-by-side display is modeled for students, and they are then asked to recreate it in their journals for a new relationship (y = 4x). Assess whether students organize all four representations on the same page with clear labels and logical flow, rather than scattering them randomly. Walk the room and note whose journal layout supports easy comparison versus whose is disorganized.

---

### Guided Practice — *Proportional or Not? Card Sort* (12 minutes)

Pairs receive a set of 12 cards showing tables, graphs, equations, and scenarios. Students sort them into two groups: **Proportional** and **Not Proportional**, then write one sentence justifying each card's placement.

Include two deliberate "traps": a graph that is linear but does *not* pass through the origin (y = 2x + 1) and a table where ratios are close but not equal.

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information:** Students must cross-check each card using *both* the ratio test and the origin test rather than relying on appearance alone. Observe pairs who catch the linear-but-not-proportional graph as the strongest evidence of Level 2 thinking. Ask probing questions: *"Your eyes say proportional — does the math agree?"*

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information:** After sorting, each pair compares their card groupings with another pair and must resolve any disagreements using mathematical evidence, not opinion. Circulate and listen for students who cite specific ratio calculations versus those who say *"it just looks right."*

---

### Structured Discussion — *Which Representation Do You Trust Most?* (10 minutes)

Pose the question to the class: **"If you had to decide whether a relationship is proportional, which representation — table, graph, equation, or description — is most reliable? Which is most likely to trick you?"**

Students spend 2 minutes writing their position, then debate in a whole-class discussion.

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices:** Students must argue for one representation while honestly acknowledging the weaknesses of their choice. Level 2 is evident when a student says something like: *"The equation is most reliable because k is explicit, but if the equation is written incorrectly, you'd miss it — so I'd verify with the table too."* Superficial answers (*"graphs are easy to read"*) reflect Level 1.

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making:** Assess whether students build their argument on mathematical properties (constant ratio, origin) rather than personal preference. Push back gently: *"What if the graph is hand-drawn and slightly off — does that change your answer?"* Students who adjust their reasoning in response to new complications demonstrate Level 2.

---

### Independent Practice — *Error Analysis* (10 minutes)

Students receive a worked example showing a student who incorrectly labeled y = 5x + 2 as proportional because it is linear. Students must:
1. Identify the error
2. Correct it with evidence from two representations
3. Write a note to the fictional student explaining their mistake kindly but clearly

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes:** After correcting the error, students answer in their journal: *"Have you ever made this same mistake? What were you thinking at the time, and what would you do differently now?"* Level 2 is demonstrated when students connect this error to a specific past misconception and describe a concrete strategy they will use going forward (e.g., *"I will always check the origin before calling something proportional"*). Vague answers like *"I'll be more careful"* reflect Level 1.

---

### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students independently answer:
> *"Give one example of a proportional relationship and one non-example. Represent each as a table AND an equation. Explain in one sentence how you know the difference."*

This serves as a summative check on CRI1a, CRI1b, and CRI4a. Collect and review before the next class to identify misconceptions for reteaching.

---

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations.
4.  Determine the constant of proportionality from various representations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with tables and graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Calculators (optional, for efficiency)
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore a really important concept in math: proportional relationships. You've already worked with ratios and rates. Can anyone remind us what a ratio is? What about a unit rate?"
*   *(Briefly review ratios and unit rates, connecting them to real-world examples like miles per hour or cost per item. For instance, 'If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?')*
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll see how these ideas connect to something called a 'proportional relationship' and how we can spot them in different ways."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice: Defining Proportionality (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "A **proportional relationship** exists between two quantities when one quantity is a constant multiple of the other. This constant multiple is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by 'k'. In simpler terms, if you divide the 'y' value by the 'x' value, you always get the same number, 'k'."
*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** "Let's try to put that in our own words. Turn to your elbow partner and explain what 'constant multiple' means in the context of a proportional relationship. What are the two key things we need to look for when deciding if a relationship is proportional?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen to student discussions. I will observe if students are accurately rephrasing the definition, identifying "constant ratio" and "relationship between two quantities" as key ideas. I'll ask a few pairs to share their rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in their language.
*   **Teacher:** "We can see proportional relationships in different forms. Let's start with tables."
    *   *Display a table on the board:*
        | x (hours) | y (miles) |
        | :-------- | :-------- |
        | 1         | 50        |
        | 2         | 100       |
        | 3         | 150       |
    *   **Teacher:** "For each pair (x, y), let's calculate y/x. What do you notice?" (Students should find y/x = 50 for all pairs). "Since y/x is constant, this is a proportional relationship, and k = 50. The equation would be y = 50x."
    *   *Display a non-proportional table:*
        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
        | :---------- | :------- |
        | 1           | $1.50    |
        | 2           | $2.50    |
        | 3           | $3.50    |
    *   **Teacher:** "Now, let's check this one. What do you find for y/x?" (Students should find different ratios). "Is this proportional? Why not?" (No constant ratio).
*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships also have a special look on a graph. They always form a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0)."
    *   *Quickly sketch the graph of y=50x, showing it's a straight line through the origin.*

**III. Activity 1: Table & Graph Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn! In pairs, you'll get a handout with several tables. Your task is to determine if each relationship is proportional. If it is, you'll find the constant of proportionality (k), write the equation, and then graph it on the provided graph paper."
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Remember, to be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers. You need to check *every* pair in the table. If even one ratio is different, it's not proportional. Be rigorous in your checks!"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and observe pairs. I will specifically look for students systematically calculating y/x for *all* rows in the tables, not just the first one. I'll ask groups, "How did you confirm this table is proportional? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why is checking all of them important?"
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** "When you're working with your tables and graphs, make sure your work is clear and organized. Label your axes on the graph, use a consistent scale, and show your calculations for 'k' clearly next to each table."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will check student handouts for clear organization. Are tables filled out neatly? Are calculations for 'k' shown consistently for each row? Are graphs clearly labeled with axes, units, and a consistent scale? Do the plotted points accurately reflect the table data in an orderly fashion?
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** As students work, they will constantly make decisions: "Is this proportional?", "What's the constant?", "How do I write the equation?". They must use the definition and their calculations to justify their answers.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** During group work, I will listen to their discussions. Are they using the definition (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to justify their decisions? For example, if a group incorrectly identifies a non-proportional relationship, I'll ask, "What evidence from your calculations or graph led you to that conclusion? Does it meet *all* the criteria for proportionality, including passing through (0,0)?"

**IV. Activity 2: Equation & Diagram Connection (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Great work with tables and graphs! Now let's connect to equations and verbal descriptions."
*   *Provide a new scenario:* "A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."
*   **Teacher:** "Is this a proportional relationship? How do you know? What's the constant of proportionality if we consider flour to milk?" (k = 2/3). "What if we consider milk to flour?" (k = 3/2).
*   **Teacher:** "How would we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)?" (F = (2/3)M). "And milk (M) in terms of flour (F)?" (M = (3/2)F).
*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Imagine you're explaining this recipe to someone who's never cooked before. Which representation – the verbal description, a table, a graph, or an equation – do you think would be most helpful for them to understand the relationship between flour and milk? Why? Are there situations where another representation might be better or worse?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will listen to student responses during a brief class discussion. Do they articulate the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? For example, "The verbal description is easy to start with, but an equation is better if you need to scale the recipe up a lot because you can just plug in numbers." or "A graph gives a quick visual, but a table shows exact values for specific amounts."
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, create your own short verbal description of a proportional relationship. Then, write its equation and sketch a simple diagram (like a ratio table or a simple picture showing groups) that represents it. Make sure your example is clear and accurate."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will review their created examples. Do their verbal descriptions clearly imply proportionality? Is the equation correctly derived from the description? Does the diagram accurately represent the relationship? I'll look for examples that are clear, concise, and mathematically sound, avoiding vague or incorrect relationships.

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Let's quickly review. What are the key characteristics of a proportional relationship in a table? (Constant ratio y/x) In a graph? (Straight line through the origin) In an equation? (y = kx)"
*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, I want you to answer two questions:
        1.  What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today?
        2.  What strategy did you use, or what 'aha!' moment did you have, that helped you overcome that challenge?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will collect exit tickets. I'll look for responses that demonstrate metacognitive awareness. Do students identify specific difficulties (e.g., "remembering to check (0,0) on a graph," "calculating k consistently when numbers were fractions") and describe a strategy they used (e.g., "I made sure to divide y by x for every single point," "I drew a quick sketch to see if it went through the origin before plotting all points")? This shows they are reflecting on their learning process and adjusting their thinking.

---
**Differentiation:**
*   **Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality.
*   **Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but isn't. Have them explore inverse proportionality or multi-step proportional problems.

**Assessment:**
*   Informal observation during activities and discussions.
*   Review of student handouts (tables, graphs, equations, created examples).
*   Exit ticket responses.

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a brief review: “What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”  
- Show a simple example: If 1 apple costs $2, then 2 apples cost $4, 3 apples cost $6, etc.  
- Write the ratio and explain the constant of proportionality (unit rate).  
- State the lesson goal: “Today, we will learn how to recognize and represent proportional relationships in different ways.”

*CCR Critical Thinking - Clarifying Information (CRI1a)*  
**Activity:** During this introduction, ask students to explain in their own words what proportional means.  
**Observation:** Listen for students who clarify the idea precisely (e.g., “The ratio stays the same no matter how much you have”) versus those who give vague or incorrect explanations.  

---

**2. Guided Practice: Tables and Graphs (15 minutes)**  
- Present a table showing quantities of pencils and their total cost (e.g., 1 pencil = $1.50, 2 pencils = $3.00, 3 pencils = $4.50).  
- Ask students to identify if the relationship is proportional by checking if the ratio is constant.  
- Then, plot these points on graph paper and draw the line. Emphasize that the graph should be a straight line through the origin for proportional relationships.  
- Discuss how the table and graph represent the same relationship.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Organizing Information (CRI1b)*  
**Activity:** Have students create their own table and graph from a new proportional relationship problem on the handout.  
**Observation:** Assess how well students organize data into tables and graphs, noting if their work follows a logical, clear structure or if it is scattered or inconsistent.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)*  
**Activity:** Prompt students to check their table and graph for consistency (e.g., do all points lie on the same line? Are ratios equal?).  
**Observation:** Look for students who cross-check their work and correct errors versus those who accept their first answer without verification.

---

**3. Representing Proportional Relationships with Equations (10 minutes)**  
- Introduce the equation form y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.  
- Using the pencil example, write y = 1.5x.  
- Show how the equation matches the table and graph.  
- Give students a few pairs of values and ask them to write the equation representing the proportional relationship.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)*  
**Activity:** Discuss why the equation y = kx is a reliable way to represent proportional relationships compared to just listing numbers.  
**Observation:** Identify students who demonstrate understanding of why equations provide a precise, authoritative representation versus those who rely on less accurate or incomplete methods.

---

**4. Verbal Descriptions and Diagrams (10 minutes)**  
- Present a word problem describing a proportional relationship (e.g., “For every 3 hours of work, you earn $45.”)  
- Guide students to identify the quantities, write the ratio, and represent it in a diagram (e.g., a double number line or tape diagram).  
- Have students explain the relationship verbally and write the corresponding equation.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)*  
**Activity:** Ask students to compare different ways to represent the relationship (table vs. graph vs. equation vs. diagram) and discuss which might be best in different situations.  
**Observation:** Listen for students who thoughtfully evaluate the advantages and limitations of each representation, considering clarity, ease of use, and context.

---

**5. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (5 minutes)**  
- Present a scenario: “If a recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar, how much flour is needed for 9 cups of sugar?”  
- Have students explain their reasoning step-by-step, using proportional reasoning to solve the problem.  
- Encourage them to justify their approach clearly.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Applying Sound Reasoning (CRI4a)*  
**Activity:** During student explanations, prompt them to anticipate possible mistakes or alternative approaches and explain why their method works best.  
**Observation:** Assess students’ ability to provide logical, clear reasoning that anticipates complexities (e.g., checking if the ratio is maintained).

---

**6. Reflection and Metacognition (5 minutes)**  
- Ask students to write a brief reflection: “How did you figure out if two quantities are proportional? What strategies helped you understand the relationships better?”  
- Encourage them to consider what they found challenging and how they adjusted their thinking.

*CCR Critical Thinking - Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)*  
**Activity:** Collect reflections and look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as students describing how they changed their approach or recognized errors.  
**Observation:** Identify students who demonstrate deep reflection and strategic adjustment versus those who only mention surface-level reasoning.

---

**7. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Provide a quick problem requiring students to identify and represent a proportional relationship in any form (table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description).  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding and critical thinking skills.

Ask probing questions: *"Your eyes say proportional — does the math agree?"* Look specifically for students who calculate y/x for every row in a table, not just the first one, and who verify the graph passes through (0, 0) before making a decision.

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information:**
After sorting, each pair compares their card groupings with another pair and must resolve any disagreements using mathematical evidence, not opinion. Circulate and listen for students who cite specific ratio calculations versus those who say *"it just looks right."*

---

### Equations, Verbal Descriptions, and Diagrams (5 minutes)

Present a new scenario: *"A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."*

- Is this proportional? How do you know?
- What is k if we consider flour relative to milk? (k = 2/3)
- How do we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)? (F = (2/3)M)
- How might we show this as a diagram — for example, a double number line or tape diagram?

Guide students to connect the verbal description to the equation and diagram representations, reinforcing that all forms encode the same relationship.

---

### Structured Discussion — *Which Representation Do You Trust Most?* (10 minutes)

Pose the question to the class: **"If you had to decide whether a relationship is proportional, which representation — table, graph, equation, or verbal description — is most reliable? Which is most likely to trick you?"**

Students spend 2 minutes writing their position, then debate in a whole-class discussion.

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices:**
Students must argue for one representation while honestly acknowledging the weaknesses of their choice. Level 2 is evident when a student says something like: *"The equation is most reliable because k is explicit, but if the equation is written incorrectly, you'd miss it — so I'd verify with the table too."* Or: *"A graph gives a quick visual, but a hand-drawn graph can be slightly off and mislead you."* Superficial answers (*"graphs are easy to read"*) reflect Level 1.

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making:**
Assess whether students build their argument on mathematical properties (constant ratio, origin) rather than personal preference. Push back gently: *"What if the graph is hand-drawn and slightly off — does that change your answer? What if the equation has a typo?"* Students who adjust their reasoning in response to new complications, anticipating real-world limitations of each representation, demonstrate Level 2.

---

### Independent Practice — *Error Analysis* (5 minutes)

Students receive a worked example showing a student who incorrectly labeled y = 5x + 2 as proportional because it is linear. Students must:
1. Identify the error
2. Correct it with evidence from two representations
3. Write a note to the fictional student explaining their mistake kindly but clearly

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes:**
After correcting the error, students answer in their journal: *"Have you ever made this same mistake — assuming linear means proportional? What were you thinking at the time, and what would you do differently now?"* Level 2 is demonstrated when students connect this error to a specific past misconception and describe a concrete strategy they will use going forward (e.g., *"I will always check whether the graph passes through the origin before calling something proportional"*). Vague answers like *"I'll be more careful"* reflect Level 1.

---

### Exit Ticket (5 minutes)

Students independently answer:

> *"Give one example of a proportional relationship and one non-example. Represent each as a table AND an equation. Explain in one sentence how you know the difference."*

This serves as a summative check on CRI1a, CRI1b, and CRI4a. Collect and review before the next class to identify misconceptions for reteaching.

## Differentiation

**Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with labeled scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist: (1) Is y/x the same for every pair? (2) Does the graph pass through (0, 0)?

**Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that *appears* proportional but is not, and explain why. Have them explore what happens when y = kx + b with b ≠ 0, or investigate inverse proportionality.

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — *The Lemonade Stand* (8 minutes)

Display this scenario on the board:

> *"Maya sells lemonade. For every 2 cups of lemon juice, she makes 8 cups of lemonade. For every 5 cups of lemon juice, she makes 20 cups. For every 3 cups of lemon juice, she makes 10 cups."*

Ask students: **"Which of these is the odd one out? Why?"**

Students write their answer in journals before sharing with a partner. Cold-call two or three students to share their reasoning aloud. Briefly connect to prior knowledge of ratios and unit rates: *"You've already worked with ratios. Today, we'll see how a constant ratio connects to something called a proportional relationship."*

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information:**
Students must identify which data point breaks the proportional pattern (3:10 ≠ constant ratio). Observe whether students can articulate *precisely* what "constant ratio" means, not just which one looks different. Listen for language like *"the ratio of lemon juice to lemonade must always equal 1:4."* Students who say only *"that one is wrong"* without explaining the ratio demonstrate Level 1; those who explicitly define and apply the constant ratio reach Level 2.

---

### Direct Instruction — *What Makes a Relationship Proportional?* (10 minutes)

Using the board, introduce the four representations side by side for the relationship y = 3x:

- **Table:** (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9) — show that y/x = 3 for every pair
- **Graph:** Plotted points forming a straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x (unit rate as the coefficient k)
- **Verbal:** *"Every ticket costs $3, so the total cost is always 3 times the number of tickets."*

Explicitly name the two conditions for proportionality: **(1) constant ratio (k = y/x)** and **(2) the graph passes through the origin (0, 0).**

Then display a non-proportional table as a contrast:

| x (cookies) | y (cost) |
|---|---|
| 1 | $1.50 |
| 2 | $2.50 |
| 3 | $3.50 |

Ask: *"What do you find when you calculate y/x for each row? Does this qualify as proportional? Why not?"*

**CRI1b — Organizing Information:**
The side-by-side four-representation display is modeled for students, who are then asked to recreate it in their journals for a new relationship (y = 4x). Assess whether students organize all four representations on the same page with clear labels and logical flow, rather than scattering them. Walk the room and note whose journal layout supports easy comparison versus whose is disorganized.

---
