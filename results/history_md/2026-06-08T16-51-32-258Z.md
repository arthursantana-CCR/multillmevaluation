## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Objective:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Materials
- Whiteboard/projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Trail Mix” task handout  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Deal Is Better? — 7 minutes

Display two snack deals:

- **Deal A:** 3 granola bars for $4.50  
- **Deal B:** 5 granola bars for $7.00  

Ask students: “Which deal gives the lower price per granola bar? How do you know?”

Students solve independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Bring the class together to discuss unit rates.

Emphasize that proportional relationships often involve a constant rate, such as dollars per item.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify relevant quantities: number of bars, total cost, and cost per bar.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clearly explaining what each number means, such as “$4.50 is the cost for 3 bars, so I divide by 3 to find the cost for 1 bar.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Introduce the definition:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities is constant. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

Model using a table:

| Cups of Lemonade | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $2 |
| 2 | $4 |
| 3 | $6 |
| 4 | $8 |

Ask:
- “What is the cost per cup?”
- “Is the ratio cost/cups always the same?”
- “What equation represents this?”

Students should identify:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Then show the graph. Explain that proportional relationships graph as a straight line through the origin \((0,0)\).

Next, show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $27 |
| 4 | $32 |

Ask students to check whether the ratio is constant.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship in multiple forms: table, equation, graph, and verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students arrange work logically, such as labeling columns, computing ratios consistently, and matching the equation to the table.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Trail Mix Table and Graph — 12 minutes

Present the situation:

“A recipe uses 2 cups of raisins for every 5 cups of cereal.”

Ask students to complete a table:

| Cereal, \(x\) | Raisins, \(y\) |
|---|---|
| 5 | 2 |
| 10 | 4 |
| 15 | 6 |
| 20 | 8 |

Students then:
1. Find the constant of proportionality.
2. Write an equation.
3. Graph the ordered pairs.
4. Write a verbal description.

Guide students toward:

\[
\frac{y}{x} = \frac{2}{5}
\]

\[
y = \frac{2}{5}x
\]

Discuss why the graph must pass through the origin: if there is 0 cereal, there should be 0 raisins.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing Validity of Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check whether the relationship is proportional by using the table, equation, and graph.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify using more than one representation: “The ratios are all \(2/5\), and the graph is a straight line through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which information is most useful for proving proportionality: ratios, origin, straight line, or equation form.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students choosing mathematically relevant evidence instead of weak evidence, such as “It looks like a pattern” without explaining the constant ratio.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Are They Proportional? — 13 minutes

Give each pair three relationship cards.

**Card 1:**
| \(x\) | \(y\) |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

**Card 2:**
| \(x\) | \(y\) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 13 |

**Card 3:**  
“A taxi charges $3 before the ride begins and $2 per mile.”

Partners must decide whether each relationship is proportional. They must represent each using at least two forms: table, equation, graph, diagram, or verbal explanation.

Expected conclusions:
- Card 1 is proportional: \(y = 3x\)
- Card 2 is not proportional because ratios are not constant
- Card 3 is not proportional because the equation is \(y = 2x + 3\), not \(y = kx\), and the graph does not pass through the origin

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose which representation best supports their argument: table, graph, equation, or verbal explanation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask pairs: “Which representation made your decision easiest? Which was least helpful?” Listen for comparison, such as “The table helped us check ratios quickly, but the graph helped us see it did not pass through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students make a decision about proportionality and justify it with mathematical reasoning.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether claims are supported by valid reasoning: constant ratio, equation in the form \(y = kx\), or graph through the origin.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 6 minutes

Invite selected pairs to explain one card. Encourage students to respectfully question reasoning:

- “Where do you see the constant of proportionality?”
- “How do you know the graph would or would not pass through the origin?”
- “Could this equation be written as \(y = kx\)?”

Clarify misconceptions:
- A straight line alone is not enough; it must pass through the origin.
- Adding a starting fee means the relationship is not proportional.
- A pattern in numbers does not always mean a constant ratio.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 7 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. Determine whether the relationship is proportional:

| \(x\) | \(y\) |
|---|---|
| 3 | 12 |
| 5 | 20 |
| 8 | 32 |

2. Write an equation if it is proportional.
3. Explain how you checked.
4. Reflection: “At first I thought ___, but then I checked ___, so now I think ___.”

Expected answer:

\[
y = 4x
\]

The relationship is proportional because \(\frac{y}{x} = 4\) for all pairs.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students complete the reflection sentence on the exit ticket.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for evidence that students are monitoring and adjusting their thinking, such as recognizing an initial assumption and explaining how checking ratios, graphs, or equations changed or confirmed their conclusion.

---

## Assessment

**Formative Assessment:**  
- Warm-up partner discussion  
- Guided practice table, equation, and graph  
- Partner task explanations  
- Teacher questioning during discussion  

**Summative Check for the Lesson:**  
The exit ticket will show whether students can identify a proportional relationship, write an equation, and explain their reasoning.

## Differentiation

**Support:** Provide sentence frames:
- “This relationship is proportional because ___.”
- “The constant of proportionality is ___.”
- “I checked by ___.”

**Challenge:** Ask students to create their own proportional and non-proportional relationship, then represent each in two different ways.
