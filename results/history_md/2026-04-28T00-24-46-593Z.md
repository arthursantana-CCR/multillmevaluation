## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Are the Best Evidence

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Materials:** Notebook paper, pens, sticky notes, whiteboard

---

## HOOK — *What Do You Wonder?* (8 minutes)

Walk in and write this on the board: **"The most dangerous question you can ask is 'Why?'"**

Give students 90 seconds of silence to write their gut reaction in their notebooks. No right answer. Then ask three or four volunteers to share.

Now flip it: *"Is that statement true or false? And how would you prove it?"*

Tell students that today they're going to do two things at once — practice being genuinely curious AND learn how to turn that curiosity into a powerful written argument.

---

## DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *What Makes an Argument?* (10 minutes)

Write the argument framework on the board:

- **Claim** — What you believe
- **Reasons** — Why you believe it
- **Evidence** — Proof that supports your reasons
- **Counterclaim** — What someone who disagrees would say
- **Rebuttal** — Why your argument still holds up

Explain: *"A strong argument isn't just someone yelling their opinion louder. It's someone who has actually gotten curious enough to look at ALL sides and still land somewhere."*

Give a quick, relatable model. Write on the board:

> **Claim:** Middle schoolers should have fewer homework assignments.
> **Reason:** Sleep deprivation hurts academic performance.
> **Evidence:** Studies show teens need 8–10 hours of sleep, yet most average 6–7.
> **Counterclaim:** Some argue homework builds discipline and reinforces learning.
> **Rebuttal:** Discipline can be built through quality, not quantity — focused practice matters more than volume.

Point out: *"Notice the writer had to be curious enough to ask — 'What would someone who disagrees say?' That question made the argument stronger, not weaker."*

---

## ACTIVITY 1 — *The Curiosity Wall* (10 minutes)

Hand out three sticky notes to each student. Ask them to write one genuine question per note — things they've actually wondered about. Topics can be wild, serious, silly, or personal. Examples: *Why do we grade students? Should zoos exist? Does social media make people lonelier?*

Students post their notes on a designated wall or board. Give everyone two minutes to silently walk around and read the questions. Each student puts a small checkmark on the two questions that spark the most curiosity in them personally.

Tally the top four or five most-checked questions as a class. These become the **argument topics** students choose from.

---

## ACTIVITY 2 — *Curiosity Fuels the Claim* (5 minutes)

Students pick one question from the Curiosity Wall that genuinely interests them. In their notebooks, they answer:

1. What do I *think* is true about this?
2. What would someone who completely disagrees say?
3. What would I need to know to prove my side?

Explain: *"Question three is the most important. Curious writers don't just argue — they investigate. Even if you can't research right now, naming what evidence you'd need is half the battle."*

---

## ACTIVITY 3 — *Draft the Argument* (15 minutes)

Students write a short structured argument (one solid paragraph or up to three short paragraphs) using the framework from the board. Circulate and prompt students who are stuck with questions like:

- *"What's the strongest thing someone against you would say?"*
- *"Does your evidence actually connect to your claim, or is it just interesting?"*
- *"What made you curious about this in the first place? Put that energy in your writing."*

Remind students: acknowledging a counterclaim isn't weakness. It shows you were curious enough to take the other side seriously.

---

## SHARE & DISCUSS — *Argue Like a Curious Person* (5 minutes)

Ask two or three volunteers to read their arguments aloud. After each one, ask the class a single question: *"Did this writer seem curious or did they seem like they already had their mind made up before they started?"*

Discuss briefly: what's the difference in tone? In honesty? In strength?

---

## CLOSING — *The Takeaway* (2 minutes)

Return to the opening statement: *"The most dangerous question you can ask is 'Why?'"*

Ask: *"Do you agree or disagree now — and could you write an argument to prove it?"*

Leave students with this: **Curiosity isn't just a feeling. It's a skill. And in argument writing, it's your most powerful tool — because the writer who asks the best questions always has the most to say.**

Assign the argument draft for homework if not completed, or as a polished revision for next class.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt (provided below)  
- Chart paper or projector for examples  
- Sticky notes  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us become better thinkers and learners.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci) who used curiosity to discover or create something important.  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different viewpoints — all skills important for writing strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our claims  
  - Consider other opinions or opposing claims  
- Tell students that today they will practice using curiosity to write an argument.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- On the board or projector, display the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim** – Your main point or opinion  
  2. **Reasons** – Why you believe your claim is true  
  3. **Evidence** – Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claim** – Recognizing another viewpoint  
  5. **Response to Opposing Claim** – Explaining why your claim still holds  
- Provide a simple example:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* Studies show schools with uniforms have fewer bullying incidents.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Response:* However, students can express themselves through accessories and behavior.  
- Highlight how curiosity helps us find good reasons and evidence and understand other opinions.

**4. Guided Practice: Exploring a Prompt with Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Hand out the following prompt:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- Ask students to brainstorm questions they have about this topic on sticky notes. Examples:  
  - Why do teachers assign homework?  
  - Does homework help students learn better?  
  - What are the negative effects of homework?  
  - What do students think about homework?  
- Collect a few sticky notes and discuss how these questions show curiosity and can guide research or thinking.

**5. Writing Activity: Drafting an Argumentative Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Instruct students to choose a side (for or against banning homework).  
- Using their curiosity questions, have them write a paragraph that includes:  
  - A clear claim  
  - At least two reasons with supporting evidence (can be from prior knowledge or logical examples)  
  - An acknowledgment of an opposing claim  
  - A response to that opposing claim  
- Circulate and support students as they write.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each, ask the class:  
  - What curious questions might have helped the writer?  
  - How did the writer acknowledge the other side?  
- End with a brief reflection:  
  *“How can being curious help you become a better writer and thinker?”*  
- Encourage students to keep asking questions whenever they write or learn something new.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to find an article or video about a topic they are curious about and write down three questions they have after exploring it. They will bring these questions to the next class to discuss.

---

**Summary:**  
This lesson uses curiosity as a foundation to engage students in argumentative writing. By encouraging them to ask questions and consider multiple viewpoints, students practice writing clear, well-supported arguments aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1.
