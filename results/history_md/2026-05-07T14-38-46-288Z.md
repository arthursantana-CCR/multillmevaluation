## Candidate 1
# 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade:** 7 | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2
**Setting:** Traditional Classroom | **Duration:** 55 Minutes

---

## Lesson Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Explain *why* a relationship is or is not proportional using precise mathematical language
- Evaluate multiple representations and justify their reasoning

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Rate Card" scenario sheets (one per pair)
- Graph paper
- Colored pencils
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Structure

---

### HOOK – Launch (5 minutes)

**Activity:** Display two scenarios on the board:
- *Scenario A:* A car travels 60 miles per hour.
- *Scenario B:* A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: *"Are both of these proportional? How do you know?"* Allow 60 seconds of quiet thinking, then take 2–3 responses. Do **not** confirm or deny yet — tell students: *"By the end of today, you'll be able to prove your answer."*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
- *Targeting moment:* Students commit to an initial claim before instruction begins.
- *Assessment:* Listen for students who hedge ("I think…but I'm not sure why"). Note who changes their answer later and can articulate why.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION – What Makes a Relationship Proportional? (10 minutes)

**Activity:** Teacher presents a clear, structured explanation using a table of values for Scenario A (60 mph):

| Hours | Miles |
|-------|-------|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

Teacher models three checks: (1) constant ratio y/x = 60, (2) graph passes through the origin, (3) equation is of the form y = kx.

Then revisit Scenario B: the table shows the ratio is *not* constant, the graph does not pass through the origin, and the equation is y = 2x + 3.

**CRI1a – Clarifying information**
- *Targeting moment:* Teacher explicitly defines "proportional relationship" with precise criteria — constant ratio, origin on graph, y = kx form — and contrasts it with a near-miss (linear but not proportional).
- *Assessment:* Ask students to restate the definition in their own words using one sentence. Look for precision ("the ratio must always be the same") vs. vagueness ("the numbers go up together").

**CRI1b – Organizing information**
- *Targeting moment:* Teacher uses a side-by-side structured comparison of both scenarios across all three representations (table, graph, equation), building a logical sequence.
- *Assessment:* Check whether students' notes mirror the organized structure or are fragmented. During partner work, observe if students refer back to this framework.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE – Rate Card Analysis (15 minutes)

**Activity:** Student pairs receive a "Rate Card" sheet with four scenarios presented in different representations (one table, one graph, one equation, one verbal description). Students must classify each as proportional or not and provide *written justification*.

**CRI2a – Assessing validity of information**
- *Targeting moment:* One scenario intentionally includes a table where the ratio appears consistent for the first two rows but breaks at the third. Students must check *all* values, not just the first two.
- *Assessment:* Circulate and ask: *"How many data points did you check before deciding?"* Students demonstrating level 2 will cross-check all values and note the inconsistency explicitly.

**CRI2b – Assessing quality of information**
- *Targeting moment:* Students are asked: *"Which representation gives you the most reliable evidence that this is proportional — and why?"* They must select and defend the strongest evidence.
- *Assessment:* Look for students who explain that the equation y = kx is definitively reliable, while a table with only two points is insufficient alone.

---

### COLLABORATIVE CHALLENGE – The "Better Deal" Debate (15 minutes)

**Activity:** Groups of three receive a real-world problem: *Two streaming services — StreamFast charges $8/month flat; QuickStream charges $0 for the first month, then $5/month.* Groups must:
1. Build tables and graphs for both
2. Identify which is proportional
3. Decide which is the "better deal" — and for whom

**CRI3a – Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
- *Targeting moment:* Students must argue that the "better deal" depends on *how many months* the service is used. There is no single correct answer — trade-offs are required.
- *Assessment:* Listen for groups who acknowledge *both* short-term and long-term perspectives. Superficial answers say one is always better; strong answers specify a break-even point.

**CRI4a – Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
- *Targeting moment:* Groups present their recommendation with a stated condition ("StreamFast is better *if* you use it more than 4 months because…").
- *Assessment:* Evaluate whether reasoning is grounded in the mathematical evidence (the table/graph intersection) rather than personal preference or unsupported claims.

---

### CLOSURE – Exit Ticket + Reflection (10 minutes)

**Exit Ticket (7 minutes):** Each student independently completes two tasks:
1. Given the equation y = 9x, identify it as proportional and explain using *two* different representations.
2. Write one sentence: *"At the start of class I thought ______. Now I know ______ because ______."*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on one's thinking processes** *(revisited)*
- *Targeting moment:* The sentence stem explicitly requires students to compare their prior thinking to their current understanding and name the evidence that shifted their thinking.
- *Assessment:* Score responses on depth — level 2 responses name a *specific moment or representation* that changed their thinking, not just "I learned more."

**Whole-Class Debrief (3 minutes):** Return to the Hook. Ask: *"Who changed their answer? What convinced you?"* Cold-call two students to model metacognitive narration aloud.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a graphic organizer with the three proportionality checks pre-labeled
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own "trick" scenario that looks proportional but isn't

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a real-world scenario: “Imagine you’re buying tickets for a concert. Each ticket costs $8. How much would 3 tickets cost? 5 tickets?”  
- Ask students to share how they figured out the total cost.  
- Introduce the concept of proportional relationships: when two quantities increase or decrease at the same rate.  
- Write the definition on the board: *A proportional relationship is one where the ratio between two quantities is constant.*

---

**2. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) – Activity & Assessment (10 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Present a table showing the number of tickets and total cost (e.g., Tickets: 1, 2, 3, 4; Cost: 8, 16, 24, 32). Ask students to identify what information is relevant to determine if the relationship is proportional.  
- Guide students to clarify that the ratio of cost to tickets is constant (8:1).  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students explicitly stating the constant ratio and explaining why it matters. For example, a student might say, “The cost per ticket stays the same, so the relationship is proportional.” This shows precise clarification of ideas.

---

**3. Organizing Information (CRI1b) – Activity & Assessment (10 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Provide students with mixed data: a table, a graph, and a verbal description of a proportional relationship. Ask them to organize the information by creating a diagram or equation that represents the relationship clearly.  
- For example, from the table and graph, students write the equation y = 8x.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check students’ work for logical flow—do they correctly translate data from one form to another? Are their equations consistent with the table and graph? Students who arrange information coherently demonstrate level 2.

---

**4. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) – Activity & Assessment (5 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Present two different verbal descriptions of a proportional relationship, one accurate and one with a subtle error (e.g., “The cost doubles every time the number of tickets doubles” vs. “The cost increases by $8 every time the number of tickets doubles”).  
- Ask students to evaluate which description correctly represents a proportional relationship and explain why.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students cross-checking the descriptions against their understanding of proportionality, identifying the error in the second description. This shows critical evaluation of information validity.

---

**5. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b) – Activity & Assessment (5 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Provide students with two sources of information about a proportional relationship: a textbook excerpt and a student’s informal explanation. Ask students to judge which source is more reliable and why.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Students who justify their choice by citing clarity, accuracy, and authority of the textbook demonstrate sophisticated judgment (level 2). Those who accept both without evaluation show lower levels.

---

**6. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a) – Activity & Assessment (7 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Present two methods for representing proportional relationships: using tables vs. using graphs. Have students work in pairs to discuss advantages and disadvantages of each method (e.g., tables show exact values, graphs show trends visually).  
- Then, have pairs share their conclusions with the class.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Assess whether students systematically evaluate trade-offs, considering which method is better for different purposes (e.g., quick calculations vs. visual understanding). Students who consider multiple factors and long-term implications demonstrate level 2.

---

**7. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a) – Activity & Assessment (8 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** Give students a problem: “A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar. If you want to make a batch with 12 cups of sugar, how much flour do you need?”  
- Ask students to explain their reasoning step-by-step, using proportional relationships to find the answer.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for clear, logical reasoning that anticipates complexities (e.g., “Since 3 cups sugar corresponds to 2 cups flour, for 12 cups sugar, which is 4 times 3, I multiply 2 cups flour by 4 to get 8 cups flour.”). This shows sophisticated reasoning.

---

**8. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a) – Activity & Assessment (5 minutes)**  
- **Activity:** At the end of the lesson, ask students to write a brief reflection on how they approached understanding proportional relationships today. Prompt them to consider what strategies helped them and what was challenging.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing when they needed to slow down, check their work, or use multiple representations to understand the concept. Students demonstrating strategic adjustment of thinking processes show level 2.

---

**9. Closure and Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Summarize key points: proportional relationships have a constant ratio, can be represented in multiple ways, and understanding them requires careful reasoning and evaluation.  
- Distribute a quick exit ticket with a short problem asking students to identify if a relationship is proportional from a table or graph and explain their reasoning briefly.  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding.

- *Assessment:* Evaluate whether reasoning is grounded in the mathematical evidence (the table/graph intersection) rather than personal preference or unsupported claims. Look for step-by-step logic that directly links evidence to the conclusion.

---

### CLOSURE – Exit Ticket + Reflection (10 minutes)

**Exit Ticket (7 minutes):** Each student independently completes two tasks:
1. Given the equation y = 9x, identify it as proportional and explain using *two* different representations.
2. Write one sentence: *"At the start of class I thought ______. Now I know ______ because ______."*
   Additionally: *"What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on one's thinking processes** *(revisited)*
- *Targeting moment:* The sentence stem and reflection prompt explicitly require students to compare their prior thinking to their current understanding and name the evidence or strategy that shifted their thinking.
- *Assessment:* Score responses on depth — level 2 responses name a *specific moment or representation* that changed their thinking, not just "I learned more." Look for metacognitive specificity, such as "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line, so next time I will always check the ratio y/x for every point."

**Whole-Class Debrief (3 minutes):** Return to the Hook. Ask: *"Who changed their answer? What convinced you?"* Cold-call two students to model metacognitive narration aloud.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a graphic organizer with the three proportionality checks pre-labeled
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own "trick" scenario that looks proportional but isn't
