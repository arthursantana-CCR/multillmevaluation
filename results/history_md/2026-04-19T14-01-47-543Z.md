## Candidate 1
# The Curious Arguer: Why Questions Make Better Writers

## A 55-Minute Middle School ELA Lesson

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA / Writing
**Central Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1
**Soft Skill Focus:** Curiosity
**Materials Needed:** Whiteboard/projector, sticky notes (two colors), printed "Curiosity Cards," lined paper or notebooks, pencils, one mystery object in a paper bag (suggestion: a geode, an unusual kitchen tool, or a strange-looking seed pod)

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

Students will discover that the best argumentative writers are not people who already have all the answers — they are people who ask the best questions. Through hands-on exploration, guided discussion, and structured writing practice, students will learn to use curiosity as a tool for building stronger, more honest, and more persuasive arguments.

**Essential Question:** *What happens to your argument when you get genuinely curious about the other side?*

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Define curiosity as an active thinking habit, not just a feeling
2. Identify how acknowledging opposing claims strengthens an argument
3. Draft a claim supported by at least two reasons and one piece of evidence
4. Write a counterargument acknowledgment using a curiosity-driven approach

---

## LESSON TIMELINE

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 1: THE MYSTERY BAG HOOK (0:00 – 0:08)

**Purpose:** Activate curiosity immediately; establish it as a classroom value

**What the Teacher Does:**

Walk into the room holding a brown paper bag. Do not explain it. Set it on the front desk. Let students wonder. Wait about fifteen seconds in silence.

Then say:

*"I can already see some of you leaning forward. Some of you want to ask me something. What do you want to ask?"*

Take 4–5 questions from students. Write each question on the board exactly as the student says it. Don't answer any of them yet.

Then say:

*"Look at what just happened. Nobody told you to care about this bag. Nobody gave you a grade for wondering about it. You just... got curious. That feeling — that leaning-forward feeling — is one of the most powerful tools a writer can have. Today we're going to find out why."*

**Reveal the object** from the bag (the geode, strange tool, etc.). Pass it around if time allows. Spend 60 seconds letting students observe.

Then say:

*"Notice something: none of your questions were stupid. They were all different. Every single one of you was curious about something slightly different. That's exactly how good argument writers think — they stay curious, especially about the things they think they already know."*

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — WHAT IS CURIOSITY AND WHY DO WRITERS NEED IT? (0:08 – 0:18)

**Purpose:** Build conceptual understanding of curiosity as a writing strategy

**Teacher Talk:**

Write this on the board:

> **Curiosity:** The habit of asking genuine questions — especially about things that challenge or complicate what you already believe.

Say:

*"Here's something most people get wrong about arguments. They think the goal of an argument is to WIN. To prove the other person is completely wrong and you are completely right. But here's the problem with that approach — when you're only focused on winning, you stop paying attention to anything that might challenge you. You stop being curious. And that actually makes your argument weaker."*

Write this on the board:

> **Closed Arguer:** Already knows everything. Ignores the other side.
> **Curious Arguer:** Asks questions. Understands the other side. Uses that understanding to argue even better.

*"The curious arguer is scarier to disagree with — because they've already thought about what you're going to say."*

**Connect to the Standard:**

Write CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 in student-friendly language:

> *Write arguments with a clear claim, strong reasons, relevant evidence, AND acknowledge the other side.*

Say:

*"Did you catch that last part? Acknowledge the other side. The standard itself is telling you: a great argument isn't one-sided. It understands what the other person believes — and it responds to it. That takes curiosity. You have to actually wonder why someone might disagree with you."*

**The Curiosity-Argument Connection (3 Key Points — write each on the board):**

1. **Curious writers ask "Why might someone disagree with me?"** — This leads to a stronger counterargument section.
2. **Curious writers ask "What evidence am I missing?"** — This makes their reasoning more honest and complete.
3. **Curious writers ask "What if I'm partly wrong?"** — This makes their writing more trustworthy to the reader.

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 3: CURIOSITY CARDS ACTIVITY (0:18 – 0:28)

**Purpose:** Practice generating questions as a prewriting habit; build the skill of perspective-taking

**Setup:**
Pre-print or write "Curiosity Cards" — index cards each featuring a debatable topic relevant to middle schoolers. Suggested topics:

- *Should students be allowed to use their phones during lunch?*
- *Should homework be banned?*
- *Should junk food be sold at school?*
- *Should gym class be required every day?*
- *Should kids be allowed to grade their own tests?*

**Instructions:**

Each student (or pair) receives one Curiosity Card.

**Round 1 — Take a Side (2 minutes):**
Students write down their initial opinion on the topic. One sentence. They think they know what they believe.

**Round 2 — Get Curious (4 minutes):**
Now students flip the card over (or just flip their thinking) and write down at least **three genuine questions** they could ask someone who disagrees with them. Not sarcastic questions. Not trick questions. Real, honest questions they don't already know the answer to.

Model one for them first:

*"Let's say my topic is 'Should homework be banned?' and I believe homework should stay. What would I genuinely want to ask someone who wants to get rid of it? I might ask: 'What do you think students would do with that extra time at home?' or 'Have you seen research showing that homework doesn't help certain age groups?' Those are curious questions."*

**Round 3 — Quick Share (2 minutes):**
Two or three students share their best curiosity question with the class. The teacher affirms the quality of the questions without judging the opinions.

**Debrief (2 minutes):**

Ask:
- *"Did any of your questions make you slightly less certain about your own opinion? That's okay — that's actually a sign of intellectual honesty."*
- *"How do you think asking those questions might make your argument stronger, not weaker?"*

Key takeaway to say aloud:

*"You cannot write a good counterargument if you haven't been genuinely curious about the other side. Curiosity is the door you walk through to get there."*

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 4: MODELING — ANATOMY OF A CURIOUS ARGUMENT (0:28 – 0:38)

**Purpose:** Deconstruct a model argument; show students what curiosity looks like in writing

**Display or read aloud the following sample paragraph:**

---

*Schools should require students to participate in at least one extracurricular activity per semester. Students who are involved in activities outside of academics are more likely to develop important life skills such as teamwork, time management, and perseverance. According to a 2019 study by the American Academy of Pediatrics, students who participate in structured extracurricular activities report higher levels of school engagement and lower rates of behavioral issues. Some people argue, however, that mandatory participation would feel forced and might take the joy out of activities students currently choose freely. This is a reasonable concern — nobody wants to resent something they used to love. But the solution is not to eliminate the requirement; it is to expand the options. If schools offer a wide enough variety of activities — from robotics to drama to community service — every student can find something that genuinely interests them. The requirement is not about forcing passion. It is about opening doors.*

---

**Think-Aloud Analysis:**

Go through the paragraph piece by piece. Ask students to help you identify:

| Part of the Argument | Where Is It in the Paragraph? |
|---|---|
| **Claim** | "Schools should require..." |
| **Reason** | "Students who are involved develop important life skills..." |
| **Evidence** | "According to a 2019 study..." |
| **Counterargument (Acknowledgment)** | "Some people argue, however, that mandatory participation would feel forced..." |
| **Rebuttal** | "But the solution is not to eliminate the requirement..." |
| **Curiosity Moment** | "This is a reasonable concern — nobody wants to resent something they used to love." |

Point to the "curiosity moment" specifically:

*"Right here — this is where the writer stopped trying to win for a second and actually admitted the other side has a point. They got curious about why someone might feel that way. And look what it did: it made the argument MORE convincing, not less. Because now the reader trusts the writer. They think: 'This person actually thought about my concern.' That's the power of curiosity in writing."*

**Sticky Note Activity (2 minutes):**

Give each student two sticky notes — one color for "evidence" and one color for "curiosity moment." Have them quickly label those two sections in their own copy (or just hold up the right color when you point to each part on the board).

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 5: GUIDED WRITING PRACTICE (0:38 – 0:50)

**Purpose:** Students apply the skill independently with scaffolding

**Prompt (write on board):**

> *Think about something you genuinely believe should change — at school, at home, in your community, or in the world. Write an argument paragraph that includes: a clear claim, at least two reasons, one piece of evidence (real or reasonably imagined), and a counterargument acknowledgment that shows you got curious about the other side.*

**Scaffolding Options:**

Provide a sentence frame on the board for students who need support:

- **Claim:** *"I believe ________ because it would ________."*
- **Reason 1:** *"First, ________."*
- **Reason 2:** *"Additionally, ________."*
- **Evidence:** *"According to ________, ________."*
- **Counterargument:** *"Some people might argue that ________. This concern makes sense because ________."*
- **Rebuttal:** *"However, ________."*

**Teacher Circulates:**

Walk the room. Look for students who write a counterargument that sounds dismissive (e.g., "Some people stupidly think..."). Gently redirect:

*"Try to be genuinely curious here. What's the best possible version of the argument against your claim? Write that one."*

Look for students who skip the counterargument entirely. Prompt them:

*"Pause for a second. Who is most likely to disagree with you? What's their biggest concern? Write it down — and actually try to understand it."*

**For advanced students:** Challenge them to write TWO counterarguments and respond to both, or to find a real statistic or quote to use as evidence.

---

### ⏱ SEGMENT 6: CLOSING — THE CURIOUS WRITER'S PLEDGE & REFLECTION (0:50 – 0:55)

**Purpose:** Consolidate learning; connect curiosity as a life habit, not just a writing trick

**Share Out (2 minutes):**

Ask two or three volunteers to read just their counterargument sentence — the moment where they got curious about the other side.

Celebrate the quality of the acknowledgment, not the "rightness" of the opinion.

**The Curious Writer's Pledge (read together — display on board):**

> *As a writer, I will stay curious.*
> *I will ask why someone might disagree with me before I try to convince them.*
> *I will give the other side its best chance — because that makes my argument stronger.*
> *I will write not to win, but to think out loud — honestly, carefully, and with an open mind.*

**Exit Ticket (3 minutes — on a half-sheet or sticky note):**

Students answer ONE of the following:

1. *In your own words: how does curiosity make an argument stronger?*
2. *What's one question you could ask someone who disagrees with your argument from today?*
3. *Finish this sentence: "The most interesting thing about the other side of my argument is ________."*

Collect as students leave.

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For Struggling Writers:**
- Provide the sentence frame scaffold as a printed handout
- Allow oral brainstorming with the teacher or a partner before writing
- Reduce the requirement to one reason and one counterargument acknowledgment
- Use a graphic organizer with labeled boxes for each argument component

**For Advanced Writers:**
- Require two counterarguments with two separate rebuttals
- Ask students to research a real statistic to use as evidence
- Challenge them to write two paragraphs — one from their perspective and one genuinely arguing the other side, then reflect on what they learned

**For ELL Students:**
- Pair with a bilingual partner for the Curiosity Cards activity
- Allow students to write their initial ideas in their home language, then translate key phrases
- Provide a word bank of transition phrases (however, on the other hand, in contrast, despite this)

## ASSESSMENT

**Formative:**
- Teacher observation during Curiosity Cards activity
- Sticky note responses during model analysis
- Exit ticket responses

**Summative (for extended unit):**
- Full argumentative essay using the same curiosity-driven approach, assessed with a rubric aligned to W.7.1 that includes a specific category for **quality of counterargument acknowledgment**

**Suggested Rubric Category — Curiosity in Argument (4-point scale):**

| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| **4** | Counterargument is acknowledged generously and specifically; rebuttal shows genuine engagement with the opposing view |
| **3** | Counterargument is acknowledged clearly; rebuttal addresses it adequately |
| **2** | Counterargument is mentioned but treated dismissively; rebuttal is underdeveloped |
| **1** | No counterargument present, or counterargument misrepresents the opposing view |

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments (55 minutes)**

---

### Objective:
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear argumentative paragraph supporting a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

### Standards Addressed:
- **CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1** — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Student notebooks or writing paper
- Printed handout with a short argumentative writing prompt (provided below)
- Chart paper or projector for displaying examples
- Sticky notes

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**

- **Engage students with a question:**  
  “What does curiosity mean to you? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new or solve a problem?”

- **Brief discussion:**  
  Write student responses on the board. Highlight key ideas such as asking questions, exploring new ideas, and wanting to understand more.

- **Explain:**  
  Curiosity is the desire to learn and understand more. It drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and think deeply — all important skills for writing strong arguments.

---

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (10 minutes)**

- **Explain the writing standard:**  
  “When we write arguments, we make a claim and support it with reasons and evidence. Being curious helps us find good reasons and evidence because we ask questions like ‘Why?’ and ‘How do I know this is true?’”

- **Show an example claim:**  
  “School uniforms should be required.”  
  Ask students: “What questions would a curious person ask about this claim?”  
  Write their questions on the board (e.g., “Do uniforms improve student behavior? What do students think? Are there any downsides?”).

- **Discuss:**  
  How curiosity leads to gathering evidence and considering opposing views.

---

#### 3. **Mini-Lesson on Writing Arguments (10 minutes)**

- **Review the structure of an argumentative paragraph:**  
  - Claim (your main point)  
  - Reason(s) supporting the claim  
  - Evidence (facts, examples, or quotes)  
  - Acknowledge an opposing claim  
  - Refute or explain why your claim still stands

- **Display a simple example paragraph** on the board or projector, highlighting each part.

---

#### 4. **Guided Practice: Writing with Curiosity (15 minutes)**

- **Prompt:**  
  “Should students have more homework or less homework? Write a paragraph arguing your position.”

- **Step 1:**  
  Students brainstorm questions they have about homework (e.g., “Does homework help students learn better? How much homework is too much?”).

- **Step 2:**  
  Students write a claim (e.g., “Students should have less homework.”).

- **Step 3:**  
  Students list at least two reasons and evidence to support their claim.

- **Step 4:**  
  Students write one sentence acknowledging an opposing claim (e.g., “Some people think more homework helps students improve.”).

- **Step 5:**  
  Students write a sentence explaining why their claim is stronger.

- Teacher circulates to assist and prompt curiosity-driven questions.

---

#### 5. **Sharing and Reflection (10 minutes)**

- **Pair-share:**  
  Students read their paragraphs to a partner and discuss how curiosity helped them find reasons and evidence.

- **Class discussion:**  
  Ask a few volunteers to share their claims and how they used curiosity to strengthen their argument.

- **Reflection prompt:**  
  On a sticky note, students write one way curiosity can help them in school or life.

- Collect sticky notes and read a few aloud.

---

### Homework (Optional):
Write a second paragraph on a different topic of their choice, using curiosity to find reasons and evidence for their claim.

---

### Summary:
This lesson helps students see curiosity as a powerful tool for learning and argument writing. By asking questions and seeking evidence, they can write stronger, more convincing arguments that consider multiple viewpoints.
