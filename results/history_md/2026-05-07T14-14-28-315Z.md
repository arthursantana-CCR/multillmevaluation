## Candidate 1
# Should Schools Have Homework?

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

- **Analyze** the key arguments for and against homework by clarifying and organizing evidence from multiple sources.
- **Evaluate** the validity and quality of information used to support claims about homework.
- **Apply** structured reasoning to weigh trade-offs and reach a defensible position on the homework debate.
- **Reflect** on how personal bias and assumptions shaped their thinking throughout the lesson.

---

## Materials

- Two short reading cards (one pro-homework, one anti-homework), each approximately 150 words and drawn from identifiable sources
- Blank T-chart graphic organizer (printed or digital)
- Source credibility checklist (printed handout)
- Whiteboard or shared display screen
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- Exit ticket slips (printed or digital form)

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — 5 Minutes

The teacher writes this question on the board: *"How much homework did you do last night, and did it help you learn?"* Students take two minutes to write a private answer, then share briefly with a partner. The teacher collects two or three responses aloud and notes them on the board without labeling them right or wrong.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** This activity surfaces students' existing assumptions about homework before any new information enters the room. Students begin noticing that their opinion is based on personal experience, which primes metacognitive awareness for the reflection activity later.

**Assessment Note:** Observe whether students simply describe what they did (*Level 1*) or whether they already question whether their experience generalizes to everyone (*Level 2*).

---

### Source Analysis — 10 Minutes

Students receive two reading cards. Card A summarizes a study supporting homework's benefits and names the researcher and publication year. Card B summarizes a study questioning homework's value and does the same. Students use the source credibility checklist to evaluate each card: Who wrote it? When? What evidence is cited? Is the sample size mentioned?

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI2a, CRI2b):** Students practice cross-checking source details rather than accepting claims at face value. The checklist provides a concrete tool for assessing both validity and quality.

**Assessment Note — CRI2a:** A *Level 1* student identifies the author's name but does not question whether the study is recent or representative. A *Level 2* student notes that one study used only 50 students and flags this as a limitation.
**Assessment Note — CRI2b:** A *Level 1* student marks both sources as "good" without differentiation. A *Level 2* student explains why one source is stronger based on sample size, recency, or institutional affiliation.

---

### Clarify and Organize — 10 Minutes

Students complete a T-chart individually. On the left they write the three strongest arguments *for* homework, using specific phrases from the reading cards. On the right they write the three strongest arguments *against*, again citing the cards. They underline any terms they found confusing and write a one-sentence plain-language definition beside each underlined term.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI1a, CRI1b):** Pulling exact phrases and then paraphrasing them builds precise clarification skills. Arranging arguments in a T-chart structure reinforces logical organization.

**Assessment Note — CRI1a:** A *Level 1* student copies sentences directly without restating them. A *Level 2* student rewrites each argument in their own words and explains what makes it relevant.
**Assessment Note — CRI1b:** A *Level 1* student lists points randomly. A *Level 2* student groups related points together and labels categories (e.g., "student well-being," "academic results").

---

### Structured Pair Debate — 15 Minutes

Pairs are assigned opposing sides. Each student has two minutes to present their position using their T-chart, then two minutes to respond to their partner's argument. The teacher circulates and prompts with: *"What is the strongest trade-off you see in your partner's argument?"* and *"Does this apply equally to all students?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI3a, CRI4a):** Students must move beyond listing pros and cons to actively weighing them. Responding to a live argument requires applying logical reasoning in real time and anticipating complexity.

**Assessment Note — CRI3a:** A *Level 1* student names one trade-off without explaining its significance. A *Level 2* student acknowledges that homework may benefit high-resource students more than low-resource students and identifies this as a long-term equity concern.
**Assessment Note — CRI4a:** A *Level 1* student says "homework is bad because I don't like it," relying on personal feeling. A *Level 2* student connects evidence from both sources to construct a conditional claim: "Homework helps when it is meaningful, but harms when it is excessive or unsupported at home."

---

### Reflection Exit Ticket — 15 Minutes

Students write independently for ten minutes responding to three prompts: (1) *What did you believe about homework before this lesson?* (2) *What changed your thinking, and what evidence caused that shift?* (3) *What assumption do you still hold that you cannot yet prove?* Students stick a colored sticky note on the board as they leave — green if their opinion changed, yellow if it stayed the same.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** This activity demands explicit metacognitive work. Students must identify not just *what* they think but *why* their thinking moved or stayed fixed.

**Assessment Note — CRI5a:** A *Level 1* student writes "I changed my mind because of the studies" without specifying which evidence was persuasive. A *Level 2* student names a specific statistic or argument from one reading card, explains why it was convincing, and honestly identifies a remaining bias they recognize but cannot resolve.

## Lesson Outline

### 1. Warm-Up: Quick Write and Share — 7 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write a brief response to the prompt: “What do you think about homework? Is it helpful or not? Why?” After 3 minutes, students share their ideas in pairs.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) by encouraging students to identify and express their initial ideas clearly.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies relevant ideas but with some vagueness (e.g., “Homework is good because it helps.”)  
Level 2: Student clarifies ideas precisely (e.g., “Homework helps me practice skills I learned in class, which makes me feel more confident.”)  

---

### 2. Source Analysis: Reading and Highlighting Arguments — 12 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students read two short articles: one supporting homework and one opposing it. They highlight key points and underline evidence used by the authors.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information), CRI2a (Assessing validity), and CRI2b (Assessing quality) by having students identify and evaluate the information presented.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies main points but does not consistently evaluate source credibility or evidence quality.  
Level 2: Student distinguishes between opinions and facts, notes source reliability, and comments on the strength of evidence.  

---

### 3. Organizing Information: Creating a T-Chart — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
In pairs, students organize the arguments for and against homework into a T-chart, listing pros on one side and cons on the other.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1b (Organizing information) by arranging ideas logically to compare perspectives clearly.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student creates a basic list with some organization but may mix unrelated points.  
Level 2: Student arranges information to strengthen logical flow, grouping related ideas and showing clear distinctions.  

---

### 4. Group Discussion: Weighing Pros and Cons — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Groups of four discuss the pros and cons from their T-charts, considering which arguments are stronger and why. They also discuss possible long-term effects of homework policies.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) by encouraging systematic evaluation and reasoning about trade-offs.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student compares options superficially, stating preferences without detailed reasoning.  
Level 2: Student evaluates trade-offs thoughtfully, considering multiple factors and anticipating consequences.  

---

### 5. Individual Writing: Formulating a Position Statement — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write a short paragraph stating their position on homework, using evidence from the texts and discussion to support their view.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) by requiring precise expression and logical justification.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student states a position with limited or unclear support.  
Level 2: Student clearly articulates a position with well-reasoned, evidence-based support.  

---

### 6. Reflection: Metacognitive Exit Ticket — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students complete an exit ticket answering: “How did your thinking about homework change today? What helped you think differently?” Then, a brief whole-class share of reflections.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes) by prompting metacognitive awareness and adjustment of thinking.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student mentions a change or assumption without deep explanation.  
Level 2: Student demonstrates deep reflection, describing specific strategies or insights that influenced their thinking.
