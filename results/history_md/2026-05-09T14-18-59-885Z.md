## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Across Multiple Forms
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions — and will think critically about *when* and *why* a relationship is proportional.

---

## Materials
- Graph paper or printed coordinate grids
- Colored markers
- "Is It Proportional?" comparison cards (two scenarios per pair)
- Mini whiteboards or scratch paper
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### HOOK — "Which Deal Is Better?" (8 Minutes)

**Activity:** Display two lemonade stand scenarios on the board:
- **Stand A:** 2 cups for $3, 4 cups for $6, 6 cups for $9
- **Stand B:** 2 cups for $3, 4 cups for $7, 6 cups for $12

Ask students: *"Are both stands charging fairly? How do you know?"*

Allow 2 minutes of partner talk, then collect responses on the board without correcting yet.

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
Students must articulate *what* makes a relationship proportional versus not. The teacher prompts: *"What exactly does 'fair' mean mathematically here?"* pushing students from vague language ("the price goes up") to precise language ("the ratio of cups to dollars stays the same").
**Assessment:** Listen for students who use ratio language versus those who describe patterns generally. Target questioning toward students using imprecise terms.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Building the Five Representations (12 Minutes)

**Activity:** Using Stand A's data, the teacher co-constructs all five representations with the class:
1. **Table** — organize the (cups, price) pairs
2. **Graph** — plot points, observe the straight line through the origin
3. **Equation** — derive y = 1.5x from the constant ratio
4. **Diagram** — draw a ratio diagram showing 2:3 scaling
5. **Verbal Description** — "For every cup of lemonade, the cost increases by $1.50"

Then overlay Stand B's graph on the same grid using a different color.

**CRI1b — Organizing Information**
Students record each representation in a structured graphic organizer (provided or drawn in notebooks), moving from concrete (table) to abstract (equation). The deliberate sequence — table → graph → equation → diagram → verbal — scaffolds logical flow.
**Assessment:** Check graphic organizers for completeness and logical ordering. Students who jump to equations without grounding in the table reveal gaps in organizational thinking.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — "Proportional or Not?" Card Sort (12 Minutes)

**Activity:** In pairs, students receive four scenario cards (e.g., distance-time for a constant speed runner, a taxi fare with a $2 booking fee, a recipe doubling, a phone plan with a flat monthly charge). They must classify each as proportional or not, and justify using at least *two* representations.

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
Students must cross-check their classification using the table AND the graph. If a student says "it looks proportional" from the table alone, their partner must verify using the graph (does it pass through the origin?). Pairs must reach consensus before recording.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask: *"How do you know your table check and your graph check agree?"* Students at Level 2 will articulate both tests and explain why both are necessary.

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
Students evaluate whether a verbal description alone is sufficient evidence. The taxi scenario says "price increases with distance" — students must judge whether this description is *enough* to confirm proportionality or whether they need the numbers.
**Assessment:** Look for students who push beyond surface-level descriptions and demand quantitative evidence before concluding.

---

### COLLABORATIVE TASK — "The Better Deal Debate" (10 Minutes)

**Activity:** Groups of three receive a real-world scenario: two streaming services with different pricing structures. They must decide which service is the "better deal" for different types of users (light users vs. heavy users) and represent their reasoning using at least three of the five forms.

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
Students must explicitly argue *for* one service under specific conditions and acknowledge when the other service becomes better. A sentence frame is provided: *"Service A is better when ___ because ___, but Service B becomes better when ___ because ___."*
**Assessment:** Groups that only argue for one service receive a challenge question: *"Is there any user for whom the other service makes more sense?"*

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
Students must connect their graphical or tabular evidence directly to their recommendation. Unsupported claims ("Service A just seems better") are redirected with: *"Show me in your graph where that becomes true."*
**Assessment:** Evaluate group presentations for logical chains: data → pattern → conclusion.

---

### CLOSING — Metacognitive Exit Ticket (8 Minutes)

**Exit Ticket (3 questions):**
1. Write the equation for a proportional relationship where y = 4 when x = 2.
2. Which representation was *easiest* for you to use today, and which was *hardest*?
3. What is one thing you *thought* was true at the start of class that you now think differently about?

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
Questions 2 and 3 explicitly ask students to examine their own reasoning process. Question 3 targets genuine conceptual shift — students who write *"I thought any increasing pattern was proportional, but now I know it has to pass through the origin and have a constant ratio"* demonstrate deep metacognitive awareness.
**Assessment:** Collect exit tickets and sort responses to Question 3 into three piles: no shift noted, surface shift, and deep conceptual shift. Use these to plan the next day's opening.

---

## Wrap-Up (5 Minutes)
Cold-call two or three students to share their Question 3 response aloud. Affirm that changing your thinking based on evidence is exactly what mathematicians do.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-labeled graphic organizers and sentence frames for justifications.
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their *own* proportional and non-proportional scenarios and challenge a partner to classify them.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a real-world scenario: “Imagine you are buying tickets for a concert. Each ticket costs $15. How much would 3 tickets cost? 5 tickets? 10 tickets?”  
- Ask students to share their answers and reasoning.  
- Introduce the concept of proportional relationships: quantities that increase or decrease at the same rate.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Clarifying Information (CRI1a)  
- **Activity:** Students explain in their own words what it means for two quantities to be proportional.  
- **Observation:** Listen for students who identify key information (constant ratio, multiplicative relationship) and clarify ideas precisely.  
- **Assessment:** Note students who can articulate the concept clearly versus those who confuse proportionality with addition or other relationships.

---

**2. Guided Practice: Representing Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Present a table showing the number of tickets and total cost:  
| Tickets | Cost ($) |  
|---------|----------|  
| 1       | 15       |  
| 2       | 30       |  
| 3       | 45       |  
| 4       | 60       |  

- Ask students to:  
  - Identify the constant of proportionality.  
  - Write an equation relating tickets (t) and cost (c).  
  - Plot the points on graph paper and draw the line.  
  - Describe the relationship verbally.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Organizing Information (CRI1b)  
- **Activity:** Students organize data from table to equation, graph, and verbal description.  
- **Observation:** Check if students arrange information logically and consistently (e.g., from table to equation to graph).  
- **Assessment:** Review student work for coherent progression and logical flow.

---

**3. Collaborative Group Activity: Assessing and Comparing Representations (15 minutes)**  
- Divide students into small groups. Provide each group with different representations of proportional relationships (some tables, some graphs, some verbal descriptions).  
- Task: Determine if the relationships are proportional. Justify their answers by cross-checking multiple representations.  
- Groups present their findings to the class.

**Critical Thinking Focus:**  
- Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)  
- Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)  

- **Activity:** Students cross-check data across representations to confirm proportionality and evaluate the accuracy of each representation.  
- **Observation:** Listen for students questioning the validity of data and selecting the most reliable sources (e.g., consistent ratios, straight-line graphs through origin).  
- **Assessment:** Evaluate group presentations for evidence of rigorous evaluation and sophisticated judgment.

---

**4. Individual Task: Weighing Pros and Cons of Representations (10 minutes)**  
- Provide students with two different ways to represent the same proportional relationship (e.g., a table vs. a graph).  
- Prompt: “Which representation helps you understand the relationship better? Why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of each?”  
- Students write a short paragraph explaining their reasoning.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)  
- **Activity:** Students analyze and compare representations, considering clarity, ease of use, and insight provided.  
- **Observation:** Look for students who systematically evaluate trade-offs and consider implications for understanding.  
- **Assessment:** Review written responses for depth of analysis and balanced consideration.

---

**5. Reasoning and Decision-Making Discussion (5 minutes)**  
- Pose a problem: “If the cost per ticket changes to $12, how does that affect the proportional relationship? How would the table, graph, and equation change?”  
- Facilitate a class discussion encouraging students to reason through the impact of changing the constant of proportionality.

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)  
- **Activity:** Students explain how changes affect all representations and justify their conclusions logically.  
- **Observation:** Note students who anticipate complexities (e.g., slope changes, intercept remains zero).  
- **Assessment:** Assess verbal reasoning for clarity and sophistication.

---

**6. Reflection and Metacognition (5 minutes)**  
- Distribute exit tickets with prompts:  
  - “What strategy helped you understand proportional relationships best today?”  
  - “What was challenging, and how did you adjust your thinking?”  
  - “How will you approach similar problems in the future?”  

**Critical Thinking Focus:** Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)  
- **Activity:** Students reflect on their learning process and thinking strategies.  
- **Observation:** Review exit tickets for evidence of metacognitive awareness and strategic adjustment.  
- **Assessment:** Identify students demonstrating deep reflection versus superficial or no reflection.

Question 3 targets genuine conceptual shift — students who write *"I thought any increasing pattern was proportional, but now I know it has to pass through the origin and have a constant ratio"* demonstrate deep metacognitive awareness. Look for specific conceptual hurdles and concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding").
**Assessment:** Collect exit tickets and sort responses to Question 3 into three piles: no shift noted, surface shift, and deep conceptual shift. Use these to plan the next day's opening.

---

### Wrap-Up (5 Minutes)
Cold-call two or three students to share their Question 3 response aloud. Affirm that changing your thinking based on evidence is exactly what mathematicians do.

## Summary of Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration

| Subcompetency | Activity/Moment | Observation/Assessment Method |
|---|---|---|
| CRI1a Clarifying Information | Hook discussion: defining "proportional" from lemonade scenario | Listen for precise ratio language; redirect vague descriptions |
| CRI1b Organizing Information | Direct instruction: building all five representations in sequence | Check graphic organizers for logical, coherent progression |
| CRI2a Assessing Validity | Card sort: cross-checking table and graph; identifying mismatched representation | Ask students to explain how both checks agree or conflict |
| CRI2b Assessing Quality | Card sort: filtering relevant vs. irrelevant information in verbal descriptions | Assess whether students demand quantitative evidence before concluding |
| CRI3a Weighing Pros and Cons | Better Deal Debate: arguing for a service under specific conditions | Listen for acknowledgment of opposing cases; challenge one-sided arguments |
| CRI4a Applying Reasoning | Better Deal Debate: linking data to recommendation | Evaluate logical chains: data → pattern → conclusion |
| CRI5a Reflecting on Thinking | Exit ticket questions 2 and 3 | Sort responses by depth of conceptual shift; use to plan next lesson |

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-labeled graphic organizers, sentence frames for justifications, and partially completed tables or graphs.
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their *own* proportional and non-proportional scenarios and challenge a partner to classify them, or explore what happens to all five representations when the constant of proportionality changes.
