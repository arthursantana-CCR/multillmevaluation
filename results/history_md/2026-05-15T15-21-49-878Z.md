## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Everywhere

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Explain *why* a relationship is or is not proportional using precise mathematical language
- Compare representations and make decisions based on proportional reasoning

---

## Materials
- Printed "Rate Card" scenarios (one per pair)
- Graph paper or mini whiteboards
- Colored markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### OPENING — Hook & Clarification (10 minutes)

**Activity: "Which Deal is Real?"**

Present two verbal scenarios on the board:

> *"At Store A, 3 notebooks cost \$6. At Store B, 5 notebooks cost \$9. Are either of these proportional relationships? How do you know?"*

Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**Teacher Prompt:** "Before we decide anything, let's make sure we understand what proportional actually means. Who can tell me — in their own words — what it means for two quantities to be proportional?"

After student responses, clarify precisely: *A proportional relationship means that as one quantity changes, the other changes at a constant rate — and the ratio between them always stays the same. We call this the constant of proportionality, k.*

**🔍 CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students articulate a definition of proportionality in their own words, then the teacher sharpens it.
- *Assessment:* Listen for whether students use precise language ("constant ratio," "unit rate") versus vague terms ("they go up together"). Observe corrections in real time.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Multiple Representations (12 minutes)

**Teacher-Led Example:**

Using the Store A scenario (3 notebooks for \$6), the teacher builds all five representations together on the board:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| 1 | \$2 |
| 3 | \$6 |
| 5 | \$10 |

Then plots the graph (straight line through the origin), writes the equation *y = 2x*, and connects it verbally: *"For every notebook, the cost increases by exactly \$2."*

**Teacher Prompt:** "Notice how I organized these — table first, then graph, then equation. Why might that order matter? Does one representation make the pattern easier to see than another?"

**🔍 CRI1b — Organizing Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Teacher explicitly sequences representations and asks students to evaluate that sequence.
- *Assessment:* Ask students to write one sentence explaining why they would choose a particular starting representation. Look for logical justification, not just preference.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — Validity Check (13 minutes)

**Activity: "Convince Me" Pairs**

Each pair receives a Rate Card with four pre-made tables — two proportional, two not. Students must:
1. Determine which tables show proportional relationships
2. Justify their answer using at least two representations (graph + equation, or table + verbal)

**Teacher Prompt:** "Don't just say it looks proportional. *Prove it* using the numbers. Would your answer change if I added one more row to the table?"

**🔍 CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students cross-check a table against a graph to confirm proportionality — neither representation alone is accepted as sufficient.
- *Assessment:* Circulate and ask: "How do you know your graph confirms the table?" Students who rely on only one source are prompted to verify.

**🔍 CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* One of the four tables contains a subtle error (an outlier data point). Students must decide whether to trust the full table or flag the inconsistency.
- *Assessment:* Observe whether students accept all data uncritically or question the anomalous value.

---

### COLLABORATIVE TASK — Weighing Representations (12 minutes)

**Activity: "Best Representation Debate"**

Groups of three receive a real-world scenario: *"A car travels at a constant speed. Which representation — table, graph, or equation — would be most useful for a driver planning a road trip? For an engineer designing a speed limit sign? For a student studying for a test?"*

Groups must argue for one representation per audience and explain trade-offs of the others.

**🔍 CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students systematically evaluate which representation serves which purpose, acknowledging limitations of each.
- *Assessment:* Listen for statements like, "A graph is better for the driver because they can see the trend quickly, but an equation is more precise for the engineer." Superficial answers ("graphs look nice") are redirected.

**🔍 CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students must defend their choice using mathematical properties of proportional relationships, not personal preference.
- *Assessment:* Groups share out; peers may challenge reasoning. Teacher notes whether justifications reference *k*, the origin, or constant ratios.

---

### CLOSING — Metacognitive Exit Ticket (8 minutes)

Students independently complete a three-part exit slip:

1. *"Give one example of a proportional relationship using any representation."*
2. *"Which representation was hardest for you today, and why?"*
3. *"If you were confused at any point during this lesson, what did you do to get un-confused?"*

**🔍 CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking**
- *Targeted Activity:* Questions 2 and 3 require students to name their struggle and describe a self-correction strategy.
- *Assessment:* Level 2 responses will name a specific misconception ("I kept forgetting the line must go through the origin") and describe an active fix ("I re-checked the table to reset my thinking"). Level 1 responses will state confusion without resolution.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a partially completed anchor chart with all five representations labeled
- **Extension:** Students create their own "Is It Proportional?" challenge card for a peer

---

## Teacher Notes
Collect exit tickets to inform the next day's warm-up. Flag students who cannot articulate a self-correction strategy — these students may need a brief one-on-one conference before the unit assessment.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction (5 minutes)**
- Begin by asking students: *“What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”*
- Briefly review the concept: Two quantities are proportional if they have a constant ratio.
- Show a simple example on the board: If 3 apples cost $6, how much do 6 apples cost? (Introduce the idea of constant ratio = 2 dollars per apple)
- Explain that today, we will explore how to recognize and represent these proportional relationships in different forms.

---

#### 2. **Direct Instruction (10 minutes)**
- Present a proportional relationship in a table format (e.g., number of pencils vs. total cost).
- Demonstrate how to check if the relationship is proportional by calculating ratios.
- Show how to represent the same relationship as:
  - A graph (plot points and draw a straight line through the origin)
  - An equation (e.g., y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality)
  - A verbal description (e.g., “The total cost is always 2 dollars per pencil.”)
- Use a diagram (e.g., double number line) to visually represent the proportional relationship.

---

#### 3. **Guided Practice (15 minutes)**
- Distribute handouts with different representations of proportional relationships.
- In pairs, students will:
  - Identify if the relationship is proportional.
  - Convert between tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
- Circulate and ask probing questions to clarify understanding.
- Example task: Given a table, write the equation and describe the relationship verbally.

---

#### 4. **Critical Thinking Activity (15 minutes)**
- Present two different proportional relationships side-by-side (e.g., two different pricing plans for phone data).
- Ask students to:
  - Compare and contrast the two plans.
  - Discuss which plan is better under different usage scenarios.
  - Justify their reasoning using tables, graphs, and equations.
- Encourage students to reflect on their thinking process: *“How did you decide which plan is better? What information helped you?”*

---

#### 5. **Closure and Reflection (5 minutes)**
- Summarize key points about recognizing and representing proportional relationships.
- Ask students to write an exit ticket answering: *“How can you tell if two quantities are proportional? Give an example.”*
- Collect exit tickets to assess understanding.

---

### Addressing CCR Critical Thinking Subcompetencies

---

#### 1. **Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**
- **Activity:** During Guided Practice, students clarify the meaning of proportional relationships by converting between different representations (table, graph, equation, verbal).
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students accurately explaining the constant ratio and how it appears in each form. For example, a student might say, “The ratio stays the same in the table, and the graph is a straight line through the origin, which means it’s proportional.”
- **Level Target:** 2 — Students clarify ideas precisely to support deeper understanding.

---

#### 2. **Organizing Information (CRI1b)**
- **Activity:** In the Critical Thinking Activity, students organize information from two different plans into tables and graphs to compare them.
- **Observation/Assessment:** Assess how well students arrange data logically to support their comparison. For example, a student might create a clear table and graph side-by-side to show differences in cost per unit.
- **Level Target:** 2 — Students arrange information to strengthen logical flow.

---

#### 3. **Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)**
- **Activity:** When comparing the two plans, students evaluate the validity of the data given (e.g., checking if the rates are consistent or if there are hidden fees).
- **Observation/Assessment:** Note if students question the data or assumptions, such as “Is the rate really constant for all usage amounts?” or “Does the graph match the table data?”
- **Level Target:** 1 — Students identify information sources and question consistency, though may not rigorously cross-check multiple sources in this lesson.

---

#### 4. **Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**
- **Activity:** Students decide which representation (table, graph, equation, verbal) best communicates the proportional relationship for a given scenario.
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students justifying their choice based on clarity and relevance, e.g., “The graph shows the trend clearly, so it’s easier to understand than the table.”
- **Level Target:** 1 — Students use somewhat relevant information and begin to judge quality.

---

#### 5. **Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**
- **Activity:** In the Critical Thinking Activity, students weigh pros and cons of two pricing plans, considering different usage levels.
- **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students systematically evaluate trade-offs, such as “Plan A is cheaper for low usage, but Plan B is better for heavy users.”
- **Level Target:** 1 — Students compare options with minimal or superficial analysis appropriate for middle school level.

---

#### 6. **Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**
- **Activity:** Students justify their choice of the better plan using proportional reasoning and evidence from tables and graphs.
- **Observation/Assessment:** Assess if students provide logical explanations, e.g., “Because the cost per unit is constant and lower in Plan B for my usage, I choose Plan B.”
- **Level Target:** 1 — Students provide reasoning that is partially logical and clear.

---

#### 7. **Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)**

- **Observation/Assessment:** Review exit tickets or listen to student discussions for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as “I first looked at the ratio, then checked the graph to make sure it was a straight line.”
- **Level Target:** 1 — Students mention personal reasoning or assumptions without deep reflection.

- *Targeted Activity:* Students must defend their representation choices using mathematical properties — constant ratio, the equation form y = kx, the line passing through the origin — not personal preference.
- *Assessment:* Groups share out; peers may challenge reasoning. Teacher notes whether justifications reference the constant of proportionality *k*, the origin, or constant ratios. Look for explanations such as: "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points, and the graph passes through the origin."

---

### CLOSING — Metacognitive Exit Ticket (8 minutes)

Students independently complete a three-part exit slip:

1. *"A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not using at least two representations."*
2. *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal) was hardest for you today, and why?"*
3. *"If you were confused at any point during this lesson, what did you do to get un-confused? If you weren't confused, what strategy helped you most?"*

**🔍 CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking**
- *Targeted Activity:* Questions 2 and 3 require students to name their specific struggle and describe a self-correction strategy, moving beyond simply labeling something "hard."
- *Assessment:* Level 2 responses will identify a precise misconception ("I kept forgetting the line must pass through the origin, not just be straight") and describe an active correction strategy ("I went back to the table and recalculated the ratios to reset my thinking"). Level 1 responses will state confusion without resolution. Level -1 responses repeat the same error without noticing it. Collect exit tickets to inform the next day's warm-up and flag students who cannot articulate any self-correction strategy for a brief follow-up conference.

- **Extension:** Students create their own "Is It Proportional?" challenge card — including one intentional error — for a peer to solve and critique

## Lesson Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions

## Materials
- Printed "Rate Card" scenario handouts (one per pair/group)
- Graph paper or mini whiteboards
- Colored markers and rulers
- Exit ticket slips

## Lesson Outline

---

### OPENING — Hook & Clarification (10 minutes)

**Activity: "Which Deal is Real?"**

Present two verbal scenarios on the board:

> *"At Store A, 3 notebooks cost $6. At Store B, 5 notebooks cost $9. Are either of these proportional relationships? How do you know?"*

Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**Teacher Prompt:** "Before we decide anything, let's make sure we understand what proportional actually means. Who can tell me — in their own words — what it means for two quantities to be proportional?"

After student responses, clarify precisely: *A proportional relationship means that as one quantity changes, the other changes at a constant rate — and the ratio between them always stays the same. We call this the constant of proportionality, k.*

**🔍 CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students articulate a definition of proportionality in their own words in pairs, then the teacher sharpens it with the whole class. Students are asked to refine their definitions to include precise language such as "constant ratio" or "unit rate."
- *Assessment:* Listen for whether students use precise language ("constant ratio," "unit rate") versus vague terms ("they go up together"). Observe whether students self-correct during discussion. Level 2 responses will include both the concept of constant ratio and the implication for all pairs of values.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Multiple Representations (12 minutes)

**Teacher-Led Example:**

Using the Store A scenario (3 notebooks for $6), the teacher builds all five representations together on the board:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| 1 | $2 |
| 3 | $6 |
| 5 | $10 |

Then plots the graph (straight line through the origin), writes the equation *y = 2x*, connects it to a double number line diagram, and restates it verbally: *"For every notebook, the cost increases by exactly $2."*

**Teacher Prompt:** "Notice how I organized these — table first, then graph, then equation. Why might that order matter? Does one representation make the pattern easier to see than another? What do all of these representations have in common?"

**🔍 CRI1b — Organizing Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* The teacher explicitly sequences representations (table → graph → equation → diagram → verbal) and asks students to evaluate that sequence and record it in an organized manner in their notes.
- *Assessment:* Ask students to write one sentence explaining why they would choose a particular starting representation. Look for logical justification referencing mathematical properties (e.g., "I'd start with the table because I can see the ratio immediately"), not just personal preference.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — Validity and Quality Check (13 minutes)

**Activity: "Convince Me" Pairs**

Each pair receives a Rate Card with four pre-made scenarios — two proportional, two not. Students must:
1. Determine which relationships are proportional
2. Justify their answer using at least two representations (graph + equation, or table + verbal)

One scenario includes a table paired with a *mismatched* graph (the graph does not accurately represent the table's data). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid.

A second scenario contains a verbal description with irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven runs at 350 degrees."). Students must identify and filter out the irrelevant details.

**Teacher Prompt:** "Don't just say it looks proportional. *Prove it* using the numbers. Would your answer change if I added one more row to the table? How do you know the graph and the table are telling the same story?"

**🔍 CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students cross-check the mismatched table and graph to confirm or deny proportionality — neither representation alone is accepted as sufficient proof.
- *Assessment:* Circulate and ask: "How do you know your graph confirms the table?" Listen for responses such as "The points on this graph don't match the (x, y) pairs in our table" or "The graph doesn't pass through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio." Students relying on only one representation are prompted to verify using a second.

**🔍 CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students work through the scenario with irrelevant information, identifying what matters and articulating why certain data points are excluded from their proportionality analysis.
- *Assessment:* Observe whether students accept all information uncritically or actively filter it. Ask groups to explain why certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's irrelevant to the proportional relationship").

---

### COLLABORATIVE TASK — Weighing Representations & Sound Reasoning (12 minutes)

**Activity: "Best Representation Debate"**

Groups of three receive a real-world scenario: *"A car travels at a constant speed of 60 miles per hour. Which representation — table, graph, or equation — would be most useful for a driver planning a road trip? For an engineer designing a speed limit sign? For a student studying for a test?"*

Groups must argue for one representation per audience, explain trade-offs of the others, and defend their choices using mathematical properties of proportional relationships.

**🔍 CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons**
- *Targeted Activity:* Students systematically evaluate which representation serves which purpose, acknowledging limitations of each alternative rather than simply picking a favorite.
- *Assessment:* Listen for statements like, "A graph is better for the driver because they can see the trend quickly and estimate values, but an equation is more precise for the engineer who needs exact calculations." Superficial answers ("graphs look nice") are redirected with the prompt: "What can the equation do that the graph cannot?"

- *Assessment:* Groups share out; peers may challenge reasoning. Teacher notes whether justifications reference the constant of proportionality *k*, the origin, or constant ratios. Look for explanations such as: "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points, and the graph passes through the origin."
