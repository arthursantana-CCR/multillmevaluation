## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout with 4 scenarios (lemonade recipes, phone plans, distance/time, pizza pricing)
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Display two lemonade recipes on the board:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups sugar : 5 cups water
- **Recipe B:** 4 cups sugar : 9 cups water

Ask: "If we double Recipe A, do we get Recipe B? Which recipe represents a proportional scaling?"

Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Teacher prompts: *"Clarify what 'proportional' means in your own words. What information do we need to identify?"*

**Assessment for CRI1a:** Listen for students precisely defining proportional relationships (constant ratio). Note students who clarify ideas like "the ratio of sugar to water must stay the same" vs. vague responses. Record on a quick-check clipboard (Level 1 vs. Level 2).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Model how to represent the proportional relationship y = 3x (cost of apples at $3 each) across **five representations**:
1. **Table** (x: 1,2,3,4; y: 3,6,9,12)
2. **Graph** (straight line through origin)
3. **Equation** (y = 3x)
4. **Diagram** (tape diagram showing groups)
5. **Verbal** ("Cost equals three times the number of apples")

Emphasize the logical flow: identify the constant of proportionality (k), check (0,0), verify constant ratio.

**Assessment for CRI1b:** During guided notes, check whether students organize the five representations into a coherent graphic organizer. Collect notebooks at end of class — Level 2 students arrange representations to show clear connections (e.g., arrows linking k across all forms).

---

### 3. Collaborative Investigation (15 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity & Quality*
Distribute the **"Is It Proportional?" Challenge** handout with 4 scenarios:
- **Phone Plan A:** $10 flat fee + $0.05/min
- **Phone Plan B:** $0.15/min
- **Distance Table:** Car travels 60 mi in 1 hr, 120 mi in 2 hr, 200 mi in 3 hr
- **Pizza Pricing:** A 10-inch pizza is $8; a 14-inch pizza is $11.20

In pairs, students must determine which scenarios are proportional, **citing evidence from multiple representations** (table AND equation AND graph). They must justify rejections too.

**Assessment for CRI2a:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you know your answer is correct? Can you verify it another way?"* Look for students cross-checking by computing ratios in a table AND testing the equation form y = kx. Level 2 students will verify with at least two methods.

**Assessment for CRI2b:** Evaluate whether students use the most relevant data points (e.g., checking multiple ratios, not just one) when claiming proportionality.

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
Pose a real-world dilemma: *"Your class is fundraising. Vendor X charges $2 per cupcake (no setup fee). Vendor Y charges $50 setup + $1.25 per cupcake. Which is the better deal for selling 30 cupcakes? 100 cupcakes? Which represents a proportional relationship?"*

Students work individually for 5 minutes, then discuss in groups of 4.

**Assessment for CRI3a (Weighing pros/cons):** Collect written responses. Level 2 students systematically compare both vendors at multiple quantities, recognizing the trade-off (Vendor X is proportional but costlier at scale; Vendor Y is non-proportional but better long-term). Level 1 students compare only one quantity.

**Assessment for CRI4a (Sound reasoning):** Look for logical reasoning chains: "Because Vendor Y has a setup fee, the graph won't pass through the origin, so it's NOT proportional — but it may still be the better choice." Level 2 anticipates that "better deal" and "proportional" are different questions.

---

### 5. Exit Ticket & Reflection (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*
Students complete a two-part exit ticket:

**Part A (Math):** Given the table (x: 2,4,6; y: 5,10,15), write the equation, identify k, and sketch the graph.

**Part B (Reflection):** 
- *"What was one mistake or misconception you had today, and how did you correct your thinking?"*
- *"Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, words) helps YOU best understand proportionality, and why?"*

**Assessment for CRI5a:** Read reflections. Level 2 responses show metacognitive awareness — e.g., "I first thought any straight line was proportional, but I realized it must pass through (0,0). Next time I'll check the y-intercept first." Level 1 responses mention reasoning without genuine adjustment. Level -1 shows no awareness of errors.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence frames.
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one proportional relationship at home (recipe, price, etc.) and represent it in all five forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportionality in the Real World: Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will recognize, represent, and analyze proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions. Students will critically evaluate data sources, organize mathematical evidence, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook & Source Evaluation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: "We are planning a school pancake breakfast. We need to know the proportional relationship between cups of pancake mix and the number of pancakes produced to scale our recipe." 
The teacher provides two information sources: 
1. A screenshot of an anonymous social media comment claiming "1 cup makes 5 pancakes, 2 cups make 12 pancakes, 3 cups make 20 pancakes."
2. An official printout from a culinary institute’s nutritional database stating a strict ratio: "1 cup yields exactly 8 pancakes."

Students work in pairs to evaluate these sources before doing any math. They must cross-check the social media claim against the culinary database to see if the social media numbers form a proportional relationship.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students discuss and select the culinary database over the social media comment, demonstrating sophisticated judgment in choosing a high-quality, authoritative source to build their mathematical model.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher listens to pair discussions and assesses whether students explicitly identify the culinary database as more reliable for establishing a constant mathematical ratio, noting this on a formative assessment checklist.

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students rigorously cross-check the social media claim by testing its internal validity. They divide the pancakes by cups (5/1, 12/2, 20/3) to prove the source is mathematically invalid because it lacks a constant of proportionality.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reviews students' scratchpad calculations to ensure they used division to cross-check the data points, successfully proving the first source's invalidity.

---

### **Part 2: Organizing and Clarifying Data (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Using the valid data (1 cup = 8 pancakes), students are given a scrambled list of bulk ingredient purchases (e.g., "400 pancakes," "10 cups," "24 pancakes," "50 cups"). They must translate this verbal and scattered data into a clear, structured format. 

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students clarify the verbal descriptions by precisely translating them into an algebraic equation ($y = 8x$, where $y$ is pancakes and $x$ is cups). This clarifies the core mathematical idea to support deeper understanding of the relationship.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses the accuracy of the equations written on students' whiteboards, ensuring they have correctly identified the independent and dependent variables.

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students arrange the scattered data points into a logical, ascending T-table, and then plot these points on a coordinate plane, drawing a straight line through the origin.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher collects the graph paper to observe if the table is structured sequentially and if the graph strengthens the logical flow of the data (straight line, passing through 0,0).

---

### **Part 3: The Bulk Discount Dilemma (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a twist: "The grocery store sells syrup. 1 bottle is $3. 2 bottles are $6. But if you buy a case of 10 bottles, it costs $25." Students must decide if the pricing is proportional and choose the best way to present this information to the school principal to justify their budget.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students must determine that the bulk pricing breaks the proportional relationship. They demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by explaining that while $3/1$ and $6/2$ share a unit rate of $3$, $25/10$ has a unit rate of $2.50$. They conclude the relationship is non-proportional.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher evaluates the students' written justifications, looking for explicit use of unit rate comparisons to support their final decision.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students debate whether to show the principal a table, a graph, or an equation to prove the syrup pricing is not proportional. They systematically evaluate trade-offs (e.g., "A graph visually shows the line bending at 10 bottles, which is easy to see, but a table shows the exact dollar amounts we will save").
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher facilitates a whole-class discussion, tracking which students successfully articulate the long-term implications and trade-offs of using visual (graph) versus numerical (table) representations for a specific audience.

---

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an Exit Ticket titled "Thinking About My Thinking." They must write a short paragraph answering: "How did your understanding of what makes a relationship proportional change from the beginning of the lesson to now? Describe a moment today where you caught a mistake in your own thinking or had to adjust your strategy."

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   *Activity/Moment:* Students reflect on their initial assumptions (e.g., assuming all store pricing is proportional) and demonstrate deep metacognitive awareness by explaining how graphing the data or finding the unit rate forced them to strategically adjust their mathematical thinking.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reads the Exit Tickets, scoring them based on the depth of self-awareness. High scores are given to students who explicitly name the strategy (like cross-checking or graphing) that helped them correct a misconception about proportionality.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** Middle School Math / Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Objective
Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Materials
- Whiteboard/projector  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Student worksheet  
- Exit ticket  
- Colored pencils  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 5 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Students write individually:  
1. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. How do you know?

Briefly discuss. Guide students to notice that in Table A, the ratio \( \frac{y}{x} \) is always 3, while in Table B, the ratio changes.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify relevant information in the tables and explain what makes one relationship proportional.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying that “proportional” means the same multiplier connects x to y every time. Student evidence may include statements like “Each y-value is 3 times the x-value.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain that a proportional relationship has three key features:

1. The ratio \( \frac{y}{x} \) is constant.  
2. The equation can be written as \( y = kx \), where \( k \) is the constant of proportionality.  
3. The graph is a straight line through the origin \((0,0)\).

Use Table A to model:

\[
\frac{3}{1}=3,\quad \frac{6}{2}=3,\quad \frac{9}{3}=3,\quad \frac{12}{4}=3
\]

So \( k = 3 \), and the equation is:

\[
y = 3x
\]

On the board, graph the points \((1,3), (2,6), (3,9), (4,12)\). Extend the line through \((0,0)\).

Then give a verbal description:  
“Each item costs $3.”

Show how all representations match: table, graph, equation, and verbal description.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship into a table, equation, graph, and sentence.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check whether students arrange information logically and consistently across representations. Look for correct labeling, ordered pairs, and an equation that matches the table.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Smoothie Recipe Problem — 15 minutes
Give students this scenario:

“A smoothie recipe uses 2 cups of fruit for every 5 cups of yogurt.”

Ask students to complete multiple representations.

#### A. Table

| Fruit cups, x | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---------------|---|---|---|---|
| Yogurt cups, y | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

#### B. Equation
Students identify:

\[
k = \frac{5}{2}
\]

So:

\[
y = \frac{5}{2}x
\]

#### C. Graph
Students graph \((2,5), (4,10), (6,15), (8,20)\), including the origin.

#### D. Diagram
Students draw repeated groups: for every 2 fruit cups, there are 5 yogurt cups.

#### E. Verbal Description
Students write:  
“The amount of yogurt is always \( \frac{5}{2} \) times the amount of fruit.”

Discuss: How can we check that all representations are telling the same story?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description to confirm they all represent the same ratio.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to explain how they verified their answer. Look for evidence such as “I checked that every point on the graph matches the table” or “The equation gives the same y-values as the table.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence that the relationship is proportional.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students selecting high-quality mathematical evidence, such as constant ratios or a graph through the origin, rather than relying only on appearance.

---

### 4. Partner Task: Which Representation Is Most Useful? — 10 minutes
Students work in pairs. Give them this problem:

“A car travels 180 miles in 3 hours at a constant speed.”

Students must create:
- A table
- A graph
- An equation
- A verbal description

Then they answer:

1. What is the constant of proportionality?  
2. Which representation is best for finding the distance after 7 hours?  
3. Which representation is best for seeing whether the relationship is proportional?  
4. Explain the pros and cons of using a table, graph, and equation.

Expected equation:

\[
d = 60t
\]

Students may say the equation is best for finding 7 hours because substituting \(t = 7\) is efficient:

\[
d = 60(7)=420
\]

They may say the graph is useful for seeing a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare tables, graphs, and equations as tools for solving different problems.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students explaining trade-offs, such as “A graph helps me see the pattern, but an equation is faster for large numbers.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose the best representation to answer specific questions and justify their choice.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate whether students’ decisions are supported by mathematical reasoning, not preference alone. Strong reasoning includes references to constant rate, substitution, or graph behavior.

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 8 minutes
Students complete three problems independently.

#### Problem 1
A printer prints 24 pages in 4 minutes.  
- Find the constant of proportionality.  
- Write an equation.  
- Create a table for 1, 2, 3, and 4 minutes.

Expected:

\[
k = 6,\quad p = 6m
\]

#### Problem 2
Determine whether the table is proportional.

| x | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 5 | 10 | 14 | 20 |

Expected: Not proportional because the ratios are not constant.

#### Problem 3
A graph shows a straight line passing through \((0,0)\), \((2,7)\), and \((4,14)\).  
- Is it proportional?  
- Write the equation.

Expected:

\[
y = \frac{7}{2}x
\]

Circulate and ask guiding questions:
- “What is staying constant?”
- “How can you prove it?”
- “Does your equation match the table or graph?”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 7 minutes
Students answer:

1. What are two ways to tell if a relationship is proportional?  
2. Which representation do you understand best: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description? Why?  
3. Describe one mistake you made or almost made today, and how you corrected your thinking.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on their own reasoning and identify how their understanding changed.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students naming a strategy or correction, such as “I first thought the table was proportional because y increased, but then I checked the ratios.”

**Assessment for CRI1b:** Collect guided-notes graphic organizers. Level 2 students arrange the five representations with arrows linking k across forms; Level 1 lists them without showing connections.

### 3. Source Evaluation Task (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Validity & Quality*
Present two sources for a pancake breakfast fundraiser:
1. An anonymous social media post: "1 cup makes 5 pancakes, 2 cups make 12, 3 cups make 20."
2. A culinary institute database: "1 cup yields exactly 8 pancakes."

In pairs, students evaluate both sources *before* using them. They cross-check the social media data by computing ratios (5/1, 12/2, 20/3) to test whether it forms a true proportional relationship.

**Assessment for CRI2a:** Review scratchwork showing students divided to test for a constant ratio, demonstrating the social media source is mathematically invalid. Level 2 students verify with multiple ratio checks.

**Assessment for CRI2b:** Listen for students explicitly identifying the culinary database as the authoritative source. Level 2 students articulate *why* one source is higher quality (peer-reviewed, expert-authored) rather than just picking one.

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
Pose a real dilemma: *"Your class is fundraising. Vendor X charges $2 per cupcake (no setup fee). Vendor Y charges $50 setup + $1.25 per cupcake. Which is the better deal for 30 cupcakes? 100 cupcakes? Which represents a proportional relationship?"*

Students work individually 5 minutes, then discuss in groups of 4. They must build a table, sketch a graph, and write an equation for each vendor.

**Assessment for CRI3a:** Collect written responses. Level 2 students systematically compare both vendors at multiple quantities, recognizing trade-offs (Vendor X is proportional but costlier at scale; Vendor Y is non-proportional but better long-term). Level 1 students compare only one quantity.

**Assessment for CRI4a:** Look for logical chains: "Because Vendor Y has a setup fee, the graph won't pass through the origin, so it's NOT proportional — but it may still be the better choice." Level 2 students anticipate that "better deal" and "proportional" are distinct questions.

---

### 5. Exit Ticket & Reflection (13 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*

**Part A (Math, 7 min):** Given the table (x: 2,4,6; y: 5,10,15), write the equation, identify k, and sketch the graph.

**Part B (Reflection, 6 min):** 
- *"How did your understanding of 'proportional' change from the beginning of the lesson to now?"*
- *"Describe one mistake you caught in your own thinking today and how you corrected it."*
- *"Which representation helps YOU best understand proportionality, and why?"*

**Assessment for CRI5a:** Read reflections. Level 2 responses show metacognitive awareness — e.g., "I first thought any straight line was proportional, but I realized it must pass through (0,0). Next time I'll check the y-intercept first." Level 1 mentions reasoning without genuine adjustment. Level -1 shows no awareness of errors.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence frames.
- **Extension:** Challenge students to design a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one proportional relationship at home (recipe, price, distance) and represent it in all five forms.
