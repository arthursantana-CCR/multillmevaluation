## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the importance of curiosity in learning and be able to write a well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Chart paper or projector  
- Handout with a short argumentative writing prompt  
- Graphic organizer for argument writing (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)  
- Sticky notes or index cards  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Hook:** Ask students, “What does curiosity mean to you?” Write their responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain curiosity as a desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas.  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous person (e.g., Marie Curie, Albert Einstein) whose curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize how curiosity drives us to ask questions and seek evidence, which is essential for strong arguments.

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us explore different sides of an issue, find evidence, and understand opposing views.  
- Introduce the standard: writing arguments with clear reasons, evidence, and acknowledging opposing claims.

#### 3. **Review Argument Writing Structure (10 minutes)**  
- Present the parts of an argument:  
  - **Claim:** Your main point or opinion  
  - **Reasons:** Why you believe your claim  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or data supporting your reasons  
  - **Counterclaim:** An opposing viewpoint  
  - **Rebuttal:** Your response to the counterclaim  
- Show a sample paragraph on the board or projector. Read it aloud and identify each part together.

#### 4. **Guided Practice: Exploring Curiosity Through Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute a writing prompt related to curiosity, for example:  
  *“Curiosity is more important than intelligence for success. Do you agree or disagree? Write an argument supporting your claim.”*  
- Hand out graphic organizers.  
- As a class, brainstorm reasons and evidence for both sides (agree/disagree). Write ideas on the board.  
- Model filling out the graphic organizer with one reason, evidence, a counterclaim, and a rebuttal.

#### 5. **Independent Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Students write their own argumentative paragraph using the graphic organizer.  
- Encourage them to use curiosity to think deeply about their reasons and evidence, and to consider opposing views fairly.

#### 6. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to share their paragraphs or key points.  
- Discuss how curiosity helped them think about different perspectives and find evidence.  
- Close by reinforcing that curiosity is a powerful tool for learning and writing strong arguments.

---

### Homework/Extension (Optional):  
Ask students to observe something they are curious about at home or school and write down questions they have. Then, write a short argument about why curiosity is important in everyday life, using reasons and evidence.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with the practice of argumentative writing, meeting the CCSS writing standard while engaging students in meaningful, thoughtful learning.
