## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Clarify and organize arguments for and against homework
- Evaluate the reliability of sources supporting each position
- Weigh trade-offs and apply reasoned judgment to form a defensible stance
- Reflect on how their thinking evolved during the lesson

---

## Materials
- Printed source cards (2–3 short excerpts: one research study supporting homework, one opposing it, one anecdotal/unreliable blog post)
- Sticky notes (two colors)
- Whiteboard or chart paper
- Argument organizer worksheet
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### **Warm-Up — 5 Minutes**

**Quick Write Prompt:** *"Do you think homework is useful? Write three sentences explaining your current opinion."*

Students write independently without discussing with peers. Collect these — they will be revisited at the end of the lesson.

**Purpose:** Establishes a baseline for reflection (CRI5a) and activates prior knowledge.

---

### **Activity 1: Clarifying the Question — 10 Minutes** *(CRI1a)*

**What happens:** The teacher writes on the board: *"Should schools have homework?"* and asks students: *"What do we actually need to know to answer this question fairly?"*

Students call out questions — the teacher records them. Examples might include: *What kind of homework? For what age group? How much? Is it graded?*

The teacher then guides students to narrow the question to: *"Should 7th graders have nightly homework assignments?"* — making the claim precise enough to argue.

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students practice moving from a vague question to a precisely scoped claim. The teacher models how imprecise framing leads to weak arguments, reinforcing that clarifying the terms of a debate is the first step in sound reasoning.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who distinguish between types or amounts of homework versus those who treat "homework" as one undifferentiated concept. Students reaching Level 2 will spontaneously add conditions and qualifications to their questions.

---

### **Activity 2: Organizing Arguments — 10 Minutes** *(CRI1b)*

**What happens:** Students receive the argument organizer worksheet with two columns: *"Arguments FOR homework"* and *"Arguments AGAINST homework."* Working in pairs, they brainstorm at least three points per side using their own knowledge.

Pairs then share one point each. The teacher writes responses on the board, deliberately placing similar ideas together and asking: *"Where does this idea belong? Is it really a new point, or is it connected to something already listed?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI1b (Organizing Information):** Students practice grouping related claims, distinguishing between evidence and opinion, and building a logical structure before writing begins.

**Assessment:** Review worksheets for logical grouping. Students at Level 2 will categorize arguments by theme (e.g., academic impact, fairness, student wellbeing) rather than listing points randomly.

---

### **Activity 3: Source Evaluation Station — 10 Minutes** *(CRI2a and CRI2b)*

**What happens:** Each pair receives three source cards:
- **Source A:** A summary of a peer-reviewed study (Duke University, Harris Cooper) showing modest benefits of homework for older students
- **Source B:** A summary of research (Stanford/Denise Pope) linking excessive homework to student stress
- **Source C:** A blog post titled *"Homework is evil — a parent's rant"* with no citations

Students answer three questions per source: *Who wrote this? What evidence do they use? Should we trust it to support an argument?*

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI2a & CRI2b (Assessing Validity and Quality):** Students must distinguish between peer-reviewed research, institutional studies, and opinion-based content. They practice recognizing that two credible sources can reach different conclusions — and that this complexity is normal in real debates.

**Assessment:** Watch for students who reject Source C immediately because it lacks evidence versus those who accept it because it agrees with their opinion. Students at Level 2 will note that Sources A and B can both be used, but with specific scope conditions attached.

---

### **Activity 4: Structured Debate Prep — 12 Minutes** *(CRI3a and CRI4a)*

**What happens:** Students are assigned a position (FOR or AGAINST) regardless of personal opinion. They have 6 minutes to build a three-point argument using only Sources A and B. Then, for 6 minutes, they must write the strongest possible counterargument against their own position.

The teacher prompts: *"If you were on the other side, what would be the hardest point to argue against?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** By requiring students to argue both sides, the lesson prevents one-sided reasoning and forces genuine engagement with trade-offs, including long-term implications like student wellbeing versus academic preparation.

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Students must connect evidence from sources to claims logically, avoiding unsupported assertions. The teacher circulates and challenges weak reasoning with: *"What's your evidence for that?"* or *"Does your source actually say that?"*

**Assessment:** Collect argument worksheets. Look for students who use source-backed claims with logical connectors ("because," "therefore," "however") versus those who rely on personal preference or exaggeration.

---

### **Activity 5: Metacognitive Reflection — 8 Minutes** *(CRI5a)*

**What happens:** Students retrieve their original warm-up responses. They read what they wrote five minutes into class and then respond to three reflection prompts on their exit ticket:

1. *"Has your opinion changed? Why or why not?"*
2. *"What was the weakest part of your original argument?"*
3. *"What would you need to find out to feel more confident in your position?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):** Comparing their initial gut response to their evidence-informed position makes the thinking process visible. Students are not just reflecting on the topic — they are reflecting on *how* their reasoning changed, which is the core of metacognition.

**Assessment:** Exit tickets scored on depth of reflection. Level 2 responses will identify a specific reasoning error they made and explain what new information or framework changed their thinking. Level 1 responses will note a change in opinion without explaining why.

---

### **Closing — 3 Minutes**

The teacher brings the class together and asks: *"Did using sources change how confident you felt in your argument?"* Brief whole-class share-out. The teacher names each critical thinking move practiced today and connects it to real-world decision-making: *"This is exactly how journalists, lawyers, and scientists build arguments."*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Debating the role of homework in schools

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify key arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support different perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of various information sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of different approaches to homework.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form a preliminary stance on the topic.
*   Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the discussion.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (short articles/summaries presenting arguments for and against homework, with varied source types – e.g., excerpt from an educational research summary, a school district's rationale, a student blog post, a parent's opinion piece).
*   Handout 2: T-chart graphic organizer
*   Exit Tickets
*   Markers/pens

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, everyone! Today, we're going to tackle a topic that affects all of us: homework. How many of you love homework? How many of you wish it didn't exist? (Allow for a quick show of hands and brief, informal comments). It's clear there are strong feelings on both sides. Today, we're going to explore this question: 'Should schools have homework?' and use our critical thinking skills to understand the different perspectives."

**II. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) & Initial Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1a: Clarifying information):** Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
    *   **Teacher:** "You'll find several short texts here, each presenting different viewpoints or facts about homework. Your first task is to read through these texts individually. As you read, I want you to do two things:
        1.  **Highlight** the main arguments or key pieces of information presented in each text.
        2.  **Circle any words or phrases** you don't understand, and try to define them using context or by asking a classmate."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1a):** Circulate around the room. Observe students' highlighting – are they identifying the core claims? Listen to their discussions about unfamiliar vocabulary. Are they attempting to clarify ideas precisely? I will look for evidence that students are accurately identifying and defining key terms and arguments, rather than just random words or superficial details. For example, if a student highlights "Homework reinforces classroom learning" and can explain what "reinforces" means in this context, they are clarifying information precisely.

**III. Organizing Information (CRI1b) & Assessing Validity/Quality (CRI2a, CRI2b) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1b: Organizing information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've read and highlighted, let's organize this information. In your small groups (3-4 students), use Handout 2, the T-chart, to list the 'Pros of Homework' on one side and the 'Cons of Homework' on the other. Make sure to draw your points directly from the texts."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1b):** Review students' T-charts. Are the points clearly categorized? Is the information presented in a structured and consistent manner? For example, are all "pros" on one side and "cons" on the other, without mixing?
*   **Activity (CRI2a: Assessing validity of information & CRI2b: Assessing quality of information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "As you're filling out your T-chart, I want you to also think critically about *where* this information is coming from. Look at the source listed for each text on Handout 1. Discuss in your groups:
        *   'How reliable do you think this source is?' (CRI2a)
        *   'Does this source provide high-quality information, or is it more opinion-based?' (CRI2b)
        *   'Which pieces of evidence from the texts do you think are the *strongest* to support either side, and why?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI2a & CRI2b):** Listen to group discussions. Do students question the authority of a blog post versus a research summary? Do they identify potential biases? Are they able to articulate *why* certain information is more credible or relevant than others? I will look for students who can explain, for instance, "The study from the Department of Education is high-quality because it's based on research data, not just one person's feelings," demonstrating sophisticated judgment.

**IV. Weighing Pros & Cons & Applying Reasoning (CRI3a, CRI4a) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've organized and evaluated the information, let's delve deeper. In your groups, discuss the following:
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools completely eliminated homework?'
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools assigned a lot more homework?'
        *   'Consider the perspectives of different stakeholders: students, teachers, parents. How might each group weigh these pros and cons differently?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI3a):** Circulate and listen to group discussions. Are students moving beyond simple listing to actively compare and evaluate the trade-offs? Do they consider the broader impact and different viewpoints? I'll note if students are discussing consequences like "If there's no homework, students might forget what they learned faster" or "Too much homework could lead to burnout and less family time," showing they are systematically evaluating implications.
*   **Activity (CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):**
    *   **Teacher:** "After your group discussion, I want each of you to individually write a short paragraph (3-4 sentences) stating your preliminary stance on the question: 'Should schools have homework?' You can say 'yes,' 'no,' or 'it depends/modified approach.' Crucially, you must provide at least two clear reasons to support your stance, drawing directly from the information we've discussed and evaluated today."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI4a):** Collect and read the individual paragraphs. Is the reasoning logical and coherent? Do their conclusions align with the evidence they present? Are there any unsupported claims or faulty logic? I will look for clear connections between their stance and the evidence provided, e.g., "I believe schools should have *some* homework because it helps reinforce learning (Pro 1) and teaches responsibility (Pro 2), as mentioned in the research summary."

**V. Reflecting on Thinking Processes (CRI5a) & Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes):**
    *   **Teacher:** "To conclude our lesson, I want you to reflect on your own thinking today. Please complete the exit ticket."
    *   **Exit Ticket Prompt:**
        *   "My initial thought on homework before this lesson was..."
        *   "After today's lesson, my thinking has [changed / stayed the same / become more nuanced] because..."
        *   "One thing I found challenging about evaluating the information or forming my opinion was..., and I tried to overcome it by..."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI5a):** Collect and review exit tickets. Do students demonstrate metacognitive awareness? Can they articulate how their understanding evolved or deepened? Do they identify specific challenges in their thinking and describe strategies they used to address them? I will look for responses that show genuine self-awareness, such as "Initially, I just hated homework, but after seeing the arguments about responsibility, I now think a little bit is okay. It was hard to let go of my bias, but I tried to focus only on the facts from the research."

### Lesson Outline and Activities

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a) — 10 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Begin with a brief class discussion: “What is homework? Why do schools assign it?”  
- Provide students with a short, clear text (about 150 words) presenting basic facts about homework (e.g., its purpose, common types, and general opinions).  
- Ask students to highlight or underline key ideas and then paraphrase one or two points in their own words.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Listen for students’ ability to identify relevant information accurately.  
- Review their paraphrased points to see if they clarify ideas precisely, avoiding vague or incorrect statements.  
- For example, a student who says, “Homework helps students practice what they learn in class,” shows clear understanding.  

---

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b) — 8 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- In pairs, students receive mixed-up statements about homework’s benefits and drawbacks.  
- Their task is to organize these statements into two columns: Pros and Cons, arranging them logically (e.g., from most to least important).  
- Pairs then share their organization with the class.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Check if students arrange information to strengthen logical flow (e.g., grouping similar ideas together, ordering points clearly).  
- Note if any students present information in a scattered or contradictory way, which would indicate a need for further support.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a) — 10 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Present students with two short excerpts about homework: one from a reputable educational website and one from an unverified blog.  
- Guide students through a checklist to evaluate the sources (author credentials, evidence cited, date of publication).  
- Students discuss in small groups which source seems more reliable and why.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Listen for students’ ability to identify source credibility and to cross-check information.  
- Look for comments that show they question the reliability of sources rather than accepting all information at face value.  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b) — 7 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Provide students with a set of statements about homework, some well-supported by research and others based on opinions or stereotypes.  
- Students work individually to select the three highest-quality statements and justify their choices in writing.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Evaluate students’ judgments for sophistication—do they choose statements backed by evidence and relevance?  
- Assess their written justifications for depth and understanding of quality criteria.  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a) — 10 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Facilitate a structured debate: “Should schools assign homework?”  
- Divide the class into two groups (Pro and Con). Each group prepares arguments considering short-term and long-term effects (e.g., learning outcomes, stress, family time).  
- After debate, students individually write a paragraph weighing the trade-offs and stating their position.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Observe the depth of analysis during debate—do students consider multiple perspectives and long-term implications?  
- Review written paragraphs for systematic evaluation of pros and cons rather than one-sided arguments.  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a) — 5 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- Ask students to explain the reasoning behind their position on homework in a brief oral or written statement.  
- Encourage them to anticipate possible counterarguments and address them.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Assess clarity and logic in students’ reasoning.  
- Look for evidence of anticipating complexities, such as acknowledging that homework can be helpful but also stressful.  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a) — 5 minutes**  
*Activity:*  
- End with a metacognitive reflection prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you change or strengthen your opinion?”  
- Students write a short reflection or share aloud.  

*Observation/Assessment:*  
- Look for evidence of deep metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing biases, questioning assumptions, or adjusting viewpoints based on new information.  
- Note if students mention specific strategies they used to think more critically.
