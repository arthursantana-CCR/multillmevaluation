## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Seeing the Connection
## CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 | 55-Minute Lesson | Grade 7

---

## Lesson Overview

**Objective:** Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.

**Materials:** Graph paper, colored markers, "Pizza Price" data cards, whiteboard, exit tickets

---

## Lesson Plan

### Warm-Up: The Mystery Comparison (8 minutes)

Display two scenarios on the board:

- *"A store sells 3 notebooks for $6. Another store sells 5 notebooks for $9. Are these the same deal?"*

Ask students to **write their initial thinking** in their math journals before discussing. After 2 minutes of independent writing, facilitate a brief pair-share.

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information**
Students must articulate *why* these deals differ, using precise language about ratios. Prompt: *"What exactly do we mean when we say something is proportional? Be specific."* Observe whether students use vague language ("the numbers are different") versus precise reasoning ("the unit rate is $2 per notebook vs. $1.80 per notebook, so the rates are not equal"). This moment targets clarification because students must define proportionality with accuracy before the lesson deepens.

*Assessment:* Listen for precise unit rate language. Cold-call two students with contrasting responses and ask the class to evaluate which explanation is clearer.

---

### Direct Instruction: What Makes It Proportional? (10 minutes)

Introduce the four representations using a single scenario: *"A car travels 60 miles per hour."*

Build each representation together:
- **Table:** (1 hr → 60 mi, 2 hr → 120 mi, 3 hr → 180 mi)
- **Graph:** Plot points; identify that the line passes through the origin
- **Equation:** d = 60h
- **Verbal:** "Distance is always 60 times the number of hours"

**CRI1b — Organizing Information**
Students copy each representation into a four-quadrant graphic organizer in their notebooks, labeling how each form connects to the others. The teacher explicitly models moving *between* representations ("Notice how the constant 60 in our equation matches the unit rate in our table and the slope of our graph").

*Assessment:* Circulate and check graphic organizers for logical structure. Are students labeling connections between representations, or treating each as isolated?

---

### Guided Practice: Pizza Price Investigation (15 minutes)

Distribute "Pizza Price" data cards. Each group receives a table of values (some proportional, some not) and must:

1. Determine whether the relationship is proportional
2. Justify using **at least two representations**
3. Compare their data set to another group's

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information**
Groups must cross-check their conclusion by verifying the constant ratio *and* confirming the graph passes through the origin. Students who rely on only one check are prompted: *"What if your table has a pattern by coincidence? How can you confirm using the graph?"* This builds rigorous verification habits.

*Assessment:* Ask groups to present their two-representation justification. Probe with: *"Could your table alone be misleading? Why or why not?"*

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information**
After groups share, display one intentionally flawed data set (a table where the ratio is nearly — but not exactly — constant). Ask: *"Is this good enough evidence of proportionality?"* Students must judge whether approximate patterns qualify, pushing them toward mathematical precision.

*Assessment:* Listen for students who distinguish between "close enough" and "exactly proportional," and those who explain *why* precision matters in real contexts (e.g., pricing, engineering).

---

### Critical Thinking Challenge: The Better Deal Debate (12 minutes)

Present this scenario: *"App A charges $5 upfront plus $1 per song. App B charges $2 per song. Which is proportional? Which is the better deal — and does it depend on how many songs you buy?"*

Students graph both relationships and determine the crossover point.

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
Students must argue *both sides*: App A is better for heavy users; App B is better for light users. Require students to write: *"App ___ is better IF ___, because ___."*

*Assessment:* Check written responses for conditional reasoning. Are students identifying the specific quantity (number of songs) that changes which option wins?

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
Students must use their graph and equation to identify the exact break-even point and justify a recommendation for a specific user type.

*Assessment:* Ask: *"If I told you I buy 10 songs a month, what would you recommend and why?"* Evaluate whether reasoning is anchored in mathematical evidence.

---

### Exit Ticket: Metacognitive Reflection (10 minutes)

Students complete a three-part exit ticket:

1. Represent a proportional relationship (y = 4x) in two ways
2. Explain *one thing that confused you* and how you worked through it
3. Answer: *"Which representation do you find most useful? Why?"*

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
Part 2 explicitly asks students to name confusion and describe their recovery strategy. Part 3 invites metacognitive preference-awareness — recognizing that individuals think differently about mathematical representations.

*Assessment:* Review exit tickets for specificity in Part 2. Generic responses ("I was confused but figured it out") score lower than specific ones ("I mixed up the equation form with a non-proportional one because of the +5, but then I checked the graph and saw it didn't cross the origin").

---

## Closure

Review two or three exit ticket responses (anonymized) whole-class. Reinforce: proportional relationships are consistent, verifiable across representations, and context-dependent in application.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations.
4.  Determine the constant of proportionality from various representations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with tables and graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Calculators (optional, for efficiency)
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore a really important concept in math: proportional relationships. You've already worked with ratios and rates. Can anyone remind us what a ratio is? What about a unit rate?"
*   *(Briefly review ratios and unit rates, connecting them to real-world examples like miles per hour or cost per item. For instance, 'If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?')*
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll see how these ideas connect to something called a 'proportional relationship' and how we can spot them in different ways."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice: Defining Proportionality (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "A **proportional relationship** exists between two quantities when one quantity is a constant multiple of the other. This constant multiple is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by 'k'. In simpler terms, if you divide the 'y' value by the 'x' value, you always get the same number, 'k'."
*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** "Let's try to put that in our own words. Turn to your elbow partner and explain what 'constant multiple' means in the context of a proportional relationship. What are the two key things we need to look for when deciding if a relationship is proportional?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen to student discussions. I will observe if students are accurately rephrasing the definition, identifying "constant ratio" and "relationship between two quantities" as key ideas. I'll ask a few pairs to share their rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in their language.
*   **Teacher:** "We can see proportional relationships in different forms. Let's start with tables."
    *   *Display a table on the board:*
        | x (hours) | y (miles) |
        | :-------- | :-------- |
        | 1         | 50        |
        | 2         | 100       |
        | 3         | 150       |
    *   **Teacher:** "For each pair (x, y), let's calculate y/x. What do you notice?" (Students should find y/x = 50 for all pairs). "Since y/x is constant, this is a proportional relationship, and k = 50. The equation would be y = 50x."
    *   *Display a non-proportional table:*
        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
        | :---------- | :------- |
        | 1           | $1.50    |
        | 2           | $2.50    |
        | 3           | $3.50    |
    *   **Teacher:** "Now, let's check this one. What do you find for y/x?" (Students should find different ratios). "Is this proportional? Why not?" (No constant ratio).
*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships also have a special look on a graph. They always form a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0)."
    *   *Quickly sketch the graph of y=50x, showing it's a straight line through the origin.*

**III. Activity 1: Table & Graph Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn! In pairs, you'll get a handout with several tables. Your task is to determine if each relationship is proportional. If it is, you'll find the constant of proportionality (k), write the equation, and then graph it on the provided graph paper."
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Remember, to be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers. You need to check *every* pair in the table. If even one ratio is different, it's not proportional. Be rigorous in your checks!"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and observe pairs. I will specifically look for students systematically calculating y/x for *all* rows in the tables, not just the first one. I'll ask groups, "How did you confirm this table is proportional? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why is checking all of them important?"
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** "When you're working with your tables and graphs, make sure your work is clear and organized. Label your axes on the graph, use a consistent scale, and show your calculations for 'k' clearly next to each table."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will check student handouts for clear organization. Are tables filled out neatly? Are calculations for 'k' shown consistently for each row? Are graphs clearly labeled with axes, units, and a consistent scale? Do the plotted points accurately reflect the table data in an orderly fashion?
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** As students work, they will constantly make decisions: "Is this proportional?", "What's the constant?", "How do I write the equation?". They must use the definition and their calculations to justify their answers.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** During group work, I will listen to their discussions. Are they using the definition (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to justify their decisions? For example, if a group incorrectly identifies a non-proportional relationship, I'll ask, "What evidence from your calculations or graph led you to that conclusion? Does it meet *all* the criteria for proportionality, including passing through (0,0)?"

**IV. Activity 2: Equation & Diagram Connection (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Great work with tables and graphs! Now let's connect to equations and verbal descriptions."
*   *Provide a new scenario:* "A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."
*   **Teacher:** "Is this a proportional relationship? How do you know? What's the constant of proportionality if we consider flour to milk?" (k = 2/3). "What if we consider milk to flour?" (k = 3/2).
*   **Teacher:** "How would we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)?" (F = (2/3)M). "And milk (M) in terms of flour (F)?" (M = (3/2)F).
*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Imagine you're explaining this recipe to someone who's never cooked before. Which representation – the verbal description, a table, a graph, or an equation – do you think would be most helpful for them to understand the relationship between flour and milk? Why? Are there situations where another representation might be better or worse?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will listen to student responses during a brief class discussion. Do they articulate the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? For example, "The verbal description is easy to start with, but an equation is better if you need to scale the recipe up a lot because you can just plug in numbers." or "A graph gives a quick visual, but a table shows exact values for specific amounts."
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, create your own short verbal description of a proportional relationship. Then, write its equation and sketch a simple diagram (like a ratio table or a simple picture showing groups) that represents it. Make sure your example is clear and accurate."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will review their created examples. Do their verbal descriptions clearly imply proportionality? Is the equation correctly derived from the description? Does the diagram accurately represent the relationship? I'll look for examples that are clear, concise, and mathematically sound, avoiding vague or incorrect relationships.

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Let's quickly review. What are the key characteristics of a proportional relationship in a table? (Constant ratio y/x) In a graph? (Straight line through the origin) In an equation? (y = kx)"
*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, I want you to answer two questions:
        1.  What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today?
        2.  What strategy did you use, or what 'aha!' moment did you have, that helped you overcome that challenge?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will collect exit tickets. I'll look for responses that demonstrate metacognitive awareness. Do students identify specific difficulties (e.g., "remembering to check (0,0) on a graph," "calculating k consistently when numbers were fractions") and describe a strategy they used (e.g., "I made sure to divide y by x for every single point," "I drew a quick sketch to see if it went through the origin before plotting all points")? This shows they are reflecting on their learning process and adjusting their thinking.

---
**Differentiation:**
*   **Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality.
*   **Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but isn't. Have them explore inverse proportionality or multi-step proportional problems.

**Assessment:**
*   Informal observation during activities and discussions.
*   Review of student handouts (tables, graphs, equations, created examples).
*   Exit ticket responses.

**2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)**  
- Present the definition of proportional relationships formally: “Two quantities are proportional if they have a constant ratio.”  
- Show examples in different forms:  
  - Table: Number of tickets vs. total cost  
  - Graph: Plot points and draw a straight line through the origin  
  - Equation: y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality  
  - Diagram: Visual ratio models (e.g., bar models)  
  - Verbal description: “For every 1 ticket, you pay $8.”  
- Emphasize the constant ratio concept across all representations.

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI1b (Organizing information)**

---

- Distribute handouts with problems that require students to identify and represent proportional relationships in various forms.  
- Example problem: “A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar. Represent this relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.”  
- Students work in pairs to complete the task.  
- Circulate to provide support and ask probing questions: “How do you know this is proportional? What is the constant ratio here?”  

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**  
*Activity:* Students evaluate whether given data sets represent proportional relationships by checking for constant ratios.  
*Assessment:* Listen for students explaining their reasoning, e.g., “The ratio changes from 2:3 to 4:7, so it’s not proportional.”  

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**  
*Activity:* Students decide which representations best communicate the proportional relationship and justify their choices.  
*Assessment:* Collect written justifications or listen to discussions for evidence of discerning quality and relevance of information.

---

**4. Independent Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Students individually complete a worksheet with mixed problems requiring them to:  
  - Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions  
  - Create at least two different representations for each proportional relationship  
- Encourage students to explain their thinking in writing or with a partner.

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**  
*Activity:* Students compare different representations (e.g., table vs. graph) and discuss which is more helpful in understanding the relationship and why.  
*Assessment:* Review student explanations for depth of analysis, noting if they consider advantages and limitations of each form.

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**  
*Activity:* Students justify their choice of representation for a given problem, using logical reasoning.  
*Assessment:* Evaluate reasoning in written or oral explanations for clarity and sophistication, checking for anticipation of complexities (e.g., “The graph shows the trend clearly, but the equation is better for calculations.”).

---

- Lead a brief class discussion: “What strategies helped you understand proportional relationships? Did your thinking change as you worked through different representations?”  
- Have students write a quick exit ticket answering: “How do you know when two quantities are proportional?” and “Which representation do you find easiest to understand and why?”

**Targeted Critical Thinking: CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes)**  
*Activity:* Exit ticket reflection on personal understanding and strategies.

| CRI5a: Reflecting on thinking | Three-part exit ticket including confusion and strategy | Specificity and depth in exit ticket Part 2 responses |

## Differentiation

**Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality (constant ratio + line through origin).

**Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but is not. Have them explore what changes when a non-proportional term (like a flat fee) is added, and what that looks like across all representations.
