## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the importance of curiosity in learning and be able to write a well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Chart paper or projector  
- Handout with a short argumentative writing prompt  
- Graphic organizer for argument writing (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)  
- Sticky notes  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Hook:** Ask students, “What does curiosity mean to you?” Write their responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain curiosity as a desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas.  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous person (e.g., Marie Curie, Albert Einstein) whose curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize how curiosity drives us to ask questions and seek evidence, which is essential for strong arguments.

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us explore different sides of an issue, find reasons and evidence, and understand opposing views.  
- Show the standard: “We will learn how to write arguments that support claims with reasons and evidence, and also consider other viewpoints.”  
- Introduce the graphic organizer that will help structure their writing.

#### 3. **Modeling Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Present a simple, relatable argumentative prompt, for example:  
  *“Should school start later in the morning?”*  
- Using the graphic organizer, model how to:  
  - State a clear claim (e.g., “School should start later.”)  
  - Provide 2-3 reasons with evidence (e.g., “Students get more sleep, which improves focus.”)  
  - Acknowledge an opposing claim (e.g., “Some say starting later reduces after-school activities.”)  
  - Offer a rebuttal (e.g., “However, better rest helps students perform better in all activities.”)  
- Write this example on the board or projector.

#### 4. **Guided Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute a new prompt related to a topic students care about, such as:  
  *“Should students have homework every night?”*  
- In pairs or small groups, students brainstorm:  
  - Their claim  
  - Reasons and evidence supporting their claim  
  - One opposing claim and a rebuttal  
- Circulate and assist groups as needed.

#### 5. **Independent Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Students write a short argumentative paragraph individually using their group’s ideas and the graphic organizer.  
- Encourage them to be curious by asking themselves questions like:  
  - “What evidence supports my claim?”  
  - “What might someone who disagrees say?”  
  - “How can I respond to that?”

#### 6. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class: “How did curiosity help you think about your argument?”  
- Summarize key points: curiosity helps us ask questions, find evidence, and understand different perspectives, which makes our writing stronger.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to observe something they are curious about at home or school and write 3 questions they have about it. Then, write a short paragraph explaining why being curious about that topic is important.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with practical argumentative writing skills aligned to CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1, engaging students in critical thinking and clear communication.
