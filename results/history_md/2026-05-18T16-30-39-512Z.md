## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Everywhere

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Lesson Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify whether a relationship is proportional using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions
- Represent proportional relationships in multiple forms
- Justify their reasoning using the constant of proportionality

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Lemonade Stand" activity sheets
- Graph paper
- Colored pencils
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### WARM-UP — 5 Minutes

Post two tables on the board:

| Hours Worked | Pay ($) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |

| Hours Worked | Pay ($) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 25 |
| 3 | 36 |

Ask: *"What do you notice? What do you wonder?"* Students share with a partner, then with the class.

**Critical Thinking — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):**
The warm-up requires students to precisely identify *what is different* between the two tables. Students must articulate whether the ratios remain consistent, pushing them toward exact language like "the ratio Pay-to-Hours is always 12 in the first table, but not in the second." **Observation:** Listen for students who use vague language ("it looks off") versus precise language ("the ratio changes between rows"). Prompt vague responses with: *"Can you be more specific about where it breaks down?"*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 10 Minutes

Introduce the definition of a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x remains constant, written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.

Model all five representations using the example "A car travels 60 miles per hour":
- **Table:** (1, 60), (2, 120), (3, 180)
- **Graph:** Plot points and draw a line through the origin
- **Equation:** d = 60t
- **Diagram:** Double number line
- **Verbal:** "For every hour, the distance increases by 60 miles"

Explicitly show a **non-example**: a taxi that charges a $5 base fee plus $2/mile. Show why this graph does NOT pass through the origin and why the ratio changes.

**Critical Thinking — CRI1b (Organizing Information):**
Students receive a partially completed graphic organizer with five labeled sections (one per representation). As instruction proceeds, they fill in each section for the car example. **Observation:** Assess whether students organize information logically across all five representations with clear labels and accurate values, rather than writing random notes in margins.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — 12 Minutes

**"Which One Is Proportional?" Gallery Walk**

Post five scenarios around the room (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal). Students rotate in groups of three, deciding whether each scenario represents a proportional relationship and recording evidence.

Scenarios include intentional distractors — for example, a graph that is linear but does NOT pass through the origin, or a table where the differences are constant but the ratios are not.

**Critical Thinking — CRI2a & CRI2b (Assessing Validity and Quality of Information):**
Students must evaluate each posted scenario, cross-checking the representation against the definition. Groups must cite *specific evidence* — not just "it looks proportional" but "the ratio y/x equals 3 in every row, so this is valid." **Observation:** Circulate and ask groups: *"How do you know that information is enough to decide?"* Look for groups who check multiple features (ratio consistency AND origin passage on graphs) rather than relying on a single clue.

---

### COLLABORATIVE ACTIVITY — 15 Minutes

**"The Lemonade Stand Problem"**

Students receive a scenario: *Maya sells lemonade. She charges $2.50 per cup. Her friend Jordan charges $1.00 per cup but also charges a $3.00 setup fee.*

Groups must:
1. Build a table for both plans (0–6 cups)
2. Graph both on the same coordinate plane
3. Write an equation for each
4. Determine which, if either, is proportional
5. Recommend which plan is "better" and for whom

**Critical Thinking — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):**
Step 5 requires students to analyze trade-offs: Maya's plan is proportional and cheaper for small quantities; Jordan's becomes cheaper for larger quantities. Students must consider *at what point* one plan surpasses the other. **Observation:** Look for written justifications that name a specific crossover point and consider the buyer's perspective (someone buying 2 cups vs. 10 cups faces a different decision).

**Critical Thinking — CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):**
Students must connect mathematical evidence — the table values, intersection point on the graph — directly to their recommendation without logical gaps. **Observation:** Require each group to state: *"We recommend ___ because the data shows ___."* Flag responses that make claims unsupported by their own tables or graphs.

---

### CLASS DEBRIEF — 8 Minutes

Groups share recommendations. The class compares conclusions and discusses whether disagreements stem from different assumptions (number of cups purchased, context of use).

**Critical Thinking — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):**
Ask students: *"Did your thinking change during the activity? What made you reconsider?"* Students write two sentences in their notebooks before sharing. **Observation:** Strong responses name a specific moment of doubt — *"I originally thought Jordan's plan was never better, but when I graphed it I saw they intersect at 5 cups"* — demonstrating genuine metacognitive adjustment rather than simply restating the final answer.

---

### EXIT TICKET — 5 Minutes

Students independently complete:
1. Is the relationship y = 4x proportional? Explain using TWO representations.
2. Write one thing that confused you today and one question you still have.

Question 2 directly reinforces **CRI5a** and gives the teacher data to adjust tomorrow's instruction.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations.
4.  Determine the constant of proportionality from various representations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with tables and graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Calculators (optional, for efficiency)
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore a really important concept in math: proportional relationships. You've already worked with ratios and rates. Can anyone remind us what a ratio is? What about a unit rate?"
*   *(Briefly review ratios and unit rates, connecting them to real-world examples like miles per hour or cost per item. For instance, 'If a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, what's its unit rate of speed?')*
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll see how these ideas connect to something called a 'proportional relationship' and how we can spot them in different ways."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice: Defining Proportionality (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "A **proportional relationship** exists between two quantities when one quantity is a constant multiple of the other. This constant multiple is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented by 'k'. In simpler terms, if you divide the 'y' value by the 'x' value, you always get the same number, 'k'."
*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** "Let's try to put that in our own words. Turn to your elbow partner and explain what 'constant multiple' means in the context of a proportional relationship. What are the two key things we need to look for when deciding if a relationship is proportional?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen to student discussions. I will observe if students are accurately rephrasing the definition, identifying "constant ratio" and "relationship between two quantities" as key ideas. I'll ask a few pairs to share their rephrased definitions with the class, looking for precision in their language.
*   **Teacher:** "We can see proportional relationships in different forms. Let's start with tables."
    *   *Display a table on the board:*
        | x (hours) | y (miles) |
        | :-------- | :-------- |
        | 1         | 50        |
        | 2         | 100       |
        | 3         | 150       |
    *   **Teacher:** "For each pair (x, y), let's calculate y/x. What do you notice?" (Students should find y/x = 50 for all pairs). "Since y/x is constant, this is a proportional relationship, and k = 50. The equation would be y = 50x."
    *   *Display a non-proportional table:*
        | x (cookies) | y (cost) |
        | :---------- | :------- |
        | 1           | $1.50    |
        | 2           | $2.50    |
        | 3           | $3.50    |
    *   **Teacher:** "Now, let's check this one. What do you find for y/x?" (Students should find different ratios). "Is this proportional? Why not?" (No constant ratio).
*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships also have a special look on a graph. They always form a straight line that passes through the origin (0,0)."
    *   *Quickly sketch the graph of y=50x, showing it's a straight line through the origin.*

**III. Activity 1: Table & Graph Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn! In pairs, you'll get a handout with several tables. Your task is to determine if each relationship is proportional. If it is, you'll find the constant of proportionality (k), write the equation, and then graph it on the provided graph paper."
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Remember, to be sure a relationship is proportional, you can't just check one pair of numbers. You need to check *every* pair in the table. If even one ratio is different, it's not proportional. Be rigorous in your checks!"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and observe pairs. I will specifically look for students systematically calculating y/x for *all* rows in the tables, not just the first one. I'll ask groups, "How did you confirm this table is proportional? Did you check every single ratio, or just a few? Why is checking all of them important?"
*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** "When you're working with your tables and graphs, make sure your work is clear and organized. Label your axes on the graph, use a consistent scale, and show your calculations for 'k' clearly next to each table."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will check student handouts for clear organization. Are tables filled out neatly? Are calculations for 'k' shown consistently for each row? Are graphs clearly labeled with axes, units, and a consistent scale? Do the plotted points accurately reflect the table data in an orderly fashion?
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** As students work, they will constantly make decisions: "Is this proportional?", "What's the constant?", "How do I write the equation?". They must use the definition and their calculations to justify their answers.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** During group work, I will listen to their discussions. Are they using the definition (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to justify their decisions? For example, if a group incorrectly identifies a non-proportional relationship, I'll ask, "What evidence from your calculations or graph led you to that conclusion? Does it meet *all* the criteria for proportionality, including passing through (0,0)?"

**IV. Activity 2: Equation & Diagram Connection (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Great work with tables and graphs! Now let's connect to equations and verbal descriptions."
*   *Provide a new scenario:* "A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk."
*   **Teacher:** "Is this a proportional relationship? How do you know? What's the constant of proportionality if we consider flour to milk?" (k = 2/3). "What if we consider milk to flour?" (k = 3/2).
*   **Teacher:** "How would we write an equation for flour (F) in terms of milk (M)?" (F = (2/3)M). "And milk (M) in terms of flour (F)?" (M = (3/2)F).
*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Imagine you're explaining this recipe to someone who's never cooked before. Which representation – the verbal description, a table, a graph, or an equation – do you think would be most helpful for them to understand the relationship between flour and milk? Why? Are there situations where another representation might be better or worse?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will listen to student responses during a brief class discussion. Do they articulate the advantages and disadvantages of each representation? For example, "The verbal description is easy to start with, but an equation is better if you need to scale the recipe up a lot because you can just plug in numbers." or "A graph gives a quick visual, but a table shows exact values for specific amounts."
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, create your own short verbal description of a proportional relationship. Then, write its equation and sketch a simple diagram (like a ratio table or a simple picture showing groups) that represents it. Make sure your example is clear and accurate."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will review their created examples. Do their verbal descriptions clearly imply proportionality? Is the equation correctly derived from the description? Does the diagram accurately represent the relationship? I'll look for examples that are clear, concise, and mathematically sound, avoiding vague or incorrect relationships.

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Let's quickly review. What are the key characteristics of a proportional relationship in a table? (Constant ratio y/x) In a graph? (Straight line through the origin) In an equation? (y = kx)"
*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, I want you to answer two questions:
        1.  What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today?
        2.  What strategy did you use, or what 'aha!' moment did you have, that helped you overcome that challenge?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** I will collect exit tickets. I'll look for responses that demonstrate metacognitive awareness. Do students identify specific difficulties (e.g., "remembering to check (0,0) on a graph," "calculating k consistently when numbers were fractions") and describe a strategy they used (e.g., "I made sure to divide y by x for every single point," "I drew a quick sketch to see if it went through the origin before plotting all points")? This shows they are reflecting on their learning process and adjusting their thinking.

---
**Differentiation:**
*   **Support:** Provide partially filled tables, pre-drawn graph axes with scales, or sentence starters for verbal descriptions. Pair struggling students with stronger peers. Offer a checklist for identifying proportionality.
*   **Challenge:** Ask students to create a scenario that is *not* proportional and explain why, or one that *appears* proportional but isn't. Have them explore inverse proportionality or multi-step proportional problems.

**Assessment:**
*   Informal observation during activities and discussions.
*   Review of student handouts (tables, graphs, equations, created examples).
*   Exit ticket responses.

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction (5 minutes)**  
- Begin with a brief review: “What does it mean for two quantities to be proportional?”  
- Show a simple example: If 1 apple costs $2, then 3 apples cost $6. Ask students: “How can we show this relationship in different ways?”  
- Write on the board: Table, Graph, Equation, Diagram, Verbal Description.

*Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information)*  
- **Activity:** Ask students to explain in their own words what “proportional” means and how the example fits that definition.  
- **Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying the idea that proportional means the ratio between quantities is constant.

---

**2. Guided Practice: Representing Proportional Relationships (15 minutes)**  
- Present a proportional relationship: “A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour. How far does it travel in 2, 3, 4 hours?”  
- **Step 1:** Create a table together showing hours and miles.  
- **Step 2:** Plot these points on graph paper and draw the line.  
- **Step 3:** Write the equation relating distance (d) and time (t): d = 60t.  
- **Step 4:** Draw a diagram (e.g., a number line or bar model) to represent the relationship.  
- **Step 5:** Write a verbal description: “The distance traveled is 60 times the number of hours.”  

*Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information)*  
- **Activity:** Students organize the same information into all five representations in their notebooks.  
- **Assessment:** Check for logical flow and completeness in their representations, ensuring each form clearly relates to the others.

*Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)*  
- **Activity:** Ask students: “How do we know this relationship is proportional? What evidence supports this?”  
- **Assessment:** Listen for students referencing the constant ratio and consistent graph line, showing they evaluate the validity of the proportional relationship.

---

**3. Independent Practice: Mixed Representations (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with different proportional relationships presented in one form (e.g., a table or verbal description).  
- Students work individually or in pairs to represent the relationship in the other four forms.  
- Circulate to provide support and ask probing questions.

*Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)*  
- **Activity:** Encourage students to choose the clearest or most effective representation for a given problem and justify their choice.  
- **Assessment:** Observe students’ reasoning about which form best communicates the proportional relationship and their ability to select high-quality representations.

*Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)*  
- **Activity:** Facilitate a brief discussion: “Which representation is easiest to understand? Which is best for solving problems? Are there situations where one form is better than another?”  
- **Assessment:** Note students’ ability to compare and contrast the usefulness of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, considering advantages and limitations.

---

**4. Group Activity: Real-World Problem Solving (15 minutes)**  
- Present a real-world scenario: “A recipe calls for 2 cups of flour to make 12 cookies. How much flour is needed for 30 cookies?”  
- In small groups, students create all five representations of the proportional relationship and explain their reasoning to the class.  
- Groups share their work and discuss any differences in approach.

*Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)*  
- **Activity:** Students justify their chosen representations and explain how they used proportional reasoning to solve the problem.  
- **Assessment:** Listen for clear, logical explanations that anticipate complexities (e.g., scaling up the recipe, checking for consistency).

*Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes)*  
- **Activity:** After presentations, ask students to write a short reflection: “What strategy helped you understand proportional relationships best? What did you find challenging?”  
- **Assessment:** Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness and adjustments in thinking.

---

**5. Closure and Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Summarize key points: proportional relationships have a constant ratio and can be represented in multiple ways.  
- Exit ticket prompt: “Give one example of a proportional relationship and show it in two different forms.”  
- Collect exit tickets to assess individual understanding.

Students must connect mathematical evidence — table values, graph intersection — directly to their recommendation without logical gaps.
**Observation:** Require each group to state: *"We recommend ___ because the data shows ___."* Flag responses that make claims unsupported by their own tables or graphs.

---

### CLASS DEBRIEF — 8 Minutes

Groups share recommendations. The class compares conclusions and discusses whether disagreements stem from different assumptions (number of cups purchased, buyer context). Briefly ask: *"Which representation — table, graph, or equation — was most useful for solving this problem? Are there situations where a different form would be better?"* This surfaces trade-offs between representations.

**Critical Thinking — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):**
Ask: *"Did your thinking change during the activity? What made you reconsider?"* Students write two sentences in their notebooks before sharing aloud.
**Observation:** Strong responses name a specific moment of doubt — *"I originally thought Jordan's plan was never better, but when I graphed it I saw they intersect at 5 cups"* — demonstrating genuine metacognitive adjustment rather than restating only the final answer.

---

### EXIT TICKET — 5 Minutes

Students independently complete:
1. Is the relationship y = 4x proportional? Explain using **two** representations.
2. What was the most challenging part of today's lesson, and what strategy helped you work through it?

Question 2 reinforces **CRI5a** and gives the teacher data to adjust the next day's instruction.

## Lesson Outline

### WARM-UP — 5 Minutes

Post two tables on the board:

| Hours Worked | Pay ($) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |

| Hours Worked | Pay ($) |
|---|---|
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 25 |
| 3 | 36 |

Ask: *"What do you notice? What do you wonder?"* Students share with a partner, then with the class.

**Critical Thinking — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):**
Students must precisely identify *what is different* between the two tables, articulating whether the ratios remain consistent — using exact language like "the ratio Pay-to-Hours is always 12 in the first table, but not in the second."
**Observation:** Listen for students who use vague language ("it looks off") versus precise language ("the ratio changes between rows"). Prompt vague responses: *"Can you be more specific about where it breaks down?"*

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 10 Minutes

Introduce the definition of a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x remains constant, written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.

Model all five representations using "A car travels 60 miles per hour":
- **Table:** (1, 60), (2, 120), (3, 180)
- **Graph:** Plot points; draw a straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** d = 60t
- **Diagram:** Double number line
- **Verbal:** "For every hour, the distance increases by 60 miles"

Explicitly show a **non-example**: a bakery charging $1.50 for the first cookie but only $1.00 per cookie after that. Show why the ratio y/x changes and why the graph does not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking — CRI1b (Organizing Information):**
Students receive a partially completed graphic organizer with five labeled sections (one per representation). As instruction proceeds, they fill in each section for the car example.
**Observation:** Assess whether students organize information logically across all five representations with clear labels and accurate values, rather than writing scattered notes.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — 12 Minutes

**"Which One Is Proportional?" Gallery Walk**

Post five scenarios around the room (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal). Students rotate in groups of three, deciding whether each scenario is proportional and recording specific evidence. Scenarios include intentional distractors — a graph that is linear but does NOT pass through the origin, and a table where differences are constant but ratios are not.

**Critical Thinking — CRI2a (Assessing Validity of Information):**
Students must evaluate each scenario by checking *every* ratio in a table or confirming a graph passes through the origin — not just checking one data point.
**Observation:** Circulate and ask groups: *"How did you confirm this? Did you check every pair, or just a few? Why does that matter?"* Look for groups who apply multiple checks rather than relying on a single feature.

**Critical Thinking — CRI2b (Assessing Quality of Information):**
Groups must cite *specific evidence* — not "it looks proportional" but "the ratio y/x equals 3 in every row, so this is valid."
**Observation:** Flag groups making surface-level judgments. Prompt: *"What exactly makes that evidence convincing?"*

---

### COLLABORATIVE ACTIVITY — 15 Minutes

**"The Lemonade Stand Problem"**

Students receive a scenario: *Maya sells lemonade at $2.50 per cup. Her friend Jordan charges $1.00 per cup but also charges a $3.00 setup fee.*

Groups must:
1. Build a table for both plans (0–6 cups)
2. Graph both on the same coordinate plane
3. Write an equation for each
4. Determine which, if either, is proportional
5. Recommend which plan is "better" — and for whom

**Critical Thinking — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):**
Step 5 requires students to analyze trade-offs: Maya's plan is proportional and cheaper for small quantities; Jordan's becomes cheaper for larger quantities. Students must identify the crossover point and consider the buyer's context.
**Observation:** Look for written justifications that name a specific crossover point and consider perspective — someone buying 2 cups faces a different decision than someone buying 10.

**Critical Thinking — CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):**
Students must connect mathematical evidence — table values, graph intersection — directly to their recommendation without logical gaps.
**Observation:** Require each group to state: *"We recommend ___ because the data shows ___."* Flag responses that make claims unsupported by their own tables or graphs.
