## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Lesson Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

---

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handouts with mixed scenarios (recipes, distance/time, pricing), exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "3 apples cost $2. 6 apples cost $4. 9 apples cost $6."
- **Scenario B:** "A taxi charges $3 to start, then $2 per mile."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Push students to **clarify their definitions**: "What exactly does 'proportional' mean here?" Guide them to articulate that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and pass through the origin.

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who restate the concept precisely (Level 2: "Every pair has the same ratio, 2:3") versus vague responses ("They go up the same"). Record observations on a clipboard checklist.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Introduce the five representations of a proportional relationship using the example "$2 per pound of apples":
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $2 for every pound."
- **Table:** Pounds (1,2,3,4) → Cost (2,4,6,8)
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** Bar/tape diagram

Have students fold a paper into 5 sections and label each representation. Model how each representation connects to the others, emphasizing the constant of proportionality (k=2).

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect the folded organizers at the end. Level 2 students will arrange representations with clear labels and visible connections (arrows, shared k-value noted). Level 1 students will fill boxes but miss connections.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Proportional or Not?" Sort (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity & Quality*
Distribute six cards with mixed representations (some proportional, some not — e.g., a table with constant differences but not constant ratios; a graph not through origin; an equation y = 3x + 1).

In pairs, students sort cards into "Proportional" / "Not Proportional" piles. They must **cite evidence** for each decision using at least two representations (e.g., "I checked the table AND the graph"). Compare findings with another pair.

**Assessment of CRI2a/2b:** Circulate and ask, *"How did you verify your answer? Did you check using a second method?"* Level 2 students cross-check using multiple representations; Level 1 students rely on a single feature without verification.

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: The Babysitting Dilemma (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a: Weighing Pros/Cons & Sound Reasoning*
Present three babysitting pay options:
- **Option A:** $8 per hour flat
- **Option B:** $5 plus $7 per hour
- **Option C:** $40 for any job up to 5 hours, then $10/hour after

Pose the question: *"Which option should you choose, and for what length of job? Which options are proportional?"*

Students work in groups of 3 to build tables and graphs for each option, then write a recommendation. They must identify which option is proportional and **justify trade-offs**: short jobs vs. long jobs, predictability, fairness.

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Review written recommendations. Level 2 responses address multiple scenarios ("For jobs under 2 hours, A is best because…; for 5+ hours, C wins because…"). Level 1 responses pick one option without comparison.

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Look for logical chains supported by mathematical evidence. Level 2: "Since Option B has a y-intercept of 5, it's not proportional, but it earns more after 3 hours because the slope of 7 exceeds A's slope of 8 only after… wait, let me recheck." Level 1: Reasoning present but skips steps.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*
Distribute exit tickets with three prompts:
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x proportional? Represent it in a table and graph."
2. **Reflection:** "What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What made you change your mind?"
3. "What strategy will you use first next time to test if something is proportional, and why?"

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read reflection responses. Level 2 students articulate a shift ("At first I thought Option B was proportional because it grew steadily, but I realized the +$5 start meant it doesn't pass through origin"). Level 1 students mention reasoning without describing a change. Level -1 students show no reflection.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Briefly share two strong reflections (with permission). Reinforce: proportional relationships share a constant ratio, pass through the origin on a graph, and take the form y = kx. Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts.

---

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship in your home (recipes, prices, schedules). Represent each in two ways and explain your reasoning.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportional Relationships in the Real World: Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will recognize, represent, and analyze proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, weigh representational trade-offs, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Structuring (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher projects a wordy, slightly confusing verbal description of a local bakery’s pricing: *"So, if you buy 3 cupcakes, it costs $12. But yesterday, my friend bought 5 and paid $20. I think 1 cupcake is $4, but I'm not sure. Oh, and if you buy 0, it costs $0."* 
Students are tasked with extracting the mathematical facts and structuring them into a clear format.

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must cut through the conversational filler to clarify the exact quantities and rates, rewriting the scenario into a precise mathematical statement that supports deeper understanding of the unit rate.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher circulates, observing whether students successfully isolate the core proportional facts (e.g., identifying the $4/cupcake unit rate) and eliminate irrelevant details on their whiteboards.
*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Once the information is clarified, students are asked to arrange this data into a logical, sequential T-table (x = cupcakes, y = cost) to strengthen the logical flow of the data.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses student tables to ensure they are organized sequentially (0, 1, 3, 5) with consistent labels, demonstrating a basic structure that strengthens logical flow.

### **Part 2: Guided Exploration and Data Validation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces the four representations of proportional relationships: tables, graphs, equations ($y = kx$), and verbal descriptions. The teacher presents a claim from a fictional student: *"This graph shows a proportional relationship where the constant of proportionality is 5."* The graph provided is slightly ambiguous. 

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students are asked to rigorously cross-check this fictional student's claim. They must verify the claim by consulting their textbook’s authoritative definition of proportionality (must pass through the origin, must be a straight line) and by translating the graph's points into an equation to see if the math holds up.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher listens to group discussions to ensure students are not just accepting the claim, but actively cross-checking it against the textbook rules and alternative representations before declaring it valid or invalid.
*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** The teacher then provides two sources of data for a new problem about city water usage: a hand-drawn, unscaled scatterplot from a social media post, and a formal data table published by the City Water Authority. Students must determine the constant of proportionality.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher evaluates students' written responses to see if they demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting the City Water Authority table as the high-quality, authoritative source for calculating an exact equation, rather than relying on the inaccurate social media graph.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem-Solving & Decision Making (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students are placed in small groups and given a scenario: A school is planning a field trip and needs to calculate the cost of bus rentals over the next 10 years based on projected student enrollment. They are asked to represent this proportional relationship. 

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Groups must decide whether to use a table, a graph, or an equation to present their 10-year prediction to the principal. They must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each alternative.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher collects a "Decision Matrix" from each group. Mastery is observed when students note comprehensive trade-offs (e.g., "A graph is highly visual for the principal to see the trend instantly, but an equation is better for calculating the exact long-term cost without having to draw a massive grid").
*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Using their chosen representation, groups must make a final recommendation on which of two bus companies to hire. Company A has a strict proportional cost ($50 per student). Company B has a lower rate ($40 per student) but a flat $500 booking fee (non-proportional). 
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses the groups' final written recommendations. Students demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by anticipating complexities—specifically, recognizing that while Company A is proportional, Company B might actually be cheaper if the student enrollment exceeds 50 students, showing they understand the limits of proportional models in complex decisions.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students complete an independent exit ticket that requires them to solve one final proportional equation ($y = 3.5x$) and graph it. The second half of the exit ticket is a guided reflection journal.

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students respond to the prompt: *"Think about a moment today when you were confused about translating a table into an equation or graph. How did you realize you were stuck, and what specific strategy did you use to adjust your thinking and find the constant of proportionality?"*
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reviews the exit tickets for deep metacognitive awareness. High-level responses will explicitly detail their internal process (e.g., "I realized my equation $y = x + 4$ was wrong because when I checked it against the graph, it didn't start at the origin. I adjusted my thinking by remembering proportional relationships use multiplication, so I changed it to $y = 4x$").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Grade:** Middle School Math  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Materials:** Board/projector, graph paper, rulers, “relationship cards” handout, exit ticket, colored pencils

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities have a proportional relationship.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Justify their reasoning using mathematical evidence.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two situations:

- **Situation A:** A bike rental costs $4 per hour.  
- **Situation B:** A bike rental costs $5 plus $4 per hour.

Students create a quick table for 0, 1, 2, and 3 hours and decide which relationship is proportional.

Teacher asks:
- “What do you notice about the cost when time is 0?”
- “Is there a constant multiplier?”
- “Which graph would pass through the origin?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students clarify relevant mathematical information by identifying the starting value, constant rate, and meaning of each quantity.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for or collect student statements such as:  
- “Situation A is proportional because at 0 hours the cost is $0, and each hour adds $4.”  
- “Situation B is not proportional because it starts at $5 before any hours.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain that a proportional relationship has the form:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

Use the bike rental example to model five representations for Situation A:

- **Verbal:** The cost is $4 for each hour.
- **Table:**

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 4 | 8 | 12 |

- **Equation:** \(y = 4x\)
- **Graph:** A straight line through \((0,0)\)
- **Diagram:** 1 hour → $4, 2 hours → $8, 3 hours → $12

Emphasize that each representation should tell the same story.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students learn to arrange information across multiple representations in a logical way.

**Observe/Assess:** Students’ notes should show organized matching between table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description. Look for written evidence such as:  
- “The table, graph, and equation all show $4 per hour.”  
- “I put hours as \(x\) and cost as \(y\), so the equation is \(y = 4x\).”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Match — 13 minutes
Give students a card set with mixed representations. Example cards include:

- Verbal: “A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 batches.”
- Table: batches 2, 4, 6; flour 3, 6, 9
- Equation: \(y = 1.5x\)
- Graph: straight line through origin with points \((2,3), (4,6), (6,9)\)
- Diagram: groups showing 2 batches matched with 3 cups

Include one nonproportional “imposter,” such as a graph that does not pass through the origin or a table with inconsistent ratios.

Students work in pairs to match the representations and identify the imposter.

Teacher prompts:
- “How can you prove these cards belong together?”
- “What evidence tells you this relationship is proportional?”
- “Which card does not fit, and why?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students cross-check information across representations instead of relying on only one clue.

**Observe/Assess:** Students should verify matches using multiple forms of evidence. Look for statements such as:  
- “The table ratio is always \(3/2\), and the graph goes through the origin, so it is proportional.”  
- “This graph cannot match because the line does not pass through \((0,0)\).”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students choose high-quality evidence, such as constant ratios, origin, and equation form, rather than weak evidence like “the numbers look similar.”

**Observe/Assess:** Ask pairs to underline their strongest evidence. Look for responses such as:  
- “The best evidence is that every \(y/x\) value equals 1.5.”  
- “One matching point is not enough; we checked all the rows.”

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Build All Five Representations — 15 minutes
Give each group one proportional situation:

Examples:
1. “A runner travels 6 miles every hour.”
2. “A store sells 5 notebooks for $10.”
3. “A machine fills 12 bottles every 3 minutes.”

Each group creates a mini-poster with:
- Verbal description
- Table
- Equation
- Graph
- Diagram
- One sentence proving the relationship is proportional

Groups must also decide which representation would be most helpful for explaining the relationship to a younger student.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students compare representations and consider which is most useful for a purpose.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for discussion such as:  
- “The graph is good because you can see the straight line, but the equation is faster for finding any value.”  
- “A table is easier for a younger student because it shows the pattern step by step.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students justify their conclusion using proportional reasoning.

**Observe/Assess:** Check that students’ decisions are supported mathematically. Look for statements such as:  
- “We chose \(y = 2x\) because $10 divided by 5 notebooks is $2 per notebook.”  
- “Our graph is proportional because it is a straight line through the origin.”

---

### 5. Gallery Walk and Reflection — 7 minutes
Students walk around, view two other posters, and leave one sticky-note comment:
- “One representation that helped me understand was…”
- “One question I still have is…”

Then students return to seats and complete a short reflection:

“Today I first thought ____. Then I realized ____. One strategy I will use next time to check proportional relationships is ____.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students reflect on how their thinking changed and identify a future checking strategy.

**Observe/Assess:** Collect reflections and look for metacognitive evidence such as:  
- “At first I only checked if the numbers went up, but then I realized I needed to check the same ratio each time.”  
- “Next time I will check both the table ratios and whether the graph goes through the origin.”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 3 minutes
Students complete independently:

A relationship is shown in the table:

| \(x\) | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| \(y\) | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

1. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
2. Write an equation.  
3. Name one other representation you could use.

Expected answer: Yes, because \(y/x = 2.5\) for every pair; equation \(y = 2.5x\).

---

## Assessment Summary
Students demonstrate mastery when they can correctly identify proportional relationships, represent them in multiple ways, and justify their reasoning using constant ratios, equations of the form \(y = kx\), and graphs through the origin.

## 55-Minute Lesson: Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Grade:** Middle School Math  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Materials:** Board/projector, graph paper, rulers, “relationship cards” handout, exit ticket, colored pencils

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities have a proportional relationship.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Justify their reasoning using mathematical evidence.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two situations:

- **Situation A:** A bike rental costs $4 per hour.  
- **Situation B:** A bike rental costs $5 plus $4 per hour.

Students create a quick table for 0, 1, 2, and 3 hours and decide which relationship is proportional.

Teacher asks:
- “What do you notice about the cost when time is 0?”
- “Is there a constant multiplier?”
- “Which graph would pass through the origin?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students clarify relevant mathematical information by identifying the starting value, constant rate, and meaning of each quantity.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for or collect student statements such as:  
- “Situation A is proportional because at 0 hours the cost is $0, and each hour adds $4.”  
- “Situation B is not proportional because it starts at $5 before any hours.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain that a proportional relationship has the form:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.

Use the bike rental example to model five representations for Situation A:

- **Verbal:** The cost is $4 for each hour.
- **Table:**

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 4 | 8 | 12 |

- **Equation:** \(y = 4x\)
- **Graph:** A straight line through \((0,0)\)
- **Diagram:** 1 hour → $4, 2 hours → $8, 3 hours → $12

Emphasize that each representation should tell the same story.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students learn to arrange information across multiple representations in a logical way.

**Observe/Assess:** Students’ notes should show organized matching between table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description. Look for written evidence such as:  
- “The table, graph, and equation all show $4 per hour.”  
- “I put hours as \(x\) and cost as \(y\), so the equation is \(y = 4x\).”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Match — 13 minutes
Give students a card set with mixed representations. Example cards include:

- Verbal: “A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 batches.”
- Table: batches 2, 4, 6; flour 3, 6, 9
- Equation: \(y = 1.5x\)
- Graph: straight line through origin with points \((2,3), (4,6), (6,9)\)
- Diagram: groups showing 2 batches matched with 3 cups

Include one nonproportional “imposter,” such as a graph that does not pass through the origin or a table with inconsistent ratios.

Students work in pairs to match the representations and identify the imposter.

Teacher prompts:
- “How can you prove these cards belong together?”
- “What evidence tells you this relationship is proportional?”
- “Which card does not fit, and why?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students cross-check information across representations instead of relying on only one clue.

**Observe/Assess:** Students should verify matches using multiple forms of evidence. Look for statements such as:  
- “The table ratio is always \(3/2\), and the graph goes through the origin, so it is proportional.”  
- “This graph cannot match because the line does not pass through \((0,0)\).”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students choose high-quality evidence, such as constant ratios, origin, and equation form, rather than weak evidence like “the numbers look similar.”

**Observe/Assess:** Ask pairs to underline their strongest evidence. Look for responses such as:  
- “The best evidence is that every \(y/x\) value equals 1.5.”  
- “One matching point is not enough; we checked all the rows.”

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Build All Five Representations — 15 minutes
Give each group one proportional situation:

Examples:
1. “A runner travels 6 miles every hour.”
2. “A store sells 5 notebooks for $10.”
3. “A machine fills 12 bottles every 3 minutes.”

Each group creates a mini-poster with:
- Verbal description
- Table
- Equation
- Graph
- Diagram
- One sentence proving the relationship is proportional

Groups must also decide which representation would be most helpful for explaining the relationship to a younger student.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students compare representations and consider which is most useful for a purpose.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for discussion such as:  
- “The graph is good because you can see the straight line, but the equation is faster for finding any value.”  
- “A table is easier for a younger student because it shows the pattern step by step.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students justify their conclusion using proportional reasoning.

**Observe/Assess:** Check that students’ decisions are supported mathematically. Look for statements such as:  
- “We chose \(y = 2x\) because $10 divided by 5 notebooks is $2 per notebook.”  
- “Our graph is proportional because it is a straight line through the origin.”

---

### 5. Gallery Walk and Reflection — 7 minutes
Students walk around, view two other posters, and leave one sticky-note comment:
- “One representation that helped me understand was…”
- “One question I still have is…”

Then students return to seats and complete a short reflection:

“Today I first thought ____. Then I realized ____. One strategy I will use next time to check proportional relationships is ____.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
**Target level: 2**  
Students reflect on how their thinking changed and identify a future checking strategy.

**Observe/Assess:** Collect reflections and look for metacognitive evidence such as:  
- “At first I only checked if the numbers went up, but then I realized I needed to check the same ratio each time.”  
- “Next time I will check both the table ratios and whether the graph goes through the origin.”

Students complete independently:

A relationship is shown in the table:

| \(x\) | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| \(y\) | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

1. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
2. Write an equation.  
3. Name one other representation you could use.

Expected answer: Yes, because \(y/x = 2.5\) for every pair; equation \(y = 2.5x\).

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Lesson Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills through clarifying, organizing, evaluating, deciding, and reflecting on mathematical information.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "relationship cards" handout, mixed-source data handout, exit tickets, clipboard checklist.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Project a wordy, conversational scenario: *"So, if you buy 3 cupcakes, it costs $12. But yesterday, my friend bought 5 and paid $20. I think 1 cupcake is $4, but I'm not sure. Oh, and if you buy 0, it costs $0."*

Students rewrite the scenario as a precise mathematical statement on their whiteboards, isolating the unit rate and eliminating filler. Partners share, then the class debates: "What exactly does 'proportional' mean here?"

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Circulate with a clipboard. Level 2 students restate concepts precisely ("Cost = $4 × number of cupcakes; at 0 cupcakes, cost is $0, so the ratio is constant"). Level 1 students capture some facts but include irrelevant details. Level -1 students miscalculate the unit rate.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Introduce the form y = kx and model five representations using "$2 per pound of apples":
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $2 for every pound."
- **Table:** Pounds (1,2,3,4) → Cost (2,4,6,8)
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 1 lb → $2

Students fold paper into five labeled sections, drawing arrows between representations that share the constant k = 2.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect organizers. Level 2: clear labels, visible cross-connections, and a note such as "All five representations show k = 2." Level 1: boxes filled but no connections drawn. Level -1: scattered or contradictory entries.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Proportional or Imposter?" Card Sort (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a: Assessing Validity*
Distribute six mixed-representation cards (verbal, table, graph, equation, diagram). Include "imposters": a table with constant differences but not constant ratios; a graph not through the origin; y = 3x + 1.

In pairs, students sort cards and must cross-check each decision using **at least two representations** (e.g., "I verified the table ratio AND checked whether the graph passes through the origin").

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Ask, "How did you verify your answer using a second method?" Level 2 students cross-check across multiple representations citing the y = kx rule. Level 1 students rely on one feature. Level -1 students accept imposters without checking.

**Mini-extension — *Targets CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information*:** Present two sources for a water-usage problem: a hand-drawn, unscaled graph from a social media post and a formal data table from the City Water Authority. Ask: "Which source should you use to determine the constant of proportionality, and why?"

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Level 2 students select the Water Authority table, citing its precision and authority. Level 1 students notice a difference but don't justify quality. Level -1 students treat both sources as equally valid.

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: The Babysitting Dilemma (15 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
Present three pay options:
- **Option A:** $8 per hour flat
- **Option B:** $5 plus $7 per hour
- **Option C:** $40 for any job up to 5 hours, then $10/hour after

In groups of three, students build tables and graphs for each option, identify which are proportional (only A is y = kx), and write a recommendation specifying *which option for what length of job*.

**Assessment of CRI3a (Weighing pros/cons):** Review recommendations. Level 2 responses systematically evaluate trade-offs across scenarios ("For jobs under 2 hours, A wins; for 5+ hours, C is best because…"). Level 1 responses pick one option with minimal comparison. Level -1 responses ignore alternatives.

**Assessment of CRI4a (Sound reasoning):** Look for logical chains supported by mathematical evidence. Level 2: "Option B has a y-intercept of 5, so it's not proportional; it only beats A after 5 hours because the $5 start gets absorbed." Level 1: correct conclusion with skipped steps. Level -1: faulty logic or unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*
Exit ticket prompts:
1. Is y = 4x proportional? Represent it in a table and a graph.
2. **Reflection:** "What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What made you change your mind?"
3. "What strategy will you use first next time to test if something is proportional, and why?"

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Level 2 students articulate a strategic shift ("At first I thought Option B was proportional because it grew steadily, but I realized the +$5 means it doesn't pass through the origin—next time I'll check the y-intercept first"). Level 1 students mention reasoning without describing change. Level -1 students show no self-awareness.

---

### 6. Closure (2 minutes)
Share two strong reflections (with permission). Reinforce: proportional relationships have a constant ratio, pass through the origin, and take the form y = kx. Preview tomorrow's lesson on the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts.

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, schedules). Represent each in two ways and explain your reasoning.
