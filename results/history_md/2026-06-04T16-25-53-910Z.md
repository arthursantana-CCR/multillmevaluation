## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Detective: Investigating Relationships
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).
**Target Audience:** 7th Grade (Middle School)
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Setting:** Traditional Classroom

---

### **Lesson Objectives**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to determine if a relationship is proportional and translate that relationship across verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations, while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate their mathematical choices.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up & Introduction (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher presents a verbal scenario: *"A local bakery sells 3 chocolate chip cookies for $4.50. A competing bakery’s website claims they sell 5 of the same cookies for $7.50, and a third source, a food delivery app, lists 8 cookies for $12.00."* Students are asked to translate this verbal description into a mathematical format to see if the pricing is consistent. 

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must extract the relevant quantities (cookies and cost) from the wordy verbal description and clarify the core mathematical ideas to support a deeper understanding of the unit rate.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher circulates to observe if students are accurately pulling the correct numbers and labeling their units (Cost vs. Cookies). Students who precisely clarify the data without being distracted by irrelevant details (like the delivery app name) demonstrate mastery.
*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students are instructed to arrange their clarified data into a structured T-table (x = cookies, y = cost).
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses students' tables to ensure they have arranged the information with a logical flow (e.g., ordering the number of cookies from least to greatest) to strengthen their ability to spot the constant of proportionality.

---

### **Part 2: Guided Investigation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher projects the students' organized tables and asks, *"Is this a proportional relationship? How do we know for sure?"* The class transitions into graphing the data and writing an equation ($y = 1.5x$). The teacher then introduces a conflicting piece of information: a blog post claiming that buying in bulk is always a non-proportional relationship because of discounts. 

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students are tasked with verifying whether the blog post's claim applies to our specific bakery scenario. They must cross-check the blog's claim against two authoritative sources: their math textbook's definition of proportionality (a constant ratio) and an online graphing calculator (Desmos) to see if the line passes through the origin.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher listens to group discussions and evaluates whether students are rigorously relying on their authoritative math tools to debunk or verify the claim, rather than just guessing.
*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** The teacher provides three different data sets representing cookie prices from different sources (a peer's messy handwritten notes, a verified grocery store pricing database, and an anonymous social media comment). Students must select the highest-quality source to use for their final mathematical proof.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher asks groups to justify their source selection. Mastery is observed when students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, choosing the verified database because it provides authoritative, unambiguous data points for calculating the constant $k$.

---

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students work in small groups on the "Proportion Detective" task. They are given a new scenario: *"A taxi charges a $3 flat fee plus $2 per mile."* They must represent this in a table, graph, and equation, and then compare it to the bakery scenario. Finally, they must decide which representation (table, graph, or equation) is the best tool for predicting the cost of a 100-mile trip.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using a table, a graph, or an equation to find the 100-mile cost. 
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reviews the groups' written justifications. Students should note that while a graph is highly visual (pro), it is inefficient for large numbers like 100 (con). They should conclude that an equation ($y = 2x + 3$) is the most efficient long-term choice, demonstrating a comprehensive evaluation of their options.
*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students must make a final decision on whether the taxi scenario is proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher assesses the logical flow of the students' arguments during a class share-out. Students demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by anticipating complexities—specifically pointing out that while the rate per mile is constant, the $3 initial fee means the graph will not pass through the origin (0,0), therefore proving it is *not* proportional. 

---

### **Part 4: Closure & Metacognitive Reflection (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher wraps up the lesson by summarizing the four representations of proportional relationships (verbal, table, graph, equation). Students are then given an Exit Ticket focused entirely on their internal learning process during the taxi problem.

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** The Exit Ticket asks: *"At what point during the taxi problem did your thinking change? If you initially thought it was proportional, what specific step (table, graph, or equation) made you adjust your thinking, and how will you approach flat-fee problems differently in the future?"*
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher reads the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. High-level responses will explicitly identify a moment of self-correction (e.g., *"I assumed it was proportional because of the $2 per mile, but when I graphed it and saw it started at (0,3), I realized my assumption was wrong. Next time, I will check the y-intercept first."*).

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Materials
- Whiteboard or projector  
- Graph paper  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 5 minutes

Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $6  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $11  

Ask students: “Which deal is better? How do you know?”

Students write a quick response, then share with a partner. Invite 2–3 students to explain. Guide students toward comparing unit rates.

**Teacher emphasis:** A proportional relationship has a constant ratio between two quantities.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify what quantities are being compared: number of bars and total cost.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clarifying, “I need to compare dollars per bar,” or “The quantities are cost and number of bars.” Assess whether students identify relevant information clearly or confuse the quantities.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a relationship is proportional when:

1. The ratio between the two quantities is constant.
2. The graph is a straight line through the origin.
3. The equation can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.
4. A table shows equivalent ratios.

Use the example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $32 |

Ask:
- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “What equation represents this relationship?”
- “What would the graph look like?”

Students should identify **k = 8**, equation **y = 8x**, and a straight line through the origin.

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $10 |
| 2 | $18 |
| 3 | $26 |
| 4 | $34 |

Ask why it is not proportional. Students should notice the ratios are not constant.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the features of proportional relationships into a four-part checklist: table, graph, equation, verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check notebooks for a logical checklist. Look for organized statements such as “Table: constant ratio; Graph: line through origin; Equation: y = kx.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 15 minutes

Give students this scenario:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours at a constant speed.”

As a class, represent the relationship in five ways.

1. **Verbal description:** The cyclist travels 6 miles every hour.  
2. **Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

3. **Equation:** y = 6x  
4. **Graph:** Plot points and draw a straight line through the origin.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw equal groups or a double number line showing 1 hour = 6 miles, 2 hours = 12 miles, etc.

Ask students to explain how each representation shows proportionality.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing Validity of Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and verbal description all match the same relationship.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to justify: “How do you know your graph matches your equation?” Listen for valid checks such as “When x = 2, y = 12 in both the equation and the table.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence that the relationship is proportional.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Students write one sentence: “The strongest evidence is ___ because ___.” Look for relevant reasoning, such as choosing the graph because it is a straight line through the origin, or the table because the ratio is constant.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give each pair a handout with four relationships.

**A.**  
| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |

**B.**  
| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 | 7 |
| 3 | 10 |

**C.** A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.

**D.** A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

For each, students must:
1. Decide whether it is proportional.
2. Explain their reasoning.
3. Represent proportional relationships in at least two ways.
4. For non-proportional relationships, explain what prevents proportionality.

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- “What ratio are you checking?”
- “Does the graph go through the origin?”
- “Can this be written as y = kx?”
- “What does the starting fee do to the relationship?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare representation choices. For example, they decide whether a table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal explanation best supports their conclusion.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for comparison language: “A graph is helpful because we can see the origin, but a table helps us check the ratios.” Students should not simply choose one representation without considering another.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students make a final decision about whether each relationship is proportional and defend it with mathematical evidence.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review partner responses for logical claims supported by evidence, such as “C is not proportional because the $4 starting fee means the graph would not pass through the origin.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Bring the class together. Discuss the taxi example because it often causes confusion.

Ask: “The taxi cost increases by the same amount each mile. Why isn’t it proportional?”

Guide students to understand that a constant increase alone does not always mean proportional. A proportional relationship must have a constant ratio and include the point (0, 0).

Have students revise one answer on their handout using a different colored pencil.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on whether their first answer changed and why.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for metacognitive comments such as “At first I thought it was proportional because it increased by 2 each time, but now I realize the starting fee means the ratio is not constant.”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete individually:

1. A lemonade mix uses 4 cups of water for every 1 cup of lemon juice. Complete a table for 0, 1, 2, 3 cups of lemon juice.  
2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. Explain how you know it is proportional.  
4. A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month. Is this proportional? Explain.

Collect exit tickets.

---

## Assessment Notes

Use student written explanations and discussion comments as evidence. For each critical thinking subcompetency, assign a level from -1 to 2 when appropriate and cite a short quote from the student’s work or speech.

Examples of evidence to collect:
- **CRI1a:** “The two quantities are cups of lemon juice and cups of water.”
- **CRI1b:** “I made a table first, then used it to write the equation.”
- **CRI2a:** “The equation and table match because when x = 3, y = 12.”
- **CRI2b:** “The graph is strongest because it shows a line through the origin.”
- **CRI3a:** “The table shows ratios better, but the graph shows the origin better.”
- **CRI4a:** “The gym is not proportional because the $20 fee means it does not start at zero.”
- **CRI5a:** “I changed my answer after checking the ratios.”
