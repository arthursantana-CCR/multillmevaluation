## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Framework:** CCR Critical Thinking

---

## Lesson Overview

Students will investigate the debate around homework in schools by examining evidence, evaluating sources, weighing perspectives, and constructing reasoned arguments. By the end of the lesson, students will be able to articulate a well-supported position on whether schools should assign homework, demonstrating each of the CCR Critical Thinking subcompetencies.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will:
- Define what "homework" means in an educational context and identify key arguments on both sides
- Evaluate the reliability and quality of sources used in the debate
- Weigh the pros and cons of assigning homework using structured reasoning
- Make and defend a decision using logical argumentation
- Reflect on how their thinking evolved throughout the lesson

---

## Materials

- Printed or digital source cards (4 short excerpts: a research study, a teacher opinion blog, a student survey, and a news article)
- Graphic organizer worksheet (T-chart + source evaluation grid)
- Sticky notes (two colors)
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

### **Warm-Up — 5 Minutes**

Open with a quick show of hands: *"Do you think homework helps you learn?"* Then ask: *"What does the word 'homework' actually mean to you? Is all homework the same?"*

Write student responses on the board. This surface-level brainstorm sets up **CRI1a (Clarifying Information)**. Students are invited to notice that "homework" is not one thing — it can mean reading, projects, repetitive drills, or creative tasks. This distinction matters for the rest of the lesson.

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who volunteer precise distinctions (e.g., "practice homework vs. project homework"). Note which students offer vague responses and which begin to refine their definitions when pushed.

---

### **Activity 1: Source Carousel — 12 Minutes**

Place four source cards around the room. Each card presents a different type of voice in the homework debate:

1. A 2023 Stanford research summary showing diminishing returns of homework beyond 90 minutes
2. A teacher's blog arguing homework builds discipline and responsibility
3. A student survey from a local school district showing 68% of students feel homework causes stress
4. A news article citing a school district that eliminated homework and saw no drop in test scores

Students rotate in small groups, reading each source and completing a **source evaluation grid** asking: *Who wrote this? Why might they hold this view? How trustworthy is this information?*

**CRI2a (Assessing Validity):** Students cross-check claims — for example, noticing that the teacher blog has no citations while the Stanford summary references peer-reviewed data.

**CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** Students rank sources from most to least reliable and justify their ranking in one sentence per source.

**Assessment:** Collect source evaluation grids. Look for students who go beyond "it's from the internet" to identify authorship, purpose, and corroboration across sources.

---

### **Activity 2: Organizing the Debate — 10 Minutes**

Using their graphic organizer T-chart, students individually organize the evidence they found into two columns: *Arguments FOR homework* and *Arguments AGAINST homework*. They must place each piece of evidence from the carousel into one side and write a brief explanation of why it belongs there.

**CRI1b (Organizing Information):** The T-chart requires students to move from scattered notes to a coherent logical structure. Students who organize effectively will group related ideas (e.g., academic benefit arguments together) rather than listing points randomly.

**Assessment:** Walk the room and check T-charts for logical grouping and consistency. Ask one or two students to explain why they placed a particular piece of evidence where they did.

---

### **Activity 3: Weighing Trade-Offs — 12 Minutes**

In pairs, students choose the *two strongest arguments* from each side of their T-chart and discuss: *"If a school eliminated homework entirely, what would be gained and what might be lost? Who benefits most, and who might be hurt?"*

Encourage students to think about different groups: students with no quiet space at home, high-achieving students, families who rely on homework to track progress, teachers planning curriculum.

**CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Students move from listing points to evaluating trade-offs with awareness of real-world complexity and different stakeholders.

**Assessment:** Listen to pair conversations. Look for students who acknowledge that one solution does not fit all students — this signals systematic evaluation rather than surface-level comparison.

---

### **Activity 4: Making a Reasoned Decision — 10 Minutes**

Each student writes a 4–5 sentence position statement answering: *"Should schools have homework? Under what conditions, if any?"* They must include at least one piece of evidence, acknowledge one counterargument, and explain their reasoning.

**CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** A strong response does not simply say "yes" or "no" but proposes a nuanced stance — for example, *"Schools should assign homework only when it serves a clear purpose and does not exceed 30 minutes, because the Stanford data shows longer assignments increase stress without improving learning."*

**Assessment:** Collect position statements. Evaluate whether the reasoning is logical, whether evidence is used accurately, and whether the student anticipates complexity rather than oversimplifying.

---

### **Exit Ticket — 6 Minutes**

Students respond in writing to two prompts on their exit slip:

1. *"What did you believe at the start of class, and what do you believe now? What changed your thinking?"*
2. *"What is one question you still have that the evidence did not fully answer?"*

**CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):** The first question targets metacognition directly — students must identify a shift (or acknowledge there was none) and explain what caused it. The second question pushes students to recognize the limits of their current reasoning.

**Assessment:** Read exit tickets for depth of self-reflection. A level-2 response might say: *"I came in thinking homework was always bad, but the source about self-discipline made me reconsider. I changed my position to 'it depends on the type.' I still wonder whether the benefits are the same for all grade levels."*

---

## Closing Teacher Note

Bring the class together for a 2-minute verbal debrief. Ask: *"Did anyone's position surprise you — including your own?"* This brief moment reinforces that good critical thinking is not about being right from the start; it is about being willing to revise.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Debating the role of homework in schools

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify key arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support different perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of various information sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of different approaches to homework.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form a preliminary stance on the topic.
*   Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the discussion.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (short articles/summaries presenting arguments for and against homework, with varied source types – e.g., excerpt from an educational research summary, a school district's rationale, a student blog post, a parent's opinion piece).
*   Handout 2: T-chart graphic organizer
*   Exit Tickets
*   Markers/pens

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, everyone! Today, we're going to tackle a topic that affects all of us: homework. How many of you love homework? How many of you wish it didn't exist? (Allow for a quick show of hands and brief, informal comments). It's clear there are strong feelings on both sides. Today, we're going to explore this question: 'Should schools have homework?' and use our critical thinking skills to understand the different perspectives."

**II. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) & Initial Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1a: Clarifying information):** Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
    *   **Teacher:** "You'll find several short texts here, each presenting different viewpoints or facts about homework. Your first task is to read through these texts individually. As you read, I want you to do two things:
        1.  **Highlight** the main arguments or key pieces of information presented in each text.
        2.  **Circle any words or phrases** you don't understand, and try to define them using context or by asking a classmate."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1a):** Circulate around the room. Observe students' highlighting – are they identifying the core claims? Listen to their discussions about unfamiliar vocabulary. Are they attempting to clarify ideas precisely? I will look for evidence that students are accurately identifying and defining key terms and arguments, rather than just random words or superficial details. For example, if a student highlights "Homework reinforces classroom learning" and can explain what "reinforces" means in this context, they are clarifying information precisely.

**III. Organizing Information (CRI1b) & Assessing Validity/Quality (CRI2a, CRI2b) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1b: Organizing information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've read and highlighted, let's organize this information. In your small groups (3-4 students), use Handout 2, the T-chart, to list the 'Pros of Homework' on one side and the 'Cons of Homework' on the other. Make sure to draw your points directly from the texts."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1b):** Review students' T-charts. Are the points clearly categorized? Is the information presented in a structured and consistent manner? For example, are all "pros" on one side and "cons" on the other, without mixing?
*   **Activity (CRI2a: Assessing validity of information & CRI2b: Assessing quality of information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "As you're filling out your T-chart, I want you to also think critically about *where* this information is coming from. Look at the source listed for each text on Handout 1. Discuss in your groups:
        *   'How reliable do you think this source is?' (CRI2a)
        *   'Does this source provide high-quality information, or is it more opinion-based?' (CRI2b)
        *   'Which pieces of evidence from the texts do you think are the *strongest* to support either side, and why?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI2a & CRI2b):** Listen to group discussions. Do students question the authority of a blog post versus a research summary? Do they identify potential biases? Are they able to articulate *why* certain information is more credible or relevant than others? I will look for students who can explain, for instance, "The study from the Department of Education is high-quality because it's based on research data, not just one person's feelings," demonstrating sophisticated judgment.

**IV. Weighing Pros & Cons & Applying Reasoning (CRI3a, CRI4a) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've organized and evaluated the information, let's delve deeper. In your groups, discuss the following:
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools completely eliminated homework?'
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools assigned a lot more homework?'
        *   'Consider the perspectives of different stakeholders: students, teachers, parents. How might each group weigh these pros and cons differently?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI3a):** Circulate and listen to group discussions. Are students moving beyond simple listing to actively compare and evaluate the trade-offs? Do they consider the broader impact and different viewpoints? I'll note if students are discussing consequences like "If there's no homework, students might forget what they learned faster" or "Too much homework could lead to burnout and less family time," showing they are systematically evaluating implications.
*   **Activity (CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):**
    *   **Teacher:** "After your group discussion, I want each of you to individually write a short paragraph (3-4 sentences) stating your preliminary stance on the question: 'Should schools have homework?' You can say 'yes,' 'no,' or 'it depends/modified approach.' Crucially, you must provide at least two clear reasons to support your stance, drawing directly from the information we've discussed and evaluated today."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI4a):** Collect and read the individual paragraphs. Is the reasoning logical and coherent? Do their conclusions align with the evidence they present? Are there any unsupported claims or faulty logic? I will look for clear connections between their stance and the evidence provided, e.g., "I believe schools should have *some* homework because it helps reinforce learning (Pro 1) and teaches responsibility (Pro 2), as mentioned in the research summary."

**V. Reflecting on Thinking Processes (CRI5a) & Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes):**
    *   **Teacher:** "To conclude our lesson, I want you to reflect on your own thinking today. Please complete the exit ticket."
    *   **Exit Ticket Prompt:**
        *   "My initial thought on homework before this lesson was..."
        *   "After today's lesson, my thinking has [changed / stayed the same / become more nuanced] because..."
        *   "One thing I found challenging about evaluating the information or forming my opinion was..., and I tried to overcome it by..."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI5a):** Collect and review exit tickets. Do students demonstrate metacognitive awareness? Can they articulate how their understanding evolved or deepened? Do they identify specific challenges in their thinking and describe strategies they used to address them? I will look for responses that show genuine self-awareness, such as "Initially, I just hated homework, but after seeing the arguments about responsibility, I now think a little bit is okay. It was hard to let go of my bias, but I tried to focus only on the facts from the research."

*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Provide students with mixed-up statements about homework’s benefits and drawbacks on index cards.  
- In small groups, students will organize these cards into two categories: Pros and Cons.  
- Then, groups arrange the cards in a logical order to present a coherent argument for each side.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Assess how well students group related ideas and sequence them logically (e.g., starting with the most important points).  
- Evidence of level 2: Students create a clear, logical flow, such as grouping academic benefits first, then social or emotional impacts.

*Time: 10 minutes*

- Students discuss in pairs which source seems more reliable and why.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Look for students identifying source types and evaluating credibility.  
- Evidence of level 2: Students cross-check claims, e.g., “The research article cites studies and experts, while the blog only shares personal opinions without evidence.”  
- Note if students accept unreliable sources without critique (level -1).  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Students review a list of facts about homework and decide which are most relevant and trustworthy to use in an argument.  
- They justify their choices briefly in writing.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Evaluate students’ judgment in selecting high-quality, authoritative information.  
- Evidence of level 2: Students choose data from studies or official reports rather than anecdotes or vague statements.

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Observe if students systematically evaluate trade-offs and consider multiple perspectives.  
- Evidence of level 2: Students mention comprehensive implications, e.g., “Homework can improve skills but may also increase stress and reduce family time.”  
- Note if students ignore alternative views or present one-sided arguments (level -1).  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Individually, students write a short opinion statement answering: “Should schools have homework? Why or why not?”

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Assess clarity and logic of reasoning, looking for anticipation of complexities (e.g., acknowledging exceptions or conditions).  
- Evidence of level 2: Students provide nuanced reasoning, e.g., “Homework should be assigned but limited in amount to balance learning and well-being.”  
- Watch for faulty logic or unsupported claims (level -1).  

---

*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** End with a metacognitive prompt: “What did you learn about your own thinking today? Did your opinion change? Why?”

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Look for evidence of deep reflection and strategic adjustment of thinking.  
- Evidence of level 2: Students recognize assumptions, reconsider evidence, or describe how they evaluated sources differently.  
- Note if students show no awareness or repeat errors without reflection (level -1).
