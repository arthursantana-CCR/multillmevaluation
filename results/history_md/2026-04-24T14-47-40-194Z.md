## Generator
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan for 7th Grade ELA

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Central Idea:** Curiosity — the habit of asking genuine questions and exploring what we don't yet know — is not just a personality trait. It is a *writing skill*. The best argumentative writers are deeply curious people who ask hard questions, seek out evidence, and genuinely consider opposing views before staking a claim.

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Explain curiosity as an active, learnable habit rather than a fixed trait
2. Generate authentic questions about a complex topic as a pre-writing strategy
3. Draft a structured argumentative paragraph that includes a claim, reasoning, evidence, and acknowledgment of a counterclaim
4. Reflect on how curiosity strengthened their argument

**Materials:**
- Whiteboard and markers
- Student journals or lined paper
- Printed "Wonder Strips" (small slips of paper — see Activity 2)
- One printed "Evidence Card Set" per group of 4 (see Activity 3)
- Exit ticket slips (half-sheets)

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### SEGMENT 1: THE HOOK — "The Worst Argument I Ever Heard" *(5 minutes)*

**Teacher opens with this story (told with energy and humor):**

> "Last year, two students in a class I worked with had an argument about whether dogs or cats make better pets. The first student said, 'Dogs are better because dogs are just better. Everyone knows this.' End of argument. The second student said, 'That's not true because cats are better and you're wrong.' Now — hands up — who won that argument?"

*(Pause. Students will likely laugh or say neither.)*

> "Exactly. Nobody won. You know why? Because neither of them was *curious*. They already had their mind made up before the conversation even started. They weren't asking questions. They weren't looking for evidence. They weren't even wondering if the other person might have a point. Today we're going to talk about what it actually takes to make a strong argument — and spoiler alert: it starts with being genuinely curious."

**Write on the board:**
> *"Curiosity is not the opposite of having an opinion. It's what makes your opinion worth listening to."*

Ask students: "What do you think that means? Turn and tell your neighbor." *(30-second pair share.)*

---

### SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Curiosity Actually Is *(8 minutes)*

**Teacher leads a short, interactive mini-lecture.**

Say to students:

> "When I say curiosity, I don't mean being nosy. I mean something specific — it's the feeling of *intellectual discomfort* when you realize you don't fully understand something, combined with the drive to go find out. Scientists call it 'perceptual curiosity.' Detectives live on it. Great writers depend on it."

**Write the "Curiosity Cycle" on the board:**

```
NOTICE something confusing or surprising
        ↓
ASK a genuine question (not one you already know the answer to)
        ↓
SEEK information from multiple sources
        ↓
SIT WITH the discomfort of complicated answers
        ↓
FORM a more honest, more defensible opinion
```

> "Here's the key word up there: *genuine*. A genuine question is one where you don't already know the answer and you actually care about finding out. Most people skip this whole cycle and jump straight to 'forming an opinion.' But when you do that, your opinion has no roots. A good argument is like a tree. The opinion is the trunk. But curiosity? Curiosity is the root system."

**Connect to the writing standard:**

> "The Common Core standard you're working toward — writing arguments with clear reasons, relevant evidence, *and* acknowledgment of opposing claims — that last part is where most students fall apart. Why? Because you can't honestly acknowledge a counterclaim unless you've actually been curious enough to take it seriously. You have to genuinely wonder, 'Wait — could they be right?'"

**Quick comprehension check:** Ask the class: "What's the difference between a genuine question and a fake one?" Take 2-3 responses.

*(Likely answers: you already know the answer to a fake one / fake questions are rhetorical / genuine questions make you feel uncertain. Affirm all reasonable answers.)*

---

### SEGMENT 3: ACTIVITY 1 — "The Wonder Sprint" *(7 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Practice generating genuine questions as a curiosity-building pre-writing strategy.

**Setup:**
Announce the topic students will eventually write about:

> **"Should middle school students be required to do community service in order to graduate?"**

Say: "Before you form ANY opinion, you're going to spend four minutes doing nothing but asking questions. Not answering them. Just asking. The rule is: every question has to be one where you genuinely don't know the answer."

**Instructions:**
1. Students open journals to a blank page and write "WONDER SPRINT" at the top
2. Set a timer for **4 minutes**
3. Students write as many genuine questions about the topic as they can — no answers, no opinions, just questions
4. Teacher circulates, prompting students who get stuck with nudges like:
   - *"Who does this affect?"*
   - *"What would make this work or not work?"*
   - *"What would someone who disagrees with you wonder about?"*
   - *"What do you actually not know?"*

**Debrief (2-3 minutes):**
Ask students to share their questions aloud. Write a sampling on the board. Then ask:

> "Look at all these questions. Do any of them surprise you? Did anyone write a question that made you realize — wait, I actually don't know the answer to this, and it matters?"

Highlight 2-3 questions from the board that represent genuine intellectual uncertainty — especially ones that would make the argument *harder* to write, not easier.

Say: "Those hard questions? Those are gold. A writer who asks the hard questions earns the trust of their reader."

---

### SEGMENT 4: ACTIVITY 2 — "Evidence in the Wild" *(10 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Model how curiosity drives evidence-gathering and how evidence complicates (and strengthens) arguments.

**Setup:**
Students work in groups of 4. Each group receives an **Evidence Card Set** — 6-8 printed "cards" (index cards or cut paper) each containing one piece of evidence about the community service topic. The evidence set deliberately includes:
- Statistics that support requiring community service
- Statistics that argue against it
- Quotes from students who benefited
- Quotes from students who felt it was burdensome
- A finding about intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation
- Data on graduation rates and community service correlation
- A counterintuitive result (e.g., mandatory service sometimes reduces voluntary service later in life)

*(Teacher note: You can generate 6-8 brief, plausible evidence statements ahead of time. Keep each card to 2-3 sentences. Label each card A through H.)*

**Sample Evidence Cards:**

> **Card A:** A 2019 survey of 1,200 high school graduates found that 67% who completed required community service reported feeling "more connected to their community" after graduation.

> **Card B:** Researchers at the University of Maryland found that students who were *required* to volunteer were 34% less likely to volunteer on their own as adults, compared to students who chose to volunteer.

> **Card C:** Maryland is the only U.S. state that requires community service for high school graduation and has maintained this requirement since 1992.

> **Card D:** A student quoted in *Education Week*: "I resented every hour of it. It felt like punishment, not service."

> **Card E:** Studies on motivation show that when people are rewarded or required to do something they might naturally enjoy, the external pressure can reduce their internal desire to do it — a phenomenon called "the overjustification effect."

> **Card F:** Schools with community service requirements report higher rates of civic engagement among alumni in local elections.

> **Card G:** Many low-income students struggle to complete service hours because they work after-school jobs, raising equity concerns about such requirements.

> **Card H:** In a pilot program in Portland, Oregon, students who chose their own service projects reported 2x higher satisfaction than those assigned to projects.

**Group Task (6 minutes):**

Each group must:
1. Read all cards aloud together
2. Answer these three questions in their journals:
   - Which piece of evidence surprised you the most? Why?
   - Which piece of evidence would someone who *disagrees* with you use? (You must name what that disagreement is.)
   - Did any evidence make you *change or complicate* your thinking?

**Whole-class debrief (3-4 minutes):**

> "Raise your hand if any of the evidence changed your thinking or made it more complicated." *(Expect several hands.)*

> "That feeling of your thinking getting more complicated? That's curiosity doing its job. A curious writer doesn't ignore the cards that make things harder. They use those cards to make their argument *smarter*."

---

### SEGMENT 5: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Anatomy of an Argument Paragraph *(5 minutes)*

**Teacher models the structure on the board using the acronym C-R-E-E-C:**

| Letter | Stands For | What It Sounds Like |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **C** | **Claim** | "Middle school students should / should not be required to complete community service." |
| **R** | **Reason** | "Because..." (your logical reasoning) |
| **E** | **Evidence** | "For example, research shows..." (cite from cards) |
| **E** | **Explain** | "This matters because..." (connect evidence to claim) |
| **C** | **Counterclaim + Response** | "Some people argue... However..." |

Say: "Notice that last step. The counterclaim. Most students skip it because it feels like weakening your own argument. But here's the curious writer's secret: *acknowledging a counterclaim actually makes you look stronger, not weaker.* It shows the reader you've thought this through honestly."

**Model one completed example verbally:**

> "Middle school students should NOT be required to complete community service in order to graduate. The purpose of community service is to build genuine civic values, but research on motivation suggests that requiring an activity can destroy the very desire it's meant to create — a phenomenon called the overjustification effect. In fact, a study found that students who were required to volunteer were 34% less likely to volunteer as adults compared to those who chose to do so, suggesting that mandatory service may backfire on its own goals. Some might argue that community service builds connection — and indeed, 67% of students who completed required service felt more tied to their community. However, this benefit appears to come at the long-term cost of self-motivated civic engagement, making optional or incentivized service a more effective approach."

> "Notice what I did there? I was *curious* enough to use the evidence that made my argument harder. And because I used it honestly, the argument is stronger."

---

### SEGMENT 6: ACTIVITY 3 — STUDENT WRITING *(12 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Students apply the curiosity cycle and the C-R-E-E-C structure to produce an argumentative paragraph.

**Instructions:**

1. Students decide their position: *Should* or *Should Not* require community service
2. Students draft one full C-R-E-E-C paragraph independently in their journals
3. They must use **at least two evidence cards** — including at least one that complicates or challenges their position
4. They should circle the counterclaim sentence(s) when finished

**Teacher circulates** during writing, offering targeted coaching:
- To students who skip the counterclaim: *"Where's the 'some people argue' part? What's the strongest thing someone on the other side would say?"*
- To students who use only easy evidence: *"What's the card that makes your argument hardest? Can you use it?"*
- To students stuck on a position: *"You don't have to be 100% certain. What do you lean toward, and why?"*

**Pair share (2 minutes at end of writing time):**
Students read their paragraph to a partner. Partner answers: "What was the counterclaim? Do you think it was addressed honestly?"

---

### SEGMENT 7: CLOSING — "The Curiosity Reflection" *(8 minutes)*

**Whole-class discussion (4 minutes):**

Bring the class back together. Ask:

1. **"What question from your Wonder Sprint ended up being the most useful to your writing?"**
2. **"Did writing this argument change your mind at all — even a little?"**
3. **"How is a curious writer different from a stubborn writer?"**

Guide students toward this synthesis:

> "Stubborn writers start with a conclusion and only look for reasons to support it. We have a name for that — it's called *confirmation bias*, and it makes for terrible arguments because any smart reader can punch holes in it. Curious writers start with questions, gather real evidence, take the opposition seriously, and *then* form a position. Their arguments are harder to knock down because they were built honestly."

**Exit Ticket (4 minutes):**

Hand out half-sheet exit tickets. Students answer TWO questions:

**1.** Finish this sentence: *"Before today, I thought curiosity was ________. Now I think it's ________."*

**2.** Write one sentence that describes how you used curiosity in your argumentative paragraph today.

Collect exit tickets as students leave.

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a sentence starter bank:
  - *"I chose this position because..."*
  - *"One piece of evidence that supports my claim is..."*
  - *"Someone who disagrees might say..., but I would respond that..."*
- Allow students to dictate their paragraph to a partner who writes it down
- Pre-highlight 2 evidence cards for them to use (one supporting, one opposing)

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to write TWO paragraphs taking *opposite* positions using the same evidence, then reflect: which was stronger and why?
- Ask: "Could you write this argument without taking a side at all? What would that look like, and why might that be valuable in some contexts?"
- Have them evaluate the quality of the evidence cards: "Which card is the weakest evidence? Why?"

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide a bilingual glossary of key terms: *claim, evidence, counterclaim, reasoning*
- Allow Wonder Sprint questions to be written in home language first, then translated
- Pair with a bilingual peer during group work

Scientists call it 'perceptual curiosity.' Detectives live on it. Great writers depend on it.\"

**Write the \"Curiosity Cycle\" on the board:**


NOTICE something confusing or surprising
        ↓
ASK a genuine question (not one you already know the answer to)
        ↓
SEEK information from multiple sources
        ↓
SIT WITH the discomfort of complicated answers
        ↓
FORM a more honest, more defensible opinion


> \"Here's the key word up there: *genuine*. A genuine question is one where you don't already know the answer and you actually care about finding out. Most people skip this whole cycle and jump straight to 'forming an opinion.' But when you do that, your opinion has no roots. A good argument is like a tree. The opinion is the trunk. But curiosity? Curiosity is the root system.\"

**Connect to the writing standard:**

> \"The Common Core standard you're working toward — writing arguments with clear reasons, relevant evidence, *and* acknowledgment of opposing claims — that last part is where most students fall apart. Why? Because you can't honestly acknowledge a counterclaim unless you've actually been curious enough to take it seriously. You have to genuinely wonder, 'Wait — could they be right?'\"

**Quick comprehension check:** Ask the class: \"What's the difference between a genuine question and a fake one?\" Take 2-3 responses.

*(Likely answers: you already know the answer to a fake one / fake questions are rhetorical / genuine questions make you feel uncertain. Affirm all reasonable answers.)*

### SEGMENT 3: ACTIVITY 1 — \"The Wonder Sprint\" *(7 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Practice generating genuine questions as a curiosity-building pre-writing strategy.

**Setup:**
Announce the topic students will eventually write about:

> **\"Should middle school students be required to do community service in order to graduate?\"**

Say: \"Before you form ANY opinion, you're going to spend four minutes doing nothing but asking questions. Not answering them. Just asking. The rule is: every question has to be one where you genuinely don't know the answer.\"

**Instructions:**
1. Students open journals to a blank page and write \"WONDER SPRINT\" at the top
2. Set a timer for **4 minutes**
3. Students write as many genuine questions about the topic as they can — no answers, no opinions, just questions
4. Teacher circulates, prompting students who get stuck with nudges like:
   - *\"Who does this affect?\"*
   - *\"What would make this work or not work?\"*
   - *\"What would someone who disagrees with you wonder about?\"*
   - *\"What do you actually not know?\"*

**Debrief (2-3 minutes):**
Ask students to share their questions aloud. Write a sampling on the board. Then ask:

> \"Look at all these questions. Do any of them surprise you? Did anyone write a question that made you realize — wait, I actually don't know the answer to this, and it matters?\"

Highlight 2-3 questions from the board that represent genuine intellectual uncertainty — especially ones that would make the argument *harder* to write, not easier.

Say: \"Those hard questions? Those are gold. A writer who asks the hard questions earns the trust of their reader.\"

---

### SEGMENT 4: ACTIVITY 2 — \"Evidence in the Wild\" *(10 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Model how curiosity drives evidence-gathering and how evidence complicates (and strengthens) arguments.

**Setup:**
Students work in groups of 4. Each group receives an **Evidence Card Set** — 6-8 printed \"cards\" (index cards or cut paper) each containing one piece of evidence about the community service topic. The evidence set deliberately includes a variety of perspectives and types of evidence.

*(Teacher note: You can generate 6-8 brief, plausible evidence statements ahead of time. Keep each card to 2-3 sentences. Label each card A through H.)*

**Group Task (6 minutes):**

Each group must:
1. Read all cards aloud together
2. Answer these three questions in their journals:
   - Which piece of evidence surprised you the most? Why?
   - Which piece of evidence would someone who *disagrees* with you use? (You must name what that disagreement is.)
   - Did any evidence make you *change or complicate* your thinking?

**Whole-class debrief (3-4 minutes):**

> \"Raise your hand if any of the evidence changed your thinking or made it more complicated.\" *(Expect several hands.)*

> \"That feeling of your thinking getting more complicated? That's curiosity doing its job. A curious writer doesn't ignore the cards that make things harder. They use those cards to make their argument *smarter*.\"

### SEGMENT 5: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — Anatomy of an Argument Paragraph *(5 minutes)*

**Teacher models the structure on the board using the acronym C-R-E-E-C:**

| Letter | Stands For | What It Sounds Like |
|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **C** | **Claim** | \"Middle school students should / should not be required to complete community service.\" |
| **R** | **Reason** | \"Because...\" (your logical reasoning) |
| **E** | **Evidence** | \"For example, research shows...\" (cite from cards) |
| **E** | **Explain** | \"This matters because...\" (connect evidence to claim) |
| **C** | **Counterclaim + Response** | \"Some people argue... However...\" |

Say: \"Notice that last step. The counterclaim. Most students skip it because it feels like weakening your own argument. But here's the curious writer's secret: *acknowledging a counterclaim actually makes you look stronger, not weaker.* It shows the reader you've thought this through honestly.\"

**Model one completed example verbally:**

> \"Middle school students should NOT be required to complete community service in order to graduate. The purpose of community service is to build genuine civic values, but research on motivation suggests that requiring an activity can sometimes reduce the very desire it's meant to create — a phenomenon called the overjustification effect. In fact, some studies suggest that mandatory service may backfire on its own goals. Some might argue that community service builds connection — and indeed, many students report feeling more tied to their community after completing service. However, this benefit appears to come at the long-term cost of self-motivated civic engagement, making optional or incentivized service a more effective approach.\"

> \"Notice what I did there? I was *curious* enough to use the evidence that made my argument harder. And because I used it honestly, the argument is stronger.\"

### SEGMENT 6: ACTIVITY 3 — STUDENT WRITING *(12 minutes)*

**Purpose:** Students apply the curiosity cycle and the C-R-E-E-C structure to produce an argumentative paragraph.

**Instructions:**

1. Students decide their position: *Should* or *Should Not* require community service
2. Students draft one full C-R-E-E-C paragraph independently in their journals
3. They must use **at least two evidence cards** — including at least one that complicates or challenges their position
4. They should circle the counterclaim sentence(s) when finished

**Teacher circulates** during writing, offering targeted coaching:
- To students who skip the counterclaim: *\"Where's the 'some people argue' part? What's the strongest thing someone on the other side would say?\"*
- To students who use only easy evidence: *\"What's the card that makes your argument hardest? Can you use it?\"*
- To students stuck on a position: *\"You don't have to be 100% certain. What do you lean toward, and why?\"*

**Pair share (2 minutes at end of writing time):**
Students read their paragraph to a partner. Partner answers: \"What was the counterclaim? Do you think it was addressed honestly?\"

### SEGMENT 7: CLOSING — \"The Curiosity Reflection\" *(8 minutes)*

**Whole-class discussion (4 minutes):**

Bring the class back together. Ask:

1. **\"What question from your Wonder Sprint ended up being the most useful to your writing?\"**
2. **\"Did writing this argument change your mind at all — even a little?\"**
3. **\"How is a curious writer different from a stubborn writer?\"**\
