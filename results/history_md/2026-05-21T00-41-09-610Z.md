## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with 4 scenarios (some proportional, some not), exit ticket.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is This a Fair Deal?" (8 minutes)
Display two pricing options on the board:
- **Store A:** 3 notebooks for $6, 5 notebooks for $10, 8 notebooks for $16
- **Store B:** 2 notebooks for $5, 4 notebooks for $9, 6 notebooks for $12

**Prompt:** "Which store charges a constant price per notebook? How do you know?"

Students discuss in pairs, then share. Teacher records observations, guiding students to compute unit rates and notice that Store A maintains $2/notebook while Store B does not.

**→ CRI1a (Clarifying information):** As students explain their reasoning, push them to define terms precisely: "What do you mean by 'fair'? What does 'constant' mean mathematically?"
- *Assessment:* Listen for students restating ideas using precise language like "unit rate" or "constant ratio." Record on a clipboard rubric (Level 1: identifies the ratio; Level 2: clarifies why equivalent ratios indicate proportionality).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the definition: A proportional relationship exists when two quantities have a constant ratio (k), expressible as y = kx.

Walk through Store A using all five representations:
- **Verbal:** "Each notebook costs $2."
- **Table:** Display (3,6), (5,10), (8,16).
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin.
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing 1 notebook = $2 unit.

Emphasize two tests: (1) constant ratio y/x, (2) graph passes through origin.

**→ CRI1b (Organizing information):** Have students copy a four-column graphic organizer (Representation | Example | How to Check Proportionality | Key Feature).
- *Assessment:* Collect organizers at lesson end; Level 2 work shows logical sequencing where each representation connects back to k.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Scenarios (12 minutes)
Distribute a handout with four scenarios (mix of tables, graphs, verbal descriptions). Examples:
- A: Cell phone plan: $20 base fee + $0.10/text
- B: Recipe: 2 cups flour per 3 cups sugar (table provided)
- C: Graph showing distance vs. time, line through origin
- D: Age of sibling vs. your age

Students determine proportional vs. non-proportional and justify.

**→ CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Require students to cross-check using **two methods** (e.g., test constant ratio AND check graph passes through origin).
- *Assessment:* Review written justifications; Level 2 responses use multiple verification strategies.

**→ CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Ask: "Which piece of information in scenario A is most useful for deciding? Why is the $20 fee critical?"
- *Assessment:* Cold-call students; quality responses identify the y-intercept as disqualifying evidence.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choose Your Plan (12 minutes)
Present a real-world decision: "Two streaming services—one charges $8/month flat, another charges $2 per movie watched. Which is the better deal?"

Groups of 3 create a table, graph both relationships on the same axes, write equations, and recommend a choice.

**→ CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must list at least three trade-offs (cost at low usage, cost at high usage, predictability).
- *Assessment:* Review group posters; Level 2 work considers long-term implications (e.g., "If you watch >4 movies/month, plan A wins").

**→ CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Each group writes a recommendation backed by their graph's intersection point.
- *Assessment:* Look for reasoning that anticipates "what if" scenarios. Level 2: "Plan A is proportional but only better above 4 movies; for occasional viewers, Plan B wins."

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (11 minutes)

**Quick reflection journal (5 min):** Students respond to:
> "What's one mistake you almost made today, or a moment your thinking changed? What helped you see it differently?"

**→ CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** 
- *Assessment:* Read journals; Level 2 entries describe a strategy shift (e.g., "I first thought any line was proportional, but then I realized it needs to pass through the origin—now I always check that first").

**Exit Ticket (6 min):** 
1. Given the table (1,3), (2,6), (4,12)—is this proportional? Justify two ways.
2. Write the equation.
3. Sketch the graph.
4. Describe in words.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence frames ("This is proportional because ___ and ___").
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world example of a proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, speed) and represent it in all five forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

  - Equations (y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality)

---

- Distribute handouts with mixed representations of proportional relationships.

  - Identify if the relationship is proportional.

- Circulate and prompt students to explain their reasoning.  

*Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)*  
- **Activity:** Students check if the ratios are constant and justify their answers.  
- **Assessment:** Listen for students’ use of evidence (calculations, graph characteristics) to validate proportionality.  

*Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)*  
- **Activity:** Encourage students to select the most appropriate representation for a given problem.  
- **Assessment:** Note if students choose representations that best clarify the relationship rather than arbitrary or less effective ones.  

---

**4. Class Discussion (10 minutes)**  
- Invite pairs to share one example and their reasoning.  
- Discuss any misconceptions or errors.  
- Pose a question: “What might happen if the ratio is not constant? How does that change the relationship?”  
- Highlight the importance of constant ratio and straight-line graphs through the origin.  

*Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)*  
- **Activity:** Facilitate a discussion comparing different representations (e.g., when is a graph better than a table?).  
- **Assessment:** Observe students’ ability to articulate advantages and disadvantages of each representation.  

*Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)*  
- **Activity:** Ask students to justify why a certain representation is more effective for a particular problem.  
- **Assessment:** Evaluate the logical coherence and depth of their reasoning.  

---

**5. Independent Practice (10 minutes)**  
- Students individually complete a short worksheet with problems requiring them to:  
  - Identify proportional relationships.  
  - Convert between tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.  
- Include one problem that is not proportional to test their critical thinking.  

*Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes)*  
- **Activity:** At the end of the worksheet, include a reflection prompt: “How did you decide if a relationship was proportional? What strategies helped you?”  
- **Assessment:** Review reflections for evidence of metacognitive awareness and strategic thinking.  

---

**6. Closure and Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Summarize key points: constant ratio, multiple representations, and how to check proportionality.  
- Exit ticket question: “Give an example of a proportional relationship and explain how you know it is proportional.”

- A: Cell phone plan: $20 base fee + $0.10/text
- B: Recipe: 2 cups flour per 3 cups sugar (table provided)
- C: Graph showing distance vs. time, line through origin
- D: Age of sibling vs. your age

Students determine proportional vs. non-proportional and justify.

**→ CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students must cross-check using **two methods** (e.g., test constant ratio AND verify graph passes through origin). They must prove k is consistent across table, graph, and equation.
- *Assessment:* Review written justifications; Level 2 responses rigorously cross-check across multiple representations without contradiction.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choose Your Plan (15 minutes)
Present: "Two streaming services—Plan A charges $2 per movie watched; Plan B charges $8/month flat. Which is the better deal?"

Groups of 3 create a table, graph both relationships on the same axes, write equations, and recommend a choice.

**Twist (mid-task):** "Plan A now adds a one-time $5 sign-up fee. Is it still proportional? Justify."

**→ CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups list at least three trade-offs (cost at low usage, cost at high usage, predictability) and discuss long-term implications.
- *Assessment:* Review group posters; Level 2 work systematically evaluates trade-offs ("If you watch >4 movies/month, Plan B wins; below that, Plan A is cheaper").

**→ CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Each group writes a recommendation backed by their graph's intersection point AND addresses the sign-up fee twist.
- *Assessment:* Look for sophisticated reasoning anticipating complexity—Level 2 students recognize the $5 fee shifts the line off the origin, making Plan A non-proportional even though it still has a constant per-movie rate.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

**Quick reflection (4 min):** Students respond to:
> "How did your thinking about proportional relationships change today? What's one mistake you almost made, or a strategy you'll use next time?"

**→ CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** 
- *Assessment:* Read journals; Level 2 entries describe strategic shifts (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I verify the line passes through the origin AND cross-check the equation").

**Exit Ticket (6 min):** 
1. Given the table (1,3), (2,6), (4,12)—is this proportional? Justify two ways.
2. Write the equation.
3. Sketch the graph.
4. Describe the relationship in words.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Partially completed tables and sentence frames ("This is proportional because ___ and ___").
- **Extension:** Create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.
