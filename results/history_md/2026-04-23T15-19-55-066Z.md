## Generator
# The Curious Arguer: Why Questions Make Better Writers

### A 55-Minute Lesson for 7th Grade ELA

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Central Idea:** Curiosity is the engine of great argumentative writing. When we genuinely wonder about a question, we write with more depth, consider more viewpoints, and make stronger arguments.

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
- Explain how curiosity strengthens argumentative writing
- Generate genuine questions about a debatable topic
- Construct a claim supported by reasons, evidence, and acknowledgment of an opposing viewpoint
- Organize an argument logically using a structured framework

**Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard or projector
- Sticky notes (2 per student)
- Printed or projected "Wonder Wall" template
- Argument Organizer handout (described below)
- Pens/pencils
- Timer

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### SEGMENT 1: THE HOOK — "Bored Writers Write Boring Arguments" (8 minutes)

**[0:00–2:00] — Opening Provocation**

Write the following sentence on the board in large letters before students arrive:

> *"You should eat breakfast every day."*

As students settle, ask them to read it silently. Then say:

*"I want you to rate how interested you are in reading an essay about this sentence. Hold up fingers — one through five. One means you'd rather watch paint dry. Five means you're genuinely curious."*

Observe the (likely low) ratings. Nod knowingly.

*"Now — keep that feeling. We're going to come back to it."*

---

**[2:00–5:00] — Story: The Scientist Who Couldn't Stop Asking Why**

Tell this brief story in a conversational tone:

*"In the 1960s, a physicist named Richard Feynman was asked to be on a government panel to review science textbooks for schools. He agreed, and he sat down with a huge stack of books. His colleagues flipped through them quickly and approved most of them. But Feynman couldn't stop asking questions. He'd read a sentence like 'Energy makes things move' and think — wait, but WHY does energy make things move? What IS energy, really? He ended up rejecting almost every textbook because they gave answers without ever making kids curious enough to actually care about those answers.*

*Feynman later said that the best scientists aren't the ones with the most answers — they're the ones who are most comfortable sitting inside a question."*

Pause. Let it land.

*"Today, we're going to become people who are comfortable sitting inside questions — because it turns out that's exactly what makes you a powerful argumentative writer."*

---

**[5:00–8:00] — Brief Discussion**

Ask the class:
- *"What's the difference between a question you actually want answered and one you don't care about at all?"*
- Take 2–3 responses. Guide students toward the idea that **genuine curiosity feels like an itch you need to scratch.**

Say: *"Real curiosity is personal. It means YOU actually don't know the answer, or you're not sure, or the question genuinely bothers you. That's the feeling we want to bring into our writing today."*

---

### SEGMENT 2: BUILDING THE CONCEPT — Curiosity + Argument = Power (12 minutes)

**[8:00–11:00] — The "Flat" vs. "Curious" Argument**

Display two short argument excerpts side by side on the board or projector:

---

**Argument A:**
*"Students should not have homework. Homework is bad. It takes up too much time and kids are already tired from school. Therefore, schools should get rid of homework."*

**Argument B:**
*"Should students have homework at all? I used to think the answer was obvious — of course not, nobody likes it. But then I started wondering: if homework disappeared tomorrow, would students actually learn less? Some researchers say yes. Others say the type of homework matters more than the amount. After looking at this carefully, I believe schools should replace traditional homework with student-chosen practice, because evidence shows that self-directed learning produces stronger long-term retention — though I understand why some teachers argue that structured practice keeps all students on the same page."*

---

Ask students:
- *"Which argument sounds like the writer actually cared about the topic?"*
- *"What's different about how Argument B is built?"*
- *"Where do you see curiosity showing up in Argument B?"*

Take responses. Draw out these key ideas and write them on the board:
1. The writer started with a **genuine question**
2. The writer **considered the other side** (because they were actually curious, not just trying to win)
3. The writer used **evidence** because they actually went looking for answers
4. The argument is **organized** because the thinking is clear

---

**[11:00–16:00] — Direct Instruction: The Argument Architecture**

Say: *"Before we practice being curious, let's make sure we know what a strong argument actually looks like. Think of it like a building."*

Draw a simple building diagram on the board (or display it):

```
       ┌─────────────────────────────┐
       │         CLAIM               │  ← Your answer to a real question
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │  REASON 1  │  REASON 2      │  ← Why your claim is true
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │  EVIDENCE  │  EVIDENCE      │  ← Proof, facts, examples
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │   OPPOSING VIEW + REBUTTAL  │  ← Acknowledging the other side
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │         CONCLUSION          │  ← Tying it all together
       └─────────────────────────────┘
```

Walk through each floor briefly:

- **Claim:** *"This is your position — your answer to a debatable question. Not a fact. Not an opinion. A claim that can be argued."*
- **Reasons:** *"These are the 'because' statements. WHY should someone believe you?"*
- **Evidence:** *"This is your proof — statistics, expert quotes, examples, research. This is what you find when curiosity sends you looking."*
- **Opposing View + Rebuttal:** *"This is the floor that most writers skip — and it's the most important one. When you acknowledge what the other side believes, you show you actually thought about this. Then you explain why your position still stands."*
- **Conclusion:** *"Bring it home. Restate why this matters."*

---

**[16:00–20:00] — The Curiosity-Argument Connection**

Say: *"Here's the secret. Every single floor of this building gets stronger when you start with genuine curiosity. Let me show you."*

Return to the board. Write: *"Should schools have a dress code?"*

Ask: *"Who actually doesn't know the answer to this? Who has genuinely mixed feelings or wants to know more?"*

Invite 2–3 students to share what they're actually curious about within this question. Model writing their questions on the board:
- *"Does wearing a uniform actually change how students behave?"*
- *"Do dress codes affect boys and girls the same way?"*
- *"Who benefits most from dress codes — students or schools?"*

Say: *"See how those questions immediately push you toward finding evidence? Curiosity sends you looking. And when you look, you find the material that fills your argument building."*

---

### SEGMENT 3: STUDENT ACTIVITY — The Wonder Wall + Argument Build (25 minutes)

**[20:00–25:00] — Wonder Wall: Generating Curious Questions**

Distribute two sticky notes to each student.

Display the following list of debatable topics on the board:

1. Should social media have a minimum age requirement?
2. Should school start later in the morning?
3. Should students be allowed to grade their teachers?
4. Should junk food be banned from school cafeterias?
5. Should video games be used as learning tools in class?

Say: *"Pick ONE topic that genuinely interests you — or that you genuinely don't know the answer to. On your sticky notes, write two questions you actually want answered about that topic. Not 'What is junk food?' — something you truly wonder about. Something that makes you go 'Hm.'"*

Give students 3 minutes to write their questions independently.

Then ask 4–5 students to share one question each. Celebrate the most genuinely curious, complex questions:
*"Oh, THAT'S a good question — that would actually be hard to answer. That means your argument is going to be interesting."*

Post a few sticky notes on a designated "Wonder Wall" section of the board to display the class's curiosity.

---

**[25:00–43:00] — Argument Organizer: Building the Case**

Distribute the **Argument Organizer handout** (described below). Students will work independently to fill this out for their chosen topic.

**Argument Organizer — "The Curious Arguer"**

```
NAME: _______________________     TOPIC: _______________________

MY BURNING QUESTION:
(What do I genuinely want to know about this topic?)
______________________________________________________________

MY CLAIM:
(My answer to the question — a debatable position, not a simple fact)
______________________________________________________________

REASON 1: ____________________________________________________
EVIDENCE/EXAMPLE that supports it: ____________________________
______________________________________________________________

REASON 2: ____________________________________________________
EVIDENCE/EXAMPLE that supports it: ____________________________
______________________________________________________________

WHAT WOULD SOMEONE WHO DISAGREES WITH ME SAY?
(Write their best argument — try to be fair to them)
______________________________________________________________

MY REBUTTAL:
(Why does my position still hold up even considering that?)
______________________________________________________________

CONCLUSION — Why does this matter?
______________________________________________________________
```

**Teacher's role during this time:**
- Circulate constantly
- Ask students: *"Are you actually curious about this, or are you just writing what sounds right?"*
- When students struggle with the opposing view, prompt: *"What would your parent, your teacher, or someone who thinks completely differently say about this?"*
- When students have weak evidence, ask: *"How do you KNOW that? What would prove it?"*
- Celebrate genuine complexity: *"It's okay if you're not 100% sure. That means the question is real."*

**[43:00–45:00] — Pair Share**

Ask students to turn to a partner and share:
1. Their burning question
2. Their claim
3. The opposing view they acknowledged

Partners give one piece of feedback: *"The most curious-sounding part of your argument is ___."*

---

### SEGMENT 4: SYNTHESIS + REFLECTION (8 minutes)

**[45:00–50:00] — Whole-Class Debrief**

Call the class back together. Ask:

- *"Did anyone change their claim or their thinking while they were filling out the organizer? What made you rethink it?"* (This demonstrates that curiosity is actually working — it opens minds.)

- *"What was the hardest part to fill out?"* (The opposing view and rebuttal are almost always hardest — validate this and explain that's because it requires genuine empathy and openness.)

- *"How is starting with a question different from starting with an answer?"*

Bring it back to Feynman: *"Remember the scientist who sat inside questions? That's what you just did. You let yourselves not know the answer for a while — and look at how much more interesting your arguments became."*

---

**[50:00–53:00] — Return to the Opening**

Return to the original sentence on the board: *"You should eat breakfast every day."*

Ask: *"If we applied what we learned today, what question could we start with that would make this WAY more interesting?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Students might generate:
- *"Does the TYPE of breakfast actually matter, or just eating something?"*
- *"Does skipping breakfast affect students differently than adults?"*
- *"Are there students for whom a breakfast requirement is actually unfair?"*

Say: *"Now THOSE are curious arguments waiting to happen. The sentence on the board was an answer. Your questions are the beginning of thinking."*

---

**[53:00–55:00] — Exit Ticket**

Students answer two quick questions on a half-sheet or in their notebook:

1. **In one sentence:** What is ONE way that curiosity makes an argument stronger?

2. **For next time:** Write ONE question about your topic that you still want answered — something you'd actually look up tonight if you had time.

Collect or ask students to keep for next class.

---

## CLOSING WORD TO STUDENTS

As students pack up, say:

*"The best writers — and the best thinkers — aren't the ones who already have all the answers. They're the ones who stay curious long enough to find the real ones. Next time you sit down to write an argument, don't start with your claim. Start with your question. The claim will get stronger because of it."*

---

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a pre-selected topic rather than choosing from the list
- Offer sentence starters for the organizer (e.g., "I believe ___ because ___." / "Someone might disagree and say ___.")
- Allow partner work during the organizer activity

**For advanced students:**
- Challenge them to find TWO opposing arguments, not one, and address both
- Ask them to identify what KIND of evidence would be most convincing (research study, personal testimony, statistics, expert opinion) and explain why
- Ask: *"Could you argue the other side just as well? What does that tell you?"*

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the topic list in both English and the student's home language if possible
- Allow students to generate their curious questions in their home language first, then translate to English
- Pair with a supportive partner during the pair-share activity

---

## ASSESSMENT

**Formative (during class):**
- Teacher observation during the organizer activity
- Pair-share listening
- Exit ticket responses

**Connection to Future Assessment:**
The completed Argument Organizer serves as the pre-writing scaffold for a full argumentative essay draft in the following class session, which will be assessed against a rubric aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 criteria: claim clarity, reason quality, evidence relevance, acknowledgment of opposing claims, logical organization, and conclusion strength.

**[2:00–5:00] — Story: The Scientist Who Couldn't Stop Asking Why**

Tell this brief story in a conversational tone:

*\"In the 1960s, a physicist named Richard Feynman was asked to serve on a state committee to review science and math textbooks for California schools. He agreed, and he sat down with a huge stack of books. His colleagues flipped through them quickly and approved most of them. But Feynman couldn't stop asking questions. He'd read a sentence like 'Energy makes things move' and think — wait, but WHY does energy make things move? What IS energy, really? He ended up rejecting or heavily criticizing most of the textbooks he reviewed because they gave answers without ever making kids curious enough to actually care about those answers.*

*Feynman believed deeply that not knowing — sitting with a question rather than rushing to an answer — was at the heart of real scientific thinking. He wrote about this in his own words, describing uncertainty and curiosity as essential to discovery.\"*

Pause. Let it land.

*\"Today, we're going to become people who are comfortable sitting inside questions — because it turns out that's exactly what makes you a powerful argumentative writer.\"*

---

**[5:00–8:00] — Brief Discussion**

Ask the class:
- *\"What's the difference between a question you actually want answered and one you don't care about at all?\"*
- Take 2–3 responses. Guide students toward the idea that **genuine curiosity feels like an itch you need to scratch.**

Say: *\"Real curiosity is personal. It means YOU actually don't know the answer, or you're not sure, or the question genuinely bothers you. That's the feeling we want to bring into our writing today.\"*

### SEGMENT 2: BUILDING THE CONCEPT — Curiosity + Argument = Power (12 minutes)

**[8:00–11:00] — The \"Flat\" vs. \"Curious\" Argument**

Display two short argument excerpts side by side on the board or projector:

---

**Argument A:**
*\"Students should not have homework. Homework is bad. It takes up too much time and kids are already tired from school. Therefore, schools should get rid of homework.\"*

**Argument B:**
*\"Should students have homework at all? I used to think the answer was obvious — of course not, nobody likes it. But then I started wondering: if homework disappeared tomorrow, would students actually learn less? Some researchers say yes. Others say the type of homework matters more than the amount. After looking at this carefully, I believe schools should replace traditional homework with student-chosen practice, because evidence shows that self-directed learning produces stronger long-term retention — though I understand why some teachers argue that structured practice keeps all students on the same page.\"*

---

Ask students:
- *\"Which argument sounds like the writer actually cared about the topic?\"*
- *\"What's different about how Argument B is built?\"*
- *\"Where do you see curiosity showing up in Argument B?\"*

Take responses. Draw out these key ideas and write them on the board:
1. The writer started with a **genuine question**
2. The writer **considered the other side** (because they were actually curious, not just trying to win)
3. The writer used **evidence** because they actually went looking for answers
4. The argument is **organized** because the thinking is clear

---

**[11:00–16:00] — Direct Instruction: The Argument Architecture**

Say: *\"Before we practice being curious, let's make sure we know what a strong argument actually looks like. Think of it like a building.\"*

Draw a simple building diagram on the board (or display it):


       ┌─────────────────────────────┐
       │         CLAIM               │  ← Your answer to a real question
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │  REASON 1  │  REASON 2      │  ← Why your claim is true
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │  EVIDENCE  │  EVIDENCE      │  ← Proof, facts, examples
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │   OPPOSING VIEW + REBUTTAL  │  ← Acknowledging the other side
       ├─────────────────────────────┤
       │         CONCLUSION          │  ← Tying it all together
       └─────────────────────────────┘


Walk through each floor briefly:

- **Claim:** *\"This is your position — your answer to a debatable question. Not a fact. Not an opinion. A claim that can be argued.\"*
- **Reasons:** *\"These are the 'because' statements. WHY should someone believe you?\"*
- **Evidence:** *\"This is your proof — statistics, expert quotes, examples, research. This is what you find when curiosity sends you looking.\"*
- **Opposing View + Rebuttal:** *\"This is the floor that most writers skip — and it's the most important one. When you acknowledge what the other side believes, you show you actually thought about this. Then you explain why your position still stands.\"*
- **Conclusion:** *\"Bring it home. Restate why this matters.\"*

---

**[16:00–20:00] — The Curiosity-Argument Connection**

Say: *\"Here's the secret. Every single floor of this building gets stronger when you start with genuine curiosity. Let me show you.\"*

Return to the board. Write: *\"Should schools have a dress code?\"*

Ask: *\"Who actually doesn't know the answer to this? Who has genuinely mixed feelings or wants to know more?\"*

Invite 2–3 students to share what they're actually curious about within this question. Model writing their questions on the board:
- *\"Does wearing a uniform actually change how students behave?\"*
- *\"Do dress codes affect boys and girls the same way?\"*
- *\"Who benefits most from dress codes — students or schools?\"*

Say: *\"See how those questions immediately push you toward finding evidence? Curiosity sends you looking. And when you look, you find the material that fills your argument building.\"*

### SEGMENT 3: STUDENT ACTIVITY — The Wonder Wall + Argument Build (25 minutes)

**[20:00–25:00] — Wonder Wall: Generating Curious Questions**

Distribute two sticky notes to each student.

Display the following list of debatable topics on the board:

1. Should social media have a minimum age requirement?
2. Should school start later in the morning?
3. Should students be allowed to grade their teachers?
4. Should junk food be banned from school cafeterias?
5. Should video games be used as learning tools in class?

Give students 3 minutes to write their questions independently.

Then ask 4–5 students to share one question each. Celebrate the most genuinely curious, complex questions:
*\"Oh, THAT'S a good question — that would actually be hard to answer. That means your argument is going to be interesting.\"*

Post a few sticky notes on a designated \"Wonder Wall\" section of the board to display the class's curiosity.
