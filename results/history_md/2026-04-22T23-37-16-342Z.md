## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Chart paper or projector  
- Handout with a short argumentative writing prompt  
- Graphic organizer for argument writing (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim)  
- Sticky notes  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Hook:** Ask students, “What is curiosity? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new or solve a problem?”  
- Write a few student responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain: Curiosity is the desire to learn or know more about something. It drives us to ask questions, explore ideas, and discover new things.  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Thomas Edison) and how their curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science or history but also in writing and thinking critically.

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us ask questions like “Why do I believe this?” and “What evidence supports my idea?”  
- When writing arguments, being curious means looking for good reasons and evidence, and also considering other points of view.  
- Introduce the writing standard: Writing arguments means making a claim, supporting it with reasons and evidence, and recognizing opposing ideas.

#### 3. **Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Display or distribute a graphic organizer with these parts:  
  - Claim (your main idea)  
  - Reason 1 + Evidence  
  - Reason 2 + Evidence  
  - Counterclaim (opposing view) + Rebuttal  
- Review a short sample paragraph together. Identify each part aloud.  
- Highlight how the writer shows curiosity by exploring different sides of the issue.

#### 4. **Guided Practice: Writing with Curiosity (15 minutes)**  
- Present a simple, engaging prompt related to students’ interests or current events. Example:  
  *“Should school start later in the morning?”*  
- As a class, brainstorm claims for and against the prompt. Write them on the board.  
- In pairs, students choose a side and fill out the graphic organizer with at least two reasons and evidence, plus one counterclaim and rebuttal.  
- Circulate and support students as they think critically and ask questions to deepen their reasoning.

#### 5. **Independent Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Students write a short argumentative paragraph using their graphic organizer.  
- Encourage them to be curious by asking themselves:  
  - Why do I believe this?  
  - What facts or examples support my reasons?  
  - What might someone who disagrees say? How can I respond?  

#### 6. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class: How did curiosity help you write your argument?  
- Close by reminding students that curiosity is a powerful tool for learning and writing, helping us understand the world and communicate clearly.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Write a second paragraph on a different topic of your choice, practicing curiosity by including a claim, reasons, evidence, and a counterclaim.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with practical writing skills aligned to CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1, helping students become thoughtful, curious writers and thinkers.
