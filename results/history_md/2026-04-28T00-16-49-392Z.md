## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Objective
Students will practice asking genuine questions to fuel argumentative writing, drafting a claim supported by reasons and evidence while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Lined paper or notebooks
- Printed "Question Starter" cards (one per student)
- A jar labeled "The Curiosity Jar"

---

## Lesson Outline

### HOOK — The Curiosity Jar *(8 minutes)*

As students enter, they see a jar on the teacher's desk filled with folded slips of paper.

Tell students: *"Every slip in this jar is a question someone was too nervous to ask. Today, those questions become powerful."*

Draw one slip and read it aloud — pre-written by the teacher, something genuinely debatable:

> *"Should students be allowed to grade their own teachers?"*

Ask for a quick show of hands: Who says yes? Who says no? Who's not sure?

Tell students: **Curiosity is the engine of argument.** Every strong essay begins not with an answer, but with a question that genuinely puzzles you. Today, you're going to use your own curiosity to write something worth reading.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Makes an Argument Strong? *(10 minutes)*

Write this framework on the board:

**CLAIM → REASONS → EVIDENCE → COUNTERCLAIM → REBUTTAL**

Walk through each part briefly using the jar question as a live example:

- **Claim:** Students should be allowed to evaluate their teachers annually.
- **Reason:** Students experience teaching firsthand, every single day.
- **Evidence:** A 2019 study found student feedback improved teacher performance in 67% of cases when reviewed constructively.
- **Counterclaim:** Some argue students aren't qualified to judge teaching methods.
- **Rebuttal:** However, feedback doesn't require expertise — it requires honesty, which students can absolutely provide.

Emphasize: *A curious writer doesn't just defend their opinion. They genuinely wonder, "What would someone who disagrees say?" That wondering makes your argument stronger, not weaker.*

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — Finding Your Question *(10 minutes)*

Hand out "Question Starter" cards with prompts:

- *Should... ?*
- *Is it fair that... ?*
- *What would happen if... ?*
- *Does... really matter?*
- *Who gets to decide... ?*

Students have **3 minutes of silent thinking** to write down three questions they genuinely wonder about. These can be about school, their community, technology, sports, food — anything real to them.

Then students share one question with a partner. Partners respond with: *"That's interesting because I never thought about..."* — forcing authentic engagement.

Call on four or five pairs to share. Record their questions on the board. Tell the class: *"These are your writing prompts today. Real questions from real curious minds."*

---

### INDEPENDENT WRITING — Draft Your Argument *(20 minutes)*

Students choose one question from the board or their own list and begin drafting. They must include:

1. A clear **claim** that answers their question
2. At least **two reasons**
3. One piece of **evidence** (real or reasonably imagined for today)
4. One **counterclaim** (what would someone who disagrees say?)
5. A **rebuttal** (why your argument still stands)

Circulate the room. Encourage students who are stuck by asking: *"What made you curious about this? Start there."* For students rushing through, ask: *"What's the best argument against your claim? Have you addressed it?"*

Remind students halfway through: curiosity isn't just how we start — it's how we keep going. When you get stuck, ask a new question.

---

### SHARE & REFLECT *(7 minutes)*

Ask two or three volunteers to read their claim and counterclaim aloud.

After each reading, ask the class one question: *"Did the writer take the opposing side seriously?"*

Brief discussion — what's the difference between dismissing an opposing view and genuinely wrestling with it?

Close with this: **The best arguers are the most curious people in the room.** They want to understand the other side, not just defeat it.

---

### CLOSING — Exit Ticket *(5 minutes)*

On a sticky note, students write:

1. Their claim in one sentence
2. One thing they're still curious about regarding their topic

Collect sticky notes. These become the seeds for a full argumentative essay in the next writing cycle.

---

## Teacher Note
Resist the urge to validate only "safe" questions. When students bring real curiosity — even uncomfortable topics — treat it as the asset it is. Modeled curiosity from the teacher is the most powerful instruction in this lesson.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Lesson Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity as a tool for learning and inquiry, and will be able to write a clear argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging an opposing viewpoint.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt and graphic organizer (claim, reasons, evidence, opposing claim, rebuttal)  
- Example argumentative paragraph (projected or printed)  
- Sticky notes  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Engage:** Begin by asking students:  
  *“What does curiosity mean to you? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new?”*  
- Write a few student responses on the board.  
- Explain:  
  *Curiosity is the desire to learn or know more about something. It drives us to ask questions, explore new ideas, and solve problems. Being curious helps us become better thinkers and learners.*  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Albert Einstein) who used curiosity to make important discoveries.  
- **Quick Activity:**  
  Give each student a sticky note and ask them to write one question they are curious about right now (anything they want to learn or understand better). Collect and read a few aloud to show how curiosity varies and sparks learning.

---

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity also helps us become better writers, especially when writing arguments.  
- When we are curious, we ask questions like:  
  - *Why do I believe this?*  
  - *What evidence supports my idea?*  
  - *What might someone who disagrees say?*  
- These questions help us build strong arguments that are clear and convincing.  
- Introduce the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - **Claim:** The main point or opinion.  
  - **Reasons:** Why you believe the claim.  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons.  
  - **Opposing Claim:** A different opinion or argument.  
  - **Rebuttal:** Your response to the opposing claim, explaining why your claim still holds.  
- Show a simple example on the board or projector:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* Studies show fewer bullying incidents in schools with uniforms.  
  *Opposing Claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Rebuttal:* But students can express themselves through other ways like hairstyles or accessories.

---

#### 3. **Guided Practice: Writing an Argument (20 minutes)**  
- Distribute the handout with a prompt and graphic organizer. Example prompt:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- Read the prompt aloud and discuss briefly.  
- Model filling out the graphic organizer with the class:  
  - Claim: Homework should not be banned.  
  - Reason 1: Homework helps reinforce what we learn in class.  
  - Evidence 1: Research shows students who do homework perform better on tests.  
  - Reason 2: Homework teaches responsibility and time management.  
  - Evidence 2: Teachers report students improve these skills through homework.  
  - Opposing Claim: Some say homework causes too much stress.  
  - Rebuttal: While homework can be stressful, proper time management and breaks can reduce stress.  
- After modeling, have students work individually or in pairs to complete their own graphic organizer with a claim, reasons, evidence, opposing claim, and rebuttal related to the prompt.  
- Circulate and provide support as needed.

---

#### 4. **Sharing and Reflecting (10 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to share their claims and reasons with the class.  
- Highlight how curiosity helped them think about different sides of the argument and find evidence.  
- Discuss how asking questions like “Why do I believe this?” and “What might someone else say?” made their arguments stronger.  
- Reinforce that curiosity is not just about asking questions but also about seeking evidence and understanding different viewpoints.

---

#### 5. **Closing and Homework (5 minutes)**  
- Summarize key points:  
  - Curiosity helps us learn and write better arguments.  
  - Strong arguments include clear claims, reasons, evidence, and address opposing views.  
- Homework assignment:  
  Write a full argumentative paragraph based on the graphic organizer they completed in class. Remind students to use curiosity by asking themselves questions to make their writing clear and convincing.  
- Optional: Encourage students to bring a question they are curious about for the next class to explore how curiosity can lead to research and writing.

---

### Additional Notes:  
- Throughout the lesson, emphasize a growth mindset by praising curiosity and effort rather than just “right answers.”  
- Use questioning techniques to prompt deeper thinking, such as “What evidence can you find?” or “How might someone disagree with you?”  
- Adapt the argumentative prompt to topics relevant or interesting to your students to boost engagement.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with the essential writing skill of crafting well-supported arguments, meeting the CCSS writing standard while fostering a love of inquiry and critical thinking.
