## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout with mixed scenarios (proportional and non-proportional)
- Whiteboard and markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "3 apples cost $1.50, 6 apples cost $3.00, 9 apples cost $4.50"
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit"

**Question:** "Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"

Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Teacher records student observations without confirming answers yet.

**🎯 CRI1a – Clarifying Information:** Students must articulate what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through origin). *Assessment:* Listen for precise language during pair-share—are students clarifying terms like "ratio," "rate," and "constant"? Note who restates ideas with growing precision.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Teacher introduces the four representations of proportional relationships:
1. **Table** — check constant ratio (y/x = k)
2. **Graph** — straight line through origin
3. **Equation** — y = kx
4. **Verbal/Diagram** — describes constant rate

Using Scenario A, model each representation. Then revisit Scenario B and show why it fails each test (graph doesn't pass through origin, ratio changes).

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Students copy a four-quadrant graphic organizer (Table | Graph | Equation | Verbal) into their notebooks. *Assessment:* Collect notebooks; check whether students structure each representation logically and link them to the constant of proportionality.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Stations (15 minutes)
Four stations around the room, each presenting one representation of a relationship. Students rotate every 3 minutes, deciding whether each is proportional and justifying with evidence.

- **Station 1:** Table of values (distance/time)
- **Station 2:** Graph (some through origin, some not)
- **Station 3:** Equations (y = 3x, y = 3x + 2)
- **Station 4:** Word problem ("A recipe uses 2 cups flour for every 3 cookies…")

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** At Station 2, two graphs look similar but only one passes through the origin. Students must verify by checking points—not just appearance. *Assessment:* Review station response sheets; do students cite specific coordinates or ratios as evidence rather than guessing?

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** At Station 1, the table includes a "decoy" row with a computational error. Students must determine whether the relationship is truly proportional or whether the data itself is flawed. *Assessment:* Look for students who flag the inconsistency rather than accepting the table at face value.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision-Making Task (12 minutes)
**Scenario:** "You're planning a class fundraiser. Option 1: Sell bracelets at $4 each (no setup cost). Option 2: Sell t-shirts at $10 each, but with a $50 setup fee."

In groups of 3-4, students must:
- Create a table, graph, and equation for each option
- Identify which is proportional
- Decide which option to recommend and justify with math

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Groups must compare both options, considering quantity sold, profit, and proportionality. *Assessment:* Circulate and listen for trade-off language ("If we sell fewer than X, then…"). Collect group recommendation sheets.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** Students must connect mathematical evidence (slope, y-intercept, break-even point) to their recommendation. *Assessment:* Evaluate written justifications—do students cite the constant of proportionality and break-even quantity rather than vague preferences?

---

### 5. Closure & Metacognitive Reflection (9 minutes)

**Exit Ticket (5 minutes):**
1. Give one example each of a proportional and non-proportional relationship.
2. Write the equation form for a proportional relationship.
3. **Reflection prompt:** "What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What evidence caused you to change your mind?"

**Class debrief (4 minutes):** Two volunteers share their reflection.

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** The reflection prompt explicitly asks students to identify a shift in their reasoning. *Assessment:* Read exit tickets and categorize responses: Did students simply restate facts (Level 1), or did they describe how a specific piece of evidence—like a graph not passing through the origin—revised their initial assumption (Level 2)?

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers and sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___").
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to construct a non-proportional relationship that "looks" proportional in one representation but fails in another.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, gas mileage, etc.). Represent it in all four ways and bring it to the next class.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Materials
- Projector or board  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Graph paper  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A store sells 3 notebooks for $6, 6 notebooks for $12, and 9 notebooks for $18.  
**Situation B:** A taxi charges $4 to start and $2 per mile.

Ask students to individually answer:

1. Which situation seems proportional?  
2. What makes you think so?  
3. What information is important?

Students then turn and talk with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students identify relevant quantities, such as number of notebooks, cost, starting fee, and miles.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying that proportional relationships must have a constant ratio and no added starting amount. Look for written statements like, “The taxi is not proportional because there is a $4 fee before any miles.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when two quantities have a constant ratio. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

Use Situation A:

| Notebooks | Cost |
|---|---|
| 3 | 6 |
| 6 | 12 |
| 9 | 18 |

Ask students to calculate:

\[
\frac{6}{3}=2,\quad \frac{12}{6}=2,\quad \frac{18}{9}=2
\]

So the cost is $2 per notebook, and the equation is:

\[
y = 2x
\]

Then contrast with Situation B:

| Miles | Cost |
|---|---|
| 0 | 4 |
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 8 |
| 3 | 10 |

Although the cost increases by 2 each time, the relationship is not proportional because the graph would not pass through the origin and the equation would be:

\[
y = 2x + 4
\]

not \(y = kx\).

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students see how tables, equations, and verbal descriptions connect logically.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to label each representation: table, ratio, equation, and explanation. Check whether their notes show a clear flow from data to ratio to equation to conclusion.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations Stations — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs through three teacher-led tasks on the handout.

#### Task 1: Table
A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for 5 servings, 4 cups for 10 servings, and 6 cups for 15 servings.  
Students decide if it is proportional and write an equation.

Expected reasoning:

\[
\frac{5}{2}=2.5,\quad \frac{10}{4}=2.5,\quad \frac{15}{6}=2.5
\]

If \(x\) is cups of flour and \(y\) is servings:

\[
y = 2.5x
\]

#### Task 2: Graph
Students examine a graph showing points \((1,3), (2,6), (3,9), (4,12)\). They determine whether the graph shows a proportional relationship.

Expected reasoning: The graph forms a straight line through the origin, so it is proportional. The constant of proportionality is 3.

#### Task 3: Verbal Description
“A gym charges a $20 membership fee plus $5 per visit.”  
Students explain why this is not proportional.

Expected reasoning: There is an initial fee, so the relationship does not begin at \((0,0)\).

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students cross-check whether a relationship is proportional using more than one representation: table ratios, graph through origin, and equation form.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask pairs to justify each answer using at least two checks, such as “The ratios are equal and the equation is \(y = 2.5x\)” or “The graph is straight and passes through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students distinguish useful evidence from less useful evidence. For example, “the numbers go up by the same amount” is not enough to prove proportionality.  
**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students rejecting incomplete evidence. Check written explanations for statements such as, “Equal increases do not always mean proportional; I need to check the ratios or the origin.”

---

### 4. Partner Challenge: Choosing the Better Deal — 13 minutes

Present the problem:

Two bike rental shops have different prices.

- **Shop A:** $8 per hour  
- **Shop B:** $5 sign-up fee plus $6 per hour

Students complete a table for 0–5 hours, graph both relationships on the same coordinate plane, and write equations.

Shop A:

\[
y = 8x
\]

Shop B:

\[
y = 6x + 5
\]

Students answer:

1. Which relationship is proportional?  
2. Which shop is cheaper for 1 hour? 3 hours? 5 hours?  
3. Which shop would you choose, and why?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare two pricing options and recognize that the better choice can depend on the number of hours.  
**Observe/Assess:** Look for students comparing specific values rather than making a quick assumption. Strong responses include, “Shop B is cheaper after 3 hours, but Shop A is proportional because it has no starting fee.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students use tables, graphs, and equations to support a decision.  
**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to defend their choice with mathematical evidence. Listen for reasoning such as, “At 1 hour, Shop A costs $8 and Shop B costs $11, so Shop A is cheaper. At 5 hours, Shop A costs $40 and Shop B costs $35, so Shop B is cheaper.”

---

### 5. Individual Reflection and Exit Ticket — 10 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. How can you tell from a table whether a relationship is proportional?  
2. How can you tell from a graph?  
3. Write one example of a proportional relationship and one non-proportional relationship.  
4. What mistake did you almost make today, or what strategy helped you decide?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on their reasoning and identify strategies or misconceptions.  
**Observe/Assess:** Read responses for metacognitive comments such as, “I first thought equal increases meant proportional, but then I checked the ratios,” or “Graphing helped me see that the taxi example did not start at zero.”

3. **Equation** — y = kx
4. **Verbal/Diagram** — constant rate, no starting fee

Model with Scenario A: 6/3 = 12/6 = 18/9 = 2, so y = 2x. Contrast with Scenario B: y = 5x + 20, graph does not pass through origin.

**🎯 CRI1b – Organizing Information:** Students copy a four-quadrant graphic organizer (Table | Graph | Equation | Verbal) into their notebooks, linking each representation to the constant of proportionality. *Assessment:* Collect notebooks; verify logical flow from data → ratio → equation → conclusion.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Stations (15 minutes)
Four stations, rotating every 3–4 minutes:
- **Station 1:** Table of distance/time values (includes a "decoy" row with a computational error)
- **Station 2:** Two graphs that look similar—only one passes through the origin
- **Station 3:** Equations (y = 3x vs. y = 3x + 2)
- **Station 4:** Word problem ("2 cups flour for every 3 cookies…")

**🎯 CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information:** At Station 2, students must verify by checking actual coordinates rather than relying on appearance, and cross-check the constant k across table, graph, and equation. *Assessment:* Review station response sheets for specific coordinates or ratios cited as evidence.

**🎯 CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information:** At Station 1, students must flag the decoy row and recognize that "numbers going up by the same amount" is insufficient evidence of proportionality. *Assessment:* Look for students who identify the inconsistency and reject incomplete evidence in writing.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision-Making Task (12 minutes)
**Scenario:** "Your class is renting bikes for a field trip. Shop A charges $8 per hour. Shop B charges a $5 sign-up fee plus $6 per hour."

Groups of 3–4 must:
- Build a table (0–5 hours), graph both lines on one plane, and write both equations
- Identify which is proportional
- Recommend a shop and justify mathematically

**Twist (mid-task):** Shop A adds a one-time $10 helmet fee. Is the relationship still proportional?

**🎯 CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons:** Groups must systematically evaluate trade-offs across different hour amounts (Shop A cheaper short-term, Shop B cheaper long-term). *Assessment:* Listen for trade-off language ("If we rent fewer than X hours, then…") and collect group recommendation sheets.

**🎯 CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning:** The helmet-fee twist requires students to anticipate that the added constant breaks proportionality (line no longer passes through origin). *Assessment:* Evaluate written justifications—do students cite slope, y-intercept, and break-even quantity, avoiding faulty logic?

---

### 5. Closure & Metacognitive Reflection (9 minutes)

**Exit Ticket (5 minutes):**
1. Give one proportional and one non-proportional example.
2. Write the equation form for a proportional relationship.
3. **Reflection prompt:** "What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What evidence caused you to revise your reasoning, and what will you do differently next time?"

**Class debrief (4 minutes):** Two volunteers share their reflection.

**🎯 CRI5a – Reflecting on Thinking Processes:** *Assessment:* Categorize responses—did students restate facts (Level 1), or did they describe how specific evidence (e.g., a graph not passing through the origin) revised their initial assumption and changed their strategy (Level 2)?

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Partially completed organizers and sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___").
- **Challenge:** Construct a non-proportional relationship that "looks" proportional in one representation but fails in another.
- **English Learners:** Pre-teach: *proportional, ratio, constant, origin, equation.*
