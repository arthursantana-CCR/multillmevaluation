## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" problem handout, exit ticket slips.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes) — Targets **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A smoothie shop charges $5 for the first smoothie and $2 for each additional one."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Push students to define "proportional" precisely: a constant ratio between two quantities, passing through (0,0).

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who clarify vague language (e.g., "It grows the same way") into precise statements ("The cost per smoothie stays constant"). Score Level 2 when students refine peer definitions to support deeper understanding.

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes) — Targets **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Using Scenario A, model all five representations side-by-side on a graphic organizer:
- **Verbal:** "$3 per smoothie"
- **Table:** (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** A straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 3 equal units per smoothie

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k = 3)** appearing in each form. Have students copy the organizer into notebooks.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect graphic organizers. Level 2 evidence: students label connections between representations (e.g., arrows linking the slope on the graph to k in the equation). Level 1: representations are present but disconnected.

### 3. Guided Practice: Detective Work (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI2a: Assessing Validity** and **CRI2b: Assessing Quality**
Distribute four "data cards," each claiming to show a proportional relationship:
- Card 1: A table where y/x = 4 consistently ✓
- Card 2: A graph that's linear but doesn't pass through the origin ✗
- Card 3: An equation y = 2x + 1 ✗
- Card 4: A verbal description of unit pricing ✓

In pairs, students must **cross-check each claim** using at least two methods (e.g., verify the table's ratios AND graph the points).

**Assessment of CRI2a/2b:** Circulate and ask, *"How did you verify this?"* Level 2 students use multiple methods (computing ratios, graphing, testing the equation) to confirm or reject claims. Level -1: students accept claims without testing.

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Phone Plan (15 minutes) — Targets **CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons** and **CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning**
Present three phone plans:
- **Plan X:** $0.10 per text (proportional)
- **Plan Y:** $5 flat fee + $0.05 per text (non-proportional)
- **Plan Z:** $15 flat fee, unlimited texts (non-proportional)

In groups of 4, students:
1. Identify which plan is proportional and justify with a representation.
2. Determine which plan is best for someone sending 50 texts/month, 200 texts/month, and 500 texts/month.
3. Write a recommendation with reasoning.

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Review group recommendations. Level 2: students systematically compare trade-offs across multiple usage levels and acknowledge that the "best" plan depends on context. Level 1: students pick one plan without considering alternatives.

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Look for logical chains in written recommendations. Level 2: students anticipate complexities ("If usage varies month to month, Plan Y balances risk"). Level -1: claims like "Plan X is best because it's cheapest" without supporting math.

### 5. Independent Check (6 minutes)
Students individually solve: *"A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk. Represent this proportional relationship in a table, equation, and graph."*

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (5 minutes) — Targets **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**
Students respond to two prompts on an exit slip:
1. Solve: *Is y = 7x proportional? Justify.*
2. **Reflection:** *"What was one moment today when your thinking changed? What helped you see proportionality more clearly—the table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?"*

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read reflections. Level 2: students articulate metacognitive shifts ("At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but checking the origin changed my strategy"). Level 1: students mention what they learned without analyzing their thinking. Level -1: no reflection or repeating misconceptions.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers and sentence stems ("This is/isn't proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship (grocery prices, gas mileage, etc.) and represent it in all five formats.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships multiple ways.

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships by checking for a constant ratio/unit rate.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
3. Explain how they know whether a relationship is proportional.
4. Compare different representations and choose the most useful one for a situation.

### Materials

- Whiteboard/projector  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and word problems  
- Graph paper  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which One Is Proportional? — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $15 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $31 |

Ask students:  
“Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then turn and talk. Invite 2–3 students to share. Guide students toward noticing that Table A has a constant ratio of $8 per hour, while Table B does not.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the relevant quantities and clarify what “proportional” means using the warm-up tables.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students saying or writing evidence such as, “The money goes up by $8 each hour,” or more precisely, “The ratio of money to hours is always 8, so the unit rate is constant.” Use the -1 to 2 scale: a 2 shows precise clarification of quantities and ratios.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain: A proportional relationship has a constant ratio between two quantities. It can be written as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality, or unit rate.

Model using Table A:

\[
\frac{8}{1}=8,\quad \frac{16}{2}=8,\quad \frac{24}{3}=8,\quad \frac{32}{4}=8
\]

So the equation is:

\[
y = 8x
\]

Show how this same relationship can be represented:

- **Table:** ordered pairs like \((1,8), (2,16)\)
- **Graph:** a straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** \(y=8x\)
- **Diagram:** 1 hour → $8, 2 hours → $16
- **Verbal description:** “A student earns $8 per hour.”

Emphasize: A graph of a proportional relationship must be a straight line through \((0,0)\).

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students record the five representations in a graphic organizer: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students arrange the information logically and connect each representation to the same unit rate. Strong evidence might be: “All representations show 8 dollars for 1 hour, so they match.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A cyclist travels 12 miles every hour.”

As a class, complete the following:

1. Verbal description: “The cyclist travels 12 miles per hour.”
2. Table:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 12 |
| 2 | 24 |
| 3 | 36 |
| 4 | 48 |

3. Equation: \(y=12x\)
4. Graph: plot \((0,0), (1,12), (2,24), (3,36)\)
5. Diagram: show repeated groups of 12 miles per hour

Ask: “How can we check that all five representations show the same relationship?”

Students should identify the unit rate of 12 miles per hour in each form.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students cross-check the table, graph, and equation to verify that they all represent the same relationship.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students using multiple representations to confirm accuracy, such as, “The point \((2,24)\) is on the graph, and it also fits \(y=12x\), so the graph and equation match.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which information is most useful for identifying the constant of proportionality.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask, “Which representation helped you find the unit rate most clearly?” Strong responses explain quality, such as, “The table is useful because I can divide miles by hours, but the equation gives the unit rate immediately.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Is It Proportional? — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs on three situations.

**Situation 1:**  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

**Situation 2:**  

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |
| 4 | 21 |

**Situation 3:**  
A taxi charges a starting fee of $4 plus $2 per mile.

For each, students must:

1. Decide if it is proportional.
2. Explain why or why not.
3. If proportional, represent it with a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.
4. If not proportional, explain what breaks the proportional relationship.

Circulate and ask probing questions:
- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “Does the graph go through the origin?”
- “Can the relationship be written as \(y=kx\)?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students choose which representation best supports their explanation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for comparisons such as, “The graph makes it easy to see the taxi situation is not proportional because it does not start at zero, but the equation also shows the extra $4 fee.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify whether each relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Strong reasoning includes statements like, “Situation 2 is not proportional because \(5/1=5\), \(10/2=5\), \(15/3=5\), but \(21/4=5.25\), so the ratio is not constant.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Review the partner task. Highlight common misconceptions:

- “Adding the same amount” does not always prove proportionality unless the relationship also starts at zero and has a constant ratio.
- A proportional graph must be a straight line through the origin.
- A starting fee means the relationship is usually not proportional.

Ask: “Why is the taxi situation not proportional even though the cost increases by $2 each mile?”

Expected answer: Because the $4 starting fee means the equation is \(y=2x+4\), not \(y=kx\), and the graph does not pass through \((0,0)\).

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete:

1. A relationship is shown by the equation \(y=6x\). Create a table with three ordered pairs.
2. Graph the relationship.
3. Write a verbal description.
4. Explain how you know it is proportional.
5. Reflection: “What representation helped you understand proportional relationships best today, and why?”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students complete the reflection question on the exit ticket.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for metacognitive evidence such as, “At first I only checked if the numbers increased by the same amount, but then I realized I needed to check the ratio and whether the graph goes through zero.”

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" problem handout, data cards, exit ticket slips.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes) — Targets **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A (official bakery pricing sheet):** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B (overheard customer claim):** "A smoothie shop charges $5 for the first smoothie and $2 for each additional one."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students turn-and-talk, then share. Push students to rewrite each verbal description into precise mathematical statements identifying independent and dependent variables, and to define "proportional" precisely: a constant ratio between two quantities, passing through (0,0).

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Review students' written restatements. Level 2: students refine vague language ("It grows the same way") into precise statements ("The cost per smoothie stays constant at $3"). Level -1: students confuse variables or misstate the relationship.

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes) — Targets **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Using Scenario A, model all five representations side-by-side on a graphic organizer:
- **Verbal:** "$3 per smoothie"
- **Table:** (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** A straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 3 equal units per smoothie

Emphasize the **constant of proportionality (k = 3)** appearing in each form. Students copy the organizer into notebooks with axes properly labeled and x-values in ascending order.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect graphic organizers. Level 2: students label connections between representations (arrows linking slope to k in the equation, ratios in table matching unit rate). Level 1: representations present but disconnected.

### 3. Guided Practice: Detective Work (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI2a: Assessing Validity** and **CRI2b: Assessing Quality**
Distribute four "data cards" claiming to show proportional relationships, drawn from sources of varying credibility (official price lists vs. vague social media posts):
- Card 1: A table where y/x = 4 consistently ✓
- Card 2: A graph that's linear but doesn't pass through the origin ✗
- Card 3: An equation y = 2x + 1 ✗
- Card 4: A verbal description of unit pricing ✓

In pairs, students cross-check each claim using at least two methods (compute ratios AND graph points AND test the equation), and judge whether the underlying source is authoritative enough to trust.

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Circulate asking, *"How did you verify this?"* Level 2: students use multiple methods to confirm or reject claims. Level -1: students accept claims without testing.

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Level 2: students articulate why an authoritative pricing sheet provides higher-quality data than a vague anecdotal claim, and select the strongest evidence for their conclusion.

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choosing a Phone Plan (15 minutes) — Targets **CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons** and **CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning**
Present three phone plans:
- **Plan X:** $0.10 per text (proportional)
- **Plan Y:** $5 flat fee + $0.05 per text (non-proportional)
- **Plan Z:** $15 flat fee, unlimited texts (non-proportional)

In groups of 4, students:
1. Identify which plan is proportional and justify with a representation.
2. Determine which plan is best for someone sending 50, 200, or 500 texts/month.
3. Write a recommendation with reasoning.

**Twist:** Introduce a one-time $10 activation fee on Plan X. Is it still proportional? Why or why not?

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Level 2: students systematically compare trade-offs across multiple usage levels and acknowledge that the "best" plan depends on context. Level 1: students pick one plan without considering alternatives.

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Level 2: students anticipate complexities ("The $10 fee shifts the line off the origin, so it's no longer proportional even though the per-text rate is constant"). Level -1: claims like "Plan X is best because it's cheapest" without supporting math.

### 5. Independent Check (6 minutes)
Students individually solve: *"A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of milk. Represent this proportional relationship in a table, equation, and graph."*

### 6. Exit Ticket & Reflection (5 minutes) — Targets **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**
Students respond to:
1. *Is y = 7x proportional? Justify.*
2. **Reflection:** *"What was one moment today when your thinking changed? Which representation—table, graph, equation, diagram, or words—helped you see proportionality most clearly, and why?"*

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Level 2: students articulate metacognitive shifts ("At first I thought any straight line was proportional, but checking the origin changed my strategy"). Level 1: students mention what they learned without analyzing their thinking. Level -1: no reflection or repeating misconceptions.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers and sentence stems ("This is/isn't proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship and represent it in all five formats.
