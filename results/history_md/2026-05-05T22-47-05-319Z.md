## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Framework:** CCR Critical Thinking

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to clarify and organize arguments about homework, evaluate the reliability of sources supporting different positions, weigh the pros and cons of having or eliminating homework, apply logical reasoning to defend a position, and reflect on how their thinking evolved during the lesson.

---

## Materials

- Printed source cards (4 sources: 2 pro-homework, 2 anti-homework, one of which is unreliable)
- Graphic organizer worksheet
- Whiteboard and markers
- Exit ticket slips
- Timer

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up — "Quick Take" (5 minutes)

Students respond independently in their notebooks to this prompt: *"Do you think schools should assign homework? Write 2–3 sentences explaining your current opinion and why you hold it."*

After writing, the teacher cold-calls three or four students to share. The teacher does not affirm or challenge any opinion yet, simply recording key words on the board: fairness, learning, time, stress, family, practice.

This moment plants the seed for **CRI5a** by asking students to externalize their prior thinking before instruction begins.

---

### Activity 1 — Clarifying the Question (8 minutes) | *CRI1a*

The teacher writes two versions of the debate question on the board:

- *"Is homework good?"*
- *"Should schools assign homework as a regular academic requirement?"*

Students discuss in pairs: *Are these the same question? What is the difference?* The class then co-constructs a precise working definition of "homework" for the lesson — distinguishing it from independent reading, studying for tests, or passion projects.

**Target subcompetency — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students move from a vague, emotionally charged question toward a precise, debatable claim. The teacher observes whether students can articulate the distinction between the two questions and contribute meaningfully to the shared definition. A student operating at Level 2 will note that the second question implies institutional policy, not just personal preference.

---

### Activity 2 — Organizing the Debate Landscape (10 minutes) | *CRI1b*

Students receive a graphic organizer divided into four quadrants: Academic Benefits, Academic Drawbacks, Social/Emotional Benefits, Social/Emotional Drawbacks. Working individually, they brainstorm at least two entries per quadrant using only their prior knowledge.

Pairs then compare organizers and identify gaps or overlaps. The teacher circulates and prompts students who have clustered everything under one quadrant to redistribute their thinking.

**Target subcompetency — CRI1b (Organizing Information):** The teacher assesses whether students can separate categories logically rather than listing ideas randomly. A Level 2 response shows clear distinctions between academic and social dimensions, with items placed in the correct quadrant and connected to the central question.

---

### Activity 3 — Source Evaluation Station (12 minutes) | *CRI2a and CRI2b*

Each table group receives four source cards:

1. A peer-reviewed study from a university education journal supporting structured homework in middle school
2. A national teachers' union policy statement questioning homework's effectiveness
3. A parenting blog post titled "Homework Is Destroying Our Kids!" with no cited data
4. A meta-analysis published by an educational psychology association summarizing 30 years of research

Groups rank the sources from most to least reliable and complete a short justification for each ranking. They must answer: *Who wrote it? What evidence is provided? Does the source have a reason to be biased?*

**Target subcompetencies — CRI2a (Assessing Validity) and CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** The teacher listens for students who distinguish between anecdote and data, and who recognize that even institutional sources (like the union) may carry bias. A Level 2 performance involves cross-referencing characteristics across sources rather than relying on a single criterion like "it sounds professional."

---

### Activity 4 — Structured Academic Controversy (15 minutes) | *CRI3a and CRI4a*

Students are assigned a position — pro-homework or anti-homework — regardless of personal belief. In groups of four (two per side), they present their argument for three minutes, then switch and argue the opposite position for three minutes. Finally, the group drops all assigned positions and has four minutes to reach a consensus recommendation, such as: homework should exist but with specific conditions.

**Target subcompetency — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** The teacher looks for students who move beyond "homework takes too long" toward systemic considerations like equity (not all students have quiet home environments) or long-term academic habits. Level 2 students will reference trade-offs between short-term stress and long-term skill development.

**Target subcompetency — CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** During the consensus phase, the teacher notes whether students anchor their recommendation to evidence from the source cards rather than reverting to personal preference or peer pressure. Faulty reasoning — such as "most students hate it, so it must be bad" — is gently named and redirected.

---

### Closing — Metacognitive Exit Ticket (5 minutes) | *CRI5a*

Students respond to three sentence starters on their exit slip:

1. *"Before today, I thought homework was… because…"*
2. *"A moment in today's lesson that changed or complicated my thinking was…"*
3. *"One question I still have that I did not have at the start is…"*

**Target subcompetency — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking):** The teacher reads exit tickets to assess depth of metacognition. A Level 1 response restates a new fact learned. A Level 2 response identifies a specific moment — such as ranking the source cards or arguing the opposing side — and explains how that experience revealed an assumption the student had not previously examined. Students who write that nothing changed their mind, without any acknowledgment of encountering challenging evidence, flag a potential Level -1 or 0 performance on this subcompetency.

---

## Teacher Notes

Equity note: During Activity 4, ensure students discussing homework equity have permission to draw on personal experience — some students in the room may lack reliable home study environments, and that lived knowledge is valid evidence. Treat it as such.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Debating the role of homework in schools

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify key arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support different perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of various information sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of different approaches to homework.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form a preliminary stance on the topic.
*   Reflect on their own thinking processes throughout the discussion.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (short articles/summaries presenting arguments for and against homework, with varied source types – e.g., excerpt from an educational research summary, a school district's rationale, a student blog post, a parent's opinion piece).
*   Handout 2: T-chart graphic organizer
*   Exit Tickets
*   Markers/pens

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, everyone! Today, we're going to tackle a topic that affects all of us: homework. How many of you love homework? How many of you wish it didn't exist? (Allow for a quick show of hands and brief, informal comments). It's clear there are strong feelings on both sides. Today, we're going to explore this question: 'Should schools have homework?' and use our critical thinking skills to understand the different perspectives."

**II. Clarifying Information (CRI1a) & Initial Exploration (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1a: Clarifying information):** Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
    *   **Teacher:** "You'll find several short texts here, each presenting different viewpoints or facts about homework. Your first task is to read through these texts individually. As you read, I want you to do two things:
        1.  **Highlight** the main arguments or key pieces of information presented in each text.
        2.  **Circle any words or phrases** you don't understand, and try to define them using context or by asking a classmate."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1a):** Circulate around the room. Observe students' highlighting – are they identifying the core claims? Listen to their discussions about unfamiliar vocabulary. Are they attempting to clarify ideas precisely? I will look for evidence that students are accurately identifying and defining key terms and arguments, rather than just random words or superficial details. For example, if a student highlights "Homework reinforces classroom learning" and can explain what "reinforces" means in this context, they are clarifying information precisely.

**III. Organizing Information (CRI1b) & Assessing Validity/Quality (CRI2a, CRI2b) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI1b: Organizing information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've read and highlighted, let's organize this information. In your small groups (3-4 students), use Handout 2, the T-chart, to list the 'Pros of Homework' on one side and the 'Cons of Homework' on the other. Make sure to draw your points directly from the texts."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI1b):** Review students' T-charts. Are the points clearly categorized? Is the information presented in a structured and consistent manner? For example, are all "pros" on one side and "cons" on the other, without mixing?
*   **Activity (CRI2a: Assessing validity of information & CRI2b: Assessing quality of information):**
    *   **Teacher:** "As you're filling out your T-chart, I want you to also think critically about *where* this information is coming from. Look at the source listed for each text on Handout 1. Discuss in your groups:
        *   'How reliable do you think this source is?' (CRI2a)
        *   'Does this source provide high-quality information, or is it more opinion-based?' (CRI2b)
        *   'Which pieces of evidence from the texts do you think are the *strongest* to support either side, and why?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI2a & CRI2b):** Listen to group discussions. Do students question the authority of a blog post versus a research summary? Do they identify potential biases? Are they able to articulate *why* certain information is more credible or relevant than others? I will look for students who can explain, for instance, "The study from the Department of Education is high-quality because it's based on research data, not just one person's feelings," demonstrating sophisticated judgment.

**IV. Weighing Pros & Cons & Applying Reasoning (CRI3a, CRI4a) (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):**
    *   **Teacher:** "Now that you've organized and evaluated the information, let's delve deeper. In your groups, discuss the following:
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools completely eliminated homework?'
        *   'What are the potential *long-term implications* if schools assigned a lot more homework?'
        *   'Consider the perspectives of different stakeholders: students, teachers, parents. How might each group weigh these pros and cons differently?'"
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI3a):** Circulate and listen to group discussions. Are students moving beyond simple listing to actively compare and evaluate the trade-offs? Do they consider the broader impact and different viewpoints? I'll note if students are discussing consequences like "If there's no homework, students might forget what they learned faster" or "Too much homework could lead to burnout and less family time," showing they are systematically evaluating implications.
*   **Activity (CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):**
    *   **Teacher:** "After your group discussion, I want each of you to individually write a short paragraph (3-4 sentences) stating your preliminary stance on the question: 'Should schools have homework?' You can say 'yes,' 'no,' or 'it depends/modified approach.' Crucially, you must provide at least two clear reasons to support your stance, drawing directly from the information we've discussed and evaluated today."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI4a):** Collect and read the individual paragraphs. Is the reasoning logical and coherent? Do their conclusions align with the evidence they present? Are there any unsupported claims or faulty logic? I will look for clear connections between their stance and the evidence provided, e.g., "I believe schools should have *some* homework because it helps reinforce learning (Pro 1) and teaches responsibility (Pro 2), as mentioned in the research summary."

**V. Reflecting on Thinking Processes (CRI5a) & Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

*   **Activity (CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes):**
    *   **Teacher:** "To conclude our lesson, I want you to reflect on your own thinking today. Please complete the exit ticket."
    *   **Exit Ticket Prompt:**
        *   "My initial thought on homework before this lesson was..."
        *   "After today's lesson, my thinking has [changed / stayed the same / become more nuanced] because..."
        *   "One thing I found challenging about evaluating the information or forming my opinion was..., and I tried to overcome it by..."
*   **Observation/Assessment (CRI5a):** Collect and review exit tickets. Do students demonstrate metacognitive awareness? Can they articulate how their understanding evolved or deepened? Do they identify specific challenges in their thinking and describe strategies they used to address them? I will look for responses that show genuine self-awareness, such as "Initially, I just hated homework, but after seeing the arguments about responsibility, I now think a little bit is okay. It was hard to let go of my bias, but I tried to focus only on the facts from the research."

Lesson Plan: Should Schools Have Homework?  
Grade: 7th

**Lesson Objectives:**  
- Students will clarify and organize information about homework.  
- Students will assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.  
- Students will weigh pros and cons of homework policies.  
- Students will apply sound reasoning to form and support their own opinions.  
- Students will reflect on their thinking processes throughout the lesson.  

---

### Lesson Outline and Critical Thinking Subcompetency Integration

---

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Begin with a brief class discussion: “What is homework? Why do schools assign it?”  
- Provide students with a short, clear text (about 150 words) presenting basic facts about homework (e.g., its purpose, common types, and general opinions).  
- Ask students to highlight or underline key ideas and then paraphrase the main points in pairs.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Observe students’ ability to identify relevant information and restate it clearly.  
- Look for precise clarifications in their paraphrasing, such as distinguishing between homework’s purpose (practice vs. assessment) and its effects.  
- Example evidence: Students say, “Homework helps us practice what we learn in class,” rather than vague or incorrect statements.  

---

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Provide students with mixed-up statements about homework’s advantages and disadvantages (e.g., “Homework improves learning,” “Homework causes stress,” “Homework helps develop responsibility,” “Too much homework can reduce free time”).  
- In small groups, students will organize these statements into two columns: Pros and Cons.  
- Then, groups will arrange the points in a logical order, from strongest to weakest argument.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Assess how well students arrange information to create a coherent flow.  
- Look for logical grouping and sequencing that strengthens understanding (e.g., grouping academic benefits separately from emotional effects).  
- Note if students avoid scattering or contradictory placement of ideas.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Present students with two short excerpts from different sources about homework (e.g., a teacher’s blog and a scientific study summary).  
- Guide students to identify the source of each excerpt and discuss which source might be more reliable and why.  
- Encourage them to think about author expertise, evidence provided, and possible bias.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Listen for students’ ability to identify sources and evaluate their trustworthiness.  
- Look for reasoning that includes cross-checking facts or questioning the source’s authority.  
- Example evidence: “The scientific study is more reliable because it uses data, while the blog is just one person’s opinion.”  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Show students two versions of a statement about homework: one vague and unsupported (“Homework is bad because it’s annoying”) and one detailed and supported (“Research shows that excessive homework can increase stress and reduce sleep, which affects student health”).  
- Discuss as a class which statement is higher quality and why.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Observe students’ ability to judge relevance and depth of information.  
- Look for use of criteria such as evidence, detail, and clarity in their judgments.  
- Students might say, “The second statement is better because it explains why homework can be bad and uses research.”  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** In pairs, students will list pros and cons of having homework and then discuss which side they find more convincing.  
- Prompt them to consider short-term and long-term effects (e.g., immediate stress vs. long-term learning benefits).  
- Each pair will share one trade-off they considered with the class.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Assess the depth of their comparison and whether they consider multiple perspectives and consequences.  
- Look for systematic evaluation rather than superficial listing.  
- Example evidence: “We think homework helps learning but too much can cause burnout, so maybe a balanced amount is best.”  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Individually, students write a short paragraph stating their opinion on whether schools should have homework, supporting it with reasons and anticipating counterarguments.  
- Encourage them to use logical connectors (because, however, therefore) and avoid fallacies.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Evaluate clarity and sophistication of reasoning.  
- Look for anticipation of complexities, such as acknowledging exceptions or trade-offs.  
- Example evidence: “I think schools should have homework because it helps practice skills, but it should not be too much because that can cause stress.”  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** End with a metacognitive reflection prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you decide your opinion?”  
- Students write a brief reflection or share aloud.  

**Assessment/Observation:**  
- Look for evidence of self-awareness about their reasoning process, such as recognizing assumptions or adjusting opinions based on new information.  
- Example evidence: “At first, I thought homework was just annoying, but after learning about its benefits and drawbacks, I realized it can be helpful if balanced.”  

---

- Short text about homework (150 words)  
- Mixed-up pros and cons statements  
- Two source excerpts on homework  
- Examples of low- and high-quality statements  
- Writing materials for reflections and opinion paragraphs
