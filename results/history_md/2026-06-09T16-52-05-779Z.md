## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate, organize, and reason about proportional scenarios.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout (data sets), exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop sells 3 smoothies for $9, 5 for $15, and 8 for $24."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per class."

Ask students: *"Which scenario shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?"* Students discuss with partners for 2 minutes, then share.

**Targeting CRI1a:** Students must clarify what "proportional" means by identifying the constant ratio in Scenario A ($3 per smoothie) versus the fixed fee in Scenario B. 
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language during pair-share. Look for students who clearly articulate, "The ratio y/x is constant in A but not in B." Award Level 2 for precise clarification; Level 1 for partial identification (e.g., "A goes up evenly").

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Introduce the four representations using the smoothie example:
- **Table:** Organize (smoothies, cost) pairs and show the constant ratio column
- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** Derive y = 3x; define constant of proportionality (k)
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $3 times the number of smoothies."

Model how each representation connects to the others using a graphic organizer on the board.

**Targeting CRI1b:** Students copy the four-quadrant graphic organizer into notebooks, arranging the same relationship across four representations.
**Assessment:** Collect notebooks at end of class; check whether organizers logically flow from table → graph → equation → verbal. Level 2: clean connections shown with arrows/annotations. Level 1: representations present but disconnected.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Which Data Set Is Proportional?" (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information*
Distribute a handout with **four data sets** (tables) from fictional sources: "Mia's Tutoring Log," "An Online Ad," "Textbook Example," and "Teacher's Verified Table." Two are proportional; two contain errors or non-proportional data disguised as proportional.

Students work in pairs to determine which sets are truly proportional by cross-checking ratios across all rows.

**Targeting CRI2a:** Students must verify each data set by computing y/x for *every* pair, not just one—cross-checking before trusting the source label.
**Targeting CRI2b:** Students judge which data sets are "high-quality" evidence of proportionality versus misleading.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How did you verify this?" Level 2 students check all ratios systematically and explain why one row isn't enough. Level 1 students check one or two ratios. Level -1 students accept the "Textbook" label without verification.

### 4. Collaborative Task — "Choose the Best Plan" (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a: Weighing Pros/Cons and Applying Sound Reasoning*
Present a real-world decision: *"You're planning a class fundraiser. Plan A: Sell bracelets at $2 each (proportional). Plan B: Sell bracelets at $3 each but pay $10 supply fee (non-proportional). Plan C: Bulk pricing at $1.50 each for orders over 20."*

Groups of 3-4 must:
1. Represent each plan as a table, equation, and graph
2. Determine which are proportional
3. Recommend a plan with reasoning, considering small vs. large sales volumes

**Targeting CRI3a:** Students must compare all three plans, weighing benefits at different sales levels.
**Targeting CRI4a:** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical reasoning grounded in their representations.
**Assessment:** Each group presents a 30-second recommendation. Level 2: considers multiple sales scenarios and trade-offs ("Plan A is best for small sales, but Plan C wins after 20 units"). Level 1: picks a plan with surface reasoning ("Plan A is simplest"). Level -1: ignores math entirely.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes*
Exit ticket prompts:
1. Sketch a graph of a proportional relationship and write its equation.
2. **Reflection question:** "What was your *first* assumption about proportional relationships at the start of class? How did your thinking change? What strategy worked best for you when checking if something is proportional?"

**Targeting CRI5a:** Students articulate metacognitive shifts.
**Assessment:** Review exit tickets. Level 2: student names a specific shift ("I used to think any increasing line was proportional, but now I check for the origin and equal ratios"). Level 1: vague reflection ("I learned a lot"). Level -1: no reflection or repeats earlier errors.

### 6. Closing (5 minutes)
Summarize: A proportional relationship has a constant ratio k, passes through the origin on a graph, and follows y = kx. Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying k from various representations.

---

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price tag, etc.). Represent it in all four forms and write one sentence reflecting on how you verified it was proportional.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Puzzle: Connecting Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will recognize, represent, and justify proportional relationships across multiple formats while applying critical thinking skills to organize data, assess mathematical sources, weigh representation trade-offs, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Concept Clarification (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The lesson begins with a verbal description: "A local bakery sells 3 cookies for $4.50, 5 cookies for $7.50, and charges a $2 flat delivery fee for any order." Students are asked to translate this verbal description into a precise mathematical definition of what makes a relationship proportional versus non-proportional. 
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must extract the relevant numbers, eliminate the "noise," and clarify the core mathematical idea by writing a precise definition of proportionality (constant rate of change and passing through the origin) to determine if the bakery's pricing is proportional.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and listen to peer discussions, assessing whether students precisely clarify that the $2 delivery fee disrupts the constant ratio, demonstrating a deeper understanding of the origin (0,0) requirement.

### **Part 2: Structuring the Data (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a disorganized list of raw data points representing the distance a car travels over various random intervals of time (e.g., 2 hours = 120 miles, 0.5 hours = 30 miles, 4 hours = 240 miles). They must arrange this data into a structured table and plot it on a coordinate plane.
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students arrange the scattered data points into a logical, ascending x-y table and translate that table into a properly scaled graph. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect the tables and graphs to assess if the information is arranged with a logical flow (e.g., chronological order of hours, consistent intervals on graph axes) that strengthens the mathematical proof of a straight line.

### **Part 3: Cross-Checking and Source Evaluation (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are presented with a claim from a fictional student's blog: "The equation $y = 60x^2$ represents the car's proportional speed." Students must verify or debunk this claim. They are provided three sources of information: the fictional blog post, their textbook’s glossary definition of proportional equations ($y = kx$), and a digital graphing calculator (Desmos). 
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students rigorously cross-check the blog's claim by graphing $y = 60x^2$ on Desmos and comparing the resulting parabola against the textbook’s definition of a proportional linear equation.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students actively toggling between the textbook definition and the graphing app, assessing their ability to use multiple authoritative sources to invalidate the blog's claim.
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must write a brief justification selecting the highest-quality source to rely on for their final answer. They must explain why the textbook and the graphing calculator are authoritative mathematical sources compared to an unverified blog post.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the students' written justifications to ensure they demonstrate sophisticated judgment, explicitly identifying why the textbook and calculator provide accurate, high-quality verification over the blog.

### **Part 4: Evaluating Trade-offs in Representations (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher asks: "If you need to find the exact cost of 1,452 cookies, which representation should you use: a table, a graph, or an equation?" Students work in small groups to debate the pros and cons of each method.
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each representation. For example, they must articulate that while graphs are excellent for quick visual confirmation of proportionality, equations ($y = 1.5x$) are far more efficient and precise for calculating large, long-term values.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the group whiteboards where students list the pros and cons, looking for comprehensive analysis that acknowledges the limitations of extending a table or graph to 1,452.

### **Part 5: Complex Decision-Making (5 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a final, complex scenario: "A streaming service costs $10 a month, but the first month is free." They must decide if the total cost is proportional to the number of months and justify their conclusion using an equation or graph.
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must anticipate the complexity of the "free month" (which creates an x-intercept of 1, rather than passing through the origin) and use sophisticated logical reasoning to prove the relationship is non-proportional.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final written arguments, checking that they do not use faulty logic (e.g., just seeing the $10 rate and assuming proportionality) but instead apply sound reasoning regarding the y-intercept.

### **Part 6: Metacognitive Closure (5 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket focused entirely on their internal learning process during the lesson. 
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students answer the prompt: "Think about a moment today when you thought a relationship was proportional but realized it wasn't. How did you adjust your thinking process to correct your misunderstanding?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the exit tickets to assess deep metacognitive awareness, looking for evidence that the student recognized a flaw in their initial assumptions (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I strategically check if the graph hits zero").

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.  
**Materials:** Projector/board, graph paper, rulers, calculators, student handout, colored pencils, exit ticket.

---

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes

Display two situations:

1. A bike rental costs $6 per hour.  
2. A bike rental costs $5 to sign up plus $6 per hour.

Ask students to individually answer:

- What quantities are being compared?
- Which situation seems proportional? Why?
- What information would help you decide?

After 3 minutes, students pair-share. Then discuss as a class. Emphasize that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality. Its graph is a straight line through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the quantities, units, and starting cost in each bike rental situation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clarifying that “$6 per hour” compares cost to time, while the $5 signup fee changes the relationship. Check whether students accurately separate relevant from irrelevant information.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes

Model this example:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

Create a table:

| Sugar | Flour |
|---|---|
| 2 | 3 |
| 4 | 6 |
| 6 | 9 |
| 8 | 12 |

Ask: “What is the ratio of flour to sugar each time?” Students should notice the ratio is always **3/2**, so the relationship is proportional.

Then represent it in multiple ways:

- **Verbal:** Flour is always 1.5 times the amount of sugar.
- **Equation:** \( y = 1.5x \), where x = cups of sugar and y = cups of flour.
- **Graph:** Plot the points and show the line goes through the origin.
- **Diagram:** Use a ratio diagram or double number line.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students copy the example into a five-part organizer: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Review student organizers for logical arrangement and consistency across representations.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representation Stations — 15 minutes

Students work in small groups. Give each group one relationship card:

A. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 miles in 3 hours.  
B. A taxi charges $4 plus $2 per mile.  
C. A store sells 5 notebooks for $7.50.  
D. A plant is 3 inches tall and grows 2 inches each week.

Groups must decide whether their relationship is proportional and create at least three representations: table, equation, graph, diagram, or verbal description.

Require students to use at least two “validity checks”:

1. Do the ratios stay constant?  
2. Does the graph go through the origin?  
3. Can the equation be written as \( y = kx \)?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Groups verify whether their relationship is proportional using at least two mathematical tests.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask groups, “How do you know?” Look for students cross-checking with ratios, graphs, and equations rather than guessing from the story.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which given values are most useful for proving proportionality. For example, they should notice that a starting fee or starting height may make a relationship nonproportional.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students use relevant information such as constant ratios and initial values, rather than relying only on surface patterns like “the numbers increase.”

---

### 4. Class Discussion: Choosing the Best Representation — 8 minutes

Invite two groups to present. Ask:

- Which representation made the proportional relationship easiest to see?
- When is a table useful?
- When is a graph useful?
- When is an equation most efficient?
- What are the limitations of each representation?

For example, students may say a table makes ratios easy to compare, while a graph clearly shows whether the line passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare the usefulness of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students naming strengths and weaknesses, such as “A graph shows the origin quickly, but the table makes exact ratios easier to calculate.”

---

### 5. Independent Practice: “Proportional or Not?” — 10 minutes

Students complete three problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A gym charges $12 for 3 classes and $20 for 5 classes. Is the relationship proportional? Represent it with an equation if possible.

**Problem 2:**  
A candle is 10 inches tall and burns 2 inches every hour. Is the height proportional to time? Explain.

**Problem 3:**  
A fruit stand sells 4 pounds of apples for $6. Create a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.

Students must write a claim, evidence, and reasoning for at least one problem.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students make a decision about proportionality and justify it with mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for explanations such as “The ratio 12/3 and 20/5 are both 4, so the cost per class is constant. The equation is y = 4x.”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students answer:

1. What is one way to tell if a relationship is proportional?  
2. Which representation helped you most today: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?  
3. Describe one mistake you made or almost made, and how you corrected your thinking.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on their strategy, representation choice, and possible errors.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for metacognitive comments such as “I first thought it was proportional because both numbers increased, but then I checked the ratios and they were not equal.”

---

### Assessment Summary

By the end of the lesson, students should be able to:

- Identify relevant quantities and units.
- Organize proportional relationships in multiple representations.
- Verify proportionality using ratios, graphs, and equations.
- Choose useful information and appropriate representations.
- Justify decisions with mathematical reasoning.
- Reflect on how their thinking changed during the lesson.

* **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a, Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**

- What quantities are being compared?
- Which situation seems proportional? Why?
- What information would help you decide?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the quantities, units, and starting cost in each bike rental situation.

### 2. Direct Instruction: Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes

Model this example:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

Create a table:

| Sugar | Flour |
|---|---|
| 2 | 3 |
| 4 | 6 |
| 6 | 9 |
| 8 | 12 |

Ask: “What is the ratio of flour to sugar each time?” Students should notice the ratio is always **3/2**, so the relationship is proportional.

Then represent it in multiple ways:

- **Verbal:** Flour is always 1.5 times the amount of sugar.
- **Equation:** \( y = 1.5x \), where x = cups of sugar and y = cups of flour.
- **Graph:** Plot the points and show the line goes through the origin.
- **Diagram:** Use a ratio diagram or double number line.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students copy the example into a five-part organizer: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Review student organizers for logical arrangement and consistency across representations.

A. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 miles in 3 hours.

C. A store sells 5 notebooks for $7.50.  
D. A plant is 3 inches tall and grows 2 inches each week.

Groups must decide whether their relationship is proportional and create at least three representations: table, equation, graph, diagram, or verbal description.

Require students to use at least two “validity checks”:

1. Do the ratios stay constant?  
2. Does the graph go through the origin?  
3. Can the equation be written as \( y = kx \)?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Groups verify whether their relationship is proportional using at least two mathematical tests.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask groups, “How do you know?” Look for students cross-checking with ratios, graphs, and equations rather than guessing from the story.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which given values are most useful for proving proportionality. For example, they should notice that a starting fee or starting height may make a relationship nonproportional.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students use relevant information such as constant ratios and initial values, rather than relying only on surface patterns like “the numbers increase.”

### 4. Class Discussion: Choosing the Best Representation — 8 minutes

Invite two groups to present. Ask:

- Which representation made the proportional relationship easiest to see?
- When is a table useful?
- When is a graph useful?
- When is an equation most efficient?
- What are the limitations of each representation?

For example, students may say a table makes ratios easy to compare, while a graph clearly shows whether the line passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare the usefulness of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students naming strengths and weaknesses, such as “A graph shows the origin quickly, but the table makes exact ratios easier to calculate.”

### 5. Independent Practice: “Proportional or Not?” — 10 minutes

Students complete three problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A gym charges $12 for 3 classes and $20 for 5 classes. Is the relationship proportional? Represent it with an equation if possible.

**Problem 2:**  
A candle is 10 inches tall and burns 2 inches every hour. Is the height proportional to time? Explain.

**Problem 3:**  
A fruit stand sells 4 pounds of apples for $6. Create a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.

Students must write a claim, evidence, and reasoning for at least one problem.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students make a decision about proportionality and justify it with mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for explanations such as “The ratio 12/3 and 20/5 are both 4, so the cost per class is constant. The equation is y = 4x.”

Students answer:

1. What is one way to tell if a relationship is proportional?  
2. Which representation helped you most today: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?  
3. Describe one mistake you made or almost made, and how you corrected your thinking.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on their strategy, representation choice, and possible errors.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for metacognitive comments such as “I first thought it was proportional because both numbers increased, but then I checked the ratios and they were not equal.”

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate, organize, and reason about proportional scenarios.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout (data sets), exit tickets, whiteboard, colored pencils.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop sells 3 smoothies for $9, 5 for $15, and 8 for $24."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per class."

Ask students: *"Which scenario shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?"* Students discuss with partners for 2 minutes, then share.

**Targeting CRI1a:** Students must clarify what "proportional" means by identifying the constant ratio in Scenario A ($3 per smoothie) versus the fixed fee in Scenario B.
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language during pair-share. Look for students who clearly articulate, "The ratio y/x is constant in A but not in B." Award Level 2 for precise clarification; Level 1 for partial identification (e.g., "A goes up evenly"); Level -1 if students confuse "increasing" with "proportional."

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Introduce the five representations using the smoothie example:
- **Table:** Organize (smoothies, cost) pairs and show the constant ratio column
- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** Derive y = 3x; define constant of proportionality (k)
- **Diagram:** Build a double number line or ratio diagram
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $3 times the number of smoothies."

Model how each representation connects to the others using a graphic organizer on the board.

**Targeting CRI1b:** Students copy a five-part organizer (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal) into notebooks, arranging the same relationship across all representations.
**Assessment:** Collect notebooks at end of class; check whether organizers logically flow between representations. Level 2: clean connections shown with arrows/annotations. Level 1: representations present but disconnected. Level -1: contradictory values across representations.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Verify the Claim" (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information*
Distribute a handout with a fictional blog claim: *"The equation y = 60x² represents a car's proportional speed."* Also provide three data sets from sources labeled "Mia's Tutoring Log," "Textbook Example," and "Teacher's Verified Table." Some are proportional; some contain errors disguised as proportional.

Students work in pairs to (a) debunk or verify the blog using the textbook definition (y = kx) and a quick graph sketch, and (b) cross-check ratios across every row of the data tables.

**Targeting CRI2a:** Students must verify by computing y/x for *every* pair and comparing against the y = kx definition—cross-checking before trusting any source label.
**Targeting CRI2b:** Students write a brief justification naming which sources are highest quality (verified definition + computed ratios) versus which are unreliable (unsupported blog).
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, "How did you verify this?" Level 2 students systematically check all ratios and explain why one row isn't enough. Level 1 students check one or two ratios. Level -1 students accept the "Textbook" label without verification or repeat the blog's error.

### 4. Collaborative Task — "Choose the Best Plan" (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a: Weighing Pros/Cons and Applying Sound Reasoning*
Present a real-world decision: *"You're planning a class fundraiser. Plan A: Sell bracelets at $2 each (proportional). Plan B: Sell bracelets at $3 each but pay $10 supply fee (non-proportional). Plan C: Bulk pricing at $1.50 each for orders over 20."*

Groups of 3-4 must:
1. Represent each plan as a table, equation, and graph
2. Determine which are proportional
3. Recommend a plan with reasoning, considering small vs. large sales volumes
4. Write a claim-evidence-reasoning statement justifying their choice

**Targeting CRI3a:** Students compare all three plans, weighing benefits at different sales levels and naming trade-offs of each representation (e.g., "A graph shows the origin quickly, but the table makes exact ratios easier to calculate").
**Targeting CRI4a:** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical reasoning grounded in their representations.
**Assessment:** Each group presents a 30-second recommendation. Level 2: considers multiple sales scenarios and trade-offs ("Plan A is best for small sales, but Plan C wins after 20 units"). Level 1: picks a plan with surface reasoning ("Plan A is simplest"). Level -1: ignores math entirely or uses faulty logic.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes*
Exit ticket prompts:
1. Sketch a graph of a proportional relationship and write its equation.
2. **Reflection question:** "Think about a moment today when you thought a relationship was proportional but realized it wasn't. What was your *first* assumption? How did you adjust your thinking? What strategy worked best for you when checking if something is proportional?"

**Targeting CRI5a:** Students articulate metacognitive shifts and strategy adjustments.
**Assessment:** Review exit tickets. Level 2: student names a specific shift ("I used to think any increasing line was proportional, but now I check for the origin and equal ratios"). Level 1: vague reflection ("I learned a lot"). Level -1: no reflection or repeats earlier errors without awareness.

### 6. Closing (5 minutes)
Summarize: A proportional relationship has a constant ratio k, passes through the origin on a graph, and follows y = kx. Preview tomorrow's lesson on identifying k from various representations.
