## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout: "Smoothie Shop Scenarios"
- Whiteboard and markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is This a Deal?" (8 minutes)
Display two pricing options on the board:
- **Option A:** 3 smoothies for $9
- **Option B:** 5 smoothies for $14

Ask: *"Which is the better deal? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Guide them to recognize unit rates ($3.00 vs. $2.80 per smoothie).

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must identify the relevant quantities (price, quantity) and clarify what "better deal" means mathematically.
- **Assessment:** Listen for precise language like "per smoothie" or "unit rate." Note students who clarify ambiguous terms versus those who guess.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Defining Proportional Relationships (10 minutes)
Introduce the definition: *Two quantities are proportional if their ratio (y/x) is constant (k), expressed as y = kx.*

Walk through Option A as an example, building a table:

| Smoothies (x) | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost (y) | $3 | $6 | $9 | $12 |

Show that y/x = 3 always → equation: y = 3x. Plot points on a graph; emphasize the line passes through the origin.

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to structure data across representations—table → equation → graph.
- **Assessment:** During guided notes, check that students' tables, equations, and graphs are aligned and labeled logically.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Comparing Representations (12 minutes)
Distribute the "Smoothie Shop Scenarios" handout with three scenarios:
- **Scenario 1:** A table showing proportional data
- **Scenario 2:** A graph that does NOT pass through origin (non-proportional)
- **Scenario 3:** A verbal description: "Maria earns $15 plus $8 per hour babysitting."

Students work in pairs to determine which are proportional and justify using multiple representations.

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity) & CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must verify whether each scenario truly represents a proportional relationship by cross-checking the ratio across multiple data points—not relying on one piece of evidence.
- **Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you know for sure? Did you check more than one ratio?"* Students at Level 2 will check multiple points and reference the y = kx structure; Level 1 students may check only one ratio.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: "Choose Your Plan" (15 minutes)
Present a real-world decision:
> *A gym offers two membership plans:*
> - **Plan X:** $5 per visit
> - **Plan Y:** $20 flat fee + $2 per visit

In groups of 3, students must:
1. Create a table for each plan
2. Write an equation for each
3. Graph both on the same coordinate plane
4. Identify which is proportional and explain why
5. Recommend a plan based on visit frequency

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must evaluate trade-offs—Plan X is proportional and better for fewer visits; Plan Y is cheaper long-term but not proportional.
- **Assessment:** Group posters should explicitly compare scenarios (e.g., "If you visit 10+ times, Plan Y saves money, but it isn't proportional because of the flat fee"). Look for systematic comparison versus one-sided reasoning.

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Recommendations must be supported by mathematical evidence.
- **Assessment:** Evaluate whether conclusions logically follow from the data. A Level 2 response anticipates complexity: *"It depends on how often you go—at exactly 6.67 visits, costs are equal."*

---

### 5. Closure: Metacognitive Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

Each student completes the following on an exit slip:

1. **Math content:** Give one example and one non-example of a proportional relationship. Justify each.
2. **Reflection prompts:**
   - "What strategy helped you most today—tables, equations, or graphs? Why?"
   - "What was a mistake you almost made (or did make), and how did you catch it?"
   - "If you tackled this lesson again, what would you do differently?"

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** The reflection prompts force students to articulate their reasoning strategies and self-correct.
- **Assessment:** Level 2 responses show metacognitive awareness (e.g., *"I assumed any straight line was proportional, but then I realized it has to go through the origin—I'll check that first next time"*). Level 1 responses mention strategies superficially without analyzing them.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-made tables for struggling students; pair strategically.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to create their own non-proportional scenario that *looks* proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world example of a proportional relationship (recipe, price tag, map scale). Represent it in all four forms: table, equation, graph, and verbal description.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Many Faces of Proportions: Tables, Graphs, and Equations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to recognize proportional relationships from real-world scenarios and represent them accurately using verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations. 

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Introduction (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world scenario displayed on the board: *"A local bakery sells 3 chocolate chip cookies for $4.50. How much would 10 cookies cost? How can we prove the relationship between cookies and cost is proportional?"* 

**CRI1a: Clarifying information**
*   **Activity:** Before doing any math, students independently read the verbal description and annotate the text. They must define the independent variable ($x$ = number of cookies) and the dependent variable ($y$ = total cost), clarifying the exact quantities being compared to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
*   **Assessment:** The teacher circulates to observe students’ annotations and written definitions of the variables. Mastery is demonstrated when a student precisely identifies the variables and the unit rate context rather than just guessing numbers.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**
*   **Activity:** Once the variables are clarified, students are tasked with organizing this verbal information into a T-chart (table). They must arrange the data sequentially (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4 cookies) to establish a logical flow that reveals the constant rate of change.
*   **Assessment:** The teacher reviews the students' tables to ensure the information is structured logically, with $x$ and $y$ in the correct columns and the values scaling consistently without scattered or contradictory data jumps.

---

### **Part 2: Direct Instruction and Guided Practice (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher guides the class in translating their organized table into a coordinate graph and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). The teacher explains that the constant of proportionality ($k$) is the unit rate ($1.50 per cookie). 

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
*   **Activity:** Students are asked to cross-check the validity of their table data by plotting the points on a coordinate plane. They use the graph as a secondary mathematical "source" to verify their initial calculations. If the graph forms a straight line through the origin $(0,0)$, their table data is validated. 
*   **Assessment:** The teacher observes students actively comparing their table to their graph. If a point is off the linear path, the teacher assesses whether the student rigorously cross-checks their math to find the error rather than ignoring the discrepancy.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
*   **Activity:** To write the equation $y = 1.5x$, students must calculate $k = y/x$. The teacher provides a complex graph with some points landing on grid intersections (whole numbers) and others floating in the middle of grid squares (decimals/fractions). Students must select the highest-quality, most reliable data points from the graph to calculate $k$.
*   **Assessment:** The teacher asks students to justify *which* coordinate pair they chose to calculate the constant. High-level performance is observed when students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting clear, exact intersection points rather than estimating fractional coordinates.

---

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a set of "Scenario Cards." Some cards feature proportional relationships (e.g., earning $15 per hour), while others feature non-proportional relationships (e.g., a gym membership with a $20 startup fee plus $10 a month). Groups must represent their scenarios in all forms (table, graph, equation) and determine if they are proportional.

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
*   **Activity:** The teacher pauses the group work and asks: *"If you needed to find the cost of 1,000 cookies, which representation—a table, a graph, or an equation—would be the best choice?"* Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each alternative representation.
*   **Assessment:** The teacher listens to group discussions and reads their written conclusions. Students should be assessed on their ability to weigh the visual clarity of a graph against the long-term mathematical efficiency of an equation for large numbers.

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
*   **Activity:** Groups must make a final decision on whether the gym membership scenario is proportional. They must write a justification applying mathematical rules to their decision.
*   **Assessment:** The teacher evaluates the groups' written justifications. Sophisticated reasoning is demonstrated when students anticipate complexities, correctly noting that while the gym fee increases at a constant rate, it does not pass through the origin $(0,0)$, and therefore the ratio $y/x$ is not constant.

---

### **Part 4: Closure and Exit Ticket (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher reviews the core concepts of the standard, showing how tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions are all interconnected languages telling the same mathematical story. Students are then given a reflective Exit Ticket.

**CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
*   **Activity:** On the Exit Ticket, students respond to the following prompt: *"Think about a moment today when you were confused, made a mistake, or had to change your mind (for example, mixing up the x and y axes, or miscalculating the constant of proportionality). How did you realize your error, and how did you adjust your thinking to fix it?"*
*   **Assessment:** The teacher reads the exit tickets to assess students' metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly detail the student's internal thought process, demonstrating self-awareness and a strategic adjustment in their problem-solving approach rather than just stating "I got the wrong answer and fixed it."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Grade Level:** Middle School, Grade 7  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships by checking for a constant ratio or unit rate.
2. Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal statement.
3. Explain how they know a relationship is or is not proportional.
4. Compare different representations and decide which is most useful for a given question.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- “Proportional or Not?” handout
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display the following situation:

“A movie theater charges $8 per ticket.”

Ask students to write two things they notice and one thing they wonder. Then ask:

- “If 1 ticket costs $8, how much do 2 tickets cost?”
- “How much do 5 tickets cost?”
- “What pattern do you see?”

Briefly discuss that the cost increases by the same amount for each ticket and that the ratio of cost to tickets stays constant.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students identify relevant quantities: number of tickets, total cost, and cost per ticket.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students accurately naming the two quantities and the unit rate. Check whether students can state, “The cost is $8 for each ticket.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when two quantities have a constant ratio. In a proportional relationship:

- The ratio between quantities stays the same.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- The equation can be written as `y = kx`, where `k` is the constant of proportionality.

Use the movie ticket example.

Create a table:

| Tickets | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask students to calculate `cost ÷ tickets` for each row.

Then show the equation:

`y = 8x`

Explain that `x` represents tickets, `y` represents cost, and 8 is the constant of proportionality.

Graph the points `(1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)` and point out that the graph forms a straight line through `(0,0)`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students see the same relationship arranged in a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.

**Observe/Assess:** Check whether students organize the relationship logically in their notes and connect the same unit rate across multiple forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Match the Representations — 12 minutes

Give students a handout with mixed representations. Example:

**Verbal description:** A car travels 60 miles per hour.  
**Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

**Equation:** `y = 60x`  
**Graph:** Straight line through `(0,0), (1,60), (2,120)`

Students work in pairs to match verbal descriptions, tables, equations, and graphs that represent the same relationship.

Include one non-proportional example:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 50 |
| 2 | 100 |
| 3 | 140 |

Ask: “Is this proportional? How do you know?”

Students should notice that the ratios are not constant: `50 ÷ 1 = 50`, `100 ÷ 2 = 50`, but `140 ÷ 3 ≠ 50`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and verbal description agree with one another.

**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify matches using at least two representations. For example: “The equation matches the table because each y-value is 60 times the x-value.”

---

### 4. Group Task: Build All Five Representations — 15 minutes

Assign each group one scenario:

1. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 batches of cookies.
2. A printer prints 12 pages per minute.
3. A store sells 4 notebooks for $10.
4. A cyclist rides 15 miles every hour.

Each group must create:

- A verbal description
- A table with at least four rows
- A graph
- An equation
- A diagram or ratio model

For example, if the scenario is “A printer prints 12 pages per minute,” students might write:

- Verbal: The printer prints 12 pages each minute.
- Table: 1 minute = 12 pages, 2 minutes = 24 pages, etc.
- Equation: `y = 12x`
- Graph: A line through `(0,0), (1,12), (2,24)`
- Diagram: Groups of 12 pages for each minute

Remind students that good mathematical representations need clear labels, accurate values, and a consistent scale.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide whether their representations are complete, accurate, and useful.

**Observe/Assess:** Circulate and ask: “Are your axes labeled?” “Does your graph include the origin?” “Does your equation match every row in your table?” Use these questions to assess whether students are selecting and presenting high-quality mathematical information.

---

### 5. Discussion: Which Representation Is Best? — 6 minutes

Ask groups:

- “Which representation helped you see the unit rate most clearly?”
- “Which representation helped you predict larger values?”
- “Which representation made it easiest to explain the relationship to someone else?”

Students may say:

- Tables are useful for seeing repeated values.
- Graphs are useful for seeing whether the line goes through the origin.
- Equations are useful for calculating any value quickly.
- Diagrams are useful for visualizing equal groups.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare the strengths and limitations of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students giving reasons, not just preferences. For example, “The equation is best for finding 20 minutes because I can multiply directly.”

---

### 6. Individual Practice and Reasoning — 5 minutes

Display:

“A taxi charges $4 to start the ride and $2 for each mile.”

Ask students: “Is this relationship proportional? Explain.”

Students should identify that it is **not proportional** because there is an initial charge. The equation would be `y = 2x + 4`, not `y = kx`, and the graph would not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students must make a decision and justify it using mathematical evidence.

**Observe/Assess:** Look for reasoning such as: “It is not proportional because when miles are 0, the cost is $4, not $0.”

---

### 7. Exit Ticket: Reflect and Represent — 2 minutes

Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a table represents a proportional relationship?”
2. “What is one mistake you made or almost made today, and how did you fix your thinking?”
3. Represent this situation with an equation: “A dog walker earns $9 per dog walked.”

Expected equation: `y = 9x`

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on their own reasoning and describe how they adjusted their thinking.

**Observe/Assess:** Review written responses for evidence of metacognition, such as “I first forgot to check the origin, but then I realized proportional graphs must go through `(0,0)`.”

---

## Assessment

Use student work from the group task, individual reasoning question, and exit ticket. Students demonstrate mastery if they can:

- Identify constant ratios in tables.
- Explain why a relationship is proportional or not proportional.
- Create accurate tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
- Justify decisions using mathematical evidence.

For the Critical Thinking subcompetencies, use a simple `-1 to 2` scale during observation and written review:

- `2`: Clear, accurate, well-supported reasoning
- `1`: Partially clear or incomplete reasoning
- `0`: Not enough evidence
- `-1`: Incorrect or misleading reasoning

Focus on evidence from what students say, write, draw, and explain during the lesson.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Grade Level:** Middle School, Grade 7  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships by checking for a constant ratio or unit rate.
2. Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal statement.
3. Explain how they know a relationship is or is not proportional.
4. Compare different representations and decide which is most useful for a given question.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- “Proportional or Not?” handout
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display the following situation:

“A movie theater charges $8 per ticket.”

Ask students to write two things they notice and one thing they wonder. Then ask:

- “If 1 ticket costs $8, how much do 2 tickets cost?”
- “How much do 5 tickets cost?”
- “What pattern do you see?”

Briefly discuss that the cost increases by the same amount for each ticket and that the ratio of cost to tickets stays constant.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Students identify relevant quantities: number of tickets, total cost, and cost per ticket.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students accurately naming the two quantities and the unit rate. Check whether students can state, “The cost is $8 for each ticket.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** when two quantities have a constant ratio. In a proportional relationship:

- The ratio between quantities stays the same.
- The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- The equation can be written as `y = kx`, where `k` is the constant of proportionality.

Use the movie ticket example.

Create a table:

| Tickets | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask students to calculate `cost ÷ tickets` for each row.

Then show the equation:

`y = 8x`

Explain that `x` represents tickets, `y` represents cost, and 8 is the constant of proportionality.

Graph the points `(1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)` and point out that the graph forms a straight line through `(0,0)`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Students see the same relationship arranged in a table, equation, graph, and verbal description.

**Observe/Assess:** Check whether students organize the relationship logically in their notes and connect the same unit rate across multiple forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Match the Representations — 12 minutes

Give students a handout with mixed representations. Example:

**Verbal description:** A car travels 60 miles per hour.  
**Table:**

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 60 |
| 2 | 120 |
| 3 | 180 |

**Equation:** `y = 60x`  
**Graph:** Straight line through `(0,0), (1,60), (2,120)`

Students work in pairs to match verbal descriptions, tables, equations, and graphs that represent the same relationship.

Include one non-proportional example:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 50 |
| 2 | 100 |
| 3 | 140 |

Ask: “Is this proportional? How do you know?”

Students should notice that the ratios are not constant: `50 ÷ 1 = 50`, `100 ÷ 2 = 50`, but `140 ÷ 3 ≠ 50`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Students cross-check whether the table, graph, equation, and verbal description agree with one another.

**Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify matches using at least two representations. For example: “The equation matches the table because each y-value is 60 times the x-value.”

---

### 4. Group Task: Build All Five Representations — 15 minutes

Assign each group one scenario:

1. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 batches of cookies.
2. A printer prints 12 pages per minute.
3. A store sells 4 notebooks for $10.
4. A cyclist rides 15 miles every hour.

Each group must create:

- A verbal description
- A table with at least four rows
- A graph
- An equation
- A diagram or ratio model

For example, if the scenario is “A printer prints 12 pages per minute,” students might write:

- Verbal: The printer prints 12 pages each minute.
- Table: 1 minute = 12 pages, 2 minutes = 24 pages, etc.
- Equation: `y = 12x`
- Graph: A line through `(0,0), (1,12), (2,24)`
- Diagram: Groups of 12 pages for each minute

Remind students that good mathematical representations need clear labels, accurate values, and a consistent scale.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Students decide whether their representations are complete, accurate, and useful.

**Observe/Assess:** Circulate and ask: “Are your axes labeled?” “Does your graph include the origin?” “Does your equation match every row in your table?” Use these questions to assess whether students are selecting and presenting high-quality mathematical information.

---

### 5. Discussion: Which Representation Is Best? — 6 minutes

Ask groups:

- “Which representation helped you see the unit rate most clearly?”
- “Which representation helped you predict larger values?”
- “Which representation made it easiest to explain the relationship to someone else?”

Students may say:

- Tables are useful for seeing repeated values.
- Graphs are useful for seeing whether the line goes through the origin.
- Equations are useful for calculating any value quickly.
- Diagrams are useful for visualizing equal groups.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Students compare the strengths and limitations of tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

**Observe/Assess:** Listen for students giving reasons, not just preferences. For example, “The equation is best for finding 20 minutes because I can multiply directly.”

---

### 6. Individual Practice and Reasoning — 5 minutes

Display:

“A taxi charges $4 to start the ride and $2 for each mile.”

Ask students: “Is this relationship proportional? Explain.”

Students should identify that it is **not proportional** because there is an initial charge. The equation would be `y = 2x + 4`, not `y = kx`, and the graph would not pass through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Students must make a decision and justify it using mathematical evidence.

**Observe/Assess:** Look for reasoning such as: “It is not proportional because when miles are 0, the cost is $4, not $0.”

---

### 7. Exit Ticket: Reflect and Represent — 2 minutes

Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a table represents a proportional relationship?”
2. “What is one mistake you made or almost made today, and how did you fix your thinking?”
3. Represent this situation with an equation: “A dog walker earns $9 per dog walked.”

Expected equation: `y = 9x`

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Students reflect on their own reasoning and describe how they adjusted their thinking.

**Observe/Assess:** Review written responses for evidence of metacognition, such as “I first forgot to check the origin, but then I realized proportional graphs must go through `(0,0)`.”

---

Use student work from the group task, individual reasoning question, and exit ticket. Students demonstrate mastery if they can:

- Identify constant ratios in tables.
- Explain why a relationship is proportional or not proportional.
- Create accurate tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
- Justify decisions using mathematical evidence.

For the Critical Thinking subcompetencies, use a simple `-1 to 2` scale during observation and written review:

- `2`: Clear, accurate, well-supported reasoning
- `1`: Partially clear or incomplete reasoning
- `0`: Not enough evidence
- `-1`: Incorrect or misleading reasoning

Focus on evidence from what students say, write, draw, and explain during the lesson.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is This a Deal?" (8 minutes)
Display two pricing options:
- **Option A:** 3 smoothies for $9
- **Option B:** 5 smoothies for $14

Before calculating, students annotate the problem, defining the independent variable (x = smoothies) and dependent variable (y = cost). They discuss with a partner: *"Which is the better deal? How do you know?"* Guide them to unit rates ($3.00 vs. $2.80 per smoothie).

**🎯 CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must precisely identify the relevant quantities and clarify what "better deal" means mathematically.
- **Assessment:** Circulate to review annotations. Listen for precise language like "per smoothie" or "unit rate." Mastery is shown when students name variables exactly rather than guessing.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Defining Proportional Relationships (10 minutes)
Introduce: *Two quantities are proportional if their ratio (y/x) is constant (k), expressed as y = kx, and the graph passes through the origin.*

Build Option A across representations:

| Smoothies (x) | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost (y) | $3 | $6 | $9 | $12 |

Show y/x = 3 always → equation y = 3x. Plot the points and draw a diagram (groups of $3 per smoothie). Emphasize the line passes through the origin.

**🎯 CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to structure the same relationship across table → equation → graph → diagram in a logical, parallel sequence.
- **Assessment:** Review guided notes for aligned, labeled representations where each form consistently shows k = 3.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Verifying Representations (12 minutes)
Distribute the "Smoothie Shop Scenarios" handout with three scenarios:
- **Scenario 1:** A table of proportional data
- **Scenario 2:** A graph NOT passing through the origin (with some clean intersection points and some fractional/estimated points)
- **Scenario 3:** Verbal description: "Maria earns $15 plus $8 per hour babysitting."

Pairs determine which are proportional, justifying with multiple representations. For Scenario 2, students must choose *which* coordinate points to use to compute k.

**🎯 CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students cross-check by verifying the ratio across multiple data points and checking that table, graph, and equation agree.
- **Assessment:** Ask, *"How do you know for sure? Did you check more than one ratio?"* Level 2 students check multiple points and reference y = kx; Level 1 students check only one.

**🎯 CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must select high-quality, exact coordinate points (clean grid intersections) rather than estimating from fractional positions.
- **Assessment:** Ask students to justify *which* points they chose. Sophisticated judgment is shown when they pick reliable, exact values and explain why estimated points weaken the calculation.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: "Choose Your Plan" (15 minutes)
Present:
> *A gym offers two membership plans:*
> - **Plan X:** $5 per visit
> - **Plan Y:** $20 flat fee + $2 per visit

In groups of 3, students:
1. Create a table for each plan
2. Write an equation for each
3. Graph both on one coordinate plane
4. Identify which is proportional and explain why
5. Recommend a plan based on visit frequency

Then pose: *"Which representation—table, graph, or equation—is best for predicting the cost of 100 visits, and why?"*

**🎯 CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students evaluate trade-offs between plans AND between representations (tables show patterns, graphs show origin behavior, equations scale efficiently).
- **Assessment:** Posters should explicitly compare: *"If you visit 10+ times, Plan Y saves money, but it isn't proportional because of the flat fee."* Look for systematic comparison versus one-sided reasoning.

**🎯 CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Recommendations must follow logically from mathematical evidence, noting that Plan Y fails proportionality because at x=0, y=$20, not $0.
- **Assessment:** Level 2 responses anticipate complexity: *"At about 6.67 visits, costs are equal—below that, Plan X is cheaper."*

---

### 5. Closure: Metacognitive Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

Students complete:

1. **Content:** Give one example and one non-example of a proportional relationship. Justify each. Then represent this situation as an equation: *"A taxi charges $4 to start plus $2 per mile."* (Expected: y = 2x + 4; not proportional.)
2. **Reflection prompts:**
   - "What strategy helped you most—tables, equations, graphs, or diagrams? Why?"
   - "Describe a moment you were confused or made a mistake (e.g., mixing up axes, forgetting the origin). How did you realize and adjust your thinking?"

**🎯 CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Prompts force students to articulate strategies and self-correct.
- **Assessment:** Level 2: *"I assumed any straight line was proportional, then realized it must pass through the origin—I'll check that first next time."* Level 1 responses mention strategies superficially without analysis.
