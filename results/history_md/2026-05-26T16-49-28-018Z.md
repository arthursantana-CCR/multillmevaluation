## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with mixed scenarios (some proportional, some not), exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $3 per visit."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional? How can you tell?"* Students discuss with a partner for 2 minutes, then share. Record student ideas without judgment, encouraging precise language ("doubles when…", "constant ratio…").

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Listen for students refining vague statements ("it goes up") into precise claims ("the cost per smoothie stays the same at $3"). *Assessment:* Note which students push peers to define "proportional" clearly during turn-and-talk.

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Define a proportional relationship: two quantities where y/x = k (constant of proportionality). Demonstrate four representations using Scenario A:
- **Table:** (1, $3), (2, $6), (3, $9)
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Verbal:** "Cost is three times the number of smoothies."

Contrast each with Scenario B, showing the graph does NOT pass through the origin, the ratio y/x changes, etc.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Provide students a four-quadrant graphic organizer to record each representation side-by-side. *Assessment:* Collect organizers and check whether students placed corresponding information in logically aligned cells with consistent labeling.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Sorting Activity (12 minutes)
Distribute six cards per pair, each showing a different representation (some tables, some graphs, some equations, some verbal). Students sort cards into "Proportional" and "Not Proportional" piles and must justify each placement using TWO different representations (e.g., verify with both a ratio check AND graphing through origin).

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Require students to cross-check their classification using at least two methods (ratio table + equation form, or graph + verbal description). *Assessment:* Circulate and ask: "How do you know this is proportional? Did you confirm it a second way?" Document on a clipboard which pairs verified using multiple representations versus those relying on one.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Include one "trick" card with a near-proportional table (e.g., (2, 6), (4, 12), (6, 19)) where students must determine whether the data quality supports a proportional claim. *Assessment:* Look for students who notice the irregularity rather than assuming proportionality from the first two ratios.

### 4. Collaborative Decision Task (10 minutes)
Present this problem: *"You need to print 200 flyers. Print Shop X charges $0.15 per flyer. Print Shop Y charges $10 setup plus $0.10 per flyer. Print Shop Z charges $30 flat. Which is the best deal? Represent each as an equation and graph."*

Groups of three must produce a recommendation with mathematical justification.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students must compare all three options, noting which is proportional and at what quantity each becomes preferable. *Assessment:* Review each group's written recommendation for evidence of comparing trade-offs (e.g., "Shop X is best under 200 flyers, but Shop Z wins at higher volumes").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Groups must justify their final choice using the equations and break-even points, anticipating "what if" scenarios. *Assessment:* During share-outs, ask one probing question per group: "What would change your recommendation?" Note logical coherence of responses.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Students individually complete an exit ticket:
1. Create a table, graph, and equation for the proportional relationship: "A car travels 60 miles per hour."
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"What was one mistake or confusion you had today about proportional relationships? How did your thinking change by the end of class?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** The reflection prompt explicitly asks students to identify and adjust their reasoning. *Assessment:* Read exit tickets for metacognitive depth—do students describe a specific misconception (e.g., "I thought any line was proportional, but now I know it must pass through the origin") and how they corrected it?

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Reinforce the four-representation framework. Preview tomorrow's lesson: identifying the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Decide whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain their reasoning using the constant of proportionality.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Graph paper  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Rulers  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A store sells apples for $2 per pound.  
**Situation B:** A taxi charges $3 to start, plus $2 per mile.

Ask students to write:  
“Which situation shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

After 3 minutes, have students share with a partner, then discuss as a class. Guide students to identify that Situation A is proportional because the ratio of cost to pounds is constant and starts at 0. Situation B is not proportional because of the starting fee.

Emphasize:  
A proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality. Its graph is a straight line through the origin.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Ways to Represent Proportions — 10 minutes
Use the example: “Movie tickets cost $6 each.”

Create five representations together:

**Verbal:** Each movie ticket costs $6.  
**Table:**

| Tickets | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

**Equation:** \( y = 6x \)  
**Graph:** Plot points \((1,6), (2,12), (3,18), (4,24)\); connect with a straight line through \((0,0)\).  
**Diagram:** Draw groups showing $6 for each ticket.

Ask: “Where do we see the same idea in every representation?” Students should identify that the value 6 appears as the unit rate or constant of proportionality.

---

### 3. Partner Task: Sorting Representations — 12 minutes
Give pairs a set of cards with mixed representations: tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Some are proportional and some are not.

Examples:
- Table: \((1,4), (2,8), (3,12)\)  
- Table: \((1,5), (2,9), (3,13)\)  
- Equation: \( y = 3x \)  
- Equation: \( y = x + 3 \)  
- Graph: line through origin  
- Graph: line not through origin  
- Verbal: “A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 1 cup of sugar.”  
- Verbal: “A gym charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $5 per visit.”

Students sort cards into:
1. Proportional  
2. Not proportional  
3. Not sure yet

They must write one reason for each “not sure yet” card and one reason for two proportional cards.

Teacher circulates and asks:
- “What stays constant?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Can this be written as \( y = kx \)?”
- “What evidence supports your choice?”

---

### 4. Group Challenge: Represent One Relationship in Five Ways — 15 minutes
Each group receives a scenario:

**Scenario:** A cyclist travels 12 miles every hour.

Groups must create:
1. A verbal description  
2. A table with at least four ordered pairs  
3. A graph  
4. An equation  
5. A diagram or ratio model  

They must also answer:
- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “How do all five representations show the same relationship?”
- “How could you check that your answer is valid?”

Groups post their work or share from desks.

During sharing, require students to use sentence frames:
- “The relationship is proportional because…”  
- “The constant of proportionality is…”  
- “I know this representation matches because…”  
- “One possible mistake would be…”  

---

### 5. Critical Thinking Focus and Assessment Moments — Embedded Throughout

**CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Activity: During the warm-up and sorting task, students identify what information matters: unit rate, origin, and constant ratio.  
Observation/assessment: Listen for precise explanations such as, “The taxi example is not proportional because the $3 fee means the cost is not 0 when miles are 0.”  
Target level: 2 — clarifies ideas precisely.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Activity: In the group challenge, students organize one relationship into five connected representations.  
Observation/assessment: Check whether the table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description match logically. Evidence might include: “Our table uses multiples of 12, so our equation is \( y = 12x \).”  
Target level: 2 — strengthens logical flow.

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Activity: Students verify whether cards are proportional by checking more than one feature, such as constant ratio and graph through origin.  
Observation/assessment: Look for students cross-checking: “This table has a constant ratio, and the graph would go through the origin, so it is proportional.”  
Target level: 2 — uses multiple checks.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Activity: Students decide which evidence is most useful for proving proportionality.  
Observation/assessment: Ask, “Which evidence is stronger: one matching pair or a constant ratio across the table?” Look for responses such as, “One point is not enough; we need to check all the ratios.”  
Target level: 2 — selects high-quality mathematical evidence.

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Activity: In pairs, students compare strategies: using a table, graph, or equation to decide proportionality.  
Observation/assessment: Listen for trade-off thinking: “The graph is quick if it is already drawn, but the table helps us calculate the constant ratio exactly.”  
Target level: 1–2 depending on depth.

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Activity: Students justify why each card belongs in proportional or not proportional categories.  
Observation/assessment: Look for logical claims supported by evidence: “This is not proportional because \( y = x + 3 \) cannot be written as \( y = kx \).”  
Target level: 2 — sound reasoning.

**CRI5a: Reflecting on thinking processes**  
Activity: Before the exit ticket, students answer: “What is one mistake you almost made today, and how did you correct your thinking?”  
Observation/assessment: Look for metacognitive responses such as, “I thought every straight line was proportional, but then I remembered it also has to pass through the origin.”  
Target level: 2 — adjusts thinking strategically.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 8 minutes
Students complete independently:

1. A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |
| 8 | 40 |

Is this proportional? Explain.

2. Write an equation for the relationship.

3. Describe what the constant of proportionality means.

4. Reflection: “What representation helps you recognize proportional relationships best, and why?”

Expected responses:
- Yes, proportional because \( y/x = 5 \) for every pair.
- Equation: \( y = 5x \)
- The constant of proportionality is 5; it means each x-unit corresponds to 5 y-units.

---

End by reinforcing:  
“To prove a relationship is proportional, we need evidence. We can use tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words, but all of them should show the same constant of proportionality.”

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students complete a five-section graphic organizer aligning each representation. *Assessment:* Collect organizers; check that corresponding values (the k=2) appear consistently across cells with logical labeling.

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Sorting (12 minutes)
Pairs receive 6–8 cards mixing tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Examples: y = 3x; y = x + 3; a line through origin; a recipe of 2 cups flour per 1 cup sugar; a "$10 sign-up plus $5 per visit" gym. Include a near-proportional "trick" table (2,6), (4,12), (6,19).

Students sort into Proportional / Not Proportional / Not Sure, justifying each placement using **two** different representations (e.g., ratio check AND equation form).

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students cross-check classifications using multiple methods. *Assessment:* Circulate with a clipboard; ask "How did you confirm that a second way?" and document pairs who verify versus those relying on one check.

### 4. Collaborative Decision Task (15 minutes)
Present: *"Your school needs 450 custom t-shirts. Print Shop X charges $0.15 per shirt. Print Shop Y charges $10 setup + $0.10 per shirt. Print Shop Z charges $30 flat. Represent each with an equation and graph. Which is the best deal?"*

Groups of three produce a recommendation. Then introduce a twist: *"Shop X adds a $20 shipping fee. Is the relationship still proportional?"*

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must systematically compare all three options, identifying which are proportional and at what quantity each becomes preferable. *Assessment:* Review written recommendations for trade-off analysis (e.g., "Shop X wins under 200; Shop Z wins at higher volume").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Groups justify their final choice using equations and break-even points, then analyze how the $20 fee makes the line no longer pass through the origin. *Assessment:* During share-outs, ask "What would change your recommendation?" Listen for logical coherence and anticipation of complexity rather than faulty claims.

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes)
Students individually:
1. Create a table, graph, equation, and diagram for: "A car travels 60 miles per hour."
2. **Reflection prompt:** *"What is one mistake or confusion you had today about proportional relationships? How did your thinking change, and what will you do differently next time?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** *Assessment:* Read exit tickets for metacognitive depth — look for specific misconceptions identified and corrected (e.g., "I thought every straight line was proportional, but now I check whether it passes through the origin and can be written as y = kx").

### 6. Closure (2 minutes)
Reinforce: "All five representations should reveal the same constant of proportionality. Tomorrow we'll interpret k in real-world contexts."
