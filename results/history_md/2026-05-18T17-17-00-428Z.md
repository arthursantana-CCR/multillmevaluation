## Generator
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

## Lesson Objectives
Students will recognize proportional relationships and represent them across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills through analysis and reasoning.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- "Smoothie Shop" handout (recipe scenarios)
- Exit ticket slips
- Whiteboard / projector

---

## Lesson Outline

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A smoothie shop charges $5 for the first smoothie, then $2 for each additional one."

Ask students: *"Which scenario shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?"* Students discuss in pairs for 2 minutes, then share. Record observations on the board.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify the meaning of "proportional" by identifying that Scenario A has a constant ratio (price/smoothie = $3) while Scenario B does not.
- **Assessment:** Listen for precise language during pair-share. Look for statements like "the ratio stays the same" vs. vague answers like "it goes up evenly." Note students using mathematical vocabulary precisely (Level 2) vs. partially (Level 1).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Introduce the constant of proportionality (k) and y = kx. Walk through Scenario A using all five representations:
- **Verbal:** "$3 per smoothie"
- **Table:** (1, $3), (2, $6), (3, $9), (4, $12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram or ratio bars

Emphasize the **three tests** for proportionality: (1) constant ratio, (2) graph is a line through the origin, (3) equation form y = kx.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to organize the same relationship across five representations in a clear, logically connected sequence. Use a graphic organizer on the board showing how each representation connects.
- **Assessment:** Later, when students create their own organizers, check whether information flows logically from verbal → table → equation → graph (Level 2) or appears scattered (Level 1).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Detective Work" (10 minutes)
Distribute the Smoothie Shop handout with four different pricing scenarios (some proportional, some not). Each scenario is presented in a different format (one table, one graph, one equation, one verbal). Students work in pairs to determine which scenarios are proportional.

Include one scenario with conflicting/incorrect information embedded: *"Maya claims her lemonade stand is proportional because she sold 2 cups for $3 and 4 cups for $7."* Students must evaluate this claim.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must verify Maya's claim by cross-checking the ratios (3/2 = 1.5 vs. 7/4 = 1.75). They should compare against the mathematical "authority" of the definition.
- **Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Look for students who systematically test multiple ratio pairs (Level 2) versus those who accept the claim without verification (Level -1 or 1).

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Ask: *"What's the most reliable way to test proportionality—checking one ratio, checking multiple ratios, or graphing it?"*
- **Assessment:** Listen for students who recognize that multiple checks or graphing provide stronger evidence (Level 2) versus relying on a single data point (Level 1).

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Choose Your Plan (15 minutes)
Present a real-world dilemma: *"You're starting a dog-walking business. Plan A charges $5 per dog walked. Plan B charges $10 flat fee plus $3 per dog. Plan C charges $4 per dog but requires a $20 membership."*

In groups of 3, students:
1. Represent each plan as a table, graph, and equation
2. Identify which is proportional
3. Recommend the best plan for different customers (occasional vs. frequent users)

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must analyze trade-offs between the three plans for different customer types.
- **Assessment:** Review group recommendations. Look for analysis considering multiple customer scenarios and long-term cost implications (Level 2) versus simply picking the cheapest option at one data point (Level 1).

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must justify recommendations using mathematical evidence from their representations.
- **Assessment:** Evaluate written justifications: Do students reference the equations, intersection points on graphs, or ratio comparisons (Level 2)? Or make unsupported claims (Level -1)?

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
**Metacognitive Reflection Prompt:**
*"Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What did you initially believe about proportionality, and what made you rethink it? Which representation (table, graph, equation, diagram, or words) helps YOU understand proportional relationships best, and why?"*

Students write responses on exit tickets.

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** This prompt directly requires metacognitive reflection.
- **Assessment:** Read exit tickets. Level 2 responses identify specific shifts in understanding (e.g., "I thought any increasing pattern was proportional, but I learned it must pass through the origin"). Level 1 responses mention preferences without deep insight. Level -1 responses show no awareness of learning.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence frames.
- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price, distance) and represent it in all five forms.
