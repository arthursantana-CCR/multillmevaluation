## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Asking "What If?" Makes You a Better Writer

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Materials:** Paper, pencils, whiteboard, sticky notes

---

## HOOK — The Unanswered Door (5 minutes)

Walk in and write one sentence on the board — nothing else:

**"School should start at 10 AM."**

Don't explain it. Don't greet students normally. Just write it and stand silently.

Wait for someone to ask *why* or push back. When they do, stop and say: *"That feeling you just had — that need to question, challenge, or find out more — that's curiosity. And today, curiosity is your most important tool."*

Tell students that curious people don't just accept statements. They ask: *Who says? Why? What if the opposite were true? What am I missing?* Great argument writers do exactly the same thing.

---

## DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Makes an Argument? (10 minutes)

Introduce the four pillars of CCSS argumentative writing on the board:

- **Claim** — Your position
- **Reasons & Evidence** — Why you're right, with proof
- **Counterclaim** — What someone who disagrees might say
- **Rebuttal** — Why your claim still stands

Emphasize the **counterclaim**. Tell students this is where curiosity lives in writing. Most people skip it because they're afraid of the other side. But the best writers *chase* the other side because they want to understand it.

Say: *"Being curious about why someone disagrees with you actually makes your argument stronger — not weaker."*

Write this on the board: **"Curious writers ask: What does the other side know that I might be missing?"**

---

## MODELING — Think-Aloud Argument (8 minutes)

Model building a quick argument using the school start time topic. Think aloud as you go, demonstrating genuine curiosity about the opposing view:

*"My claim is that school should start at 10 AM. My reason is that teenagers need more sleep for brain development — I've seen studies on this. But wait — someone might argue that parents who work early can't drop kids off later. Hmm. That's actually a real problem. Let me think about that honestly... Okay, but what if school offered a supervised early arrival option? That addresses their concern while keeping my claim intact."*

Point out that the counterclaim made the argument richer, not weaker. Curiosity led to a smarter rebuttal.

---

## GUIDED PRACTICE — Sticky Note Argument Map (12 minutes)

Give each student four sticky notes in different colors (or label them C1, C2, C3, C4).

Present this prompt: **"Middle schoolers should be required to take a class on financial literacy."**

Students build their argument map:
- **Yellow** → Claim (their position)
- **Blue** → Two reasons with one piece of evidence each
- **Pink** → Counterclaim (write the BEST argument against your claim)
- **Green** → Rebuttal

Walk the room. When students struggle with the counterclaim, prompt them: *"Pretend you're someone who completely disagrees with you. What would they care about? What would worry them?"*

This is curiosity in action — perspective-taking as a writing skill.

---

## INDEPENDENT WRITING — First Draft (15 minutes)

Students choose ONE of the following prompts and write a structured argumentative paragraph (or short essay if ready):

1. *Should students be allowed to use phones during lunch?*
2. *Should all middle schoolers participate in a community service project?*
3. *Should junk food be banned from school vending machines?*

**Requirements:**
- Clear claim in the opening sentence
- At least two reasons with evidence or logical support
- One honest counterclaim
- A rebuttal that addresses the counterclaim directly

Remind them: *"Your counterclaim should be the strongest version of the opposing argument — not a weak one you can easily knock down. Stay curious about the other side."*

---

## SHARE & REFLECT — Curiosity Check (5 minutes)

Ask two or three volunteers to read their counterclaims aloud. After each one, ask the class: *"Does that feel like a real concern someone might have? Is the writer being genuinely curious — or are they setting up a fake argument?"*

Close with this reflection question students answer on paper in two sentences:

**"What did you learn about the other side of your argument today that surprised you?"**

---

## CLOSING — The Curious Writer (0 minutes built in — weave into reflection)

Tell students: *"Curiosity doesn't mean you change your mind about everything. It means you're brave enough to look at the full picture before you decide. That's what great thinkers do. That's what great writers do. That's what you did today."*

**Exit Ticket:** One sentence — their claim. One sentence — the best counterargument. Hand it in on the way out.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt (provided below)  
- Chart paper or projector for examples  
- Sticky notes  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us become better thinkers and learners.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci) who used curiosity to discover or create something important.  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different perspectives — all important skills for writing strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our claims  
  - Consider other viewpoints to make our argument stronger  
- Introduce the day’s writing standard: writing arguments with clear reasons, relevant evidence, and acknowledging opposing claims.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- On the board or projector, display a simple argument example:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason 1:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* A study showed schools with uniforms had fewer bullying incidents.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Response:* However, students can express themselves through accessories and hairstyles.  
- Break down the example, highlighting:  
  - Clear claim  
  - Reasons supported by evidence  
  - Acknowledgment of opposing claim  
  - Logical organization  
- Ask students to identify each part in the example.

**4. Guided Practice: Exploring Curiosity Through a Prompt (10 minutes)**  
- Hand out the following prompt:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- Ask students to spend 3 minutes writing down questions they have about this topic on sticky notes (e.g., “Does homework help students learn?” “How much time do students spend on homework?”).  
- Collect a few sticky notes and read them aloud to model curiosity.  
- Then, as a class, brainstorm reasons for and against banning homework, encouraging students to think curiously about both sides.

**5. Independent Writing: Crafting an Argument Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Instruct students to write a short argumentative paragraph responding to the prompt: *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- Remind them to:  
  - State their claim clearly  
  - Provide at least two reasons with evidence or examples  
  - Acknowledge an opposing claim and respond to it  
  - Organize their ideas logically  
- Circulate the room to support students as needed.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing claim.  
- End with a brief discussion:  
  *“How did being curious help you write your argument today?”*  
  *“Why is it important to consider other viewpoints when making an argument?”*

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to find a news article or opinion piece on a topic they are curious about and write down one claim, two reasons, and one opposing viewpoint they notice.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson encourages students to embrace curiosity by asking questions and exploring different perspectives, which strengthens their ability to write clear, evidence-based arguments. Through discussion, modeling, and practice, students learn to support their claims thoughtfully and respectfully acknowledge opposing views.
