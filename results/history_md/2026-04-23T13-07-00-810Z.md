## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Are the Best Evidence

### A 55-Minute Middle School ELA Lesson

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA / Writing
**Central Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1
**Lesson Theme:** Curiosity as a habit of mind and a tool for stronger argumentative writing
**Setting:** Traditional Classroom

---

## 🎯 LESSON OBJECTIVES

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Define curiosity and explain why it strengthens argumentative writing
2. Identify the key components of an argument: claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, and rebuttal
3. Use curiosity-driven questioning to anticipate opposing claims
4. Draft a structured paragraph argument that includes a counterclaim and rebuttal
5. Reflect on how asking "What if I'm wrong?" makes their writing more honest and persuasive

---

- Whiteboard or projector
- Printed **"Curiosity Argument Graphic Organizer"** (one per student — template described in lesson)
- Sticky notes (two colors — e.g., yellow and pink, enough for each student to have 2 of each)
- Pencils/pens
- Timer (visible to students)
- Optional: Short video clip (1–2 min) showing a scientist or inventor describing a moment of curiosity — e.g., a clip of a NASA engineer, Marie Curie quote on a slide, or Neil deGrasse Tyson soundbite

## 🔶 SEGMENT 1: HOOK — The Curiosity Experiment *(7 minutes)*

### Teacher Script & Directions:

As students enter, the following prompt is already written on the board:

> **"If you could know the answer to ONE question — any question in the universe — what would you ask?"**

**[2 minutes]** Give students 90 seconds to silently write their question on a yellow sticky note. Tell them not to worry about spelling or whether the question is "smart enough." There are no wrong answers.

**[2 minutes]** Ask 4–5 volunteers to share their questions aloud. Write them on the board without comment at first. Examples might include:
- *"Why do we dream?"*
- *"Is there life on other planets?"*
- *"Why do people do bad things?"*
- *"What happens after you die?"*

**[1 minute]** Then ask the class:
> *"Look at these questions. What do they all have in common?"*

Accept responses. Guide students toward noticing that these questions **don't have easy answers** — they're complex, they make us wonder, and different people might answer them differently.

**[2 minutes]** Deliver this key idea out loud with enthusiasm:

> *"Here's something powerful: the best argument writers in the world — lawyers, scientists, journalists, activists — they don't start with answers. They start exactly where you just did. With a question they can't stop thinking about. That feeling? The one where you just HAVE to know something? That's curiosity. And today we're going to learn that curiosity isn't just a personality trait — it's a writing superpower."*

---

## 🔷 SEGMENT 2: MINI-LESSON — What Is Curiosity + Argument Anatomy *(12 minutes)*

### Part A: Defining Curiosity *(4 minutes)*

Write on the board:

> **Curiosity** = the desire to learn, understand, or explore something — especially something uncertain or unknown.

Ask students:
> *"Has anyone ever argued with a friend or family member and LATER found out you were missing some information? What happened?"*

Take 2 brief responses. Then make the connection:

> *"When we argue without being curious, we only see ONE side. We miss things. And when a reader finds something we missed, our whole argument falls apart. Curiosity protects us from that. Curious writers ask: 'What am I missing? Who might disagree with me? Why might they be right?'"*

Write this on the board and have students copy it:

> **A curious writer asks: "What if I'm wrong — and what would I say back?"**

---

### Part B: Argument Anatomy *(8 minutes)*

Tell students you're going to build the "skeleton" of a strong argument together.

Draw or project this structure on the board:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              STRONG ARGUMENT                │
│                                             │
│  1. CLAIM — What do I believe?              │
│  2. REASON — Why do I believe it?           │
│  3. EVIDENCE — What proof supports me?      │
│  4. COUNTERCLAIM — What would my            │
│     opponent say? (THIS IS CURIOSITY!)      │
│  5. REBUTTAL — Why is my side still right?  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Use this simple, relatable example to model each part:**

**Topic:** *Should students have homework over the weekend?*

Walk through each component aloud:

- **Claim:** "Students should NOT have homework over the weekend."
- **Reason:** "Students need time to rest, recharge, and pursue interests outside school, which actually makes them better learners."
- **Evidence:** "A 2019 Stanford study found that students with more than 1.5 hours of daily homework reported higher stress and lower well-being scores."
- **Counterclaim:** *(Pause dramatically)* "Now here's where curiosity comes in. I have to ask: 'Who disagrees with me, and WHY might they have a point?' Someone might argue: 'Without weekend homework, students forget material between Friday and Monday, causing a learning gap.'"
- **Rebuttal:** "However, research on spaced learning shows that quality rest between study sessions — not cramming — leads to deeper long-term memory retention."

Check for understanding quickly:
> *"Thumbs up if you can tell me: which part of the argument required the most curiosity?"*

Confirm: the **counterclaim** requires curiosity because you have to genuinely wonder what someone who disagrees with you is thinking — and take it seriously.

## 🔸 SEGMENT 3: GUIDED PRACTICE — Curious Questioning as a Writing Tool *(10 minutes)*

### "Flip the Claim" Activity

**[2 minutes — Setup]**
Tell students you're going to give them a claim and their job is to USE CURIOSITY to build the counterclaim together as a class.

Write this claim on the board:

> **"Middle school students should be allowed to use their phones during lunch."**

**[3 minutes — Think-Pair-Share]**
Ask students to turn to a partner and discuss:
1. What are TWO reasons someone might SUPPORT this claim?
2. What are TWO reasons someone might OPPOSE this claim?

Remind them: **"Be genuinely curious. Pretend you're a student who disagrees. Why would THEY feel strongly?"**

**[3 minutes — Class Share]**
Take responses from 3–4 pairs. Write ideas on the board in two columns:

| PRO (Support the Claim) | CON (Oppose the Claim) |
|---|---|
| Students can relax during social time | Phones are distracting; students stop talking face-to-face |
| Emergency contact access | Cyberbullying risk increases |
| Personal freedom and trust | Mental health impact of social media |

**[2 minutes — Key Insight]**
Point to the CON column and say:

> *"Every single one of these counterarguments came from you being CURIOUS about how someone else sees the world. This is exactly what CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 asks you to do — to acknowledge alternate or opposing claims. You just did it. And notice: knowing those counterarguments doesn't weaken your position. It makes you a more honest and believable writer."*

Distribute the **Curiosity Argument Graphic Organizer** (described below).

## 🔹 SEGMENT 4: INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Draft Your Argument Paragraph *(15 minutes)*

### Setup *(2 minutes)*

Tell students they will now choose ONE of the following prompts and use their graphic organizer to write a **full argument paragraph** (minimum 8 sentences).

Write the prompts on the board:

**Choose ONE:**
1. 🐕 "Schools should allow students to bring pets to class for one day a month."
2. 📚 "Every student should be required to read at least one book per month outside of school."
3. 🏫 "The school day should start one hour later to help students get more sleep."
4. 🌍 "Learning a second language should be required for all middle school students."
5. **[Open choice]** — Students may propose their own topic if approved by the teacher

Remind students:

> *"You must include all five parts of the argument. The counterclaim is NOT optional — and it's where I'll be looking to see how CURIOUS you were about the other side. A curious writer doesn't ignore disagreement. They face it head-on."*

### Writing Time *(13 minutes)*

Students work independently and silently. Teacher circulates the room, offering brief guidance. Look for:
- Students who are skipping the counterclaim (gently prompt: *"Who would argue against you? What's their best point?"*)
- Students who are writing a weak counterclaim like "Some people might not like this idea" — encourage specificity: *"WHY wouldn't they like it? What would they be worried about?"*)
- Students who finish early — encourage them to strengthen their evidence or add a second reason

**Sentence starters posted on the board for support:**

> - *"I believe that... because..."*
> - *"Evidence shows that... / According to... / For example..."*
> - *"Some people might argue that... because..."*
> - *"However, this argument overlooks the fact that..."*
> - *"Therefore, it is clear that..."*

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin with a brief discussion:  
  *Ask:* “What does curiosity mean to you?”  
  *Write student responses on the board.*  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn and understand more about the world. It drives us to ask questions, explore new ideas, and solve problems.  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci, or a scientist who made a discovery because they asked questions).  
- Emphasize how curiosity helps us become better thinkers and learners.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us gather information and think deeply about different sides of an issue.  
- When writing an argument, being curious means we don’t just pick one side; we explore reasons, evidence, and even opposing views to make our writing stronger.

**3. Mini-Lesson on Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Review the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - Claim (your main point)  
  - Reasons (why you believe your claim)  
  - Evidence (facts, examples, or details that support your reasons)  
  - Acknowledging opposing claims (showing you understand other viewpoints)  
  - Logical organization (clear and easy to follow)  
- Use a simple example on the board:  
  *Claim:* “School uniforms should be required.”  
  *Reason:* “They help reduce bullying.”  
  *Evidence:* “A study showed schools with uniforms had fewer bullying incidents.”  
  *Opposing claim:* “Some say uniforms limit self-expression.”  
  *Response:* “However, students can express themselves through accessories and hairstyles.”  
- Highlight how curiosity helps us find evidence and understand opposing views.

**4. Guided Practice: Exploring Curiosity and Opposing Views (10 minutes)**  
- Present a prompt related to a familiar topic, for example:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- In pairs, students brainstorm:  
  - Reasons to support banning homework  
  - Reasons against banning homework  
- Encourage students to be curious and think about why people might disagree.  
- Share some ideas as a class and write them on the board.

**5. Independent Writing (15 minutes)**  
- Students choose a side (for or against banning homework) and write a short argumentative paragraph using the structure discussed.  
- Remind them to:  
  - State their claim clearly  
  - Provide at least two reasons with evidence or examples  
  - Acknowledge an opposing claim and respond to it  
  - Organize their ideas logically  
- Circulate and provide support as needed.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class: “How did curiosity help you think about the topic and write your argument?”  
- End with a quick reflection:  
  *“Write one sentence on a sticky note about something new you learned today about curiosity or argument writing.”*  
- Collect sticky notes to review later.

---

Write a second paragraph that explores the opposing viewpoint in more detail, showing curiosity about why someone might disagree with your claim.
