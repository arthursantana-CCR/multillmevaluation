## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Mystery Scenarios" handout, exit tickets

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a: Clarifying Information*
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "Maria buys 3 notebooks for $6. Later she buys 5 notebooks for $10."
- **Scenario B:** "A taxi charges $3 to start, plus $2 per mile."

Ask: *"Which situation shows a proportional relationship? What information do we need to clarify before deciding?"*

Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide students to articulate that proportional relationships require a constant ratio AND must pass through the origin.

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who precisely clarify key terms ("constant rate," "starts at zero"). Level 2 students will distinguish between the flat fee in Scenario B and the constant unit rate in Scenario A. Level 1 students identify relevant info but may miss the y-intercept distinction. Document responses on a quick observation checklist.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b: Organizing Information*
Introduce the five representations using one anchor example: "A printer prints 4 pages per minute."

Build a graphic organizer on the board with five sections:
- **Table:** (1,4), (2,8), (3,12)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 4x
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 4 pages per minute
- **Verbal:** "Pages printed are proportional to minutes elapsed"

Students copy this organizer into notebooks, ensuring all five representations connect to the constant of proportionality (k = 4).

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks at lesson's end. Level 2 organizers show clear labeling, arrows connecting representations, and explicit identification of k across all forms. Level 1 organizers contain the information but lack connective structure.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Source Evaluation (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality*
Distribute a handout with **three "student claims"** about whether y = 3x + 1 is proportional:
- Claim 1: "It's proportional because it's a straight line." (flawed)
- Claim 2: "It's not proportional because the graph doesn't pass through (0,0)." (correct)
- Claim 3: "It's proportional because numbers are increasing." (flawed)

Students work in pairs to evaluate each claim and verify by testing values, graphing, and checking ratios from multiple angles.

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Circulate to hear pairs cross-checking claims using more than one method (table AND graph AND equation). Level 2 students reference multiple representations as "authoritative checks." 

**Assessment of CRI2b:** Review written justifications. Level 2 students cite the most rigorous evidence (e.g., "Testing y/x: 4/1=4, 7/2=3.5 — not constant"). Level 1 students rely on a single, surface-level check.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Weighing Representations (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
Pose this problem: *"A bakery sells 12 muffins for $18. You need to explain this proportional relationship to three audiences: a younger sibling, a math teacher, and a customer. Which representation works best for each, and why?"*

Groups of 3-4 discuss trade-offs of each representation (table = clear data; graph = visual trend; equation = precise; diagram = intuitive; verbal = accessible).

**Assessment of CRI3a (Weighing pros/cons):** Listen for systematic comparisons. Level 2 groups discuss multiple criteria (clarity, precision, accessibility) and consider audience needs comprehensively. Level 1 groups make surface comparisons ("graphs look nice").

**Assessment of CRI4a (Sound reasoning):** Each group presents one matched pair (audience + representation) with justification. Level 2 reasoning anticipates complications (e.g., "A graph might confuse my sibling if they don't understand axes, so a diagram is better"). Level 1 reasoning is logical but doesn't anticipate complexity.

---

### 5. Independent Practice & Reflection (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*
Students complete two problems independently:
1. Determine if the table {(2,5), (4,10), (6,15)} is proportional. Justify using two representations.
2. Write the equation and identify k.

Then students complete a **metacognitive exit ticket**:
- *What strategy did you use first, and why?*
- *Did you change your approach at any point? What made you adjust?*
- *Which representation do you trust most, and what does that reveal about how you think?*

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read exit tickets carefully. Level 2 reflections show students naming specific moments where they revised thinking ("I started with the equation but switched to a table because I wasn't sure the ratios matched"). Level 1 responses mention thinking but don't analyze it deeply.

---

### 6. Closure (3 minutes)
Recap the constant of proportionality across all five representations. Preview tomorrow's lesson: solving real-world problems using proportional reasoning.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed graphic organizers
- **Extension:** Challenge students to construct a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.) and represent it in all five ways.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proving Proportionality in the Real World
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Setting:** Traditional Classroom

**Lesson Objective:** 
Students will be able to recognize, represent, and verify proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, organize mathematical models, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: Hook and Information Gathering (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: The city is changing how it charges for municipal water. Students are given a packet containing mixed information about the new water rates. The packet includes a vague verbal description from a town hall meeting, scrambled raw data points, an official municipal water rate chart, and a random social media post complaining about the prices.

**CRI1a: Clarifying information**
*   **Specific Activity:** Students read the vague verbal claim from the town hall ("Water costs more the longer you run the hose") and must clarify it precisely into a strict mathematical definition. They rewrite the claim to state, "The total cost of water is directly proportional to the gallons used, represented by a constant ratio."
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher circulates and reviews the students' rewritten statements, assessing whether they use precise mathematical vocabulary (constant of proportionality, origin) to clarify the vague idea into a precise concept that supports deeper understanding.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
*   **Specific Activity:** Before building their mathematical models, students must evaluate the provided sources (the official municipal chart vs. the social media post) to determine which data to use. They must justify selecting the high-quality, authoritative source to find their constant of proportionality.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher evaluates students' written justifications, looking for sophisticated judgment. Students achieve mastery if they explicitly reject the social media post as unreliable and select the municipal chart as the authoritative source for accurate mathematical modeling.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**
*   **Specific Activity:** Using the reliable municipal data, students are given scrambled, out-of-order data points (e.g., 10 gallons for $5, 2 gallons for $1, 8 gallons for $4). They must arrange this information into a logically flowing input/output table (ascending order) and plot it on a coordinate plane.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher checks student tables and graphs for logical flow. Mastery is observed when students consistently structure the table from lowest to highest x-values, strengthening the logical flow and making the linear, origin-passing trend visually obvious.

---

### **Part 2: Cross-Checking and Complex Reasoning (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students work in small groups to translate their organized tables and graphs into algebraic equations ($y = kx$). The teacher then introduces a complication: A neighboring town claims to have a proportional water rate, but they offer a "Bulk Discount" where the price per gallon drops after 50 gallons. 

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
*   **Specific Activity:** Students are presented with a claim from a local news article stating that the neighboring town's water rate is "perfectly proportional." Students must rigorously cross-check this claim by comparing the news article's summary against the neighboring town's actual published billing data and a consumer protection website's analysis.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher listens to group discussions and reviews their worksheets to ensure students are actively cross-checking the news claim against multiple authoritative sources, discovering the mathematical discrepancy in the unit rate.

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
*   **Specific Activity:** Students must decide whether the neighboring town's "Bulk Discount" scenario represents a true proportional relationship. They must apply mathematical reasoning to prove why a changing unit rate breaks the rule of proportionality.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews student responses to see if they demonstrate sophisticated reasoning. Students should successfully anticipate the complexities of piecewise pricing, logically concluding that because the graph would bend and the ratio $y/x$ is not constant, it cannot be modeled by a single $y = kx$ equation.

---

### **Part 3: Evaluating Options and Metacognition (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** 
Students return to their original, proportional municipal water data. The teacher asks them to predict the exact cost of using 15,500 gallons of water over the course of a year. 

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
*   **Specific Activity:** Students must choose which representation (extending their hand-drawn graph, continuing their table, or using their $y = kx$ equation) is the best method to find the cost of 15,500 gallons. They must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each mathematical tool.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher facilitates a class discussion and assesses student contributions. Mastery is shown when students articulate comprehensive long-term implications—for example, noting that while graphs are visually intuitive for small numbers, equations are vastly superior and less prone to human error for large-scale, long-term projections.

**CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
*   **Specific Activity:** For the exit ticket, students write a metacognitive reflection on how their understanding of proportionality evolved during the lesson. They are prompted to identify a specific moment where they had to adjust their thinking (e.g., realizing a straight line isn't proportional if it doesn't pass through the origin, or realizing they initially trusted a bad data source).
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reads the exit tickets looking for deep metacognitive awareness. High-level reflections will explicitly state how the student recognized a flaw in their initial assumptions and strategically adjusted their mathematical thinking to align with the strict definition of proportionality.

Which representation works best for each, and why?"* Then add a complication: *"What if the bakery offers a bulk discount after 50 muffins—is it still proportional?"*

Groups of 3-4 discuss trade-offs of each representation (table = clear data; graph = visual trend; equation = precise; diagram = intuitive; verbal = accessible) and reason about whether the bulk-discount scenario satisfies y = kx.

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Listen for systematic comparisons. Level 2 groups discuss multiple criteria (clarity, precision, accessibility, scalability) and consider audience needs comprehensively. Level 1 groups make surface comparisons ("graphs look nice").

**Assessment of CRI4a:** Each group presents one matched pair (audience + representation) AND a verdict on the bulk-discount scenario. Level 2 reasoning anticipates complications ("A graph might confuse my sibling without axis knowledge, so a diagram works better"; "The bulk discount bends the graph, so y/x isn't constant—not proportional"). Level 1 reasoning is logical but doesn't anticipate complexity.

---

### 5. Independent Practice & Reflection (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking*
Students complete two problems independently:
1. Determine if {(2,5), (4,10), (6,15)} is proportional. Justify using two representations.
2. Write the equation and identify k.

Then complete a **metacognitive exit ticket**:
- *What strategy did you use first, and why?*
- *Did you change your approach at any point? What made you adjust?*
- *Which representation do you trust most, and what does that reveal about how you think?*

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read exit tickets carefully. Level 2 reflections name specific moments of revision ("I started with the equation but switched to a table because I wasn't sure the ratios matched" or "I first trusted the social media post until I cross-checked the chart"). Level 1 responses mention thinking but don't analyze it deeply.

---

### 6. Closure (3 minutes)
Recap the constant of proportionality across all five representations. Preview tomorrow: solving real-world problems using proportional reasoning.
