## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: "Should Schools Have Homework?"
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Lesson Overview

Students will explore the debated question of whether schools should assign homework by examining multiple perspectives, evaluating sources, weighing evidence, and making reasoned decisions. By the end of the lesson, students will have practiced every dimension of the CCR Critical Thinking framework through collaborative and individual activities.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
- Clarify and organize arguments about homework
- Evaluate the validity and quality of evidence
- Weigh competing perspectives to reach a reasoned conclusion
- Reflect on how their thinking evolved during the lesson

---

## Materials Needed

- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed or projected source cards (3 sources: a research study, a parent blog post, and a news article)
- Graphic organizer handout (T-chart + reflection box)
- Exit ticket slips
- Timer

---

## Lesson Sequence

---

### **Warm-Up — 5 Minutes**

**Activity:** Quick Poll + Think-Pair-Share

Begin by asking students to silently answer: *"Do you think homework helps you learn? Yes, No, or It depends — and why?"* Students write one sentence, then share briefly with a partner. Take a quick show of hands for each position.

**Teacher Move:** Write three columns on the board: *For Homework / Against Homework / It Depends.* Tell students these columns will grow throughout the lesson as they gather evidence.

---

### **Activity 1: Clarifying the Question — 8 Minutes**
*(Targets: **CRI1a – Clarifying Information**)*

**Activity:** Whole-class vocabulary and concept unpacking.

Ask students: *"What do we actually mean by 'homework'? Is all homework the same? What does 'should' imply — morally, practically, legally?"*

Guide students to distinguish between types of homework (reading, drill practice, creative projects), types of schools (elementary vs. high school), and the difference between correlation and causation when discussing homework and grades.

**Why it matters:** Students often debate a vague version of the question. Clarifying the terms prevents them from talking past each other and sets the foundation for precise argumentation.

**Assessment Observation:** Listen for whether students can articulate a specific, bounded version of the question. A student demonstrating Level 2 (CRI1a) might say, *"We should specify whether we mean nightly drill homework or long-term projects, because those might have different effects."*

---

### **Activity 2: Organizing the Evidence — 10 Minutes**
*(Targets: **CRI1b – Organizing Information**)*

**Activity:** Source Card Sorting

Distribute three source cards summarizing different pieces of information about homework:
- **Card A:** A 2006 Duke University study suggesting homework has modest positive effects for middle schoolers
- **Card B:** A parent blog post arguing homework destroys family time and causes anxiety
- **Card C:** A 2019 CNN Health article reporting that excessive homework increases stress without improving outcomes

Students use the T-chart graphic organizer to place arguments under *Supports Homework* or *Questions Homework*, and a third column for *Needs More Context.*

**Assessment Observation:** Look at students' graphic organizers. Level 2 (CRI1b) is demonstrated when a student places an argument in the correct column with a clear connecting explanation rather than copying text verbatim. Scattered or contradictory placements indicate Level -1.

---

### **Activity 3: Evaluating Sources — 10 Minutes**
*(Targets: **CRI2a – Assessing Validity** and **CRI2b – Assessing Quality**)*

**Activity:** Source Credibility Ranking

Ask students to rank the three source cards from most to least reliable and write one sentence justifying each ranking. Then discuss as a class: *"What makes a source trustworthy? Does a parent's experience count as evidence? Why or why not?"*

Introduce three quick criteria: **Who wrote it? When was it written? What is it based on?**

**Assessment Observation:** A student at Level 2 (CRI2a/CRI2b) will recognize that the research study carries more evidentiary weight while also acknowledging that personal experiences offer real-world texture. A student at Level -1 might rank the blog post highest because *"it sounds more relatable."*

---

### **Activity 4: Weighing Pros and Cons — 12 Minutes**
*(Targets: **CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons** and **CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning**)*

**Activity:** Structured Academic Controversy (Mini-Debate)

Divide students into groups of four. Two students argue *for* homework, two argue *against.* After three minutes each side, they switch positions. Finally, the group of four must reach a consensus statement together: *"Our group believes schools should/should not/partially assign homework because..."*

This position-switching is critical — it forces students to steelman the opposing view rather than dismiss it.

**Assessment Observation:** Level 2 (CRI3a) is visible when students acknowledge trade-offs even within their assigned position (*"Even though homework builds discipline, it may disadvantage students without stable home environments"*). Level 2 (CRI4a) appears when the consensus statement uses logical connectors — *because, however, therefore* — rather than simply listing preferences. Faulty reasoning (*"Homework is bad because I hate it"*) signals Level -1.

---

### **Activity 5: Metacognitive Reflection — 7 Minutes**
*(Targets: **CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**)*

**Activity:** Exit Ticket Reflection

Students complete a structured exit ticket with three prompts:
1. *"My position on homework at the START of class was..."*
2. *"My position NOW is..."*
3. *"What changed my thinking — or why didn't my thinking change?"*

**Assessment Observation:** A Level 2 (CRI5a) response names a specific moment or piece of evidence that caused a shift, or consciously identifies a bias they brought into the lesson (*"I realized I was only thinking about my own experience, not students with no quiet place to study"*). A Level -1 response repeats the original opinion with no acknowledgment of the lesson's content.

---

### **Wrap-Up — 3 Minutes**

Return to the three board columns from the warm-up. Ask: *"Has anything moved? What did we learn about thinking, not just about homework?"*

Close with this key message: **Good thinking isn't about winning — it's about being honest with the evidence.**

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Homework Huddle - To Assign or Not to Assign?**

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** English Language Arts / Social Studies (Interdisciplinary)
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Topic:** Should schools have homework?

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
*   Identify and clarify different arguments for and against homework.
*   Organize information logically to support various perspectives.
*   Assess the validity and quality of information from different sources.
*   Weigh the pros and cons of alternative choices regarding homework policies.
*   Apply sound reasoning to form and justify their own position on homework.
*   Reflect on their thinking process and how new information influenced their perspective.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives" (contains 3-4 short, diverse texts/quotes: e.g., a snippet from a research study on homework effectiveness, a quote from a parent advocating for less homework, a quote from a teacher explaining its benefits, a short news article summary on a school that banned homework). Each text will have a clear (or intentionally less clear) source citation.
*   Handout 2: T-Chart graphic organizer ("Arguments For Homework" / "Arguments Against Homework")
*   Exit Ticket slips

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, 7th graders! Let's start with a quick poll. Raise your hand if you think homework is a good idea. Now, raise your hand if you think schools should get rid of homework. Interesting! Today, we're going to explore this very question: 'Should schools have homework?' It's a topic that affects all of us, and there are many different opinions and reasons behind them."
*   **Teacher:** "Our goal today isn't just to share our opinions, but to think critically about *why* we hold those opinions, and to understand the different sides of this complex issue using evidence and careful thought."

**II. Exploring Perspectives & Clarifying Information (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will work in pairs. Distribute Handout 1: "Homework Perspectives."
*   **Teacher:** "In your pairs, read through the different perspectives on homework provided in Handout 1. As you read, your first task is to understand what each person or source is saying. Underline or highlight the main idea or key argument from each perspective."

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Activity:** After reading, students will paraphrase each perspective in their own words on their T-chart (Handout 2). They should aim for precision, capturing the core argument without adding their own bias. For example, if a text states, "Research indicates excessive homework can lead to student burnout," students should paraphrase it as "Some studies suggest that too much homework might cause students to feel exhausted," rather than "Homework is bad because it makes kids tired."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate among pairs. Listen to student discussions and review their paraphrases on the T-chart. Look for accurate and precise restatements of the original ideas. Are they capturing the essence of the argument? Are they avoiding misinterpretation or adding personal opinions?

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Activity:** As students paraphrase, they will also categorize each argument onto their T-chart (Handout 2) under "Arguments For Homework" or "Arguments Against Homework."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe how students group the information. Are arguments consistently placed on the correct side of the T-chart? Is their organization logical and easy to follow? Do they struggle with arguments that might have nuances, indicating a need for further clarification?

**III. Assessing Information: Validity and Quality (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Bring the class back together for a brief whole-group discussion.
*   **Teacher:** "Now that you've clarified and organized the arguments, let's look at where these arguments come from. Each perspective on your handout has a source listed. Let's discuss these sources."

*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Look at the sources for each perspective. For example, one might be 'Dr. Anya Sharma, Educational Researcher,' another 'Parent Blog Post,' and another 'Local News Report.' In your pairs, discuss: Which of these sources seems most reliable or trustworthy? Why? Which might have a particular bias or limited perspective? Why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen to student discussions. Can they differentiate between a research study and a blog post? Do they articulate reasons for why one source might be more valid than another (e.g., "A researcher uses data," "A parent might only speak from their own experience")?

*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, thinking about the *information itself*, not just the source: Which arguments or pieces of evidence do you think are the strongest or most convincing for either side? Why? Which ones might be less impactful or lack depth, even if they come from a seemingly valid source?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe which arguments students identify as "strongest" and their justifications. Do they prioritize arguments backed by data or expert opinion over anecdotal evidence? Do they recognize when information is too general or lacks specific supporting details?

**IV. Weighing Choices & Applying Reasoning (15 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Students will now prepare to form their own reasoned opinion.

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Activity:** "Before you decide on your own stance, I want each pair to briefly discuss *both* sides of the argument, even if you already have a strong opinion. Imagine you are presenting to a school board. What are the strongest 'pros' for homework? What are the strongest 'cons'? Make sure you can articulate both sides fairly, considering the potential trade-offs of each choice."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Circulate and listen. Do students genuinely engage with both sides, or do they quickly dismiss the opposing view? Can they articulate the trade-offs involved in having or not having homework, considering different impacts on students, teachers, and families?

*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Activity:** "Now, based on all the information you've clarified, organized, and assessed, and after weighing the pros and cons, I want each of you individually to write a short paragraph (3-5 sentences) stating your position on whether schools should have homework. You must support your position with at least two pieces of evidence or arguments from our discussion today. Make sure your reasoning clearly connects your evidence to your conclusion."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and review the paragraphs. Look for clear claims, relevant evidence, and logical connections between the evidence and the student's conclusion. Are there any logical fallacies or unsupported claims? Is the reasoning coherent and easy to follow?

**V. Reflection & Conclusion (10 minutes)**

*   **Activity:** Distribute Exit Ticket slips.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Activity:** "On your exit ticket, please answer these two questions:
        1.  Did your initial opinion about homework change or become stronger after today's lesson? Explain why or why not, referencing specific information or discussions.
        2.  What part of our critical thinking process today (clarifying, organizing, assessing sources, weighing pros/cons) helped you the most in forming your final opinion, and why?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** Collect and read exit tickets. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness. Do students acknowledge shifts in their thinking or a deeper understanding? Can they identify specific strategies that aided their critical thinking process and explain *how* it helped them?

*   **Teacher:** "Thank you for engaging in such thoughtful discussion today. Critical thinking isn't just about having an opinion, but about understanding different perspectives, evaluating information, and forming well-reasoned conclusions. Keep practicing these skills in all your subjects!"

---

**1. Introduction and Clarifying Information (CRI1a)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Begin with a brief class discussion: “What is homework? Why do schools assign it?”  
- Provide students with a short, clear text excerpt presenting different viewpoints on homework (e.g., benefits like practice and drawbacks like stress).  
- Ask students to highlight or underline key ideas and clarify any confusing terms or concepts in pairs.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**

  - Assess whether students can restate ideas precisely, e.g., “Homework helps us practice skills outside class,” rather than vague statements.

**2. Organizing Information (CRI1b)**  
*Time: 10 minutes*  

- **Activity:** In small groups, students create a graphic organizer (e.g., a T-chart) listing arguments for and against homework based on the text and discussion.  
- Encourage them to arrange points logically, grouping similar ideas together and sequencing them from strongest to weakest.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Check if students’ organizers show a coherent structure that strengthens understanding.  
  - Note if information is grouped logically rather than scattered or contradictory.  

---

**3. Assessing Validity of Information (CRI2a)**  
*Time: 8 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Present students with two short articles or excerpts: one from a reputable educational research source and one from a less reliable blog.  
- Guide students to compare the sources, discussing questions like: “Who wrote this? Is it an expert? How do we know?”  
- Have students identify which source is more trustworthy and why.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Observe students’ ability to identify source credibility and question information.  
  - Look for evidence of cross-checking or skepticism rather than accepting all information at face value.  

---

**4. Assessing Quality of Information (CRI2b)**  
*Time: 7 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Students evaluate the content quality of the two sources, focusing on relevance, depth, and evidence provided.  
- Prompt them to discuss which source provides more detailed, relevant, and well-supported information about homework’s effects.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Assess students’ judgment in selecting high-quality information.  
  - Note if they recognize superficial or irrelevant details and prefer authoritative evidence.  

---

**5. Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices (CRI3a)**  
*Time: 8 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Groups discuss the trade-offs of having homework or not, considering short-term and long-term effects on learning, stress, family time, and responsibility.  
- Each group lists pros and cons, then shares with the class, highlighting complex implications (e.g., homework may improve skills but increase stress).  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Look for systematic evaluation of multiple factors and perspectives.  
  - Note if students consider long-term consequences, not just immediate effects.  

---

**6. Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making (CRI4a)**  
*Time: 7 minutes*  

- **Activity:** Individually, students write a short paragraph stating their position on homework (for or against) and justify it using evidence and reasoning from the lesson.  
- Encourage them to anticipate counterarguments and address them logically.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Evaluate clarity and sophistication of reasoning.  
  - Check if students anticipate complexities and avoid fallacies or unsupported claims.  

---

**7. Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes (CRI5a)**  
*Time: 5 minutes*  

- **Activity:** End with a metacognitive reflection prompt: “How did your thinking about homework change during this lesson? What helped you decide your position?”  
- Students share reflections in pairs or write brief notes.  

- **Observation/Assessment:**  
  - Look for evidence of awareness about their reasoning process and any adjustments made.  
  - Note if students recognize assumptions or strategies they used to think critically.
