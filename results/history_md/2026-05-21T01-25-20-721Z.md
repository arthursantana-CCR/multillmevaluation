## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop Scenarios" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: "Is It Proportional?" (8 minutes)
Display three relationships on the board:
- **A:** 2 apples cost $1; 4 apples cost $2; 6 apples cost $3
- **B:** A baby is 20 inches at birth, 30 inches at age 2, 36 inches at age 4
- **C:** A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours

Students work individually for 3 minutes deciding which are proportional and why, then share with a partner.

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clearly articulate what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio). 
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for precise language like "the ratio stays the same" vs. vague responses. Circulate with a checklist.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Using the smoothie example ($3 per smoothie), model all five representations:

| Smoothies | Cost ($) |
|-----------|----------|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 6 |
| 3 | 9 |

- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 1:3 ratio
- **Verbal:** "Cost is three times the number of smoothies."

Explicitly point out the constant of proportionality (k = 3) across all forms.

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a graphic organizer linking all five representations side-by-side.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers; check whether students arrange representations to show how each connects logically to the constant of proportionality.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Compare Two Smoothie Shops (10 minutes)
Present **Shop A** (verbal: "4 smoothies for $10") and **Shop B** (table: 2 smoothies → $5, 5 → $12, 8 → $20).

Students determine: Which shop offers a proportional pricing structure? Which is the better deal?

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must check each shop's data by computing ratios across multiple data points—not trusting a single ratio.
**Assessment:** Cold-call students to explain how they verified Shop B's non-proportionality by cross-checking ratios (5/2, 12/5, 20/8). Look for evidence of testing multiple points.

**🎯 Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Discuss why one data point isn't enough evidence to claim proportionality.
**Assessment:** During whole-class discussion, ask: "Why isn't checking just one ratio enough?" Score responses on depth (1 = "you need more" vs. 2 = "a non-proportional relationship could coincidentally match at one point").

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: "Pick Your Plan" (15 minutes)
Distribute the **Smoothie Shop Scenarios** handout. Groups of 3-4 receive three subscription plans:
- **Plan 1:** $2 per smoothie (equation form)
- **Plan 2:** Graph showing $15 for 6 smoothies, $25 for 10 smoothies (proportional)
- **Plan 3:** Table with a $5 sign-up fee plus $1.50 per smoothie (not proportional)

Groups must: (1) identify which plans are proportional, (2) represent each plan in all five forms, and (3) recommend the best plan for a customer buying 12 smoothies/month vs. 3 smoothies/month.

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must explicitly compare plans for different customer types.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Look for systematic trade-off language: "Plan 3 is cheaper for low users because…, but Plan 1 becomes better when…"

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify their recommendation with mathematical evidence.
**Assessment:** Rubric scores group's reasoning chain. Level 2 = anticipates the break-even point; Level 1 = correct but incomplete logic.

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection: Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

**Part A (Content):** Given the table (3, 12), (5, 20), (7, 28):
- Is this proportional? Write the equation. Sketch the graph.

**Part B (Reflection prompt):**
> "Describe one moment today when you changed your mind or your strategy. What made you rethink? If you didn't change your mind, what's one question you still have?"

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** The reflection prompt requires metacognition.
**Assessment:** Score Part B on a 3-point scale:
- **2:** Names a specific strategy shift (e.g., "I first thought Shop B was proportional because 5/2 worked, but I tested 12/5 and realized it wasn't")
- **1:** Vague mention of confusion or change
- **0/-1:** No reflection or repeats prior misconception without awareness

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence starters ("This is proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Ask early finishers to design their own non-proportional pricing scheme and challenge a peer to detect it.

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship from daily life (recipes, prices, distances). Represent each in two forms and explain your reasoning.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional using mathematical evidence.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Representation Cards” handout  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which One Is Proportional? — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |

Ask students to answer silently:

- Which table shows a proportional relationship?
- How do you know?

After 2 minutes, students turn and talk with a partner. Then discuss as a class.

Guide students to notice that in Table A, the ratio \( \frac{y}{x} \) is always 3, so it is proportional. In Table B, the values increase by 2 each time, but the ratio is not constant.

**Teacher emphasis:** A proportional relationship is not just “going up by the same amount.” It must have a constant ratio and can be written as \( y = kx \), where \( k \) is the constant of proportionality.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1a — Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students clarify what information matters: x-values, y-values, ratios, and units.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for students saying things like, “I checked \( y \div x \),” or “The difference is constant, but the ratio is not.” Students who distinguish between additive and multiplicative patterns show precise clarification.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Ways to Represent Proportions — 10 minutes

Use the example: “A bike rental costs $8 per hour.”

Represent it in five ways:

1. **Verbal description:** The total cost is $8 for each hour.
2. **Table:**

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 8 | 16 | 24 | 32 |

3. **Equation:** \( y = 8x \)
4. **Graph:** A straight line through the origin with points \((1,8), (2,16), (3,24)\).
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing $8 added for each hour: 1 hour = $8, 2 hours = $16, etc.

Explain that all five representations tell the same mathematical story.

**Critical Thinking: CRI1b — Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students copy a five-section organizer labeled: verbal description, table, equation, graph, diagram.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether students place information in the correct category and connect the same constant of proportionality, 8, across all representations.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Build the Relationship — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“Maria earns $12 for each hour she babysits.”

As a class, complete:

- Verbal description
- Table for 0–5 hours
- Equation
- Graph
- Diagram

Ask guiding questions:

- What is the constant of proportionality?
- How do we know the graph should pass through the origin?
- What does the point \((3,36)\) mean in this situation?
- How does the equation match the table?

Students work in notebooks while the teacher circulates.

**Critical Thinking: CRI2a — Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check the relationship across representations. For example, if the equation is \( y = 12x \), then the table, graph, and diagram must all match that rule.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to explain, “How can you prove your graph is valid?” Look for answers that use multiple checks: “The points match the table, the line goes through the origin, and the equation gives the same values.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Card Sort — 15 minutes

Give pairs a set of cards. Some cards represent proportional relationships, and some do not.

Example cards:

- Table: \( x: 1,2,3,4 \), \( y: 5,10,15,20 \)
- Equation: \( y = 7x \)
- Graph: straight line through \((0,0)\) and \((2,10)\)
- Verbal: “A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile.”
- Table: \( x: 1,2,3,4 \), \( y: 2,4,8,16 \)
- Diagram: equal groups of 6 repeated for each unit

Students sort cards into three groups:

1. Definitely proportional
2. Definitely not proportional
3. Need more information

Then students choose one proportional card and create the missing four representations.

For example, if they choose \( y = 7x \), they create a table, graph, diagram, and verbal description.

**Critical Thinking: CRI2b — Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which cards provide enough high-quality mathematical evidence to prove proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Watch for students rejecting weak evidence, such as “it goes up” or “the numbers look patterned,” and instead using constant ratio, origin, or equation form \( y = kx \).

**Critical Thinking: CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare which representation is most useful for deciding proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask pairs: “Was the table, graph, equation, or verbal description easiest to use? Why?” Strong responses might say, “The equation was fastest because \( y = kx \) shows proportionality immediately,” or “The table was useful because we could check all ratios.”

---

### 5. Class Discussion: Defend Your Decision — 6 minutes

Select two pairs to present one card they sorted as proportional and one card they sorted as not proportional.

Students must explain using sentence frames:

- “This relationship is proportional because…”
- “The constant of proportionality is…”
- “This relationship is not proportional because…”
- “One representation that proves my thinking is…”

Encourage classmates to respectfully challenge or confirm the reasoning.

**Critical Thinking: CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students justify whether a relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for logical reasoning based on constant ratio, \( y = kx \), or a graph through the origin. If a student says, “It is proportional because it increases,” prompt: “Does increasing always mean proportional? What else must be true?”

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students complete independently:

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 9 | 18 | 27 | 36 |

Is it proportional? Explain.

2. Write an equation for the relationship if it is proportional.

3. Reflection: “At first I thought proportional meant ________. Now I know ________. One strategy I used today was ________.”

**Critical Thinking: CRI5a — Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students reflect on how their understanding changed and identify a strategy they used.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Review reflections for metacognitive statements, such as “I used to only look at differences, but now I check ratios,” or “I checked my equation against the table to make sure it worked.”

---

## Differentiation

**Support:**  
Provide partially completed tables and graph axes. Offer sentence frames and ratio reminders such as “Find \( y \div x \).”

**Challenge:**  
Ask students to create a non-proportional relationship that looks proportional at first glance, then explain the “trap.”

## Assessment

Students demonstrate mastery if they can correctly identify proportional relationships, find the constant of proportionality, create multiple representations, and justify their decisions with mathematical evidence.

|-----------|----------|
| 1 | 3 |
| 2 | 6 |
| 3 | 9 |

- **Graph:** Plot points; emphasize the straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 1:3 ratio
- **Verbal:** "Cost is three times the number of smoothies."

Explicitly point out the constant of proportionality (k = 3) across all forms.

**🎯 CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a five-section graphic organizer linking all representations side-by-side.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers; check whether students arrange representations so each connects logically to k = 3.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Compare Two Smoothie Shops (10 minutes)
Present **Shop A** (verbal: "4 smoothies for $10") and **Shop B** (table: 2 → $5, 5 → $12, 8 → $20).

Students determine: Which shop offers proportional pricing? Which is the better deal?

**🎯 CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students check each shop by computing ratios across multiple data points—not trusting a single ratio.
**Assessment:** Cold-call students to explain how they verified Shop B's non-proportionality by cross-checking 5/2, 12/5, 20/8. Level 2 = tests multiple points and recognizes that one matching ratio is insufficient evidence.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: "Pick Your Plan" (15 minutes)
Distribute the **Smoothie Shop Scenarios** handout. Groups of 3–4 receive three subscription plans:
- **Plan 1:** $2 per smoothie (equation form)
- **Plan 2:** Graph showing $15 for 6, $25 for 10 (proportional)
- **Plan 3:** Table with $5 sign-up fee plus $1.50 per smoothie (not proportional)

Groups must: (1) identify which plans are proportional, (2) represent each in all five forms, and (3) recommend the best plan for a customer buying 12 smoothies/month vs. 3 smoothies/month.

**🎯 CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups compare plans across customer types.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Look for trade-off language: "Plan 3 is cheaper for low users because…, but Plan 1 becomes better when…"

**🎯 CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify recommendations with mathematical evidence, including identifying the break-even point.
**Assessment:** Rubric scores the reasoning chain. Level 2 = anticipates the break-even point and explains why Plan 3 is non-proportional (line doesn't pass through origin); Level 1 = correct but incomplete logic.

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection: Exit Ticket (10 minutes)

**Part A (Content):** Given the table (3, 12), (5, 20), (7, 28):
- Is this proportional? Write the equation. Sketch the graph.

**Part B (Reflection):** Complete the sentence frames:
> "At first I thought proportional meant __________. Now I know __________. One strategy I used today was __________. One moment I changed my mind was __________."

**🎯 CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):**
**Assessment:** Score Part B on a 3-point scale:
- **2:** Names a specific strategy shift (e.g., "I first thought Shop B was proportional because 5/2 worked, but I tested 12/5 and realized it wasn't")
- **1:** Vague mention of confusion or change
- **0/-1:** No reflection or repeats prior misconception without awareness

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Partially completed tables and sentence starters ("This is proportional because…").
- **Extension:** Early finishers design a non-proportional pricing scheme that *looks* proportional and challenge a peer to detect the "trap."
