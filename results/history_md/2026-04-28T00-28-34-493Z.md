## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Materials:** journals, sticky notes, whiteboard

---

## Hook — The Unanswered Question (8 minutes)

Walk in and write ONE question on the board — nothing else:

**"Why do we stop asking why?"**

Give students 90 seconds of total silence to just *think* about it. No writing yet. Then ask two or three volunteers to share whatever came to mind. Resist the urge to validate or correct answers. Simply say, *"Interesting. Hold onto that."*

Tell students: *"Today you're going to write a real argument — one with a claim, evidence, and counterarguments. But first, we need to build the most important tool a writer can have."*

Pause. Let them wonder what it is.

*"Curiosity."*

---

## Direct Instruction — What Curiosity Has to Do With Arguing (10 minutes)

Explain that the worst arguments are written by people who already made up their minds before they started. The best arguments come from writers who genuinely asked questions first.

Write this on the board:

> **Curious writers ask → discover → argue with confidence.**

Walk through the components of CCSS W.7.1 briefly using student-friendly language:

- **Claim** = your answer to a question you actually care about
- **Reasons + Evidence** = what you found when you looked around curiously
- **Counterclaim** = what someone who asked the same question but landed differently might say
- **Logical organization** = the map that helps your reader follow your thinking

Key point to emphasize: *Acknowledging a counterclaim isn't weakness — it proves you were curious enough to explore more than one side.*

---

## Guided Practice — The Sticky Note Curiosity Web (12 minutes)

Give each student three sticky notes.

Pose this question: **"Should phones be allowed in school?"**

Round 1 — *Curiosity notes:* Students write one genuine question per sticky note — things they actually wonder about this topic. Not opinions yet. Questions. (Example: *"Do phones help students with disabilities?"* or *"What does research say about distraction?"*)

Post all sticky notes on the board. Group them loosely into categories together as a class: learning, safety, distraction, fairness, etc.

Point out: *"Look at all the places curiosity just took us. Every single one of these could become evidence, a reason, or a counterclaim in an argument."*

This is the moment students see that curious thinking generates the raw material of good writing.

---

## Independent Work — Draft a Curious Argument (20 minutes)

Students choose ONE of the following prompts — but before writing a single word of argument, they must spend **3 minutes writing questions** in their journal:

**Prompts:**
1. Should middle schoolers have homework?
2. Should school start later in the morning?
3. Should junk food be banned from school cafeterias?

**Writing structure to follow:**

- **Introduction + Claim** (2–3 sentences): Start with your most interesting question, then answer it with your position.
- **Reason + Evidence** (1 paragraph): Use something you know, read, or observed. Be specific.
- **Counterclaim + Rebuttal** (1 paragraph): Name what someone on the other side would say — then explain why your position still holds.
- **Conclusion** (2–3 sentences): End with a question that leaves the reader thinking.

Circulate the room. When students get stuck, instead of giving answers, ask: *"What made you curious about this topic in the first place? Go back there."*

---

## Share + Reflect — Curiosity in Action (5 minutes)

Ask two or three volunteers to read ONLY their counterclaim paragraph aloud.

After each one, ask the class: *"Did it feel like the writer had actually thought about the other side — or were they just checking a box?"*

Discuss briefly. Students will naturally notice the difference between genuine engagement and going through the motions.

---

## Closing — Return to the Opening Question (part of the 5 minutes)

Return to the board: **"Why do we stop asking why?"**

Give students 60 seconds to write a one-sentence answer in their journals — this time, shaped by everything they just did.

Close with this: *"Every argument you will ever write begins with a question you were brave enough to sit with. Keep asking."*

---

**Exit Ticket:** On a half-sheet, students write their claim and one question they still have about their topic — proof that good thinking never fully stops.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt (provided below)  
- Chart paper or projector for examples  
- Sticky notes  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us become better thinkers and learners.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci) who used curiosity to discover or create something important.  
- Emphasize that curiosity drives us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different viewpoints — all skills important for writing strong arguments.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us:  
  - Ask questions about the topic  
  - Look for reasons and evidence to support our ideas  
  - Consider other opinions or opposing claims  
- Tell students that today they will practice using curiosity to write an argument that is clear, logical, and fair.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- On the board or projector, display the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim** – Your main point or opinion  
  2. **Reasons** – Why you believe your claim is true  
  3. **Evidence** – Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claims** – Recognizing other viewpoints  
  5. **Conclusion/Logical Organization** – Wrapping up your argument clearly  
- Provide a simple example:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* Studies show fewer bullying incidents in schools with uniforms.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Response:* However, students can express themselves through other ways like hairstyles or accessories.  
- Highlight how curiosity helps us find good reasons and evidence and understand other opinions.

**4. Guided Practice: Curiosity Brainstorm (10 minutes)**  
- Hand out a prompt for students to write about. Example prompt:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- Ask students to spend 5 minutes writing down questions they have about the topic on sticky notes. Encourage them to be curious and think about different sides. Examples:  
  - Why do teachers assign homework?  
  - Does homework help students learn better?  
  - What do students think about homework?  
  - Are there negative effects of homework?  
- Collect a few sticky notes and discuss as a class, grouping questions into categories (benefits, drawbacks, opinions).  
- Explain that these questions will help them find reasons and evidence for their argument.

**5. Independent Writing: Drafting an Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Instruct students to choose a side (for or against banning homework) and write a paragraph that includes:  
  - A clear claim  
  - At least two reasons with supporting evidence (they can use their questions to guide their thinking)  
  - Acknowledgment of an opposing claim and a response to it  
- Remind them to organize their ideas logically and use curiosity to think deeply about the topic.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each, ask the class:  
  - What curious questions might have helped the writer?  
  - How did the writer acknowledge other opinions?  
- End with a brief reflection:  
  *“How can being curious help you become a better writer and thinker?”*  
- Encourage students to keep asking questions whenever they write or learn something new.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to find a news article or short video on a topic they are curious about and write down three questions they have after reading or watching it. They will bring these questions to the next class to practice argument writing again.

---

**Summary:**  
This lesson combines teaching curiosity with the skills needed to write a strong argumentative paragraph. Students learn to ask questions, gather reasons and evidence, consider opposing views, and organize their writing logically — all while fostering a curious mindset that supports critical thinking and effective communication.
