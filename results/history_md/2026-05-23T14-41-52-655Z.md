# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to justify reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Lemonade Stand" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Hook & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Present two scenarios on the board:
- **A:** "Maria earns $8 per hour babysitting."
- **B:** "Jamal pays a $5 fee plus $2 per ride at the fair."

Ask: *"Which is proportional? How do you know?"* Students think-pair-share. Push them to define key terms: constant of proportionality, unit rate, origin.

**Assessment:** Listen for precise definitions. Look for students who clarify *why* B is not proportional (the $5 fee breaks the ratio). Document the level (1 vs. 2) based on precision of explanations during share-out.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Five Representations (10 minutes) — **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Using the lemonade stand context ($3 per cup), model how to organize the same relationship five ways:
- **Table:** cups vs. dollars
- **Graph:** points through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram
- **Verbal:** "Each cup costs $3."

Students fill in a graphic organizer linking all five representations.

**Assessment:** Collect organizers. Score for logical alignment across representations (Level 2) vs. partial connection (Level 1).

---

### 3. Group Investigation: Evaluating Claims (12 minutes) — **CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity & Quality of Information**
Distribute four "student work samples" (fictional). Each sample claims a relationship is proportional and offers evidence (a table, a graph, an equation, or a description). Two samples contain errors:
- Sample 2: A graph that doesn't pass through origin but is labeled proportional.
- Sample 4: A table where ratios are inconsistent.

In groups of 4, students must verify each claim using a *second representation* (cross-check by building a table from the equation, etc.).

**Assessment (CRI2a):** Observe whether students cross-reference multiple representations rather than accepting one source. Level 2 students systematically check using two or more methods.

**Assessment (CRI2b):** Review group worksheets — do students cite the *strongest* evidence (e.g., "the graph must pass through origin AND be linear") rather than weak/irrelevant evidence?

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Choosing the Best Deal (12 minutes) — **CRI3a & CRI4a**

**Scenario:** "You need to buy 15 notebooks. Three stores offer different pricing plans (presented as a table, an equation, and a verbal description). Which store should you choose, and is each plan proportional?"

Students must:
1. Convert each plan into a common representation.
2. List pros and cons of each store (price, proportionality, scalability for buying more later).
3. Make a justified recommendation.

**Assessment (CRI3a — Weighing Pros/Cons):** Review written justifications. Level 2 students consider trade-offs beyond price (e.g., "Store B is cheaper now but not proportional, so buying more later costs more per unit"). Level 1 students compare only on price.

**Assessment (CRI4a — Sound Reasoning):** Check whether reasoning chains are logically valid. Level 2 students anticipate edge cases ("If I buy fewer than 10, Store A is better; more than 10, Store C wins"). Flag fallacies like assuming cheapest unit price always wins.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**

**Exit Ticket Prompts:**
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x + 1 proportional? Justify."
2. **Metacognitive reflection:** "What was your first instinct when checking if a relationship was proportional? Did your strategy change during today's lesson? What will you check first next time?"

**Assessment (CRI5a):** Read reflections. Level 2 students articulate *how* their thinking evolved ("I used to only check the equation, but now I verify the graph passes through the origin too"). Level 1 students mention strategy without reflection. Level -1 students show no awareness of their reasoning shifts.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Recap the five representations. Preview tomorrow's lesson: identifying the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts.

    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Topic:** Is It Proportional? Representing Relationships in Multiple Ways

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. Determine whether two quantities are in a proportional relationship.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
3. Explain their reasoning using evidence such as constant ratios, unit rates, and graphs through the origin.
4. Compare different representations and decide which best supports a conclusion.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Proportional or Not?” handout  
- Exit ticket  
- Colored pencils, optional

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display the following situation:

> A store sells notebooks: 2 notebooks cost $6, 4 notebooks cost $12, and 6 notebooks cost $18.

Ask students to write:

- What do you notice?
- What do you wonder?
- How could you tell if this relationship is proportional?

After 2 minutes, invite students to share. Guide students toward the idea that the cost increases at a constant rate: $3 per notebook.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the quantities, units, and relationship in the warm-up.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students clearly stating, “The two quantities are number of notebooks and cost in dollars,” and “Each notebook costs $3.” Students who confuse total cost with number of notebooks may need support clarifying information.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio between two quantities. In 7th grade math, students often recognize proportional relationships in several ways:

1. **Table:** The ratio \( \frac{y}{x} \) is constant.
2. **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin \((0,0)\).
3. **Equation:** The equation can be written as \( y = kx \), where \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.
4. **Diagram:** Equal groups or scaled drawings show the same multiplicative relationship.
5. **Verbal Description:** The situation describes a constant rate, such as “$3 per notebook.”

Model using the notebook example:

| Notebooks, x | Cost, y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

Ask students to calculate:

\[
\frac{6}{2}=3,\quad \frac{12}{4}=3,\quad \frac{18}{6}=3
\]

Since the ratio is constant, the relationship is proportional. The equation is:

\[
y = 3x
\]

Then sketch the graph and emphasize that it is a straight line through \((0,0)\).

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship as a table, equation, graph, and verbal rule.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check student notes to see whether information is arranged logically, such as table first, then ratio calculations, then equation and graph.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Testing Representations — 12 minutes

Give students two examples.

#### Example A

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 16 |
| 3 | 24 |
| 4 | 32 |

Ask:

- Is the relationship proportional?
- What is the constant of proportionality?
- What equation represents the relationship?

Expected answer: Yes, \( \frac{y}{x}=8 \), so \( y=8x \).

#### Example B

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 18 |
| 3 | 26 |
| 4 | 34 |

Ask:

- Is this proportional?
- Why or why not?

Students may notice that the money increases by 8 each time, but the ratios are not constant:

\[
\frac{10}{1}=10,\quad \frac{18}{2}=9,\quad \frac{26}{3}
eq 8
\]

Explain that a constant increase does not always mean a proportional relationship. The relationship must have a constant ratio and include \((0,0)\).

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students test whether claims are valid by checking ratios and graph behavior.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to justify answers with more than one piece of evidence, such as “The ratios are not equal, and the graph would not pass through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which evidence is strongest: repeated addition, constant ratio, equation form, or graph through the origin.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students rejecting weak evidence like “It goes up by the same amount” and choosing stronger evidence like “The ratio \(y/x\) stays constant.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Multiple Representations Sort — 15 minutes

Give each pair a handout with four situations. Each situation includes a verbal description, table, graph, or diagram. Students must decide whether each is proportional and complete missing representations.

Sample situations:

1. **Proportional:** A recipe uses 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar.  
2. **Not proportional:** A taxi charges a $4 starting fee plus $2 per mile.  
3. **Proportional:** A cyclist travels 12 miles every hour.  
4. **Not proportional:** A plant starts at 5 cm tall and grows 2 cm per week.

For each situation, students complete:

- Table
- Equation, if proportional
- Graph sketch
- Verbal explanation

Students must also answer:

> Which representation helped you decide most quickly? Why?

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions as decision tools.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students explaining trade-offs, such as “The table made ratios easy to check, but the graph showed quickly whether it went through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide whether each relationship is proportional and defend their conclusion.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Require students to write a complete explanation using mathematical evidence: “This is not proportional because the equation would be \(y=2x+4\), not \(y=kx\).”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 8 minutes

Bring the class together and discuss one proportional and one non-proportional situation from the partner activity.

Ask:

- What evidence proves the relationship is proportional?
- What evidence proves it is not proportional?
- Can a relationship increase at a constant rate and still not be proportional?
- Why does the origin matter?

Highlight the taxi example: even though the cost increases by $2 per mile, the $4 starting fee means the graph does not pass through \((0,0)\), so it is not proportional.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A gym charges $15 for 3 classes, $25 for 5 classes, and $40 for 8 classes. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.
2. Write one sentence explaining how to recognize a proportional relationship.
3. Which method do you trust most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description? Why?

Expected for #1: Yes, because each class costs $5, so \( y=5x \).

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on which representation helped them and why.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Read responses for metacognitive statements such as “I first checked the table, but then I used the equation to confirm my answer.”

---

## Differentiation

- “The relationship is proportional because ______.”
- “The constant of proportionality is ______.”
- “The graph does/does not pass through the origin, so ______.”

**Challenge:**  
Ask advanced students to create their own non-proportional relationship that has a constant rate of change but does not pass through the origin, then explain why it is not proportional.

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Five Representations (10 minutes) — CRI1b: Organizing Information
Using a lemonade stand ($3 per cup), model the same relationship five ways:
- **Table:** cups vs. dollars
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** tape diagram
- **Verbal:** "Each cup costs $3."

Students complete a graphic organizer linking all five representations, with x-values in ascending order and properly labeled axes.

**Assessment:** Collect organizers. Score for coherent alignment across representations (Level 2) vs. partial connection or scattered layout (Level 1).

---

### 3. Group Investigation: Source Quality & Validity (12 minutes) — CRI2a & CRI2b
Distribute two data sources:
- **Source 1:** An official bakery pricing sheet — "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, 8 cost $16."
- **Source 2:** A social media post — "I paid $5 for a 2-mile taxi ride, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"

Also distribute four "student work samples" claiming proportionality. Two contain errors (e.g., a graph not passing through origin labeled proportional; a table with inconsistent ratios). Groups must cross-check claims using a *second representation*.

**Assessment (CRI2a):** Observe whether students verify using two or more representations (table ratios + graph origin + equation form). Level 2 students systematically cross-check.

**Assessment (CRI2b):** Listen for students rejecting Source 2 as unreliable and citing the *strongest* mathematical evidence (constant ratio + line through origin) over weak evidence ("it goes up by the same amount").

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Choosing the Best Deal (12 minutes) — CRI3a & CRI4a

**Scenario:** "Your school needs 450 custom t-shirts. Three vendors offer pricing as a table, an equation, and a verbal description. Which should you choose, and is each plan proportional?" Twist: one vendor adds a one-time $20 shipping fee.

Students must:
1. Convert each plan into a common representation.
2. List pros and cons of using a table, graph, or equation to find the cost of 450 shirts.
3. Decide whether the $20-fee vendor's relationship is still proportional and justify.

**Assessment (CRI3a):** Review written justifications. Level 2 students weigh trade-offs (extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph lacks precision at high values; an equation is exact). Level 1 students compare only on price.

**Assessment (CRI4a):** Check that students anticipate the complexity the $20 fee introduces — the graph no longer passes through the origin, so the relationship becomes y = kx + 20, which is *not* proportional. Flag fallacies like "constant increase = proportional."

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes

**Exit Ticket Prompts:**
1. A gym charges $15 for 3 classes, $25 for 5, and $40 for 8 classes. Is this proportional? Justify.
2. Is y = 4x + 1 proportional? Why or why not?
3. **Metacognitive reflection:** "What was your first instinct when checking proportionality? How did cross-checking the table, graph, and equation change your strategy? What will you check first next time?"

**Assessment (CRI5a):** Level 2 students articulate strategic shifts ("I used to just look for a pattern, but now I verify the line passes through the origin AND check that y/x is constant"). Level 1 students mention strategy without reflection.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Recap the five representations and preview tomorrow's lesson: applying the constant of proportionality in real-world contexts.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Sentence frames — "The relationship is proportional because ______." "The graph does/does not pass through the origin, so ______."
- **Extension:** Students create a non-proportional "trick" example with a constant rate of change for peers to evaluate and explain why it fails the y = kx test.
