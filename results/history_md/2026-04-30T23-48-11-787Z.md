## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Materials:** Notebook, pencil, sticky notes (3 per student)

---

## Warm-Up: The Wonder Wall (8 minutes)

As students enter, write this on the board:

**"What's something you've always wondered about but never looked up?"**

Give each student three sticky notes. They write one genuine question per note — anything goes. No wrong answers. Post them on the board wall.

Read five or six aloud without judgment. Point out that these questions *feel* different from each other. Some questions have clear answers. Some have multiple answers depending on who you ask. Some are genuinely debated by experts.

Tell students: **"The questions in that last category are exactly where great arguments are born."**

---

## Direct Instruction: What Curiosity Has to Do With Writing (10 minutes)

Explain to students that the worst arguments start with someone who already made up their mind. The best arguments start with someone who was genuinely curious and then followed the evidence.

Write this on the board:

> **Curious Writer vs. Closed Writer**
> - Closed: "I know I'm right. Let me prove it."
> - Curious: "I wonder if I'm right. Let me find out."

Discuss: Which writer is more likely to find *better* evidence? Which one is more likely to notice the other side? Which one would you trust more as a reader?

Introduce the standard. Tell students today they're going to write a short argument — but the entire process starts with a question they don't fully know the answer to yet.

Introduce the four components they'll need:
1. **A clear claim** (your answer to a real question)
2. **Reasons and evidence** (what supports your answer)
3. **Counterargument** (what someone who disagrees would say)
4. **Rebuttal** (why your claim still holds)

Emphasize: you cannot write a good counterargument unless you were curious enough to actually consider the other side.

---

## Guided Practice: Modeling the Curious Argument (12 minutes)

Use this sample question pulled from the Wonder Wall concept:

**"Should schools have homework?"**

Model thinking *out loud* — not deciding first, but wondering first.

- "What would someone who hates homework say? Let me actually think about that..."
- "What would a parent who supports homework say? Do they have a point?"
- "What does the evidence show? Are there studies?"

Build a quick outline on the board together:

**Claim:** Schools should limit homework to 20 minutes per subject because excessive homework harms student wellbeing without improving learning outcomes.

**Reason + Evidence:** Research from Stanford University found that students with more than 90 minutes of homework reported significantly higher stress and health problems.

**Counterargument:** Some argue that homework builds discipline and responsibility, preparing students for future academic demands.

**Rebuttal:** While discipline matters, those habits can be developed through structured in-class practice without sacrificing students' mental health or family time.

Ask students: *"Could we have written that counterargument if we hadn't been curious about the other side?"* Let them respond briefly.

---

## Independent Practice: Write Your Own (20 minutes)

Students choose ONE question from the following list (or propose their own for approval):

- Should social media have a minimum age of 16?
- Is it ever okay to break a rule for a good reason?
- Should schools require community service to graduate?
- Does technology make people smarter or lazier?

**They must:**
1. Spend the first 3 minutes writing down what BOTH sides might believe before picking a position
2. Write a structured argument of 1–2 paragraphs using all four components
3. Underline their counterargument in pencil

Walk the room. When students skip the "both sides first" step, redirect them back to it. Curiosity isn't optional — it's the assignment.

---

## Share & Reflect (5 minutes)

Ask two or three volunteers to read their counterargument sentence only. After each one, ask the class: *"Does this feel like the writer actually listened to the other side, or does it feel rushed?"*

Close with this reflection question — students write it in their notebooks:

> **"Did wondering about the other side change your argument at all? How?"**

---

## Exit Ticket (Optional, last 2 minutes)

On a sticky note:

*"One thing I'm still curious about from today's topic."*

Collect at the door. Use responses to fuel future writing prompts.

---

**Key Takeaway to Leave With Students:**
Curiosity isn't a distraction from good writing. It *is* good writing. The moment you stop wondering, your argument stops being honest.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a short argumentative prompt and example paragraph  
- Sticky notes  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain that curiosity is the desire to learn and understand more about the world. It drives us to ask questions, explore new ideas, and think deeply.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Albert Einstein) and how their curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science but also in writing and thinking critically.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us explore different sides of an issue.  
- Being curious means we don’t just stick to our own opinion; we ask questions like: *Why might someone disagree? What evidence supports different views?*  
- This makes our writing stronger and more convincing.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Review the key parts of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - **Claim:** The main point or opinion.  
  - **Reasons:** Why you believe your claim.  
  - **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons.  
  - **Acknowledging Opposing Claims:** Recognizing other viewpoints and explaining why your claim still holds.  
  - **Logical Organization:** Presenting ideas in a clear, sensible order.  
- Use a simple example on the board:  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason:* They reduce bullying.  
  *Evidence:* Studies show schools with uniforms have fewer bullying incidents.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  *Response:* However, students can express themselves through accessories and behavior.  
- Highlight how curiosity helps us find evidence and understand opposing views.

**4. Guided Practice: Exploring Curiosity Through Questions (10 minutes)**  
- Give students a topic to argue about, for example: *“Should homework be banned?”*  
- In pairs, students brainstorm questions they are curious about related to the topic. Examples:  
  - Why do teachers assign homework?  
  - How does homework affect students’ free time?  
  - What do students think about homework?  
  - Are there benefits to homework?  
- After 5 minutes, ask pairs to share their questions. Write some on the board.  
- Explain that these questions help us gather reasons and evidence for our argument.

**5. Independent Writing: Crafting an Argumentative Paragraph (15 minutes)**  
- Hand out a prompt: *“Write a paragraph arguing for or against banning homework.”*  
- Remind students to:  
  - State their claim clearly.  
  - Provide at least two reasons with evidence.  
  - Acknowledge one opposing claim and respond to it.  
  - Organize their ideas logically.  
- Encourage them to use their curiosity questions to find reasons and evidence.  
- Circulate and support students as they write.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each, ask the class:  
  - What curious questions might the writer have asked?  
  - How did the writer acknowledge opposing views?  
- End with a brief reflection:  
  *“How does being curious help us become better writers and thinkers?”*  
- Summarize that curiosity leads us to ask questions, seek evidence, and understand different perspectives, which makes our arguments stronger.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to find a news article or opinion piece on a topic they are curious about. They should write down one claim from the article, one reason and evidence supporting it, and one opposing claim they notice.

---

This lesson combines the development of curiosity with the practice of writing argumentative paragraphs aligned with CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1, helping students become thoughtful, curious writers.
