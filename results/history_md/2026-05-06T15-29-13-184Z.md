## Candidate 1
# Should Schools Have Homework?

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Subject:** Critical Thinking / Language Arts

---

## Learning Objectives

- **Analyze** the key claims and evidence on both sides of the homework debate to clarify what is actually being argued.
- **Evaluate** the reliability and quality of sources that support or challenge homework policies.
- **Apply** structured reasoning to weigh trade-offs and reach a defensible personal position.
- **Reflect** on how personal bias and assumptions shaped their thinking throughout the lesson.

---

## Materials

- Two short reading excerpts (one pro-homework, one anti-homework), printed or on devices
- Source fact-sheet listing author credentials, publication dates, and funding sources for each excerpt
- Sticky notes (two colors per student)
- T-chart graphic organizer ("Pros / Cons")
- Exit ticket slip (printed or Google Form)
- Whiteboard or projected display for class anchor chart

---

## Lesson Outline

### Warm-Up — 5 Minutes

The teacher displays one question on the board: *"On a scale of 1–5, how much does homework help you learn? Write two sentences explaining your number."* Students write individually in their notebooks. The teacher takes a quick show-of-hands poll and records the class distribution on the board without comment.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** This activates students' existing assumptions before any new information is introduced. Students surface their prior beliefs in writing, creating a baseline they will return to at the end of class.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student writes a score with a vague reason ("I just don't like it"). At Level 2, a student names a specific assumption ("I rated it 2 because I think I learn more from class discussion than from practice at home").

---

### Vocabulary and Claim Clarification — 8 Minutes

The teacher introduces four key terms on the anchor chart: *academic achievement*, *cognitive load*, *equity*, and *intrinsic motivation*. For each term, one student reads the definition aloud and the class generates one real-life example. The teacher then displays two sample claims — one from each reading excerpt — and asks: *"What is this sentence actually saying? Put it in your own words."* Pairs share out.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI1a):** Students practice restating complex claims with precision before they evaluate them. Paraphrasing forces students to distinguish what a source actually argues from what they assumed it argued.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student restates the claim but omits a key qualifier ("It says homework is bad"). At Level 2, a student preserves nuance ("It says *excessive* homework harms well-being, not that all homework is harmful").

---

### Source Analysis — 10 Minutes

Students read the source fact-sheet and both excerpts independently. They annotate each excerpt with sticky notes: one color for statements they find credible (and why), one color for statements they want to question. The teacher prompts: *"Who wrote this? When? Who paid for it? Does that matter?"*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI2a, CRI2b):** Students cross-check each source's credentials, recency, and potential conflicts of interest rather than accepting claims at face value.

**Assessment Note — CRI2a:** At Level 1, a student notes the author's name without evaluating what that credential means. At Level 2, a student writes, "This study is from 2006 and only looked at high schoolers, so it may not apply to us."
**Assessment Note — CRI2b:** At Level 1, a student uses one source only. At Level 2, a student explicitly compares what both sources say about the same outcome (e.g., stress levels) and notes where they agree or contradict.

---

### Structured Pros and Cons — 12 Minutes

Students complete a T-chart graphic organizer individually, listing at least three pros and three cons drawn from the texts. They then join groups of four to compare charts. Each group must identify the *strongest* argument on each side and explain why it outweighs weaker arguments on the same side.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI1b, CRI3a):** The T-chart requires students to organize information into a logical structure. The group discussion pushes them beyond listing to weighing — considering long-term implications such as student well-being, equity of access, and learning outcomes.

**Assessment Note — CRI1b:** At Level 1, a student lists points randomly without grouping related ideas. At Level 2, a student groups arguments by theme (e.g., all equity arguments together) to strengthen logical flow.
**Assessment Note — CRI3a:** At Level 1, a student says one side is stronger without explaining trade-offs. At Level 2, a student says, "The equity argument is stronger than the time argument because not every student has a quiet home to work in, which affects the fairness of the whole policy."

---

### Position Statement and Debate — 12 Minutes

Each student writes a two-sentence position statement: *"Schools should / should not assign regular homework because…"* followed by one acknowledged counter-argument and a rebuttal. The class then runs a brief structured debate: one side speaks for 90 seconds, the other responds for 90 seconds, then each side offers a 60-second closing.

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI4a):** Students must move from listing evidence to constructing a reasoned argument that anticipates the opposing view. The rebuttal structure prevents one-sided reasoning.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student states a position and lists supporting facts but ignores the counter-argument in their rebuttal. At Level 2, a student directly addresses the strongest opposing point and explains why their position still holds despite it.

---

### Reflection Exit Ticket — 8 Minutes

Students return to their warm-up response and answer three prompts in writing:
1. *Did your score change? Why or why not?*
2. *What assumption did you hold at the start that the evidence challenged?*
3. *What would you need to know to be even more confident in your position?*

**Critical Thinking Connection (CRI5a):** Students explicitly compare their pre-lesson thinking to their post-lesson thinking, identifying the moment their reasoning shifted and what caused it.

**Assessment Note:** At Level 1, a student writes "I still think the same" without examining why. At Level 2, a student writes, "I started assuming all students have the same homework experience, but the equity source made me realize that's not true, so I had to change how I weighed that argument."

## Learning Objectives  
- Analyze different perspectives on the topic of homework in schools.  
- Evaluate the quality and validity of information from multiple sources.  
- Organize and clarify information to support reasoned arguments.  
- Reflect on personal thinking processes and adjust reasoning based on new insights.  

---

## Materials  
- Printed or digital articles presenting arguments for and against homework (2–3 short texts)  
- Whiteboard or chart paper and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Exit ticket slips or digital form for reflection  
- Timer or clock  

---

## Lesson Outline  

### 1. Warm-Up: Quick Write and Share — 7 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write a brief response to the prompt: “What do you think about homework? Is it helpful or harmful? Why?” After 3 minutes, students share their ideas in pairs.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) by encouraging students to identify and express their initial ideas clearly.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies relevant ideas but with some vagueness (e.g., “Homework is good because it helps.”)  
Level 2: Student clarifies ideas precisely (e.g., “Homework helps me practice skills I learned in class, which makes me feel more confident.”)  

---

### 2. Source Reading and Highlighting — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students read two short articles: one supporting homework and one opposing it. They highlight key points and underline any unclear or confusing information.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI2b (Assessing quality of information) by having students identify important ideas and evaluate the relevance and clarity of the information.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student highlights some relevant points but misses key details or highlights irrelevant information.  
Level 2: Student highlights main arguments clearly and notes any questionable or unclear claims.  

---

### 3. Group Discussion: Organizing Arguments — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
In small groups, students organize the highlighted points into two columns: Pros and Cons of homework. They discuss how to arrange these points logically to support each side’s argument.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1b (Organizing information) by arranging ideas to strengthen logical flow.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Group organizes points in a basic list with limited logical connection.  
Level 2: Group arranges points to build a clear, logical argument with supporting details.  

---

### 4. Source Evaluation Activity — 8 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students work in pairs to evaluate the sources of the articles. They discuss who wrote them, where they were published, and whether the information seems reliable. They cross-check one fact from each article using a quick online search or a provided fact sheet.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information) and CRI2b (Assessing quality of information) by encouraging students to verify information and judge source credibility.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student identifies source but does not consistently evaluate reliability or cross-check facts.  
Level 2: Student rigorously evaluates sources and verifies facts using multiple authoritative references.  

---

### 5. Structured Debate Preparation — 10 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students prepare for a short debate by choosing a side (for or against homework). They use their organized notes and source evaluations to develop 2–3 strong arguments and anticipate counterarguments.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making) by systematically evaluating trade-offs and reasoning through complexities.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student lists arguments with minimal analysis and unclear reasoning.  
Level 2: Student systematically evaluates pros and cons, anticipating counterarguments and applying clear, logical reasoning.  

---

### 6. Mini-Debate and Reflection — 7 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Pairs or small groups conduct a brief debate presenting their arguments and responding to opposing views. Afterward, students write a short reflection on how their thinking changed or stayed the same during the lesson.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) during debate and CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes) during reflection.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student provides partially logical reasoning and mentions personal assumptions without deep reflection.  
Level 2: Student demonstrates sophisticated reasoning in debate and deep metacognitive awareness in reflection, noting how they adjusted their thinking.  

---

### 7. Exit Ticket: Final Position and Reasoning — 3 Minutes  
**What happens:**  
Students write their final position on homework (for or against) and provide two reasons supporting their view, citing evidence from the lesson.  

**Critical Thinking Connection:**  
Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information) and CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning) by requiring clear, supported conclusions.  

**Assessment Note:**  
Level 1: Student states a position with vague or unsupported reasons.  
Level 2: Student clearly states a position with precise, well-supported reasons referencing lesson content.
