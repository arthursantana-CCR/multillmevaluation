## Generator
# Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 Minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain why a relationship is or is not proportional using precise mathematical language.
3. Reflect on their own reasoning process when evaluating proportional relationships.

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed "Rate Cards" (sets of tables, graphs, and scenarios for each group)
- Graphing paper
- Exit ticket slips
- Anchor chart: "Is It Proportional? Decision Checklist"

---

## Lesson Structure

---

### Warm-Up — 7 Minutes
**Activity: "Same or Different?"**

Display two scenarios on the board:
- *Scenario A:* A car travels 60 miles every hour.
- *Scenario B:* A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: *"Which one represents a proportional relationship? How do you know?"*

Students write a 2-sentence response in their math journals before sharing aloud.

**Critical Thinking — CRI1a (Clarifying Information):**
This moment targets clarifying information. Students must articulate precisely what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through the origin) versus non-proportional (starting value, flat fee). Observe whether students use vague language ("they're both about money/speed") or precise language ("Scenario B has a starting value of $3, so the ratio changes"). Assess by listening to journal shares and cold-calling two or three students to refine their language with probing questions: *"What exactly does 'proportional' mean here?"*

---

### Direct Instruction — 10 Minutes
**Mini-Lesson: The Five Representations**

Using the anchor chart, introduce how proportional relationships appear across five representations:
- **Table:** constant ratio y/x
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = kx (no added constant)
- **Diagram:** double number line
- **Verbal:** "for every __ there is __"

Walk through one example (unit pricing: $2.50 per pound of apples) across all five forms. Emphasize: *"Each representation tells the same story — just in a different language."*

**Critical Thinking — CRI1b (Organizing Information):**
The anchor chart and the movement across representations models how information is logically arranged. Students copy the anchor chart and are prompted: *"Organize these five representations in an order that makes the most sense to YOU — from easiest to hardest to read."* This personal ordering task reveals whether students can structure information meaningfully. Assess by reviewing student anchor charts for a logical rationale (e.g., table → equation → graph), not just a random list.

---

### Guided Practice — 12 Minutes
**Activity: "Convince Me" Card Sort**

In pairs, students receive six Rate Cards showing tables, graphs, and equations — some proportional, some not. Their job: sort them into "Proportional" and "Not Proportional" and write one sentence justifying each decision.

Then pairs compare their sorts with another group. Where they disagree, they must debate using mathematical evidence.

**Critical Thinking — CRI2a (Assessing Validity of Information) & CRI2b (Assessing Quality of Information):**
When groups disagree, students must cross-check each other's reasoning against mathematical criteria (constant ratio, origin on graph). This targets validity and quality assessment — students cannot simply assert an answer; they must verify it. Observe whether students point back to specific values in the table or specific points on the graph. Assess during circulation: ask groups *"How do you know your evidence is correct?"* and look for students who recheck calculations rather than simply deferring to a louder peer.

---

### Independent Practice — 13 Minutes
**Activity: "The Better Deal" Decision Task**

Students receive a real-world scenario:
*"Store A sells notebooks for $1.50 each. Store B sells 3 notebooks for $4.00 but gives you a free notebook with every pack of 3. Which store is a better deal for buying 12 notebooks? Is either deal proportional?"*

Students must represent each store's pricing in a table and a graph, write an equation if possible, and make a final recommendation with written reasoning.

**Critical Thinking — CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons) & CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):**
This task forces students to evaluate trade-offs (lower unit price vs. bonus item) and apply logical reasoning to a multi-step decision. Assess by reviewing written recommendations: a Level 2 response anticipates edge cases ("this only works if you buy in multiples of 4"), while a Level 1 response simply picks a store without comparing unit rates systematically.

---

### Closing Reflection — Exit Ticket — 8 Minutes
**Prompt (3 questions on slip):**
1. How did you decide whether a relationship was proportional today?
2. Did you change your mind at any point? What made you reconsider?
3. What is one thing you are still unsure about?

**Critical Thinking — CRI5a (Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes):**
Question 2 directly prompts metacognition — students must identify a moment of cognitive revision. A Level 2 response names a specific misconception they held and explains what evidence changed their thinking (e.g., *"I thought any straight line was proportional until I graphed Store B and saw it didn't start at zero"*). Collect exit tickets and use responses to form small groups for the next day's re-teaching or enrichment.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a partially completed table and a graphing template for the decision task.
- **Extension:** Ask students to create their own "Not Proportional" scenario and challenge a partner to prove it.
