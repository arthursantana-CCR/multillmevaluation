# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to evaluate and justify their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with mixed scenarios, exit-ticket slips.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes) — Targets **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **B:** "A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $5 per month."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? Why?"* Students turn-and-talk for 90 seconds, then share. Guide students to clarify the defining feature of proportionality: a constant ratio with (0,0) as a starting point.

**Assessment of CRI1a:** Listen for students who precisely articulate why Scenario B is NOT proportional (the $20 fee breaks the constant ratio). Record on a quick checklist whether students identify relevant information with partial clarity (Level 1) or clarify ideas precisely (Level 2).

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Introduce the same proportional relationship ($3 per smoothie) shown five ways:
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $3 per smoothie."
- **Table:** Smoothies (1,2,3,4) → Cost (3,6,9,12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing repeated $3 units

Have students copy this organized "Five Representations" chart in their notebooks.

**Assessment of CRI1b:** Collect notebooks at the end of class. Students who arrange the five representations clearly with labeled connections demonstrate Level 2; disorganized or partial entries indicate Level 1.

---

### 3. Investigation Activity: "Is It Proportional?" (15 minutes) — Targets **CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information**
Distribute a handout with six scenarios (tables, graphs, and word problems). Some are proportional; some are not. Examples include:
- A table where ratios are 2/1, 4/2, 7/3 (not proportional — student must catch this)
- A graph that appears linear but doesn't pass through origin
- A "viral" social media claim: "If 2 workers paint 1 room in 3 hours, 10 workers paint 1 room in 0.6 hours."

In pairs, students must verify each claim by **cross-checking** using at least two representations (e.g., test the table by computing ratios AND graphing).

**Assessment of CRI2a:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you know this is true?"* Students who check using only one method score Level 1; those who cross-verify with multiple representations score Level 2.
**Assessment of CRI2b:** Review which evidence students select. Level 2 students choose the most authoritative check (e.g., testing the constant of proportionality across all rows, not just two).

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: Choosing the Best Representation (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI3a & CRI4a: Weighing Pros/Cons and Applying Sound Reasoning**
Pose a real-world problem: *"You're presenting to the school cafeteria about pricing pizza slices proportionally. Which representation (table, graph, equation, or diagram) would best convince them, and why?"*

Students individually list pros and cons of each representation in a T-chart, then commit to one choice with justification.

**Assessment of CRI3a:** Collect T-charts. Level 2 work systematically compares trade-offs (e.g., "A graph shows the trend visually but requires math knowledge; an equation is precise but abstract"). Level 1 work mentions options superficially.
**Assessment of CRI4a:** Evaluate justifications. Level 2 reasoning anticipates audience needs ("The cafeteria manager isn't a math teacher, so a table is most accessible"). Level 1 reasoning is logical but shallow.

---

### 5. Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — Targets **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**
Distribute exit-ticket slips with three prompts:
1. What was one mistake or confusion you had today, and how did you fix it?
2. Which representation is hardest for you, and what will you do next time to improve?
3. Rate your confidence (1–5) and explain.

**Assessment of CRI5a:** Read exit tickets. Level 2 responses show metacognitive awareness ("I assumed any straight line was proportional until I realized it had to pass through the origin — next time I'll always check that point first"). Level 1 responses note a feeling without strategy. Flag Level -1 responses (no awareness) for follow-up.

---

### 6. Closing (5 minutes)
Summarize the day's "big idea": *Proportional relationships share a constant ratio and can be verified across multiple representations.* Preview tomorrow's lesson on the constant of proportionality (k).
