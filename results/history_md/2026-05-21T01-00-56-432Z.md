## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Recipe" handout, exit tickets, three sample data tables (one proportional, two non-proportional).

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Opening / Hook (8 minutes)
Display two smoothie recipes on the board:
- **Recipe A:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 cups yogurt; 4 cups strawberries → 6 cups yogurt
- **Recipe B:** 2 cups strawberries → 3 cups yogurt; 4 cups strawberries → 7 cups yogurt

Ask: "Which recipe will always taste the same when scaled up? How do you know?"

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must articulate *what makes* the relationship consistent. Push them past "the numbers double" toward "the ratio stays the same."
- **Assessment:** Cold-call students to restate peers' ideas in clearer terms. Listen for precise language: "constant ratio," "equivalent fractions." Record clarity on a quick checklist.

### Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define **proportional relationship**: two quantities where y/x = k (constant of proportionality). Demonstrate using Recipe A:
- **Table:** Show ratios 3/2 = 6/4 = 1.5
- **Equation:** y = 1.5x
- **Graph:** Plot points; line passes through origin
- **Verbal:** "Yogurt is always 1.5 times the strawberries"

Emphasize the four representations as interconnected.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model a graphic organizer with four quadrants (Table | Graph | Equation | Words), filling each for Recipe A.
- **Assessment:** Students replicate the organizer in notes. Collect three samples mid-lesson to check whether information flows logically across representations.

### Guided Practice (12 minutes)
Distribute three data tables. Students work in pairs to determine which are proportional. One table contains a subtle error (e.g., 2→6, 3→9, 4→13).

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Ask: "How do you *verify* a relationship is proportional? What evidence convinces you?" Students must check every row, not just two points.
- **Assessment:** Circulate and ask pairs, "How did you confirm this?" Look for students cross-checking multiple ratios rather than assuming from one pair.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Present a fourth "claim" from a fictional student: "If two numbers both go up, it's proportional." Ask students to evaluate this claim's quality.
- **Assessment:** Listen for students who reject the claim with specific counterexamples (e.g., Recipe B above). Record responses on a participation rubric.

### Collaborative Task (12 minutes)
**"Best Deal" Activity:** Three stores sell notebooks:
- Store A: 4 notebooks for $6
- Store B: 6 notebooks for $9
- Store C: 10 notebooks for $14

Groups must (1) determine which stores use proportional pricing, (2) decide which is the best deal, and (3) justify in writing.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Groups must compare options across multiple dimensions—unit price, scalability, and whether the pricing is "fair" (proportional).
- **Assessment:** Collect group justifications. Look for trade-off language: "Store C is cheaper per notebook, but Store A's pricing scales predictably." Score 0–2 using the subcompetency rubric.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Require each group to write an equation justifying their choice and predict the cost of 25 notebooks at their chosen store.
- **Assessment:** Evaluate written reasoning chains. Sound reasoning shows: identifying k, applying y = kx, anticipating that non-proportional stores can't be reliably extrapolated. Faulty reasoning (e.g., "It's cheapest because $6 is smallest") earns a -1 or 1.

### Share-Out & Discussion (8 minutes)
Two groups present. Class debates the "best deal" using graphs sketched on the board. Highlight: a proportional relationship's graph is a straight line through the origin.

### Closing Reflection / Exit Ticket (5 minutes)
Students respond in writing:
1. Solve: "A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours at a constant speed. Write the equation and predict distance in 5 hours."
2. **Reflection prompt:** "What was one mistake you almost made today, or one moment your thinking changed? What helped you catch it?"

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** The reflection prompt requires metacognitive awareness.
- **Assessment:** Score exit tickets 0–2. A "2" response demonstrates strategic adjustment: "I assumed both tables were proportional because the numbers grew, but I learned to check every ratio." A "1" mentions reasoning without depth. A "-1" shows no awareness of process.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___").
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to create a non-proportional relationship that *looks* proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price tag, fuel economy). Represent it in all four forms.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").
