## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Hook & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — Targets **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop sells 2 smoothies for $7."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask students: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Have students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide them to clarify the definition: a proportional relationship has a constant ratio (y/x = k) and passes through the origin.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who precisely articulate why Scenario B is NOT proportional (the $20 fee disrupts the constant ratio). Record student language on the board, prompting them to refine vague statements like "they're different" into precise claims like "Scenario B has a starting cost, so the ratio between cost and visits isn't constant."

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Organizing Representations (12 minutes) — Targets **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Introduce the five representations using Scenario A:
- **Verbal:** "Cost is proportional to number of smoothies."
- **Table:** (1, 3.50), (2, 7), (3, 10.50), (4, 14)
- **Equation:** y = 3.5x
- **Graph:** Linear line through origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal parts

Have students fold a paper into 5 sections and label each with one representation. As you model, they fill in their organizer.

**Assessment:** Collect the foldables at the end of class. Look for logical flow — do students connect the constant of proportionality (k = 3.5) consistently across all five representations?

---

### 3. Source Evaluation Activity (8 minutes) — Targets **CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information**
Distribute a handout with **three "student work samples"** claiming whether different scenarios are proportional. One sample contains a common error (e.g., assuming any linear relationship is proportional). Students work in pairs to evaluate each claim, cross-checking using **two representations** (e.g., table AND graph) to verify.

Ask: *"Which student's reasoning is most reliable? Why? What evidence did they provide?"*

**Assessment:** Pairs submit a brief written justification identifying which sample is mathematically valid. Look for students who cite multiple representations as cross-checks (CRI2a level 2) and who select the highest-quality reasoning rather than the most confident-sounding answer (CRI2b level 2).

---

### 4. Weighing Representations (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons**
Pose the question: *"You need to convince your parents that buying snacks in bulk is a better deal. Which representation (table, graph, equation, or verbal description) would be MOST persuasive, and why?"*

Students rank all five representations from most to least persuasive in this context and justify their top choice in writing. Discuss as a class — emphasize that different representations have different strengths (graphs show trends visually; equations allow prediction; tables show specific values).

**Assessment:** Review written rankings. Level 2 students will articulate trade-offs (e.g., "A graph is visually persuasive but an equation lets you calculate any value, so I'd use both"). Level 1 students will pick one without comparing alternatives meaningfully.

---

### 5. Applied Reasoning Problem (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning**
Present a problem: *"A printer prints 24 pages in 3 minutes. How many pages in 11 minutes? Represent this with at least TWO representations and justify your answer."*

Students work independently. Circulate to question reasoning: *"How do you know your equation matches your table?"*

**Assessment:** Check work for logical consistency between representations. Level 2 students anticipate complexities (e.g., "This assumes the printer runs continuously without jamming"). Level 1 students compute correctly but don't justify the proportional assumption.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes) — Targets **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**
Distribute an exit ticket with two prompts:
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x + 1 proportional? Explain."
2. **Reflection:** "What was the trickiest part of today's lesson for you? What strategy helped you understand proportional relationships best, and how will you use it next time?"

**Assessment:** Read reflections after class. Level 2 students demonstrate metacognitive awareness (e.g., "I kept confusing linear with proportional, so I'm going to always check if the line passes through the origin first"). Level 1 students mention difficulty without identifying strategies. Level -1 students repeat errors without acknowledging them.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables for struggling students.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that *looks* proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price-per-unit, etc.) and represent it in all five ways.

---

This lesson explicitly engages each critical thinking subcompetency through structured opportunities for clarification, organization, evaluation, comparison, reasoning, and reflection — building mathematical and cognitive skills simultaneously.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Anatomy of Proportions: Recognizing and Representing Relationships
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, translate, and represent proportional relationships across multiple formats (verbal, table, graph, equation) while applying rigorous critical thinking to validate their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: The Hook and Verbal Translation (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world verbal description displayed on the board: *"A local bakery sells 3 chocolate chip cookies for $4.50. A competing bakery sells 5 of the same cookies for $7.00."* Students are asked to determine which bakery offers the better deal and whether the cost is proportional to the number of cookies. 

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before solving, students must rewrite the verbal prompt into precise mathematical questions and define the variables (e.g., Let $x$ = cookies, $y$ = cost). They must clarify the ambiguous phrase "better deal" into a precise mathematical concept: "lowest unit rate per cookie."
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and review students' written definitions. Mastery is observed when a student precisely clarifies the verbal scenario into a clear, solvable mathematical framework, stripping away irrelevant context and isolating the unit rate.

---

### **Part 2: Structuring Data and Cross-Checking (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students transition from the verbal description to creating tables and graphs for both bakeries. The teacher models how to set up the $x$ and $y$ axes and how to plot the coordinates. 

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with organizing the cookie pricing data into a T-chart and subsequently mapping it onto a coordinate plane. They must arrange the data sequentially to reveal the constant of proportionality ($k = y/x$).
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses students' tables and graphs to ensure the information is arranged with logical flow. A successful student will consistently structure their $x$ (independent) and $y$ (dependent) variables, creating a clear visual and numerical progression that strengthens the logical flow of their mathematical argument.

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Once the graphs are drawn, students must rigorously cross-check their visual findings. They are required to verify their graph's validity by consulting two additional authoritative sources: their math textbook’s definition of a proportional graph (a straight line passing through the origin) and a digital graphing tool (like Desmos on a class tablet) to confirm their hand-drawn plots.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students actively comparing their hand-drawn graphs against the textbook criteria and the digital tool. Mastery is demonstrated when students explicitly cite these multiple sources to validate that the first bakery's pricing is proportional.

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are given a set of additional data points from a third bakery, some of which are clearly outliers or errors (e.g., 10 cookies for $15, 11 cookies for $40). Students must select which data points are high-quality and reliable to determine the true unit rate, discarding the anomalies.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess this by asking students to justify which data points they used to write their equation. Students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by selecting only the consistent, high-quality data points that align with the established constant of proportionality, explicitly rejecting the flawed data.

---

### **Part 3: Evaluating Representations and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups. Each group is given a different scenario (e.g., a car driving at a constant speed, a gym membership with a $20 flat start-up fee plus $10 a month). They must represent their scenario as an equation ($y = kx$).

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher asks the groups: *"If I want to know the cost of the gym membership after 50 months, should I use a table, a graph, or an equation?"* Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each alternative representation. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to group discussions and require a written justification. Students achieve the highest level when they articulate that while a table or graph is excellent for visualizing early data, an equation is the superior choice for long-term implications because it avoids the tedious, error-prone process of extending a graph 50 units.

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must decide if the gym membership scenario (with the $20 start-up fee) represents a proportional relationship. They must apply mathematical reasoning to justify their final decision.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the groups' final presentations. Sophisticated reasoning is demonstrated when students anticipate complexities—noting that while the monthly rate increases constantly, the relationship is *not* proportional because the ratio of $y/x$ is not constant, and the graph will not pass through the origin $(0,0)$ due to the initial $20 fee.

---

### **Part 4: Metacognitive Closure (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students complete an independent exit ticket that requires them to look back at their initial assumptions from the beginning of the lesson regarding the bakery problem and the gym membership problem.

**Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
*   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The exit ticket asks: *"Describe a moment today when you realized your initial thought about a proportional relationship was incorrect or incomplete. How did translating the problem into a different format (like a graph or equation) force you to adjust your thinking process?"*
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. A top-tier response will demonstrate deep self-awareness, with the student explaining exactly how they initially assumed the gym membership was proportional due to the steady monthly fee, but strategically adjusted their thinking upon visualizing the $y$-intercept on the graph.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain their reasoning using precise mathematical language such as “constant of proportionality,” “unit rate,” and “passes through the origin.”

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which One Is Proportional? — 7 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |

Ask students: “Which relationship is proportional? How do you know?”

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

Guide students toward the idea that in Table A, the ratio y/x is always 3, while in Table B, the ratio changes.

**Teacher language:**  
“A proportional relationship has a constant ratio. That means one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other quantity.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes

Explain that proportional relationships can be represented in several ways:

- **Table:** The ratio y/x is constant.
- **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** It can be written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.
- **Diagram:** Equal groups or repeated units show the same ratio.
- **Verbal description:** The situation describes a constant rate or unit rate.

Use Table A from the warm-up.

Ask:

- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “What equation represents this relationship?”
- “What would the graph look like?”

Students should identify k = 3 and equation y = 3x.

Graph the points together: (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12). Emphasize that the graph is a straight line and would pass through (0, 0).

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Present this situation:

“A bike rental shop charges $5 per hour.”

As a class, represent the relationship between hours and cost.

Create a table:

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|-------|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

Ask students to write:

- Equation: C = 5h
- Verbal description: “The cost is $5 for each hour.”
- Graph: Students plot the points on graph paper.
- Diagram: Students draw groups of $5 for each hour.

Then ask: “How can we prove this relationship is proportional?”

Expected student response: “The ratio of cost to hours is always 5, and the graph is a straight line through the origin.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give pairs a handout with four representations:

1. A table showing distance traveled at a constant speed.
2. A graph that is a straight line but does not pass through the origin.
3. A verbal description: “A gym charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $8 per month.”
4. A diagram showing 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar.

For each item, students must decide:

- Is it proportional?
- What evidence supports your answer?
- If proportional, represent it in one other way.

Students must write at least one complete reasoning statement for each.

Example sentence frame:  
“This is/is not proportional because ________. I checked by ________.”

Circulate and ask probing questions:

- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Can the equation be written as y = kx?”
- “Is there an extra starting amount?”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Review the gym membership example.

Ask: “Why is $10 plus $8 per month not proportional?”

Guide students to explain that the relationship does not start at zero. If someone pays for 0 months, they still owe $10, so the graph would not pass through the origin and the equation would be y = 8x + 10, not y = kx.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete independently:

“A recipe uses 6 cups of water for every 2 cups of rice.”

1. Is this relationship proportional? Explain.
2. Find the constant of proportionality.
3. Write an equation.
4. Represent the relationship in a table with at least four values.

Expected answer: Yes, proportional. The ratio is 3 cups of water per 1 cup of rice. Equation: w = 3r.

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 6 | 8 | 10 |

Students write individually for 2 minutes, then share with a partner.

Guide students toward the idea that in Table A, the ratio y/x is always 3, while in Table B, the ratio changes.

**Teacher language:**  
“A proportional relationship has a constant ratio. That means one quantity is always multiplied by the same number to get the other quantity.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes

Explain that proportional relationships can be represented in several ways:

- **Table:** The ratio y/x is constant.
- **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** It can be written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.
- **Diagram:** Equal groups or repeated units show the same ratio.
- **Verbal description:** The situation describes a constant rate or unit rate.

Use Table A from the warm-up.

Ask:

- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “What equation represents this relationship?”
- “What would the graph look like?”

Students should identify k = 3 and equation y = 3x.

Graph the points together: (1, 3), (2, 6), (3, 9), (4, 12). Emphasize that the graph is a straight line and would pass through (0, 0).

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Present this situation:

“A bike rental shop charges $5 per hour.”

As a class, represent the relationship between hours and cost.

Create a table:

| Hours | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|-------|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 5 | 10 | 15 | 20 |

Ask students to write:

- Equation: C = 5h
- Verbal description: “The cost is $5 for each hour.”
- Graph: Students plot the points on graph paper.
- Diagram: Students draw groups of $5 for each hour.

Then ask: “How can we prove this relationship is proportional?”

Expected student response: “The ratio of cost to hours is always 5, and the graph is a straight line through the origin.”

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give pairs a handout with four representations:

1. A table showing distance traveled at a constant speed.
2. A graph that is a straight line but does not pass through the origin.
3. A verbal description: “A gym charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $8 per month.”
4. A diagram showing 2 cups of flour for every 3 cups of sugar.

For each item, students must decide:

- Is it proportional?
- What evidence supports your answer?
- If proportional, represent it in one other way.

Students must write at least one complete reasoning statement for each.

Example sentence frame:  
“This is/is not proportional because ________. I checked by ________.”

Circulate and ask probing questions:

- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “Does the graph pass through the origin?”
- “Can the equation be written as y = kx?”
- “Is there an extra starting amount?”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 5 minutes

Review the gym membership example.

Ask: “Why is $10 plus $8 per month not proportional?”

Guide students to explain that the relationship does not start at zero. If someone pays for 0 months, they still owe $10, so the graph would not pass through the origin and the equation would be y = 8x + 10, not y = kx.

### 6. Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

“A recipe uses 6 cups of water for every 2 cups of rice.”

1. Is this relationship proportional? Explain.
2. Find the constant of proportionality.
3. Write an equation.
4. Represent the relationship in a table with at least four values.

Expected answer: Yes, proportional. The ratio is 3 cups of water per 1 cup of rice. Equation: w = 3r.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios and student work samples, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Hook & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — Targets **CRI1a: Clarifying Information**
Display: *"A bakery sells 3 cookies for $4.50. A smoothie shop charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."* Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"*

Before solving, students rewrite each prompt into precise mathematical language, defining variables (e.g., "Let x = number of cookies, y = total cost") and clarifying vague phrases like "better deal" into "lowest unit rate." Turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide students toward the definition: a proportional relationship has a constant ratio (y/x = k) and passes through the origin.

**Assessment:** Listen for students who precisely articulate why the smoothie shop is NOT proportional ("The $20 fee disrupts the constant ratio"). Record student language on the board, pushing vague statements into precise claims.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Organizing Representations (12 minutes) — Targets **CRI1b: Organizing Information**
Introduce the five representations using "A bike rental shop charges $5 per hour":
- **Verbal:** "Cost is proportional to number of hours."
- **Table:** (0,0), (1,5), (2,10), (3,15), (4,20)
- **Equation:** C = 5h
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Groups of $5 for each hour

Students fold paper into 5 sections, labeling and completing each representation as you model. Emphasize how k = 5 appears consistently across all five forms.

**Assessment:** Collect foldables. Look for logical flow — do students consistently connect the constant of proportionality (k = 5) across all five representations?

---

### 3. Source Evaluation Activity (8 minutes) — Targets **CRI2a & CRI2b: Assessing Validity and Quality**
Distribute a handout with three "student work samples" claiming whether different scenarios are proportional, plus a data set from a third bakery with anomalies (e.g., 10 cookies for $15 but 11 cookies for $40). Pairs evaluate each claim, cross-checking using two representations (table AND graph) and deciding which data points are reliable.

**Assessment:** Pairs submit a brief written justification. Level 2 students cite multiple representations as cross-checks (CRI2a) and reject anomalous data with reasoning (CRI2b): "I excluded 11 cookies for $40 because it breaks the constant ratio established by the other points."

---

### 4. Weighing Representations (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons**
Pose: *"If you wanted to know the cost of a gym membership ($20 fee + $10/month) after 50 months, would you use a table, graph, or equation? Why?"* Students rank all representations and justify their top choice in writing using the frame: "I would use ___ because ___. A drawback is ___."

**Assessment:** Level 2 students articulate trade-offs (e.g., "A graph shows trends visually, but an equation avoids the error-prone process of extending a table to 50 rows"). Level 1 students pick one without comparing alternatives.

---

### 5. Applied Reasoning Problem (10 minutes) — Targets **CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning**
Present: *"A printer prints 24 pages in 3 minutes. How many pages in 11 minutes? Represent this with at least TWO representations and justify whether the relationship is proportional."*

Students work independently. Circulate and probe: *"How do you know your equation matches your table? Does your graph pass through the origin?"*

**Assessment:** Check for logical consistency between representations. Level 2 students anticipate complexities ("This assumes the printer runs continuously"). Level 1 students compute correctly but don't justify the proportional assumption.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket & Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes) — Targets **CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes**
Distribute an exit ticket:
1. Solve: "Is y = 4x + 1 proportional? Explain."
2. **Reflection:** "Describe a moment today when you realized your initial thinking about a proportional relationship was incomplete. What strategy helped you adjust, and how will you use it next time?"

**Assessment:** Level 2 responses demonstrate metacognitive awareness ("I first only checked for a straight line, but then remembered it also has to pass through the origin — next time I'll check both"). Level 1 students mention difficulty without identifying strategies. Level -1 students repeat errors without acknowledgment.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables for struggling students.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that *looks* proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipe, price-per-unit, etc.) and represent it in all five ways.
