## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios, exit tickets

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two recipe scenarios on the board:
- Recipe A: 2 cups flour makes 12 cookies
- Recipe B: 3 cups flour makes 18 cookies; 5 cups makes 28 cookies

Ask: "If you doubled Recipe A, how many cookies? Is Recipe B proportional?"

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must identify what "proportional" requires—a constant ratio. Call on 3 students to clarify in their own words what makes a relationship proportional.
- **Assessment:** Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "same unit rate"). Note students who restate vaguely vs. those who clarify precisely.

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships and introduce the constant of proportionality (k) where y = kx. Demonstrate four representations using the scenario "1 gallon of gas costs $3.50":
- **Table:** gallons vs. cost
- **Graph:** straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3.50x
- **Verbal:** "Cost varies proportionally with gallons purchased."

Emphasize: proportional graphs pass through the origin.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Model how to organize the same relationship across four representations in a structured chart.
- **Assessment:** During guided notes, check whether students arrange the four representations clearly in their notebooks with consistent labels.

### 3. Guided Practice — Compare & Critique (12 minutes)
Distribute handout with three scenarios, each presented in a different format:
- Scenario 1 (Table): Distance/time for a car
- Scenario 2 (Graph): A line NOT through origin
- Scenario 3 (Verbal): "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit"

Students work in pairs to determine which are proportional and justify their reasoning.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Provide one "student work sample" claiming Scenario 3 is proportional because "as visits go up, cost goes up." Students must cross-check this claim against the definition, the table values, and the graph.
- **Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Look for students referencing multiple representations (table calculations + graph behavior + definition) rather than accepting the claim at face value.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Ask students which evidence is strongest for proving non-proportionality—testing ratios in a table, checking if the graph passes through origin, or looking at the equation form.
- **Assessment:** Exit ticket question asks students to rank these methods and justify their top choice.

### 4. Collaborative Task — The Job Decision (15 minutes)
Present a real-world dilemma: "You're choosing between two summer jobs.
- **Job A:** Earns $9 per hour
- **Job B:** Pays $50 signing bonus + $7 per hour"

Students must:
1. Create tables for hours 1–10
2. Graph both on the same axes
3. Write equations for each
4. Determine which is proportional
5. Recommend which job to take and explain when each is better

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must systematically compare both jobs across multiple hour ranges and consider trade-offs (short-term bonus vs. long-term hourly rate).
- **Assessment:** Review written recommendations for evidence of analyzing the intersection point and considering different scenarios (working few vs. many hours).

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence from all four representations.
- **Assessment:** Score reasoning on a 0–2 rubric: Does the student cite specific values, the equation structure, and graphical evidence? Do they anticipate "it depends on hours worked"?

### 5. Closure & Reflection (11 minutes)

**Gallery Walk (5 min):** Pairs post their job recommendations. Students place sticky notes on solutions with stronger reasoning.

**Metacognitive Exit Ticket (6 min):**
Students respond to three prompts:
1. One example of a proportional relationship and one non-proportional, with justification.
2. "What was the biggest misconception you had at the start of class? How did your thinking change?"
3. "Which representation (table, graph, equation, verbal) helps YOU most identify proportionality, and why?"

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking):** Prompts 2 and 3 require students to articulate how their understanding evolved and which strategies work best for them.
- **Assessment:** Read exit tickets for evidence of genuine reflection (e.g., "I thought any increasing relationship was proportional, but now I check if it goes through the origin") versus surface answers.

- **Extension:** Challenge students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain the trap.

    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.

    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**

Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Objective:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

---

### Materials
- Whiteboard/projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Calculators, optional  
- “Proportional or Not?” handout  
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Agenda

### 1. Warm-Up: Notice and Wonder — 5 minutes

Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Ask students:

1. What do you notice?  
2. What do you wonder?  
3. Which table seems to show a proportional relationship? Why?

Students write independently, then share with a partner.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify important information in the tables, such as the relationship between x-values and y-values.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students explaining clearly, such as “In Table A, y is always 3 times x,” rather than simply saying “it goes up by 3.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities is constant. This constant is called the **constant of proportionality**, often represented as **k**.

For a proportional relationship:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \( k = \frac{y}{x} \).

Use Table A:

\[
\frac{3}{1}=3,\quad \frac{6}{2}=3,\quad \frac{9}{3}=3,\quad \frac{12}{4}=3
\]

Since the ratio is always 3, the relationship is proportional.

Then compare Table B:

\[
\frac{4}{1}=4,\quad \frac{7}{2}=3.5,\quad \frac{10}{3}\approx 3.33,\quad \frac{13}{4}=3.25
\]

The ratios are not equal, so the relationship is not proportional.

Emphasize that proportional relationships can be shown in multiple ways:
- **Table:** constant ratio  
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin  
- **Equation:** \( y = kx \)  
- **Diagram:** equal groups or scaled quantities  
- **Words:** one quantity is a constant multiple of another

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students create a five-column organizer labeled table, graph, equation, diagram, and words.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check whether students arrange information logically and connect each representation to the same proportional relationship.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: One Situation, Many Representations — 12 minutes

Present the situation:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

Ask students to complete the following together:

1. **Verbal description:** The total cost is 2 times the number of notebooks.  
2. **Table:**

| Notebooks | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 2 | 4 | 6 | 8 |

3. **Equation:**

\[
y = 2x
\]

4. **Graph:** Plot points \((0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8)\). Draw a straight line through the points.  
5. **Diagram:** Draw groups showing $2 for each notebook.

Ask: “How can we prove all five representations describe the same relationship?”

Students discuss and explain that each representation shows a constant rate of $2 per notebook.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students verify that the table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description all match the original situation.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Ask students to point to evidence in at least two representations, such as “The equation says multiply by 2, and the table also increases by 2 for each notebook.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation best supports their explanation and why.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for students choosing relevant evidence, such as using the graph to show the line goes through the origin or using the table to show a constant ratio.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Give pairs a handout with four scenarios.

Example problems:

1. A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, and 180 miles in 3 hours.  
2. A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $10 per month.  
3. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
4. A plant is 5 inches tall on day 1, 7 inches on day 2, and 9 inches on day 3.

For each scenario, students must:
- Decide whether it is proportional  
- Show evidence in a table, equation, graph, or diagram  
- Explain their reasoning in words  
- Identify the constant of proportionality if there is one

After 8 minutes, pairs compare answers with another pair.

Ask students to discuss:
- Which representation was most useful?
- Were there any tricky examples?
- Did any example look proportional at first but turn out not to be?

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare different representations and decide which one best helps determine proportionality.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Listen for students explaining trade-offs, such as “The table helped us compare ratios quickly, but the graph made it easier to see whether the line went through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify whether each relationship is proportional using mathematical evidence.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Check that students use valid reasoning, such as constant ratios or a graph through the origin, instead of relying only on patterns like “it adds the same amount.”

---

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion — 8 minutes

Review each scenario. Highlight common misconceptions:

- A relationship that increases by the same amount is not always proportional.
- A graph must be a straight line **and** pass through the origin.
- An equation like \( y = 10x + 20 \) is not proportional because of the added 20.
- A proportional relationship has the form \( y = kx \).

Ask students:

“Why is the gym membership not proportional?”

Expected response: Because there is a $20 starting fee, so the graph would not pass through \((0,0)\), and the equation is not in the form \( y = kx \).

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes

Students complete:

1. How can you tell from a table if a relationship is proportional?  
2. How can you tell from a graph?  
3. Write an equation for a proportional relationship with constant of proportionality 4.  
4. Reflection: “One mistake I almost made today was ___, but I corrected it by ___.”

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on a misconception, strategy, or correction they made.  
- **Observe/Assess:** Look for evidence that students can describe their own thinking, such as recognizing they confused constant difference with constant ratio.

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while engaging in critical thinking practices to evaluate, organize, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, whiteboard/projector, "Proportional or Not?" handout, exit tickets, student notebooks

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Source Evaluation (8 minutes)
Display two pricing scenarios:
- **Source A (verified bakery pricing sheet):** "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, 8 cost $16."
- **Source B (social media post):** "I think I paid $5 for a 2-mile taxi ride, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"

Ask: "Which source gives us reliable information to test for a proportional relationship? Why?"

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Before computing, students rewrite each scenario as precise mathematical statements identifying the independent and dependent variables.
- **Assessment:** Review written statements; listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "cost per cupcake") rather than vague restatements.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students evaluate the two data sources to determine which provides authoritative information for establishing a proportion.
- **Assessment:** During discussion, look for students identifying that Source B is unreliable/imprecise while Source A provides authoritative data sufficient to verify a constant rate.

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? (10 minutes)
Define: a relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between quantities is constant. This constant is the **constant of proportionality (k)**, where y = kx.

Using Source A: 6/3 = 2, 10/5 = 2, 16/8 = 2 → proportional, k = 2.

Demonstrate the five representations for "$2 per cupcake":
- **Table:** cupcakes vs. cost
- **Graph:** straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Diagram:** equal groups of $2 per cupcake
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 2 times the number of cupcakes."

Emphasize: proportional graphs MUST pass through the origin.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students create a five-column organizer (table, graph, equation, diagram, words) showing the same relationship in each form.
- **Assessment:** Check notebooks for logical arrangement — x-values in ascending order, labeled axes, consistent labels, clear connections across representations.

### 3. Guided Practice — Cross-Checking Representations (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout requiring students to verify that the table, graph, and equation for Source A all yield the same constant of proportionality.

Then provide a "student work sample" claiming a gym scenario ("$20 sign-up + $5 per visit") is proportional because "as visits go up, cost goes up." Students cross-check this claim against the definition, table values, and graph behavior.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students rigorously cross-check k across multiple representations and evaluate the flawed claim.
- **Assessment:** Collect written justifications. Look for students referencing multiple representations (table ratios + graph through origin + equation form y = kx) rather than accepting claims at face value.

### 4. Collaborative Task — The Job Decision (15 minutes)
Present a real-world dilemma: "You're choosing between two summer jobs.
- **Job A:** Earns $9 per hour
- **Job B:** Pays $50 signing bonus + $7 per hour"

Students work in pairs to:
1. Create tables for hours 1–10
2. Graph both on the same axes
3. Write equations for each
4. Determine which is proportional (and why)
5. Recommend which job to take, explaining when each is better

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students systematically compare jobs across different hour ranges, considering trade-offs (short-term bonus vs. long-term hourly rate, intersection point implications).
- **Assessment:** Review written recommendations for evidence of analyzing the break-even point and considering multiple scenarios ("If you work fewer than 25 hours...").

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify their recommendation using mathematical evidence from all representations, anticipating that "it depends" on hours worked.
- **Assessment:** Score on a 0–2 rubric: Does the student cite specific values, equation structure (y = kx vs. y = mx + b), and graphical evidence (origin vs. y-intercept of 50)? Do they avoid faulty logic?

### 5. Whole-Class Discussion (5 minutes)
Review key misconceptions:
- A relationship that increases by the same amount is not necessarily proportional.
- A proportional graph must be a straight line **AND** pass through the origin.
- y = 7x + 50 is NOT proportional because of the constant term.
- Only y = kx represents a proportional relationship.

### 6. Closure & Metacognitive Reflection (7 minutes)
Exit ticket prompts:
1. Write an equation for a proportional relationship with k = 4.
2. How can you tell from a graph whether a relationship is proportional?
3. **Reflection:** "What was the biggest misconception you had at the start of class? How did your thinking change? What will you do differently next time you see a word problem about rates?"

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students articulate how their understanding evolved and what strategies they will adjust.
- **Assessment:** Read exit tickets for deep metacognition (e.g., "I used to just look for an increasing pattern, but now I check whether the line passes through the origin and verify the equation is in y = kx form") versus surface answers.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Sentence frames ("This relationship is proportional because ___"); partially completed tables.
- **Challenge:** Create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain the trap.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, fuel) and represent it in all five forms.
