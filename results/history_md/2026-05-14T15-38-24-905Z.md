## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Seeing the Pattern Everywhere
### CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 | 55-Minute Lesson | Grade 7

---

## Lesson Overview
**Objective:** Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.
**Materials:** Graph paper, colored markers, printed data cards, whiteboard, student journals

---

## Lesson Flow

### WARM-UP — 7 Minutes

Post two scenarios on the board:

> **A:** A car travels 60 miles per hour. **B:** A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: *"Which one do you think shows a proportional relationship — and how do you know?"* Students write a prediction in their journals before any instruction occurs.

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking:**
Students articulate their *initial reasoning* before learning begins. Observation: Collect journals and look for phrases like "I think because…" or "I'm not sure if…" — evidence of metacognitive honesty. Students who simply write an answer without justification score at Level 1; students who identify *what they'd need to check* demonstrate Level 2 awareness.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 12 Minutes

Introduce the definition of a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x remains constant, written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.

Walk through a clear example using a lemonade recipe: 2 cups sugar for every 5 cups water.

Build the representation together on the board in four forms:

| Cups Water | Cups Sugar |
|---|---|
| 5 | 2 |
| 10 | 4 |
| 15 | 6 |

Then graph it, write y = (2/5)x, and state it verbally: *"For every cup of water, you use 2/5 cup of sugar."*

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information:**
The teacher explicitly names *what makes a relationship proportional* (constant ratio, passes through the origin, equation of the form y = kx) versus what does not (a y-intercept ≠ 0, like the taxi problem). Observation: During a brief check, ask students to explain in their own words what "constant ratio" means. Responses that include the origin and the ratio k reflect Level 2 clarity; vague responses ("the numbers match") indicate Level 1.

**CRI1b — Organizing Information:**
Students copy the four representations into a structured graphic organizer in their journals — table, graph, equation, and verbal description — arranged in quadrants on one page. Observation: Review organizers for logical layout and completeness. A student who places all four forms with labels and connecting arrows demonstrates Level 2 organization; a student who writes scattered notes with missing sections scores Level 1.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE — 12 Minutes

Distribute data cards. Each card shows one of four representations of a relationship (some proportional, some not). In pairs, students must:

1. Identify whether their relationship is proportional.
2. Justify using *two different representations* as evidence.
3. Decide: if it's not proportional, what would need to change to make it proportional?

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information:**
Students must cross-check their conclusion using a second representation — they cannot rely on only the table or only the graph. Observation: Listen for statements like *"The table shows a constant ratio, AND the graph passes through the origin, so we're confident."* Students who verify with two forms score Level 2; students who stop at one form score Level 1.

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information:**
Ask pairs: *"Which representation gave you the most reliable evidence? Which was easiest to misread?"* Students discuss why a graph can be misleading if the axes aren't scaled from zero. Observation: Students who identify the graph's axis scaling as a potential flaw in their reasoning demonstrate Level 2 judgment about information quality.

---

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE & DEBATE — 15 Minutes

Present this scenario:

> *Two streaming services: StreamFast charges $8/month flat. QuickStream charges $2 per movie watched.*

Students create all four representations for *both* services and then answer: *"For a customer who watches 5 movies per month, which service is the better deal? What about 2 movies? What about 10?"*

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons:**
Students must evaluate trade-offs across different usage levels rather than declaring one service universally better. Observation: Students who say "it depends on how many movies you watch" and support that with their table or graph — identifying the break-even point — demonstrate Level 2 analysis. Students who pick one service without considering the variable demonstrate Level 1.

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning:**
Students write a two-sentence recommendation explaining which service they'd choose *and* under what conditions that answer would change. Observation: Look for conditional logic ("If someone watches fewer than 4 movies, then… but if they watch more…"). Unconditional claims without mathematical support indicate Level 1 reasoning.

---

### CLOSING REFLECTION — 9 Minutes

Students return to their warm-up journal entry and write a response to: *"Was your prediction correct? What in your thinking changed — and why?"*

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking (revisited):**
This closing loop is the metacognitive payoff of the lesson. Observation: Students who identify a *specific moment* when their thinking shifted (e.g., "I changed my mind when I graphed it and saw the line didn't pass through the origin") demonstrate Level 2 metacognitive awareness. Students who simply write "I was right" or "I was wrong" without analysis score Level 1.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

- Ask students to share how they figured out the total cost.  
- Introduce the concept of proportional relationships: when two quantities increase or decrease at the same rate.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**  
*Activity:* Students clarify the relationship between number of tickets and total cost in their own words.  
*Assessment:* Listen for precise explanations that connect the quantities clearly (e.g., “The total cost is 8 times the number of tickets.”).

---

**2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)**  
- Define proportional relationships formally: two quantities x and y are proportional if y = kx for some constant k.  
- Show examples in different forms:  
  - Table: Number of tickets vs. total cost  
  - Graph: Plot points and draw a straight line through the origin  
  - Equation: y = 8x  
  - Diagram: Visual ratio model (e.g., bar model)  
  - Verbal description: “The total cost is always 8 times the number of tickets.”  
- Emphasize the constant of proportionality (k).

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**  
*Activity:* Present information in a logical sequence from concrete (table) to abstract (equation).  
*Assessment:* Check students’ notes or responses for organized understanding, e.g., correctly matching forms or explaining the flow from one representation to another.

---

**3. Guided Practice (15 minutes)**  
- Distribute handouts with several proportional relationship problems in mixed formats.  
- In pairs, students identify the constant of proportionality and represent the relationship in all five forms (table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description).  
- Circulate to support and prompt deeper thinking.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**  
- CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)  
- CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)  

*Activity:* Students verify if given data sets or graphs truly represent proportional relationships by checking for constant ratios and linearity through the origin.  
*Assessment:* Observe students questioning or confirming data validity, e.g., “This table is not proportional because the ratio changes,” or “The graph doesn’t pass through the origin, so it’s not proportional.”

---

**4. Class Discussion and Comparison (10 minutes)**  
- Invite pairs to share one example and explain their reasoning for identifying proportionality and representing it in different forms.  
- Discuss any discrepancies or errors and explore why some relationships are not proportional.  
- Encourage students to weigh pros and cons of each representation (e.g., tables are easy to read specific values, graphs show trends visually).

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**  
- CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)  
- CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)  

*Activity:* Facilitate a discussion where students compare representations and justify which is most helpful in different contexts.  
*Assessment:* Listen for systematic evaluation and logical reasoning, such as “Graphs help us see if the relationship is proportional because the line must go through the origin,” or “Equations are concise but harder to interpret quickly.”

---

**5. Independent Practice (10 minutes)**  
- Students individually complete a short set of problems requiring them to identify proportional relationships and represent them in at least two different forms.  
- Include one problem with a non-proportional relationship to challenge their reasoning.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes)**  
*Activity:* At the end of the independent work, students write a brief reflection on how they determined if a relationship was proportional and which representation helped them most.  
*Assessment:* Review reflections for metacognitive awareness, such as recognizing assumptions, adjusting strategies, or acknowledging difficulties.

---

**6. Closure and Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Summarize key points: proportional relationships have a constant ratio, can be represented in multiple ways, and understanding these helps solve real-world problems.  
- Exit ticket prompt: “Explain in your own words what makes two quantities proportional and give an example.”

# Proportional Relationships: Seeing the Pattern Everywhere
### CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 | 55-Minute Lesson | Grade 7

## Lesson Overview
**Objective:** Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
**Materials:** Graph paper, colored markers, printed data cards, student handouts with practice problems, whiteboard, rulers, student journals, exit tickets

---

## Lesson Flow

### WARM-UP — 7 Minutes

Post two scenarios on the board:

> **A:** A car travels 60 miles per hour.
> **B:** A taxi charges $3 plus $2 per mile.

Ask students: *"Which one do you think shows a proportional relationship — and how do you know?"* Students write a prediction and their reasoning in their journals before any instruction occurs.

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking (Initial):**
Students articulate their initial reasoning before learning begins, establishing a baseline for metacognitive growth.
*Observation:* Collect journals and look for phrases like "I think because…" or "I'm not sure if…" — evidence of metacognitive honesty. Students who simply write an answer without justification score Level 1; students who identify *what they would need to check* to be certain demonstrate Level 2 awareness.

---

### DIRECT INSTRUCTION — 12 Minutes

Introduce the definition of a proportional relationship: two quantities where the ratio y/x remains constant, written as y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality.

Walk through a clear example using a lemonade recipe: 2 cups of sugar for every 5 cups of water. Build all four representations together on the board:

**Table:**

| Cups Water | Cups Sugar |
|---:|---:|
| 5 | 2 |
| 10 | 4 |
| 15 | 6 |

**Graph:** Plot the points, noting the straight line passes through the origin (0, 0).
**Equation:** y = (2/5)x
**Verbal Description:** *"For every cup of water, you use 2/5 cup of sugar."*

Explicitly contrast this with the taxi scenario (y-intercept ≠ 0), reinforcing what proportional relationships are *not*.

**CRI1a — Clarifying Information:**
The teacher explicitly names what makes a relationship proportional (constant ratio, passes through the origin, equation of the form y = kx) versus what does not (a y-intercept ≠ 0, like the taxi problem).
*Observation:* During a brief check, ask students to explain in their own words what "constant ratio" means. Responses that include the origin and the ratio k reflect Level 2 clarity; vague responses ("the numbers match") indicate Level 1.

**CRI1b — Organizing Information:**
Students copy all four representations into a structured graphic organizer in their journals — table, graph, equation, and verbal description — arranged in quadrants on one page.
*Observation:* Review organizers for logical layout and completeness. A student who places all four forms with labels and connecting arrows demonstrates Level 2 organization; a student who writes scattered notes with missing sections scores Level 1.

---

### GUIDED PRACTICE: "Proportionality Puzzles" — 18 Minutes

Divide students into small groups of 3–4. Provide each group with a handout containing 3–4 different scenarios, each presenting one representation (a verbal description, a table, or a graph).

**Task:** For each scenario, students must:
1. Determine if the relationship is proportional.
2. Represent it in the *other two or three* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
3. If it is *not* proportional, explain why using mathematical evidence.

**Scenario design includes:**

- One scenario with a **mismatched graph** — the graph does not accurately represent the accompanying table's data (or does not pass through the origin despite a constant ratio in the table). Students must identify this inconsistency.
- One scenario with a **vague verbal description** containing irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays and his oven runs at 350°F."). Students must filter relevant from irrelevant information.

**CRI2a — Assessing Validity of Information:**
Students must cross-check their conclusion using a second representation — they cannot rely on only one form. They must also identify the mismatched graph scenario and explain why one representation is invalid.
*Observation:* Listen for statements like *"The table shows a constant ratio, AND the graph passes through the origin, so we're confident"* or *"The points on this graph don't match the (x, y) pairs in our table."* Students who verify with two forms and flag inconsistencies score Level 2; students who stop at one form score Level 1.

**CRI2b — Assessing Quality of Information:**
In the vague verbal description scenario, students must identify only the relevant quantities and disregard extraneous details.
*Observation:* Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality"). Students who make this judgment explicitly demonstrate Level 2; students who include irrelevant details in their work indicate Level 1.

---

### INDEPENDENT PRACTICE & ANALYSIS — 12 Minutes

Present the following scenario:

> *Two streaming services: StreamFast charges $8/month flat. QuickStream charges $2 per movie watched.*

Students individually create all four representations for *both* services, then answer:
*"For a customer who watches 5 movies per month, which service is the better deal? What about 2 movies? What about 10?"*

Students then write a two-sentence recommendation explaining which service they would choose *and* under what conditions that answer would change.

**CRI3a — Weighing Pros and Cons:**
Students must evaluate trade-offs across different usage levels rather than declaring one service universally better. They should identify the break-even point using their table or graph.
*Observation:* Students who say "it depends on how many movies you watch" and support that with their table or graph — identifying when the two services cost the same — demonstrate Level 2 analysis. Students who pick one service without considering varying usage demonstrate Level 1.

**CRI4a — Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making:**
Students write a conditional recommendation grounded in their mathematical representations.
*Observation:* Look for conditional logic ("If someone watches fewer than 4 movies, then… but if they watch more…"). Unconditional claims without mathematical support indicate Level 1 reasoning; well-reasoned, evidence-backed conditional statements indicate Level 2.

---

### CLOSING REFLECTION & EXIT TICKET — 6 Minutes

**Journal Return:** Students revisit their warm-up journal entry and write a response to: *"Was your prediction correct? What specific moment in today's lesson changed your thinking — and why?"*

**CRI5a — Reflecting on One's Thinking (Revisited):**
This closing loop is the metacognitive payoff of the lesson, allowing students to compare their initial reasoning to their developed understanding.
*Observation:* Students who identify a *specific moment* when their thinking shifted (e.g., "I changed my mind when I graphed it and saw the line didn't pass through the origin") demonstrate Level 2 metacognitive awareness. Students who simply write "I was right" or "I was wrong" without analysis score Level 1.

**Exit Ticket (written on a slip):**
1. A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this proportional? Explain using two different representations.
2. What was the most challenging part of today's lesson, and what strategy helped you work through it?
