## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while practicing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with 4 real-world scenarios, exit tickets.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join, plus $5 per visit."

Ask: *"Which is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students must clarify the definition of proportional relationships (constant ratio, passes through origin). 
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language: "constant rate," "starts at zero," "ratio stays the same." Note students who clarify peers' misconceptions.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Modeling (12 minutes)
Introduce the five representations using Scenario A:
- **Table:** (1, $3), (2, $6), (3, $9)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing 3 equal units
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 3 times the number of smoothies"

Walk through how each representation reveals the constant of proportionality (k = 3).

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing Information):** Model how to organize the five representations on a graphic organizer showing how each connects.
**Assessment:** Collect student notebooks at end of class; check whether their graphic organizers logically link representations (arrows, labels, consistent k value across forms).

---

### 3. Guided Practice — Comparing Representations (10 minutes)
Provide students with **three different scenarios** showing the same data in different forms (one table, one equation y = 4x, one verbal description of a non-proportional relationship). Ask: *"Which two are proportional, and which is not? Justify using at least two representations."*

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing Validity) and CRI2b (Assessing Quality):** Students must verify validity by cross-checking representations against each other (e.g., does the table truly produce a constant ratio? Does the graph pass through the origin?).
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask probing questions: "How did you confirm this is proportional? Did you check more than one way?" Record observations of students who cross-verify versus those who accept surface features.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision-Making Task (12 minutes)
**The Field Trip Problem:** "Your class needs to rent a bus for a field trip. Company A charges $2 per mile. Company B charges $50 flat plus $1 per mile. Company C charges $4 per mile but offers a free driver."

In groups of 4, students:
1. Create a table and graph for each company
2. Identify which represent proportional relationships
3. Recommend a company for a 30-mile trip AND a 100-mile trip

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Students must compare three options across different trip lengths, considering trade-offs.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Look for systematic comparison (e.g., "For short trips, A is cheaper, but for long trips, B becomes better because..."). Score recommendations that consider multiple distances higher.

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Students must justify choices mathematically using proportional reasoning.
**Assessment:** Evaluate written justifications for logical structure: Did they use equations? Did they anticipate that B's flat fee changes the comparison at different mileages? Flag faulty reasoning (e.g., "B is always cheapest").

---

### 5. Independent Reflection & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students complete a written exit ticket with three prompts:
1. Write the equation, draw the graph, and create a table for: *"A printer prints 8 pages per minute."*
2. Explain how you know it's proportional using **two** representations.
3. **Reflection:** *"What was the hardest part of today's lesson? Did your thinking change at any point? If you got something wrong, what would you do differently?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on Thinking Processes):** Prompt #3 explicitly asks for metacognitive reflection.
**Assessment:** Read reflections for evidence of self-awareness. Level 2 responses identify specific misconceptions ("I first thought any line was proportional, but I realized it must pass through zero"). Level 1 responses are vague ("It was hard"). Track students needing more reflective scaffolding.

---

### 6. Closing (3 minutes)
Quick whip-around: Each student shares one word describing proportional relationships. Preview tomorrow's lesson on unit rates within proportional contexts.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-filled tables for struggling students; pair strategically.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (e.g., recipe, price tag) and represent it in all five forms.

**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Project: Decoding Relationships in the Real World
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and validate proportional relationships across multiple formats (tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions) while applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Evaluation (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents two verbal descriptions of pricing models from two different local businesses. 
*Business A:* A verified official pricing sheet from a local bakery stating, "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, and 8 cost $16." 
*Business B:* A social media post from a customer stating, "I think I paid $5 for a taxi ride of 2 miles, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"
Students must read both scenarios and determine which business provides a proportional relationship.

*   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students must rewrite the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements, clarifying the exact variables (cost vs. quantity/distance) to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review students' written statements to ensure they have precisely identified the independent and dependent variables without introducing confusion or mixing up the quantities.
*   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students evaluate the two data sources (the official pricing sheet vs. the vague social media post) to determine which provides high-quality, authoritative information necessary to establish a mathematical proportion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will listen to class discussions to assess if students demonstrate sophisticated judgment, correctly identifying that Business B’s data is unreliable and lacks the depth needed to confirm a constant rate, while Business A provides authoritative, high-quality data.

### **Part 2: Organizing and Validating Representations (15 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Focusing on Business A (the bakery), the teacher guides students to translate the verbal description into a structured table, a coordinate graph, and an algebraic equation ($y = kx$). 

*   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with arranging the raw data from Business A into a logical, sequential table (ordering by quantity) and then plotting these points on a graph, ensuring the axes are properly labeled and scaled.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will check student whiteboards or guided notes to verify that the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow (e.g., $x$ values in ascending order, clear $x$ and $y$ axis labels), rather than being scattered or incoherent.
*   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rigorously cross-check their findings by comparing their table, their graph, and their equation. They must prove that the constant of proportionality ($k=2$) is valid across all three "authoritative sources" (the table's ratios, the graph's straight line through the origin, and the equation $y=2x$).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students by asking them to explicitly point out where the unit rate exists in all three representations. Mastery is observed when a student can seamlessly cross-check and validate the $k$ value across the table, graph, and equation without repeating errors.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving and Decision Making (20 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: "Your school is buying custom t-shirts. You need to find the cost of 450 shirts." They are given the proportional data in a table, a graph, and an equation. 

*   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using the table, the graph, or the equation to find the cost of exactly 450 shirts. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table 450 times is inefficient; a graph might lack precision at high numbers; an equation provides immediate accuracy).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group deliberations and grade a short written response where students compare the options. High performance is marked by a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs rather than a superficial preference.
*   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a twist: The t-shirt company charges a one-time $20 shipping fee. Students must decide if the relationship is still proportional and justify their conclusion.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the students' final decision. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning, anticipating the complexity the $20 fee adds (the line will no longer pass through the origin, making it non-proportional), avoiding faulty logic or unsupported claims.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket that focuses on how their understanding of proportional relationships evolved during the lesson.

*   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** On the Exit Ticket, students answer: "Think about how you initially decided if a relationship was proportional at the start of class versus now. How did cross-checking the graph, table, and equation change your thinking process? What will you do differently next time you encounter a word problem?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the exit tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. A high-level response will explicitly state how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I realize I must verify the line goes through the origin and check the equation, adjusting my strategy to cross-check all forms").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional using tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.
2. Represent a proportional relationship with an equation in the form **y = kx**.
3. Explain their reasoning using precise mathematical language such as *constant of proportionality*, *unit rate*, *origin*, and *constant ratio*.

### Materials
- Projector or board  
- Graph paper  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, equations, and word problems  
- Rulers  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 8 | 16 | 24 | 32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dollars | 10 | 18 | 26 | 34 |

Students answer independently:  
1. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. How do you know?

After 3 minutes, students share with a partner. Then discuss as a class. Guide students to notice that Table A has a constant ratio, while Table B increases by the same amount but does not have a constant ratio.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify what information matters: ratios, unit rate, and whether the relationship begins at zero.  
- **Observe/assess:** Listen for precise explanations such as, “Table A is proportional because dollars divided by hours is always 8,” or “Table B is not proportional because the ratios are different.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: Four Ways to Represent Proportions — 10 minutes
Introduce the key idea:

A relationship is proportional if one quantity is always a constant multiple of the other. It can be written as:

**y = kx**

where **k** is the constant of proportionality or unit rate.

Use the example:

“A movie rental service charges $4 per movie.”

Represent it in four ways:

**Verbal:** The cost is $4 for each movie.  
**Table:**

| Movies | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cost | 0 | 4 | 8 | 12 | 16 |

**Equation:** y = 4x  
**Graph:** A straight line passing through the origin.

Emphasize that proportional graphs are straight lines through **(0, 0)**.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students record the same relationship in verbal, table, equation, and graph form.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check that students arrange information logically, moving from context to table to equation to graph.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Match — 12 minutes
Give students a handout with mixed cards or listed items: word situations, tables, graphs, and equations. Students work in pairs to match equivalent proportional relationships.

Example set:
- “A cyclist travels 12 miles per hour.”
- y = 12x
- Table with 1 → 12, 2 → 24, 3 → 36
- Graph of a line through the origin with slope 12

Include one non-example:
- A graph that is a line but does not pass through the origin
- A table with a constant difference but not a constant ratio

Pairs must justify each match.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check whether a table, graph, equation, and verbal description all represent the same relationship.  
- **Observe/assess:** Ask, “How do you know these match?” Look for students verifying with more than one representation, such as checking both the unit rate in the table and the slope of the graph.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence of proportionality.  
- **Observe/assess:** Students should explain, for example, “The equation y = 12x is strong evidence because it shows a constant multiplier, but I also checked the graph to make sure it goes through the origin.”

---

### 4. Group Task: Choosing the Better Deal — 15 minutes
Present a real-world problem:

Two stores sell notebooks.

- **Store A:** 5 notebooks cost $7.50  
- **Store B:** 8 notebooks cost $11.20  

Students work in groups of 3–4 to determine which store has the better price per notebook. They must represent both relationships in at least two ways: table, equation, graph, diagram, or verbal explanation.

Expected reasoning:
- Store A: 7.50 ÷ 5 = 1.50 per notebook, so y = 1.50x  
- Store B: 11.20 ÷ 8 = 1.40 per notebook, so y = 1.40x  
- Store B is cheaper per notebook.

Then ask: “Would your decision change if you only needed one notebook? What if you needed 100 notebooks?” Students discuss how the unit rate affects short-term and larger purchases.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare Store A and Store B using unit rate and consider different quantities.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for students comparing both options and explaining trade-offs, such as total cost versus cost per item.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students make a decision about the better deal and justify it mathematically.  
- **Observe/assess:** Check whether their conclusion follows from accurate calculations and representations. Strong reasoning includes statements like, “Store B is better because each notebook costs $1.40, which is less than $1.50.”

---

### 5. Share-Out and Error Analysis — 6 minutes
Display a sample incorrect student claim:

“Store A is the better deal because 7.50 is less than 11.20.”

Ask students:
- What is wrong with this reasoning?
- What should we compare instead?

Guide students to explain that total cost alone is misleading because the number of notebooks is different. Students should compare unit rates.

This reinforces that proportional reasoning requires comparing equivalent quantities.

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes
Students complete an exit ticket:

1. A table shows x-values 2, 4, 6 and y-values 5, 10, 15. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.  
2. Write an equation for the relationship.  
3. What is one strategy you used today to check whether a relationship was proportional?

Expected answer:
- Yes, because y ÷ x = 2.5 each time.  
- y = 2.5x  
- Strategy examples: checking for a constant ratio, finding unit rate, checking if the graph passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Target — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Target level:** 2  
- **Activity/moment:** Students describe the strategy they used to determine proportionality.  
- **Observe/assess:** Look for metacognitive statements such as, “At first I looked for a pattern, but then I checked the ratios to make sure they were constant.”

- Justify decisions using unit rate and constant of proportionality.

- **Source A (official bakery pricing sheet):** "3 cupcakes cost $6, 5 cost $10, 8 cost $16."
- **Source B (social media post):** "I think I paid $5 for a 2-mile taxi ride, but my friend paid $12 for 5 miles, plus a tip maybe?"

Ask: *"Which source gives us reliable information to test for a proportional relationship? Why?"* Students discuss with a shoulder partner for 2 minutes, then share out.

**🎯 Targets CRI1a (Clarifying Information):** Students must rewrite each verbal description into precise mathematical statements, clarifying independent and dependent variables (cost vs. quantity).
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language: "constant rate," "starts at zero," "ratio stays the same." Review students' written statements to ensure they identified variables without confusion.

**🎯 Targets CRI2b (Assessing Quality of Information):** Students evaluate the official pricing sheet against the vague social media post to determine which provides authoritative data.
**Assessment:** Listen for students who articulate that Source B is unreliable (uncertain values, possible tip) while Source A is authoritative and verifiable.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Modeling (10 minutes)
Using the bakery data (Source A), introduce the five representations:
- **Verbal:** "Cost is $2 times the number of cupcakes"
- **Table:** (1, $2), (3, $6), (5, $10), (8, $16)
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Equation:** y = 2x
- **Diagram:** Bar model showing equal $2 units

Emphasize that proportional graphs are straight lines through (0,0), and **k = 2** is the constant of proportionality.

**🎯 Targets CRI1b (Organizing Information):** Model a graphic organizer showing how each representation links to the others, with arrows labeling where k appears in each form.
**Assessment:** Collect notebooks at end of class; check whether graphic organizers logically link representations with consistent k values across forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice — Representation Match & Cross-Checking (10 minutes)
Provide students with mixed cards: word situations, tables, graphs, and equations (including one non-example, such as a line not passing through the origin, or a table with constant difference but not constant ratio). Pairs match equivalent proportional relationships and justify each match.

**🎯 Targets CRI2a (Assessing Validity):** Students cross-check whether a table, graph, equation, and verbal description all represent the same relationship by verifying k appears in each.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask probing questions: "How did you confirm this is proportional? Did you check more than one way?" Look for students who verify with multiple representations.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision-Making: The Field Trip Problem (15 minutes)
"Your class needs a bus. **Company A** charges $2 per mile. **Company B** charges $50 flat plus $1 per mile. **Company C** charges $4 per mile."

In groups of 4, students:
1. Create a table and graph for each company.
2. Identify which represent proportional relationships.
3. Recommend a company for a 30-mile trip AND a 100-mile trip.

**Twist:** "What if Company A added a one-time $20 booking fee? Is the relationship still proportional?"

**🎯 Targets CRI3a (Weighing Pros and Cons):** Groups systematically evaluate trade-offs across different trip lengths.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation. Look for systematic comparison: "For short trips, A is cheaper, but for trips over 50 miles, B becomes better because the flat fee is offset by the lower per-mile rate."

**🎯 Targets CRI4a (Applying Sound Reasoning):** Students must justify choices mathematically and recognize how the $20 fee makes the relationship non-proportional (line no longer passes through origin).
**Assessment:** Evaluate justifications for logical structure. Strong responses anticipate complexity: "Adding $20 means y = 2x + 20, which is not in the form y = kx, so it's not proportional." Flag faulty reasoning like "B is always cheapest."

---

### 5. Error Analysis & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
**Quick error analysis (3 min):** Display a flawed claim: *"Company C is the best deal because $4 is the smallest number listed."* Ask: "What's wrong with this reasoning?" Guide students to recognize the importance of comparing equivalent quantities.

**Exit ticket (7 min):**
1. A printer prints 8 pages per minute. Write the equation, draw the graph, and create a table.
2. Explain how you know it's proportional using **two** representations.
3. **Reflection:** *"How did your thinking about proportional relationships change today? What strategy will you use next time to check if a relationship is proportional? If you made an error, what would you do differently?"*

**🎯 Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on Thinking Processes):** Prompt #3 explicitly elicits metacognitive reflection.
**Assessment:** Level 2 responses identify specific shifts: "I used to just look for a pattern, but now I check that the graph passes through the origin AND that the ratios are constant." Level 1 responses are vague ("It was hard"). Track students needing reflective scaffolding.

---

### 6. Closing (2 minutes)
Quick whip-around: Each student shares one word describing proportional relationships. Preview tomorrow's lesson on unit rates.

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-filled tables; pair strategically.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional at first glance.
