## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Lesson Goal:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
3. Explain how different representations show the same proportional relationship.
4. Use reasoning to justify which representation is most helpful for a given situation.

### Materials

- Board or projector  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- “Representation Cards” handout  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Are Proportional? — 7 minutes

Display three situations:

1. A bike travels 12 miles in 1 hour, 24 miles in 2 hours, and 36 miles in 3 hours.  
2. A taxi costs $4 to start and $2 per mile.  
3. A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for 2 batches and 6 cups of flour for 4 batches.

Students work independently for 3 minutes, then discuss with a partner: “Which situations show proportional relationships? How do you know?”

Bring the class together. Emphasize that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as **y = kx**, where **k** is the constant of proportionality.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the important quantities in each situation and clarify what is being compared.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students saying things like, “I am comparing miles to hours,” or “The taxi has a starting fee, so it does not start at zero.” Students who precisely identify quantities and ratios show strong clarification.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Multiple Representations — 10 minutes

Use the example: **A store sells 5 notebooks for $8.**

Guide students through five representations:

**Verbal description:** Five notebooks cost $8, so the cost increases at a constant rate.  
**Table:**

| Notebooks | Cost |
|---|---|
| 5 | 8 |
| 10 | 16 |
| 15 | 24 |
| 20 | 32 |

**Equation:** \( y = 1.6x \), where \(x\) is notebooks and \(y\) is cost.  
**Graph:** Plot points such as (5, 8), (10, 16), (15, 24), and show that the line goes through the origin.  
**Diagram:** Draw groups of 5 notebooks matched with $8 increments.

Ask: “How can we tell these all represent the same relationship?”

Students should notice that each representation shows the same constant rate: $1.60 per notebook.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students organize the same relationship into table, equation, graph, diagram, and words.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check whether students arrange information logically, label quantities correctly, and keep the same variables across representations.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Card Sort — 12 minutes

Give pairs a set of “Representation Cards.” Each set includes tables, equations, graphs, diagrams, and verbal descriptions. Some are proportional and some are not.

Students sort cards into two groups:

- Proportional relationships  
- Not proportional relationships  

Then they match cards that represent the same relationship.

Examples:

- Equation: \( y = 3x \)  
- Table: (1,3), (2,6), (3,9)  
- Verbal: “Each ticket costs $3.”  
- Graph: A straight line through the origin with points (1,3), (2,6), (3,9)

Include a non-example:

- Table: (1,4), (2,7), (3,10), which has a constant difference but not a constant ratio.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students verify whether representations truly match by cross-checking ratios, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask pairs, “What evidence proves these cards match?” Look for students checking more than one feature, such as constant ratio and whether the graph passes through the origin.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which details are most useful for proving proportionality, such as unit rate, origin, and equation form.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students distinguishing strong evidence from weak evidence. For example, “The numbers go up by the same amount” is not enough; “The ratio y/x is always 3” is stronger.

---

### 4. Group Task: Choose the Best Representation — 13 minutes

Give groups this scenario:

“A school is buying granola bars for a field trip. A pack of 6 bars costs $9.”

Each group must create all five representations: table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.

Then ask: “If you were explaining the price to a younger student, which representation would you choose and why?”

Groups discuss pros and cons. For example:

- A table is easy for exact values.
- A graph shows the overall pattern.
- An equation is efficient for any number of bars.
- A diagram helps visualize groups.
- A verbal description is simple but may not show all values.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare the usefulness of each representation for explaining the relationship.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students giving reasons beyond preference, such as “The equation is best if we need to find the cost of 50 bars.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students justify their chosen representation using mathematical reasoning.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask, “Why is that representation best for your purpose?” Strong responses connect the choice to the task, audience, and mathematical structure.

---

### 5. Whole-Class Share — 6 minutes

Invite two groups to present their representations. As they share, ask:

- “Where do we see the constant of proportionality?”
- “How does the graph show proportionality?”
- “How does the equation connect to the table?”
- “Does the diagram show equal groups?”

Correct misconceptions, especially confusing constant difference with constant ratio.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 7 minutes

Students complete the following independently:

1. A relationship is shown in the table:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 5 |
| 4 | 10 |
| 6 | 15 |
| 8 | 20 |

Is it proportional? Explain.

2. Write an equation for the relationship.

3. Describe how the graph would look.

4. Reflection: “What strategy helped you decide whether the relationship was proportional? What would you check first next time?”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on the strategy they used to recognize proportionality.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students naming a reasoning process, such as checking \( y/x \), finding the unit rate, or confirming the graph passes through the origin.

---

## Assessment

**Formative Assessment:**  
- Partner discussion during warm-up  
- Card sort explanations  
- Group representations  
- Teacher questioning during presentations  

**Exit Ticket Success Criteria:**  
Students should state that the table is proportional because \( \frac{y}{x} = 2.5 \) for each pair, write \( y = 2.5x \), and explain that the graph is a straight line through the origin.

## Differentiation

- **Support:** Provide sentence starters: “The relationship is proportional because…” and “The constant of proportionality is…”  
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create a non-proportional relationship and explain why it fails.  
- **Visual Learners:** Emphasize graphs and diagrams.  
- **Language Support:** Pre-teach vocabulary: proportional, ratio, unit rate, constant of proportionality, origin.
