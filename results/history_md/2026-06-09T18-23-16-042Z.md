## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

## Materials
- Graph paper, rulers, calculators
- Handout with mixed scenarios (some proportional, some not)
- Whiteboard, sticky notes for exit reflection

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner, then share. Record student ideas without confirming correctness yet.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means and identify the key feature — a constant ratio with zero starting value. 
- *Assessment:* Listen for precise language ("the ratio y/x is constant," "passes through the origin"). Use cold-call to push vague answers toward precision.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Using Scenario A, demonstrate all five representations:
- **Table:** (1, $3), (2, $6), (3, $9)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal units
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 3 times the number of smoothies."

Highlight the **constant of proportionality (k = 3)** across each representation. Then revisit Scenario B and show that its graph does NOT pass through origin → not proportional.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a graphic organizer with the five representations side-by-side, showing how each connects to the same relationship.
- *Assessment:* Collect organizers; check whether students arranged the five representations coherently and labeled k consistently across all forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Task (10 minutes)
Distribute a handout with 8 scenarios (mix of tables, equations, graphs, verbal descriptions). In pairs, students sort them into "Proportional" vs. "Not Proportional" piles and justify each decision in writing.

Include a "trap" item: a table that *looks* proportional at first glance (e.g., (2,5), (4,10), (6,16)) but breaks the ratio on the third entry.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity) and CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students must verify each ratio rather than trust the appearance of the data.
- *Assessment:* Review written justifications. Level 2 students cross-check every row/point; Level 1 students check only one or two. Look for the trap item — did they catch it?

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: "Best Deal" Activity (12 minutes)
Present three phone data plans:
- **Plan X:** $10 per GB (proportional)
- **Plan Y:** $15 flat fee + $5 per GB (not proportional)
- **Plan Z:** $8 per GB but $20 setup fee (not proportional)

Task: *"Which plan is the best deal for a customer who uses 4 GB per month? For 10 GB? Justify using at least two representations."*

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare trade-offs across usage levels. The "best" plan depends on usage — there's no single answer.
- *Assessment:* Look for students who explicitly state when each plan wins and acknowledge that the answer depends on usage. Level 2: addresses long-term implications (high vs. low users).

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must support claims with calculations, tables, or graphs.
- *Assessment:* Check written justifications for logical chains (e.g., "At 4 GB, Plan X = $40, Plan Y = $35, so Y wins; but at 10 GB, X = $100, Y = $65 — Y still wins. However, only Plan X is proportional."). Flag any unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Exit Reflection (8 minutes)
Students complete a "3-2-1 Reflection" on a sticky note:
- **3** things they now understand about proportional relationships
- **2** moments where their thinking changed during the lesson
- **1** question or area of confusion remaining

Prompt: *"Did you change your mind about any scenario today? What made you rethink it?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Students explicitly identify shifts in their reasoning.
- *Assessment:* Read sticky notes. Level 2 students describe a specific strategy adjustment (e.g., "I used to just look at the first two numbers, but now I check every ratio"). Level 1 students mention reasoning without depth. Use these reflections to inform tomorrow's opener.

---

### 6. Closing (5 minutes)
Quick whole-class share: 2–3 students read their reflections aloud. Teacher restates the core idea: **A proportional relationship has a constant ratio k and, when graphed, passes through the origin.** Preview tomorrow's lesson on computing unit rates from proportional graphs.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide pre-filled tables for struggling students; pair strategically.
- **Extension:** Ask advanced students to create their own "trap" scenario for classmates.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.) and represent it in all five forms.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proving Proportions: The Power of Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking skills to validate data, weigh mathematical choices, and reflect on their problem-solving processes.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Data Structuring (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world scenario. The teacher projects a verbal description: *"A local farm sells apples. A friend texted you saying they bought 3 pounds for $4.50, 5 pounds for $8.00, and 8 pounds for $12.00. However, the farm’s official website states apples are strictly $1.50 per pound, no matter how many you buy."* 

Students are asked to extract the exact quantities being compared (pounds of apples and total cost) and define the variables (x = pounds, y = cost). Next, students must arrange this raw verbal data into two separate T-charts (tables): one for the friend's text and one for the official website.

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must parse the verbal description, filtering out narrative fluff to precisely clarify the mathematical ideas (the specific x and y values) needed to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will circulate and listen to student pairs as they rephrase the problem to each other, assessing their ability to precisely identify and clarify the relevant variables and rates before doing any math.
*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students take the clarified verbal data and organize it into a structured T-chart, ensuring the independent and dependent variables are correctly placed.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will review students' T-charts to ensure they have arranged the information with a strong logical flow, correctly labeling the x-axis (pounds) and y-axis (cost) to set up future graphing.

### **Part 2: Guided Exploration and Data Validation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher introduces the concept of the constant of proportionality ($k = y/x$). Students are asked to determine which of the two data sets (the friend's text or the website) represents a true proportional relationship. They must evaluate the quality of the two information sources and then mathematically validate their conclusion by translating the website's table into a graph, a diagram (tape diagram), and an equation ($y = 1.5x$).

*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Before calculating, students discuss the reliability of the two data sources. They must recognize that the official farm website is a high-quality, authoritative source for pricing, whereas a friend's text message may contain typos or misunderstandings.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will assess students' verbal and written justifications during a class share-out, looking for sophisticated judgment in selecting the farm's official website as the authoritative source for building their mathematical model.
*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** To prove the website's pricing is truly proportional, students cannot rely on the table alone. They must rigorously cross-check the mathematical claim using multiple authoritative mathematical "sources" (representations): verifying that the graph forms a straight line through the origin, and that the equation $y = 1.5x$ holds true for all values.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will check students' whiteboards to ensure they are accurately cross-checking the constant of proportionality across the table, graph, and equation, rather than just assuming the relationship is proportional based on one representation.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Application and Decision Making (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: predicting the cost of 250 pounds of apples for a school pie-baking event. They must choose which representation (table, graph, diagram, or equation) is the best tool for solving this specific problem. They will discuss the benefits and drawbacks of each method, make a final choice, and solve the problem.

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Groups must systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each representation. For example, a graph provides a great visual but is terrible for finding the exact cost of 250 pounds; a table takes too long to extend to 250; an equation is abstract but highly efficient for large numbers.
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will listen to group deliberations, assessing whether students are systematically evaluating the trade-offs and long-term implications of using each mathematical tool before making their choice.
*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Based on their evaluation, students must make a definitive choice (likely the equation) and apply sound mathematical reasoning to calculate the final cost, anticipating complexities like ensuring they plug 250 into the correct variable ($x$).
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will collect the groups' final written solutions, evaluating them for sophisticated, logical reasoning that clearly justifies why their chosen representation was the most effective way to reach the correct conclusion of $375.

### **Part 4: Independent Reflection and Closure (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** Students complete an independent exit ticket featuring a tricky scenario: a gym membership that costs $10 a month plus a $50 sign-up fee. They must quickly graph it, determine it is *not* proportional (because it does not pass through the origin), and then write a reflective paragraph about their learning process today.

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** In the reflection paragraph, students must write about how their initial assumptions about proportionality have evolved. They are prompted to answer: *"Did you initially think the gym membership was proportional? How did using a graph change your thinking, and how will you strategically approach checking for proportionality in the future?"*
    *   *Observation/Assessment:* The teacher will read the exit tickets to assess metacognitive awareness. High-level responses will demonstrate deep self-awareness, noting how the student strategically adjusted their thinking (e.g., "I realized I can't just look at a monthly rate; I have to check if the graph starts at zero, so next time I will always check the y-intercept first").

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Setting:** Traditional classroom  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Student notebooks  
- Graph paper  
- Rulers  
- Task cards with tables, graphs, equations, and word problems  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

Ask students:  
1. What patterns do you notice?  
2. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
3. How do you know?

Students write individually, then share with a partner.

Emphasize that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio, or constant of proportionality, and can be written as **y = kx**.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Key Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes
Explain that proportional relationships can be recognized in multiple ways:

- **Table:** The ratio y/x is constant.
- **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin, point 0,0.
- **Equation:** The equation is in the form y = kx.
- **Verbal description:** One quantity is a constant multiple of the other.
- **Diagram:** Equivalent groups or scaling patterns show the same ratio.

Use the example:  
“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours.”

Create representations together:

**Table**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Miles | 6 | 12 | 18 | 24 |

**Equation:** m = 6h  
**Graph:** Points include 0,0, 1,6, 2,12, 3,18.  
**Verbal description:** The cyclist travels 6 miles per hour.  
**Diagram:** For every 1 hour, show a group of 6 miles.

Teacher models thinking aloud:  
“I know this is proportional because the ratio miles/hours is always 6, and the graph would pass through the origin.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Match — 12 minutes
Give each group a set of cards. Each set includes:
- One verbal description
- One table
- One equation
- One graph
- One diagram

Some cards match proportional relationships; others are distractors.

Example set:
- Verbal: “A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”
- Equation: f = 1.5s
- Table: sugar 2, 4, 6; flour 3, 6, 9
- Graph: line through 0,0 and 2,3
- Distractor equation: f = s + 3

Students must match the correct representations and explain why the distractor does not belong.

Teacher circulates and asks:
- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “How does the graph confirm your table?”
- “Which representation made this easiest to see?”

---

### 4. Critical Thinking Task: Choose the Best Representation — 15 minutes
Present this situation:

“Movie tickets cost $8 each.”

Students must create:
1. A table for 0–5 tickets  
2. A graph  
3. An equation  
4. A verbal description  
5. A simple diagram  

Then ask students to answer:

“Which representation best proves this relationship is proportional? Explain the strengths and limits of at least two representations.”

Students work in pairs, then share responses with the class.

Expected mathematical ideas:
- Equation: c = 8t
- Table: cost/tickets = 8
- Graph: straight line through origin
- Verbal: each ticket costs the same amount
- Diagram: groups of $8 for each ticket

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes
Students complete two problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A water faucet fills 5 gallons in 2 minutes. Is the relationship proportional? Create an equation and table.

**Problem 2:**  
A taxi charges $4 plus $2 per mile. Is the relationship proportional? Explain using an equation or graph.

Students should recognize:
- Problem 1 is proportional: g = 2.5m.
- Problem 2 is not proportional: c = 2m + 4, because it does not start at 0.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 4 minutes
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell from a table whether a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “How can you tell from a graph?”  
3. “What mistake did you almost make today, and how did you correct your thinking?”

Collect responses.

## Critical Thinking Subcompetency Connections

**Observe/assess:** Look for written explanations such as, “Table A is proportional because y divided by x is always 3,” or “The taxi relationship is not proportional because it has a starting fee.”

**Lesson moment:** Students create five representations of “Movie tickets cost $8 each.”

**Target level:** 2 — Cross-checks information using multiple reliable representations.  
**Lesson moment:** In the card match, students verify whether a table, graph, and equation describe the same relationship.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for students validating one representation with another, such as, “This graph matches the table because the point 2,3 is on the line and the equation gives f = 1.5s.”

### CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information  
**Target level:** 2 — Selects the most useful information for the mathematical purpose.  
**Lesson moment:** Students choose which representation best proves proportionality.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for judgment about usefulness, such as, “The equation is clearest because y = 8x immediately shows a constant multiplier, but the graph also helps because it goes through the origin.”

**Target level:** 1–2 — Compares options and explains trade-offs.  
**Lesson moment:** Students compare tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Observe/assess:** Evidence may include, “A table is good for checking ratios, but a graph makes it easier to see whether the relationship starts at 0.”

### CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making

**Lesson moment:** Independent practice asks students to decide whether each situation is proportional.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for reasoning such as, “The taxi problem is not proportional because c = 2m + 4, not c = km, and the graph would not pass through 0,0.”

### CRI5a: Reflecting on One’s Thinking Processes  
**Target level:** 1–2 — Reflects on reasoning and adjusts thinking.  
**Lesson moment:** Exit ticket reflection asks students to identify a mistake or near-mistake.  
**Observe/assess:** Evidence may include, “I first thought the taxi problem was proportional because it increased by 2 each mile, but I corrected myself because there was a $4 starting cost.”

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 3 | 6 | 9 | 12 |

**Table B**

| x | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| y | 4 | 7 | 10 | 13 |

1. What patterns do you notice?  
2. Which table shows a proportional relationship?  
3. How do you know?

Students write individually, then share with a partner.

Emphasize that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio, or constant of proportionality, and can be written as **y = kx**.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Key Features of Proportional Relationships — 10 minutes
Explain that proportional relationships can be recognized in multiple ways:

- **Table:** The ratio y/x is constant.
- **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin, point 0,0.
- **Equation:** The equation is in the form y = kx.
- **Verbal description:** One quantity is a constant multiple of the other.
- **Diagram:** Equivalent groups or scaling patterns show the same ratio.

Use the example:  
“A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours.”

Create representations together:

**Table**

| Hours | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Miles | 6 | 12 | 18 | 24 |

**Equation:** m = 6h  
**Graph:** Points include 0,0, 1,6, 2,12, 3,18.  
**Verbal description:** The cyclist travels 6 miles per hour.  
**Diagram:** For every 1 hour, show a group of 6 miles.

Teacher models thinking aloud:  
“I know this is proportional because the ratio miles/hours is always 6, and the graph would pass through the origin.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Match — 12 minutes
Give each group a set of cards. Each set includes:
- One verbal description
- One table
- One equation
- One graph
- One diagram

Some cards match proportional relationships; others are distractors.

Example set:
- Verbal: “A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”
- Equation: f = 1.5s
- Table: sugar 2, 4, 6; flour 3, 6, 9
- Graph: line through 0,0 and 2,3
- Distractor equation: f = s + 3

Students must match the correct representations and explain why the distractor does not belong.

Teacher circulates and asks:
- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “How does the graph confirm your table?”
- “Which representation made this easiest to see?”

### 4. Critical Thinking Task: Choose the Best Representation — 15 minutes
Present this situation:

“Movie tickets cost $8 each.”

Students must create:
1. A table for 0–5 tickets  
2. A graph  
3. An equation  
4. A verbal description  
5. A simple diagram  

Then ask students to answer:

“Which representation best proves this relationship is proportional? Explain the strengths and limits of at least two representations.”

Students work in pairs, then share responses with the class.

Expected mathematical ideas:
- Equation: c = 8t
- Table: cost/tickets = 8
- Graph: straight line through origin
- Verbal: each ticket costs the same amount
- Diagram: groups of $8 for each ticket

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes
Students complete two problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A water faucet fills 5 gallons in 2 minutes. Is the relationship proportional? Create an equation and table.

**Problem 2:**  
A taxi charges $4 plus $2 per mile. Is the relationship proportional? Explain using an equation or graph.

Students should recognize:
- Problem 1 is proportional: g = 2.5m.
- Problem 2 is not proportional: c = 2m + 4, because it does not start at 0.

1. “How can you tell from a table whether a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “How can you tell from a graph?”  
3. “What mistake did you almost make today, and how did you correct your thinking?”

Collect responses.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while applying critical thinking to validate data, weigh representational choices, and reflect on their reasoning.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, scenario handouts, sticky notes, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A smoothie shop charges $3 per smoothie."
- **Scenario B:** "A gym charges $20 to join plus $5 per visit."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with a partner, then share. Record student ideas without confirming correctness.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means — identifying the key features of a constant ratio and a zero starting value.
- *Assessment:* Listen for precise language ("the ratio y/x is constant," "passes through the origin"). Cold-call to push vague answers toward precision.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (10 minutes)
Using Scenario A, demonstrate all five representations:
- **Table:** (1, $3), (2, $6), (3, $9)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal units
- **Verbal:** "Cost is 3 times the number of smoothies."

Highlight the **constant of proportionality (k = 3)** across each representation. Then revisit Scenario B: its graph does NOT pass through the origin → not proportional.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students complete a graphic organizer with the five representations side-by-side, showing how each connects to the same relationship.
- *Assessment:* Collect organizers; check whether students arranged representations coherently and labeled k consistently across all forms.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Sorting Task with Source Check (10 minutes)
Present a scenario: *"A friend texts you that 3 lbs of apples cost $4.50, 5 lbs cost $8.00, and 8 lbs cost $12.00. The farm's official website says apples are $1.50/lb, no matter the quantity."* Then distribute a handout with 6 additional mixed scenarios (tables, equations, verbal descriptions). In pairs, students sort each into "Proportional" vs. "Not Proportional" and justify in writing.

Include a **trap item:** a table that *looks* proportional — (2, 5), (4, 10), (6, 16) — but breaks the ratio.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students discuss which source — the friend's text or the official website — is more authoritative for building a mathematical model.
- *Assessment:* Look for sophisticated judgment, e.g., "The website is the authoritative source; the text data has inconsistent ratios."

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Students must cross-check every ratio rather than trusting appearances. They verify the website's claim by confirming the table, the equation y = 1.5x, and a graph through the origin all agree.
- *Assessment:* Review justifications. Level 2 students verify every row/point and catch the trap.

---

### 4. Decision-Making Task: "Best Deal" Activity (15 minutes)
Present three phone data plans:
- **Plan X:** $10 per GB (proportional)
- **Plan Y:** $15 flat fee + $5 per GB
- **Plan Z:** $8 per GB + $20 setup fee

Task: *"Which plan is the best deal for a customer using 4 GB? 10 GB? Justify using at least two representations, and explain which representation was most useful."*

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students compare trade-offs across usage levels AND across representations (table vs. graph vs. equation). The "best" plan depends on usage — there is no single answer.
- *Assessment:* Look for explicit comparisons of when each plan wins, and statements like, "A graph shows which plan is cheaper visually, but an equation is faster for exact values."

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students must support claims with calculations from multiple representations.
- *Assessment:* Check written justifications for logical chains (e.g., "At 4 GB, Plan X = $40, Plan Y = $35, so Y wins; at 10 GB, X = $100, Y = $65 — Y still wins. However, only Plan X is proportional because it has no flat fee."). Flag unsupported claims.

---

### 5. Exit Reflection (8 minutes)
Students complete a "3-2-1 Reflection" on a sticky note:
- **3** things they now understand about proportional relationships
- **2** moments where their thinking changed during the lesson
- **1** lingering question

Prompt: *"Did you change your mind about any scenario today? What strategy will you use next time to check whether a relationship is proportional?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking processes):** Students identify shifts in reasoning.
- *Assessment:* Level 2 students describe specific strategy adjustments (e.g., "I used to just look at the first two numbers, but now I check every ratio AND look for a y-intercept of zero"). Level 1 students mention reasoning without depth.

---

### 6. Closing (5 minutes)
Two students read reflections aloud. Teacher restates: **A proportional relationship has a constant ratio k and, when graphed, passes through the origin.** Preview tomorrow's lesson on unit rates from proportional graphs.

- **Support:** Pre-filled tables; strategic pairing.
- **Extension:** Advanced students design their own "trap" scenario for classmates.
