## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Are Worth Fighting For

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Materials:** Whiteboard, sticky notes (2 per student), lined paper, pencils

---

## HOOK — *What Makes You Itch?* (8 minutes)

Walk in and write one question on the board before students arrive:

**"What's something you've always wondered about but never looked up?"**

Give students 90 seconds to write their answer on a sticky note — no sharing yet. Collect them, shuffle, and read five aloud randomly. Don't answer any of them. Just let them hang in the air.

Tell students: *"Every single one of those questions is an argument waiting to happen."*

Ask: *"Has anyone ever been told their question was weird, or off-topic, or that it didn't matter?"* Let two or three students respond briefly.

Transition: *"Today we're going to learn why curiosity isn't just a feeling — it's something worth defending."*

---

## DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *Building the Argument* (12 minutes)

Tell students that today they'll practice **argumentative writing**, which means staking a claim and backing it up with logic and evidence.

Write this claim on the board:

> **"Curiosity is one of the most important habits a person can develop."**

Walk through the four components of a strong argument:

| Component | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Claim** | Your position | Curiosity is essential to growth |
| **Reason** | Why it's true | Curious people ask better questions |
| **Evidence** | Proof/support | Studies show curious students retain more information |
| **Counterclaim** | The other side | Some argue curiosity wastes time or causes distraction |

Emphasize: *"A strong argument doesn't ignore the other side — it faces it and responds to it. That takes confidence AND curiosity."*

Model one paragraph aloud, thinking out loud as you write it on the board. Show students how you acknowledge the counterclaim before dismissing it with a rebuttal.

---

## GUIDED PRACTICE — *Curiosity on Trial* (15 minutes)

Divide the class into groups of four. Each group receives one of the following **debate scenarios** printed on a slip of paper:

1. A student asks why the school day starts so early. The principal says: *"That's just how it's always been."*
2. A student wants to study an event not in the textbook. The teacher says: *"Stick to the curriculum."*
3. A student questions whether a historical figure was actually a hero. A classmate says: *"Everyone knows they were great."*

Each group must identify:
- What question is being asked?
- Who is shutting it down and why?
- What argument could the curious student make?
- What's one counterargument they'd need to address?

Groups have 8 minutes to discuss and jot notes. Then each group shares their scenario and one strong argument point with the class. Teacher provides quick feedback — specifically praising any student who identifies a counterclaim naturally.

---

## INDEPENDENT WRITING — *Your Argument, Your Question* (15 minutes)

Students return to their sticky note from the opening.

**Prompt:** Using their original question as inspiration, students write a short argument (one to two paragraphs) defending WHY that question deserves to be explored. They must include:

- ✅ A clear claim
- ✅ At least one reason with evidence or example
- ✅ One counterclaim and a rebuttal
- ✅ A concluding sentence

Remind them: *"You're not just answering the question — you're arguing that the question itself matters."*

Circulate the room. Prompt struggling students with: *"Why would someone disagree with you? Now, why are they wrong?"*

---

## CLOSING — *The Curiosity Pledge* (5 minutes)

Bring the class back together. Ask two or three volunteers to read their arguments aloud.

After each one, ask the class: *"Did they convince you? What made it strong?"*

Close with this idea: *"Curiosity isn't passive. It's not just wondering — it's being willing to stand behind your questions, defend them, and dig until you find something real."*

Final exit ticket — students write on the back of their paper:

> *"One question I'm going to keep asking, even if it's inconvenient, is _______________."*

Collect writing samples for formative assessment.

---

**Assessment Focus:** Look for clear claim structure, presence of counterclaim, and logical organization — all aligned to CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1. Use exit tickets to gauge student engagement with curiosity as a habit of mind.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Compelling Argument

**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade

**Learning Objectives:**
*   Students will understand that curiosity is a driving force behind effective argumentative writing.
*   Students will practice generating curiosity-driven questions about a given topic.
*   Students will begin to formulate claims based on their curiosity and identify potential evidence and opposing viewpoints.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers or pens
*   Handout: "The Great Debate: Should [Topic]?" (e.g., "Should students be allowed to use cell phones during lunch?")
*   Small slips of paper for exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Hook: Sparking Curiosity (7 minutes)**

1.  **Display a Mystery (3 minutes):** Project an intriguing image or a short, silent video clip of something mysterious or unexplained (e.g., an optical illusion, a strange historical artifact, a puzzling news headline, or a "What is this?" object).
2.  **"What Do You Notice? What Do You Wonder?" (4 minutes):** Ask students to silently observe for 30 seconds. Then, have them quickly jot down 2-3 things they *notice* and 2-3 *questions* they have about the image/video. Invite a few students to share their questions. Emphasize the natural human tendency to ask "why?" and "how?" when faced with something new or unclear. Introduce this feeling as "curiosity."

**II. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (8 minutes)**

1.  **Define Curiosity (3 minutes):** Ask students: "What does it mean to be curious?" Guide them to understand it as a strong desire to know or learn something. Explain that curiosity isn't just for mysteries; it's essential for understanding the world around us.
2.  **The Argumentative Link (5 minutes):** Explain that strong arguments don't just appear; they are born from curiosity.
    *   "To write an argument (W.7.1), you need a *claim* – something you believe to be true. How do you figure out what to claim? By being curious enough to investigate!"
    *   "You need *reasons* and *evidence*. How do you find those? By being curious enough to ask questions and research!"
    *   "You need to acknowledge *alternate or opposing claims*. How do you understand other viewpoints? By being curious enough to consider perspectives different from your own!"
    *   Summarize: "Curiosity is the engine that drives a good argument."

**III. Activity: Generating Curiosity Questions (15 minutes)**

1.  **Introduce the Debate Topic (2 minutes):** Distribute the "The Great Debate" handout. The topic should be relevant and debatable for middle schoolers (e.g., "Should students be allowed to use cell phones during lunch?").
2.  **Small Group Brainstorm (10 minutes):** Divide students into small groups (3-4 students). Instruct them to brainstorm and write down as many "curiosity questions" as they can about the topic.
    *   Encourage open-ended questions that start with "Why," "How," "What if," "What are the effects of," "Who benefits/loses," "What do experts say," "What are the different perspectives?"
    *   *Teacher circulates, prompting with questions like:* "What are you curious about regarding the rules?" "What are you curious about regarding student behavior?" "What are you curious about regarding the school's perspective?"
3.  **Share a Few (3 minutes):** Ask each group to share 1-2 of their most interesting curiosity questions with the class. Write a few on the board.

**IV. Activity: From Curiosity to Claim (20 minutes)**

1.  **Modeling: Question to Claim (5 minutes):**
    *   Choose one of the shared curiosity questions from the board (e.g., "How do cell phones impact student social interaction during lunch?").
    *   Model how to turn this question into a *claim* (a statement that can be argued).
        *   *Example Claim:* "Allowing cell phones during lunch negatively impacts student social interaction."
    *   Briefly discuss what kind of *evidence* you'd need to support this claim, driven by the initial curiosity (e.g., "I'd need to look for studies on screen time and social skills, or observe students during lunch.").
2.  **Modeling: Acknowledging Opposing Claims (5 minutes):**
    *   Ask: "If someone disagreed with this claim, what would *they* be curious about? What questions would *they* ask?"
    *   Guide students to think about the opposite perspective.
        *   *Example Opposing Claim:* "Allowing cell phones during lunch provides students with important communication tools and a sense of independence."
    *   Explain that being curious about other viewpoints makes your own argument stronger because you can address them.
3.  **Individual/Pair Practice (10 minutes):**
    *   Instruct students to choose one of their own curiosity questions from Activity III.
    *   On their handout or a separate piece of paper, they should:
        *   Turn their curiosity question into a preliminary *claim*.
        *   List 2-3 types of *evidence* they would need to find to support that claim (e.g., "statistics," "expert opinions," "student surveys," "personal anecdotes").
        *   Formulate one potential *opposing claim* that someone might make.
    *   *Teacher circulates, providing feedback and guidance.*

**V. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**

1.  **Review (2 minutes):** Briefly reiterate how curiosity is the starting point for strong argumentative writing – it helps us ask questions, find evidence, and understand different perspectives.
2.  **Exit Ticket (3 minutes):** Distribute small slips of paper. Ask students to answer one of the following questions:
    *   "How does being curious help you write a better argument?"
    *   "Write one curiosity question about a topic you care about, and then turn it into a potential claim."
    *   Collect slips as students leave.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity Through Argument Writing**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
Students will understand the value of curiosity and learn how to write a clear, well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim about curiosity, using relevant reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)  
- Chart paper or projector for brainstorming  
- Timer or clock  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *What does curiosity mean to you?*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Share a brief story or example illustrating curiosity (e.g., a famous inventor or scientist whose curiosity led to a discovery).  
- Explain that curiosity is a powerful tool that helps us learn, solve problems, and grow.  
- Emphasize that today’s lesson will focus on how to express why curiosity is important through writing an argument.

**2. Understanding Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Review the components of an argumentative paragraph:  
  - A clear claim (your main point)  
  - Reasons that support the claim  
  - Evidence or examples that back up the reasons  
  - Acknowledgment of opposing or alternate views  
  - Logical organization of ideas  
- Display a simple example paragraph about curiosity (e.g., “Curiosity is important because it helps us learn new things. Some people think curiosity can be distracting, but when used wisely, it leads to success.”)  
- Discuss how the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing view are presented.

**3. Brainstorming Session (10 minutes)**  
- Ask students to brainstorm reasons why curiosity is important. Write ideas on the board or chart paper. Examples might include:  
  - Curiosity helps us discover new things.  
  - It encourages creativity and problem-solving.  
  - It motivates us to ask questions and seek answers.  
  - Some people say curiosity can be risky or distracting.  
- Encourage students to think of examples or evidence for each reason (e.g., a personal experience, a historical example, or a scientific fact).

**4. Writing Activity (15 minutes)**  
- Instruct students to write a short argumentative paragraph (5-7 sentences) supporting the claim: *“Curiosity is an important quality to have.”*  
- Remind them to:  
  - State their claim clearly.  
  - Include at least two reasons with supporting evidence.  
  - Acknowledge an opposing viewpoint and explain why their claim still holds.  
  - Organize their ideas logically.  
- Circulate the room to provide support and feedback as needed.

**5. Sharing and Reflection (8 minutes)**  
- Invite 3-4 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- After each reading, ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing view.  
- Discuss how curiosity helped them think about their writing and ideas.  
- Wrap up by reinforcing that curiosity not only helps us learn but also helps us become better thinkers and writers.

**6. Closing (2 minutes)**  
- Assign a quick exit ticket: Write one new thing you learned about curiosity today and one question you still have.  
- Collect exit tickets as students leave.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson combines teaching about curiosity with practicing argumentative writing. Students explore why curiosity matters, brainstorm supporting reasons and evidence, and write a clear argument that includes acknowledging opposing views. This approach meets the CCSS writing standard while encouraging a growth mindset centered on curiosity.
