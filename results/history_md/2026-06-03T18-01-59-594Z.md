## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** Proportional Relationships in the Real World: Analyzing Data Plans
**Grade Level:** Middle School (7th Grade)
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to recognize, represent, and analyze proportional relationships using tables, graphs, and equations, applying critical thinking to evaluate data sources and make mathematically sound decisions.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Clarifying Information (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces a real-world scenario: "You are choosing a new streaming data plan. Plan A advertises 'Only $4 per Gigabyte!' Plan B advertises '$10 to join, then $2 per Gigabyte!'." Students work in pairs to translate these verbal descriptions into precise mathematical ideas, identifying the starting costs and unit rates. 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must clarify the promotional language by defining the variables (x = gigabytes, y = total cost) and translating the verbal descriptions into precise mathematical statements to support deeper understanding of the relationships.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and listen to pair discussions, assessing whether students can precisely clarify that Plan A has a constant multiplier (unit rate) with no initial fee, whereas Plan B includes a base fee that complicates the ratio. 

### **Part 2: Organizing Data (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students are given a set of scattered, randomized data points representing the cost of Plan A and Plan B at different gigabyte usages (e.g., 3GB, 1GB, 5GB, 2GB). Students must arrange this raw data into a structured T-table and then plot the points on a coordinate graph.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students arrange the scattered data points in ascending order of gigabytes used to create a logical, sequential table, and then map this organized data onto a graph to reveal the visual trajectory of both plans.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the students' tables and graphs to ensure the information is arranged to strengthen logical flow. Mastery is observed when students independently order the x-values sequentially to easily identify the constant rate of change.

### **Part 3: Assessing Validity and Quality of Information (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces a complication. A promotional flyer from the telecom company claims "Plan B is always the most proportional and fair choice!" Students are tasked with verifying this claim. They are given three sources of information: the company’s promotional flyer, a single customer’s handwritten billing receipt, and a comprehensive data table from an independent, authoritative consumer advocacy website.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students rigorously cross-check the telecom company's claim by comparing the math in the promotional flyer against the independent consumer website's data and the customer receipt. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess students' written responses to see if they successfully cross-check the data, noting that the consumer website's data proves Plan B does not have a constant ratio of y/x, thereby invalidating the company's claim of proportionality.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students must rank the three sources (flyer, receipt, consumer website) based on their reliability for making a mathematical conclusion about proportionality.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group debates. Students demonstrate sophisticated judgment when they articulate that the consumer website is a high-quality, authoritative source because it provides comprehensive, unbiased data points, whereas the flyer is biased and the single receipt lacks the depth needed to prove a linear, proportional relationship.

### **Part 4: Weighing Options and Decision Making (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students must now write an equation for Plan A ($y = 4x$) and Plan B ($y = 2x + 10$). The teacher asks the class to predict the cost of using 150 GB of data. Students must decide whether it is better to use their graph, their table, or their equation to figure this out. Finally, they must make a final recommendation on which plan a consumer should choose.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of using a table, graph, or equation to predict large-scale future costs. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess class discussion. Students achieve the highest level when they explain that while a graph is great for quick visual comparisons, an equation is superior for long-term implications (like calculating 150 GB) because extending a graph that far is impractical and prone to error.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** Students write a final recommendation for a consumer, justifying which plan is proportional and which plan is the better financial choice based on usage.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect the written recommendations. Sophisticated reasoning is demonstrated when students anticipate complexities—concluding that Plan A is the only truly proportional relationship (passing through the origin), but Plan B actually becomes the cheaper option if the user consumes more than 5 GB of data.

### **Part 5: Closure and Metacognitive Reflection (5 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an Exit Ticket focusing on how their understanding of the problem evolved over the course of the lesson.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Activity/Moment:** The Exit Ticket asks: "At the beginning of class, which plan did you assume was better? How did organizing the data into graphs and equations, and evaluating the sources, change your thinking process?"
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the Exit Tickets to look for deep metacognitive awareness. Students demonstrate this by explicitly identifying their initial assumptions (e.g., "I thought Plan A was always better because it had no starting fee") and explaining how they strategically adjusted their thinking once they analyzed the mathematical evidence and cross-checked the data sources.

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships  
**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional by checking for a constant ratio.
2. Represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.
3. Explain their reasoning and evaluate different representations of the same relationship.

### Materials
- Whiteboard or projector  
- Graph paper  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Calculators, optional  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 7 minutes
Display two tables:

**Table A**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $8 |
| 2 | $16 |
| 3 | $24 |
| 4 | $32 |

**Table B**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $10 |
| 2 | $18 |
| 3 | $26 |
| 4 | $34 |

Ask students:  
“Which table shows a proportional relationship? How do you know?”

Students write an individual response, then briefly share with a partner.

**Teacher moves:**  
Guide students to notice that Table A has a constant ratio of 8 dollars per hour, while Table B does not.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students identify relevant quantities, units, and ratios in each table.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Listen for students clarifying, “The ratio is dollars per hour,” or “Each hour adds $8, so the constant of proportionality is 8.”  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Teacher may assign level -1 to 2 using student quotes from notebooks or discussion. For example, level 2 evidence: “I compared money divided by hours each time, and it stayed 8.”

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Explain that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio between two quantities. In seventh grade, students often describe this as:

\[
y = kx
\]

where \( k \) is the constant of proportionality.

Use Table A from the warm-up:

\[
\frac{8}{1}=8,\quad \frac{16}{2}=8,\quad \frac{24}{3}=8,\quad \frac{32}{4}=8
\]

So the equation is:

\[
y = 8x
\]

Then graph the ordered pairs:  
\((1,8), (2,16), (3,24), (4,32)\)

Ask: “What do you notice about the graph?”

Expected response: The points form a straight line through the origin.

Clarify that proportional relationships can be represented in:
- Tables
- Graphs
- Equations
- Diagrams
- Verbal descriptions

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students create a five-column organizer: table, graph, equation, diagram, verbal description.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether students organize the same proportional relationship consistently across all representations.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “The table, graph, and equation all show $8 per hour, so they match.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes
Present this scenario:

“A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.”

As a class, represent the relationship in five ways.

1. **Verbal:** 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
2. **Table:**

| Sugar | Flour |
|---|---|
| 2 | 3 |
| 4 | 6 |
| 6 | 9 |
| 8 | 12 |

3. **Ratio:** \(\frac{\text{flour}}{\text{sugar}} = \frac{3}{2}\)  
4. **Equation:** \(y = \frac{3}{2}x\), where \(x\) is cups of sugar and \(y\) is cups of flour.  
5. **Graph:** Points form a straight line through \((0,0)\).  

Ask students:  
“Why does the graph need to go through the origin for this to be proportional?”

Expected idea: If there is 0 sugar, there should be 0 flour.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students cross-check the table, graph, and equation to confirm they all show the same ratio.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Ask students to explain whether each representation is valid.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “The equation \(y=\frac{3}{2}x\) is valid because every table value gives flour divided by sugar as \(\frac{3}{2}\), and the graph goes through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence of proportionality.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Students justify whether the table, graph, or equation is most useful and why.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “The table is helpful for checking repeated ratios, but the equation is best because it shows the constant of proportionality directly.”

---

### 4. Partner Task: Choose the Better Deal — 12 minutes
Give students this problem:

“Two stores sell notebooks. Store A sells 4 notebooks for $6. Store B sells 7 notebooks for $10. Which store has the better price per notebook?”

Students work in pairs to:
1. Find the unit rate for each store.
2. Create a table for each store.
3. Write an equation for each store.
4. Decide which is the better deal.
5. Explain their reasoning.

Expected work:

Store A:

\[
\frac{6}{4}=1.50
\]

Store B:

\[
\frac{10}{7}\approx 1.43
\]

Store B is the better deal because it costs less per notebook.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students compare Store A and Store B using unit rates and explain trade-offs.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Look for students considering both options instead of choosing based only on total price.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “Store A has a lower total cost, but Store B has a lower cost per notebook, so Store B is better if I compare equal quantities.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students use mathematical reasoning to justify their final choice.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether their conclusion follows from accurate unit rates and proportional reasoning.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “I divided cost by notebooks for both stores because the number of notebooks was different. Since $1.43 is less than $1.50, Store B is cheaper per notebook.”

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 8 minutes
Students complete three problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours and 180 miles in 3 hours. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

**Problem 2:**  
A graph shows points \((0,0), (1,5), (2,10), (3,15)\). Write an equation.

**Problem 3:**  
A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 7 |
| 4 | 14 |
| 6 | 21 |
| 8 | 28 |

Is this proportional? If so, find the constant of proportionality.

Teacher circulates and asks probing questions:
- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “How do you know it stays the same?”
- “Can you represent that another way?”

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 6 minutes
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “Which representation helps you most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”  
3. “Describe one mistake you made or almost made today, and how you corrected your thinking.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Exit ticket reflection on strategies, mistakes, and corrections.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Review whether students can describe their own reasoning process.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “At first I compared the differences instead of the ratios. I fixed it by dividing y by x for every row to see if the ratio stayed constant.”

---

## Assessment Summary

Students demonstrate mastery if they can:
- Identify a constant ratio in a table.
- Recognize that a proportional graph is a straight line through the origin.
- Write an equation in the form \(y = kx\).
- Compare proportional relationships using unit rates.
- Explain their reasoning clearly in writing or discussion.

For each Critical Thinking subcompetency, the teacher should assign a level from -1 to 2 using direct evidence from student work or speech. Evidence may include written explanations, partner discussions, exit ticket responses, and completed representations.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/moment:** Students use mathematical reasoning to justify their final choice.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Check whether their conclusion follows from accurate unit rates and proportional reasoning.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “I divided cost by notebooks for both stores because the number of notebooks was different. Since $1.43 is less than $1.50, Store B is cheaper per notebook.”

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 8 minutes
Students complete three problems independently.

**Problem 1:**  
A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours and 180 miles in 3 hours. Is the relationship proportional? Explain.

**Problem 2:**  
A graph shows points \((0,0), (1,5), (2,10), (3,15)\). Write an equation.

**Problem 3:**  
A table shows:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 7 |
| 4 | 14 |
| 6 | 21 |
| 8 | 28 |

Is this proportional? If so, find the constant of proportionality.

Teacher circulates and asks probing questions:
- “What ratio are you comparing?”
- “How do you know it stays the same?”
- “Can you represent that another way?”

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 6 minutes
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “Which representation helps you most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”  
3. “Describe one mistake you made or almost made today, and how you corrected your thinking.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/moment:** Exit ticket reflection on strategies, mistakes, and corrections.  
- **Observation/assessment:** Review whether students can describe their own reasoning process.  
- **Level evidence to collect:** Level 2 example: “At first I compared the differences instead of the ratios. I fixed it by dividing y by x for every row to see if the ratio stayed constant.”

---

## Assessment Summary

Students demonstrate mastery if they can:
- Identify a constant ratio in a table.
- Recognize that a proportional graph is a straight line through the origin.
- Write an equation in the form \(y = kx\).
- Compare proportional relationships using unit rates.
- Explain their reasoning clearly in writing or discussion.

For each Critical Thinking subcompetency, the teacher should assign a level from -1 to 2 using direct evidence from student work or speech. Evidence may include written explanations, partner discussions, exit ticket responses, and completed representations.
