## Candidate 1
# The Curious Argument: Why Questions Are Your Most Powerful Tool

### A 55-Minute Middle School ELA Lesson

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA / Writing
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Central Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Argumentative Writing
**Curiosity Focus:** Students will discover that genuine curiosity — asking "why," "what if," and "what do others think?" — is the engine that drives compelling argumentative writing.

**Essential Question:** *What happens to an argument when the writer is truly curious about the topic and about people who disagree?*

**Learning Objectives:** By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Explain how curiosity strengthens argumentative writing
2. Generate a debatable claim rooted in genuine wondering
3. Identify and respectfully acknowledge a counterargument
4. Organize a brief argument using evidence and logical reasoning

**Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard or projector
- "Curiosity Cards" (index cards, one per student)
- "Argument Blueprint" graphic organizer (one per student, described below)
- Sticky notes (two colors per student — e.g., yellow and pink)
- A short mentor text (provided within the lesson)
- Timer

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 1: THE HOOK — The Curiosity Experiment (0:00–0:08)

**[8 minutes]**

**Teacher Setup:**
Before students arrive, write the following single sentence on the board and cover it:

*"Middle school students should not have homework."*

---

**Opening Move (2 minutes):**

As students settle, the teacher says:

*"Before we do anything today, I want you to close your eyes for ten seconds. Think about the last time you were genuinely, deeply curious about something — maybe something you Googled at midnight, something you argued about with a friend or family member, something that made you think: 'Wait, but WHY is that true?' Hold onto that feeling. Open your eyes."*

Give students a moment to return, then ask two or three volunteers to share what they thought of. Respond warmly and with genuine interest to each answer.

---

**The Reveal (3 minutes):**

Uncover the board. Read the sentence aloud:

*"Middle school students should not have homework."*

Ask: *"What's your gut reaction — agree or disagree?"*

Take a quick show of hands. Then ask:

*"Now here's the more interesting question: What are you CURIOUS about in this statement? What do you want to know more about before you decide? What questions pop into your head?"*

Give students 60 seconds to jot questions on their Curiosity Cards (index cards). Collect a sampling of responses and read several aloud. Examples students might generate:
- *What kind of homework? All homework or busy work?*
- *What does research actually say about homework and learning?*
- *Does it depend on the student's home situation?*
- *What do teachers think? What do parents think?*

---

**The Bridge (3 minutes):**

The teacher says:

*"Look at what just happened. The moment you got curious — the moment you started asking questions — your thinking got richer, more complex, and more interesting. That is EXACTLY what great argumentative writers do. Today, we're going to learn that curiosity isn't just a personality trait. It's a writing strategy. And the more curious you are, the stronger your argument becomes."*

Write on the board: **CURIOUS WRITERS ASK BETTER QUESTIONS → BETTER QUESTIONS BUILD BETTER ARGUMENTS**

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — What Makes an Argument Curious? (0:08–0:18)

**[10 minutes]**

---

**Mini-Lecture with Mentor Text (6 minutes):**

Distribute or project the following short mentor text. Read it aloud while students follow along.

---

**MENTOR TEXT — Student Essay Excerpt A (The Uncurious Version):**

*"Students should be allowed to use their phones in school. Phones are useful and everyone has one. Students can use them to look up information. Therefore, schools should allow phones."*

---

**MENTOR TEXT — Student Essay Excerpt B (The Curious Version):**

*"Should students be allowed to use their phones during the school day? At first, the answer seems obvious — phones are powerful learning tools that can access virtually any information within seconds. However, I started wondering: if phones are so helpful, why do so many teachers and researchers argue they hurt learning? After looking into it, I found that a 2015 study from the London School of Economics showed that test scores improved significantly in schools that banned phones — particularly for lower-achieving students. This made me rethink my position. While I still believe phones have educational value, I now think schools should create specific phone-use policies rather than an all-or-nothing approach. Those who disagree with any phone restrictions often argue that teaching digital responsibility is equally important — and they have a point. But a clear, structured policy can achieve both goals: protecting focus while building responsible habits."*

---

The teacher says:

*"Let's compare these two. What do you notice?"*

Facilitate a brief discussion. Draw out the following observations and write them on the board:

| **Uncurious Argument** | **Curious Argument** |
|---|---|
| States an opinion and stops | Wonders WHY the question is hard |
| No evidence | Seeks and cites real evidence |
| Ignores people who disagree | Asks what the other side thinks — and takes it seriously |
| Sounds rushed | Sounds like a thinker |

---

**The Three Curiosity Questions (4 minutes):**

Tell students:

*"Curious writers ask three questions before and while they write. I call these the Three Curiosity Questions, and they match perfectly with what CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 asks you to do."*

Write on the board and have students copy into their notes:

---

**THE THREE CURIOSITY QUESTIONS:**

🔍 **1. "What do I ACTUALLY know — and what am I assuming?"**
*(This drives you toward real evidence, not just gut feelings)*

🔍 **2. "Why would a smart, reasonable person disagree with me?"**
*(This drives you toward acknowledging counterarguments)*

🔍 **3. "What's the most INTERESTING or surprising thing about this topic?"**
*(This drives you toward original thinking and specific details)*

---

Say: *"These three questions are going to be your writing tools for the rest of the period — and for the rest of your life as a writer and thinker."*

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 3: GUIDED PRACTICE — Unpacking a Claim Together (0:18–0:30)

**[12 minutes]**

---

**Whole-Class Practice (12 minutes):**

Tell students: *"We're going to practice building a curious argument together before you do it on your own."*

Write a new claim on the board:

**"Schools should have a four-day school week."**

---

**Step 1 — Apply Curiosity Question #1 (3 minutes):**

Ask: *"What do we ACTUALLY know about this — and what might we be assuming?"*

Think-pair-share. Students talk to a neighbor for 90 seconds, then share with the class. The teacher records student responses on the board. The class should distinguish between:
- Things that feel true but need evidence (e.g., "kids would be more rested" — *is there proof?*)
- Things they know from experience (e.g., "we already get tired by Friday")
- Things that would require research (e.g., "how does it affect academic performance?")

Point out: *"A curious writer immediately notices the gap between what they feel and what they can actually prove. That gap is where research lives."*

---

**Step 2 — Apply Curiosity Question #2 (4 minutes):**

Ask: *"Why would a smart, reasonable person disagree with a four-day school week?"*

Give students 60 seconds to write at least two counterarguments on their PINK sticky notes.

Share out. Record counterarguments on the board. Examples:
- Parents who work five days can't arrange childcare
- Less instructional time could hurt academic achievement
- Some students rely on school for meals and safety

Then ask: *"Which of these counterarguments is the STRONGEST? The one that actually makes you think twice?"*

Students vote. Circle the strongest one.

Say: *"Notice what just happened. You just became more curious about the people who disagree with you. You stopped seeing them as wrong and started seeing them as people with real concerns. That is what makes an argument PERSUASIVE — when it shows the reader you've thought about their side, too."*

---

**Step 3 — Apply Curiosity Question #3 (3 minutes):**

Ask: *"What's the most interesting or surprising angle here?"*

Give students 45 seconds to write on their YELLOW sticky notes.

Share out. The teacher validates and elevates the most original responses. Example student ideas:
- *"The surprising thing is that some countries already do this and it works"*
- *"The interesting angle is that this affects poor kids and rich kids differently"*
- *"I'm curious whether teachers want this or if we're assuming they do"*

Say: *"See how your arguments just got way more interesting the moment you chased what surprised you? Readers remember arguments that teach them something unexpected."*

---

**Build a Quick Outline Together (2 minutes):**

Using contributions from the class, quickly map a four-part outline on the board:

1. **Claim:** Schools should adopt a four-day school week
2. **Evidence:** *(student-generated: name a fact or statistic)*
3. **Counterargument + Response:** Some parents worry about childcare — BUT schools could offer optional Friday enrichment programs
4. **Logical Conclusion:** A four-day week, with structured support, would benefit students and staff

---

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 4: INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — Your Curious Argument (0:30–0:48)

**[18 minutes]**

---

**Distribute the Argument Blueprint graphic organizer.**

*(Teachers can create this as a simple one-page handout with the following sections:)*

---

**ARGUMENT BLUEPRINT**

**STEP 1 — My Claim:**

**STEP 2 — Curiosity Question #1**
What do I actually know? What evidence would I need?

*I know: ___________________________________________________*
*I would need to find out: ____________________________________*

**STEP 3 — Curiosity Question #2**
Why would a smart, reasonable person disagree?

*Someone who disagrees might say: ____________________________*
*I think this because they value: _______________________________*
*My response to their concern: ________________________________*

**STEP 4 — Curiosity Question #3**
What is the most interesting or surprising thing about this topic?

*The most interesting angle I could take is: ______________________*

**STEP 5 — My Mini-Argument (Write 4–6 sentences)**
Using your blueprint, write a short but complete argument in paragraph form. Include your claim, at least one piece of evidence, a counterargument, and your response to it.

**Topic Menu:**

- Should junk food be banned from school vending machines?
- Should competitive sports be required in PE class?
- Should schools start later in the morning?
- Should students be allowed to choose their own reading books?
- **Your own topic** *(must be approved in 30 seconds by the teacher)*

---

**Teacher's Role During Independent Practice:**

- Circulate continuously
- Crouch down to students' level when conferencing
- Ask curiosity-sparking questions rather than giving answers:
  - *"Who would be most affected by this? Have you wondered about them?"*
  - *"What's the strongest reason someone could give against your position?"*
  - *"What surprised you when you started thinking about this?"*
- If students are stuck on a claim, say: *"Finish this sentence: 'Most people think ______, but I think ______.' That's your claim."*

### ⏱️ SEGMENT 5: SHARE & REFLECT — The Curiosity Debrief (0:48–0:55)

**[7 minutes]**

---

**Pair Share (3 minutes):**

Students pair with someone who chose a DIFFERENT topic. Each person:
1. Reads their mini-argument aloud (60 seconds)
2. Their partner asks ONE question that starts with "I'm curious about..." (30 seconds)

Partners switch. This reinforces that curiosity continues even after writing — good arguments invite more questions.

---

**Whole-Class Debrief (3 minutes):**

Bring the group back together. Ask:

*"Raise your hand if writing felt different today than it usually does when you write an argument."*

Ask two or three students: *"What felt different?"*

Then pose the reflection question:

*"Here's something I want you to think about: Most people think of arguing as a way to WIN — to prove you're right and the other person is wrong. But what did you notice today? What does it mean to argue with curiosity instead of just certainty?"*

Take two or three responses. Guide students toward this idea:

**"Curious writers aren't trying to shut down the conversation. They're trying to open it up — and that's actually MORE persuasive, not less."**

---

**Exit Ticket (1 minute):**

On the back of their Curiosity Card from the opening, students complete this sentence:

*"Before today, I thought arguing meant ____________. Now I think it also means ____________."*

Collect as students exit.

### **Lesson Objectives:**
- Students will understand the importance of curiosity as a mindset for learning and inquiry.
- Students will practice writing an argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence.
- Students will learn to acknowledge and address opposing viewpoints in their writing.
- Students will organize their reasoning logically.

---

### **Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Projector or chart paper  
- Handout with a short prompt and graphic organizer for argument writing  
- Sample argumentative paragraph (printed or projected)

---

### **Lesson Outline:**

#### **1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Engage:** Begin by asking students:  
  *“What does curiosity mean to you? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new?”*  
- Write a few student responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain:  
  *Curiosity is the desire to learn, explore, and understand more about the world. It drives us to ask questions and seek answers.*  
- Share a quick story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie, Leonardo da Vinci) and how their curiosity led to important discoveries.  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science or history but also in writing and thinking critically.

---

#### **2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us think deeply about different ideas and viewpoints.  
- When writing an argument, being curious means:  
  - Asking questions about the topic  
  - Considering different sides of an issue  
  - Finding evidence to support your ideas  
- Introduce the day’s writing task:  
  *“We will write a short argumentative paragraph about whether curiosity is more important than knowledge.”*  
- Write the prompt on the board:  
  **“Is curiosity more important than knowledge? Write an argument supporting your claim, using reasons and evidence, and acknowledge an opposing viewpoint.”**

---

#### **3. Mini-Lesson on Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Review the structure of an argumentative paragraph:  
  1. **Claim:** Your main opinion or argument.  
  2. **Reason:** Why you believe your claim.  
  3. **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reason.  
  4. **Acknowledgment of Opposing Claim:** Recognize a different opinion.  
  5. **Rebuttal:** Explain why your claim still holds.  
- Show a sample paragraph on the board or projector.  
- Highlight each part and explain how curiosity helped the writer think about both sides.

---

#### **4. Guided Practice with Graphic Organizer (10 minutes)**  
- Distribute a graphic organizer with sections for:  
  - Claim  
  - Reason 1 + Evidence  
  - Opposing Claim  
  - Rebuttal  
- As a class, brainstorm ideas for the prompt:  
  - Claim example: Curiosity is more important than knowledge.  
  - Reason: Curiosity motivates us to keep learning.  
  - Evidence: Scientists ask questions that lead to new discoveries.  
  - Opposing claim: Some might say knowledge is more important because it is the foundation for learning.  
  - Rebuttal: But without curiosity, knowledge would not grow or improve.  
- Fill out the organizer together on the board.

---

#### **5. Independent Writing (15 minutes)**  
- Students write their own argumentative paragraph using the graphic organizer as a guide.  
- Encourage them to be curious by asking themselves questions about the topic and considering different viewpoints.  
- Circulate the room to provide support and feedback.

---

#### **6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class:  
  *“How did curiosity help you write your argument? Did thinking about the opposing side change your view or make your argument stronger?”*  
- Summarize key points:  
  - Curiosity helps us explore ideas deeply.  
  - Good arguments consider multiple perspectives.  
  - Writing with curiosity makes our thinking clearer and stronger.

---

### **Extension/Homework (Optional):**  
- Have students find a news article or story about a curious person or discovery and write a short summary explaining how curiosity played a role.
