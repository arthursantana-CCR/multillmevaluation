## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, identifying the constant of proportionality (k) in y = kx.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, "Smoothie Shop" handout, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "Maria earns $12 per hour babysitting."
- **Scenario B:** "Maria charges $10 plus $8 per hour."

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? How do you know?"* Students discuss with shoulder partners, then share. Guide them to articulate that proportional relationships pass through the origin and have a constant ratio.

**🎯 CRI1a — Clarifying information:** Students must clearly articulate the difference between the two scenarios.
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language ("constant ratio," "starts at zero"). Use a quick thumbs-up/down check to gauge clarity. Record student explanations on chart paper to flag vague vs. precise wording.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations (12 minutes)
Using the smoothie shop example ("3 smoothies cost $9"), model all five representations:
- **Verbal:** "Each smoothie costs $3."
- **Table:** Smoothies (1,2,3,4) → Cost (3,6,9,12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal units

Emphasize how each representation reveals the constant of proportionality (k = 3).

**🎯 CRI1b — Organizing information:** Students copy a graphic organizer with five columns, one per representation, building a logical bridge between forms.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers at lesson's end. Score based on whether entries flow logically (verbal → table → equation → graph → diagram) and reinforce one another.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: "Spot the Imposter" (10 minutes)
Present three tables and three equations. Students must determine which represent proportional relationships and which don't.

Example table A: (2,6), (4,12), (6,18) ✓
Example table B: (1,5), (2,9), (3,13) ✗

Students justify decisions by testing y/x ratios.

**🎯 CRI2a — Assessing validity of information:** Students must verify each claim by cross-checking ratios across multiple data points rather than assuming based on one pair.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you know that's proportional? Did you check more than one pair?"* Note students who only check one ratio (Level 1) vs. those who systematically verify all (Level 2).

**🎯 CRI2b — Assessing quality of information:** Ask students which representation gave them the most reliable evidence for their conclusion.
**Assessment:** Listen during share-out for students who can distinguish between strong evidence (multiple verified ratios) vs. weaker evidence (visual estimation).

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Smoothie Shop Decision (15 minutes)
**Scenario:** "You're opening a smoothie shop. Choose one of three pricing plans."
- Plan 1: $4 per smoothie (proportional)
- Plan 2: $2 base fee + $3 per smoothie (non-proportional)
- Plan 3: Buy 5 get 1 free at $5 each (non-proportional)

In groups of 3-4, students must:
1. Create a table, graph, and equation for each plan.
2. Identify which is truly proportional.
3. Recommend a plan with justification.

**🎯 CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons:** Students must compare all three plans, considering customer appeal, profit at different volumes, and mathematical simplicity.
**Assessment:** Review written justifications for evidence that students considered multiple factors (e.g., "Plan 2 earns more at low volume but Plan 1 is simpler"). Look for trade-off language.

**🎯 CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning:** Final recommendations must be supported by mathematical evidence drawn from the representations.
**Assessment:** Use a rubric checking: (a) Is the claim supported by data from tables/graphs? (b) Does reasoning anticipate edge cases (high vs. low sales)? (c) Are conclusions free of logical errors?

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection (11 minutes)

**Gallery Walk (5 min):** Groups post their work; peers leave one sticky note: "One thing this group proved clearly" or "One question I have."

**Exit Ticket Reflection (6 min):** Students complete:
1. Define a proportional relationship in your own words.
2. Identify whether y = 7x and y = 7x + 2 are proportional. Show your reasoning.
3. **Reflection prompt:** *"What was the trickiest part of today's lesson? Did your thinking change from the beginning of class to now? How?"*

**🎯 CRI5a — Reflecting on thinking processes:** The reflection prompt requires metacognition about how their understanding evolved.
**Assessment:** Score reflections on a 3-point scale. Level 2 responses describe a specific shift ("At first I thought any line was proportional, but now I know it must pass through zero"). Level 1 responses mention difficulty without reflection. Use these to plan re-teaching for next class.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Pre-filled tables; sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___").
- **Challenge:** Real-world data sets with decimals; ask students to design their own non-proportional "trap."

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.). Represent each in two formats.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Multiple Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and translate proportional relationships across verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations, while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, organize information, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Clarifying the Problem (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson begins with a real-world scenario displayed on the board: *"A local bakery claims their cookie pricing is perfectly proportional. A messy handwritten note from the baker says: 'Buy 2 get charged $3, 5 cost $7.50, I think 10 is $15, and a dozen is maybe $18. Wait, is 1 a dollar fifty?'"* Students must rewrite this confusing verbal description into a precise mathematical statement, identifying the unit rate and the variables (cookies and cost). 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1a (Clarifying information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students work in pairs to extract the relevant numbers from the baker’s messy note, filtering out the confusing language to state the relationship precisely: "The cost is $1.50 per cookie."
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will circulate and assess students' written statements. Mastery is observed when students precisely clarify the variables (independent = cookies, dependent = cost) and state the unit rate without the misleading fluff from the original prompt.

### **Part 2: Direct Instruction and Organizing Data (15 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The teacher formally defines the constant of proportionality ($k = y/x$) and demonstrates how to represent it using the equation $y = kx$. Students are then given a raw, randomized list of data points regarding a car's distance traveled over time. They must arrange this scattered data into a sequential input/output table and plot it on a coordinate plane.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI1b (Organizing information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students take the randomized data points and organize them into a structured T-table (ordered from least to greatest $x$-values) and then translate that table into a logically scaled coordinate graph.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will review the students' tables and graphs. Success is assessed by checking if the information is arranged with a logical flow—specifically, that the table is sequential, the graph's axes are properly labeled and scaled, and the visual representation clearly shows a straight line through the origin.

### **Part 3: Collaborative Stations – Evaluating and Reasoning (20 Minutes)**

Students break into small groups and rotate through three stations. 

**Station 1: The Cell Phone Plan (Validity and Quality of Sources)**
Students are presented with a scenario where a blogger claims that "DataPlanX is a proportional relationship and costs $5 per gigabyte." Students are given two sources to verify this: a random social media comment agreeing with the blogger, and the official DataPlanX corporate pricing sheet, which shows a $10 flat fee plus $3 per gigabyte. 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2b (Assessing quality of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must select which source to use to build their mathematical model, justifying why the corporate pricing sheet is a high-quality, authoritative source compared to the social media comment.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will read the groups' written justifications to ensure they demonstrate sophisticated judgment in selecting the official pricing sheet over the unverified comment.
*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Using the authoritative pricing sheet, students rigorously cross-check the blogger's claim. They calculate the ratios ($y/x$) for multiple data points to prove the relationship is actually non-proportional (due to the flat fee).
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe group discussions and check their math work. Mastery is shown when students use multiple data points from the authoritative source to mathematically invalidate the blogger's claim.

**Station 2: Choosing the Best Representation (Weighing Options)**
Students are given a proportional relationship: a streaming service downloads 4 megabytes of data per second. They must represent this as a graph and as an equation ($y = 4x$). They are then asked: *"If you need to find out exactly how much data is downloaded in 3,600 seconds, which representation is better to use, and why?"*

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs between using a graph versus an equation for large-scale predictions. 
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will assess the students' written responses. Students should note that while a graph provides a great visual summary, it is highly impractical and imprecise for large numbers, making the equation the superior choice for this specific long-term calculation.

**Station 3: The Proportionality Test (Decision Making)**
Students are given four different diagrams and graphs. Some are straight lines that do not pass through the origin, some are curves, and one is a straight line through $(0,0)$. 

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students must decide which graph represents a proportional relationship and write a justification defending their choice while explaining why the others fail the test.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will evaluate the logical flow of their justifications. Students must demonstrate sophisticated reasoning by explicitly stating the two required complexities for a proportional graph: it must be perfectly linear *and* it must pass through the origin.

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**

**Activity:** The lesson concludes with an exit ticket utilizing the "My Favorite Mistake" protocol. Students are asked to look back at their work from the stations or the warm-up, identify a moment where they were confused or made an error (e.g., swapping the $x$ and $y$ axes, or miscalculating the constant of proportionality), and explain how they fixed it.

*   **Targeted Subcompetency: CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes)**
    *   **Specific Activity:** Students write a brief reflection on their exit ticket detailing a specific error they made in their mathematical thinking today and the strategic adjustment they made to correct it.
    *   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will collect and read the exit tickets. High-level performance is assessed when a student demonstrates deep metacognitive awareness—for example, writing, "I initially thought the cell phone plan was proportional because the cost went up steadily, but I realized I needed to check the $y/x$ ratio for multiple points. Once I did, I adjusted my thinking because the ratios were not constant."

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships in multiple ways.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`.
3. Compare different representations of relationships and justify whether each is proportional.
4. Reflect on how they decided whether a relationship was proportional.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, equations, and verbal scenarios  
- Graph paper  
- Calculators, optional  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Same or Different?” — 7 minutes

Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A bike rental costs $4 per hour.  
**Situation B:** A bike rental costs $6 plus $4 per hour.

Ask students:

- What would the cost be for 1, 2, and 3 hours?
- Are these situations the same or different? Why?

Students complete a quick table for each situation.

Guide discussion toward the idea that Situation A grows by a constant ratio from hours to cost, while Situation B has an added starting fee. Clarify that proportional relationships must go through `(0, 0)` and have a constant ratio.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the important information in each situation: hourly rate, starting fee, hours, and total cost.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students precisely stating details such as, “Situation B is not proportional because the $6 fee is added before the hourly cost.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that two quantities are proportional if the ratio between them is constant. Write:

`y = kx`

where `k` is the constant of proportionality.

Use an example:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |
| 4 | 20 |

Ask students to find `cost ÷ hours`.

They should see:

`5 ÷ 1 = 5`, `10 ÷ 2 = 5`, `15 ÷ 3 = 5`, `20 ÷ 4 = 5`

So the relationship is proportional and the equation is:

`y = 5x`

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 16 |

Ask students to calculate the ratios. Since the ratios are not the same, this is not proportional.

Emphasize the three main tests:

1. **Table:** ratios are constant.  
2. **Graph:** line is straight and passes through the origin.  
3. **Equation:** can be written as `y = kx`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students record the three proportionality tests in a graphic organizer.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check whether students organize the tests clearly under table, graph, and equation categories.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Distribute a handout with four examples:

1. A table of cups of flour and number of batches.  
2. A graph of distance over time.  
3. An equation: `y = 3.5x`.  
4. A verbal description: “A taxi charges $2.50 per mile plus a $4 starting fee.”

Work through the first example together. For each representation, students answer:

- What are the two quantities?
- Is the relationship proportional?
- What evidence supports your answer?
- If proportional, what is the constant of proportionality?

For the graph, remind students that a proportional relationship must be a straight line through `(0, 0)`. For the taxi scenario, discuss why the starting fee means the relationship is not proportional, even though there is a constant mile rate.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students check whether a representation gives enough valid evidence to prove proportionality. For example, a graph must show the origin and be linear.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to explain what evidence they used and whether that evidence is sufficient.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence: table, graph, equation, or verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for reasoning such as, “The equation `y = 3.5x` is strong evidence because it is already in the form `y = kx`.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Choose the Best Representation — 12 minutes

Students work in pairs. Give each pair a scenario:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

Pairs must represent the relationship in at least three ways:

- Table  
- Graph  
- Equation  
- Verbal description  
- Diagram, such as groups of notebooks and total cost

Then pairs answer:

“Which representation makes the proportional relationship easiest to see? Why?”

Students should compare trade-offs. For example:

- A table makes the constant ratio easy to calculate.
- A graph makes the line through the origin easy to see.
- An equation is the most compact representation.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare representations and choose which one best shows proportionality.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students naming advantages and limitations of more than one representation.

---

### 5. Independent Practice: Proportional or Not? — 8 minutes

Students independently complete three problems:

**Problem 1:**  
| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

**Problem 2:**  
`y = 7x`

**Problem 3:**  
“A gym charges a $20 joining fee and $10 per month.”

For each, students decide whether the relationship is proportional and justify their answer using mathematical reasoning.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students must make a decision and justify it with evidence, such as constant ratios, equation form, or presence of an initial fee.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review written explanations for logical reasoning, not just correct answers.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students answer:

1. What is one way to tell if a table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. What is one way to tell if a graph shows a proportional relationship?  
3. Which representation helped you most today, and why?  
4. What mistake might someone make when deciding if a relationship is proportional?

Collect exit tickets.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on which strategy helped them and identify a possible misconception.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for responses showing awareness, such as, “I used to think any constant increase was proportional, but now I know the ratio must stay the same and the graph must go through the origin.”

---

## Assessment

**Formative assessment:** Teacher circulates during guided and partner practice, listening for correct use of terms such as ratio, constant of proportionality, origin, and `y = kx`.

**Exit ticket:** Used to determine whether students can identify proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.

## Differentiation

- **Support:** Provide sentence starters: “This is proportional because…” and “The constant of proportionality is…”  
- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional relationship that looks proportional at first and explain the trap.  
- **Visual learners:** Use color-coding on graphs and tables to connect `x`, `y`, and `k`.

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships in Multiple Representations

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Lesson Focus:** Students determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships in multiple ways.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:

1. Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
2. Explain that a proportional relationship has a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`.
3. Compare different representations of relationships and justify whether each is proportional.
4. Reflect on how they decided whether a relationship was proportional.

### Materials

- Whiteboard or projector  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, equations, and verbal scenarios  
- Graph paper  
- Calculators, optional  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Same or Different?” — 7 minutes

Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A bike rental costs $4 per hour.  
**Situation B:** A bike rental costs $6 plus $4 per hour.

Ask students:

- What would the cost be for 1, 2, and 3 hours?
- Are these situations the same or different? Why?

Students complete a quick table for each situation.

Guide discussion toward the idea that Situation A grows by a constant ratio from hours to cost, while Situation B has an added starting fee. Clarify that proportional relationships must go through `(0, 0)` and have a constant ratio.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students identify the important information in each situation: hourly rate, starting fee, hours, and total cost.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students precisely stating details such as, “Situation B is not proportional because the $6 fee is added before the hourly cost.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that two quantities are proportional if the ratio between them is constant. Write:

`y = kx`

where `k` is the constant of proportionality.

Use an example:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 10 |
| 3 | 15 |
| 4 | 20 |

Ask students to find `cost ÷ hours`.

They should see:

`5 ÷ 1 = 5`, `10 ÷ 2 = 5`, `15 ÷ 3 = 5`, `20 ÷ 4 = 5`

So the relationship is proportional and the equation is:

`y = 5x`

Then show a non-example:

| Hours | Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | 8 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 16 |

Ask students to calculate the ratios. Since the ratios are not the same, this is not proportional.

Emphasize the three main tests:

1. **Table:** ratios are constant.  
2. **Graph:** line is straight and passes through the origin.  
3. **Equation:** can be written as `y = kx`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students record the three proportionality tests in a graphic organizer.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check whether students organize the tests clearly under table, graph, and equation categories.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 13 minutes

Distribute a handout with four examples:

1. A table of cups of flour and number of batches.  
2. A graph of distance over time.  
3. An equation: `y = 3.5x`.  
4. A verbal description: “A taxi charges $2.50 per mile plus a $4 starting fee.”

Work through the first example together. For each representation, students answer:

- What are the two quantities?
- Is the relationship proportional?
- What evidence supports your answer?
- If proportional, what is the constant of proportionality?

For the graph, remind students that a proportional relationship must be a straight line through `(0, 0)`. For the taxi scenario, discuss why the starting fee means the relationship is not proportional, even though there is a constant mile rate.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students check whether a representation gives enough valid evidence to prove proportionality. For example, a graph must show the origin and be linear.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to explain what evidence they used and whether that evidence is sufficient.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students decide which representation gives the clearest evidence: table, graph, equation, or verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for reasoning such as, “The equation `y = 3.5x` is strong evidence because it is already in the form `y = kx`.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Choose the Best Representation — 12 minutes

Students work in pairs. Give each pair a scenario:

“A store sells notebooks for $2 each.”

Pairs must represent the relationship in at least three ways:

- Table  
- Graph  
- Equation  
- Verbal description  
- Diagram, such as groups of notebooks and total cost

Then pairs answer:

“Which representation makes the proportional relationship easiest to see? Why?”

Students should compare trade-offs. For example:

- A table makes the constant ratio easy to calculate.
- A graph makes the line through the origin easy to see.
- An equation is the most compact representation.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students compare representations and choose which one best shows proportionality.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for students naming advantages and limitations of more than one representation.

---

### 5. Independent Practice: Proportional or Not? — 8 minutes

Students independently complete three problems:

**Problem 1:**  
| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |

**Problem 2:**  
`y = 7x`

**Problem 3:**  
“A gym charges a $20 joining fee and $10 per month.”

For each, students decide whether the relationship is proportional and justify their answer using mathematical reasoning.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students must make a decision and justify it with evidence, such as constant ratios, equation form, or presence of an initial fee.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Review written explanations for logical reasoning, not just correct answers.

---

### 6. Exit Ticket and Reflection — 5 minutes

Students answer:

1. What is one way to tell if a table shows a proportional relationship?  
2. What is one way to tell if a graph shows a proportional relationship?  
3. Which representation helped you most today, and why?  
4. What mistake might someone make when deciding if a relationship is proportional?

Collect exit tickets.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Activity/Moment:** Students reflect on which strategy helped them and identify a possible misconception.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for responses showing awareness, such as, “I used to think any constant increase was proportional, but now I know the ratio must stay the same and the graph must go through the origin.”

**Formative assessment:** Teacher circulates during guided and partner practice, listening for correct use of terms such as ratio, constant of proportionality, origin, and `y = kx`.

**Exit ticket:** Used to determine whether students can identify proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, and verbal descriptions.

## Differentiation

- **Challenge:** Ask students to create their own non-proportional relationship that looks proportional at first and explain the trap.

## Learning Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, identifying the constant of proportionality (k) in y = kx.

## Materials

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook: The Baker's Messy Note (7 minutes)
Display a real-world scenario: *"A local bakery claims their cookie pricing is perfectly proportional. The baker's handwritten note says: 'Buy 2 get charged $3, 5 cost $7.50, I think 10 is $15, and a dozen is maybe $18. Wait, is 1 a dollar fifty?'"*

In pairs, students rewrite this confusing description into a precise mathematical statement, identifying the variables and unit rate, and then decide whether all four data points are consistent with one proportional relationship.

**🎯 CRI1a — Clarifying information:** Students filter the messy language to state precisely, "The cost is $1.50 per cookie; the dozen at $18 breaks the pattern."
**Assessment:** Circulate and read pair statements. Mastery is observed when students precisely identify the independent variable (cookies), dependent variable (cost), and unit rate, and flag the inconsistent data point. Use a thumbs-up/down check for clarity.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: Five Representations & the Three Tests (12 minutes)
Using a smoothie shop example ("3 smoothies cost $9"), model all five representations:
- **Verbal:** "Each smoothie costs $3."
- **Table:** Smoothies (1,2,3,4) → Cost (3,6,9,12)
- **Equation:** y = 3x
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal units

Then summarize the three proportionality tests:
1. **Table:** ratios y/x are constant.
2. **Graph:** straight line through (0,0).
3. **Equation:** can be written as y = kx.

Show a non-example (y = 3x + 2) and ask students to apply each test.

**🎯 CRI1b — Organizing information:** Students complete a graphic organizer with five columns (one per representation) plus a row for "How this test reveals k."
**Assessment:** Collect organizers at lesson's end. Score based on whether entries flow logically (verbal → table → equation → graph → diagram) and consistently identify k = 3.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Spot the Imposter & Source Check (12 minutes)

**Part A — Spot the Imposter (6 min):** Present three tables and three equations. Students determine which represent proportional relationships by testing y/x across multiple data points.

Example table A: (2,6), (4,12), (6,18) ✓
Example table B: (1,5), (2,9), (3,13) ✗

**🎯 CRI2a — Assessing validity of information:** Students must verify each claim by cross-checking ratios across multiple data points rather than assuming based on one pair.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you know? Did you check more than one pair?"* Note students who only check one ratio (Level 1) vs. those who systematically verify all (Level 2).

**Part B — Whose Source? (6 min):** Present a scenario: "A blogger claims 'DataPlanX is proportional at $5 per gigabyte.' A social media comment agrees. The official DataPlanX corporate pricing sheet, however, shows $10 flat fee + $3 per gigabyte." Students choose which source to trust and use the authoritative pricing sheet to mathematically disprove the blogger.

**🎯 CRI2b — Assessing quality of information:** Students justify why the corporate pricing sheet outweighs the blogger and social media comment.
**Assessment:** Read written justifications for sophisticated reasoning ("The corporate sheet is authoritative; the blogger and commenter are unverified"). Listen during share-out for students who distinguish strong vs. weak evidence.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Smoothie Shop Decision (15 minutes)
**Scenario:** "You're opening a smoothie shop. Choose one of three pricing plans."
- **Plan 1:** $4 per smoothie (proportional)
- **Plan 2:** $2 base fee + $3 per smoothie (non-proportional)
- **Plan 3:** Buy 5 get 1 free at $5 each (non-proportional)

In groups of 3–4, students must:
1. Create a table, graph, and equation for each plan.
2. Identify which plan is truly proportional.
3. Recommend one plan with full mathematical justification.

**🎯 CRI3a — Weighing pros and cons:** Students compare all three plans, considering customer appeal, profit at different volumes, and mathematical simplicity.
**Assessment:** Review written justifications for trade-off language (e.g., "Plan 2 earns more at low volume, but Plan 1 is simpler and truly proportional"). Look for evidence of multiple factors weighed.

**🎯 CRI4a — Applying sound reasoning:** Final recommendations must be supported by mathematical evidence drawn from the representations.
**Assessment:** Use a rubric checking: (a) Is the claim supported by data from tables/graphs/equations? (b) Does reasoning anticipate edge cases (high vs. low sales)? (c) Are conclusions free of logical errors such as confusing "constant increase" with "constant ratio"?

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection (9 minutes)

**Gallery Walk (3 min):** Groups post their work; peers leave one sticky note: "One thing this group proved clearly" or "One question I have."

**Exit Ticket — "My Favorite Mistake" (6 min):** Students complete:
1. Define a proportional relationship in your own words.
2. Are y = 7x and y = 7x + 2 both proportional? Justify.
3. **Reflection:** *"Describe one moment today when your thinking changed or you caught a mistake. What did you do to fix it?"*

**🎯 CRI5a — Reflecting on thinking processes:** The reflection prompt requires metacognition about how understanding evolved.
**Assessment:** Score reflections on a 3-point scale. Level 2 responses describe a specific shift with a strategic adjustment ("At first I thought any steady increase was proportional, but I realized I needed to check y/x across multiple points; once the ratios weren't equal, I changed my answer"). Level 1 responses mention difficulty without reflection. Use exit tickets to plan re-teaching.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Pre-filled tables; sentence stems ("This is proportional because ___"; "The constant of proportionality is ___").
- **Challenge:** Real-world decimal data sets; ask students to design their own non-proportional relationship that looks proportional at first glance.
- **Visual learners:** Color-code x, y, and k across the five representations.
