## Prompt
Generate a 55-minute lesson to teach middle school students curiosity, in addition to the following standard, in a traditional classroom setting:
CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.


---

## Generator
# The Curiosity Argument: Asking Better Questions to Write Better Arguments

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Setting:** Traditional Classroom

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Demonstrate **curiosity** by generating genuine questions about a complex topic before forming an opinion
2. Write a structured argument that includes a clear claim, logical reasoning, and relevant evidence
3. Acknowledge and engage with an opposing viewpoint
4. Understand why curious thinkers make stronger arguers

---

## Materials Needed

- Whiteboard and markers
- Student journals or lined paper
- Printed "Curiosity Cards" (one per student — see below)
- Printed "Argument Scaffold" handout (one per student — see below)
- Timer (visible to students)
- Optional: a single mysterious object placed on the front desk (e.g., a sealed envelope, an odd tool, a strange fruit)

---

## Classroom Setup

Desks arranged in traditional rows, but students will briefly turn to a partner during one activity. The mysterious object sits on the teacher's desk at the front, visible but unexplained as students enter.

---

## Lesson Outline

---

### **HOOK — The Object on the Desk**
**(0:00 – 0:05 | 5 minutes)**

As students enter and settle, they will notice the unusual object. Do **not** explain it. Let them wonder.

Once the bell rings, say:

> *"Before we do anything else — look at that object. You have sixty seconds. Write down every question you have about it. Not answers. Just questions."*

Set a 60-second timer. Students write silently.

After the timer, ask three or four students to share one question each. Write the questions on the board without commenting on whether they're "right."

Then reveal what the object is (or what's inside the envelope — perhaps a printed debate topic for today's lesson, like: *"Should schools have homework?"*).

**Say:**
> *"Notice what just happened. Before I told you what it was, your brain was working overtime. That feeling — that itch to know more — is called curiosity. And today, we're going to learn why curious people write the most powerful arguments."*

---

### **MINI-LESSON PART 1 — What Is Curiosity, and Why Does It Matter?**
**(0:05 – 0:12 | 7 minutes)**

Write on the board:

> **Curiosity** = asking questions before assuming you already know the answer.

Briefly discuss with students:

**Ask the class:**
- *"Has anyone ever gotten into an argument with someone and felt totally sure you were right — only to find out later you were missing some important information?"*

Let two or three students share briefly.

**Explain:**
> *"Most people, when they argue, start with their opinion and then look for facts to prove they're right. That's called confirmation bias — you only pay attention to the stuff that agrees with you. But curious people do something different. They ask questions first. They look at a topic from multiple angles. They even try to understand why someone might disagree with them. And that actually makes their arguments stronger, not weaker."*

Write on the board:

| **The Non-Curious Arguer** | **The Curious Arguer** |
|---|---|
| Starts with opinion | Starts with questions |
| Ignores opposing views | Investigates opposing views |
| Uses only confirming evidence | Uses the strongest evidence available |
| Sounds one-sided | Sounds thoughtful and credible |

**Say:**
> *"Today's writing standard — CCSS W.7.1 — asks you to write arguments that include clear claims, logical reasoning, AND opposing viewpoints. That last part? That's a curiosity skill. You can't acknowledge the other side if you've never been curious enough to understand it."*

---

### **MINI-LESSON PART 2 — The Anatomy of an Argument**
**(0:12 – 0:20 | 8 minutes)**

Hand out the **Argument Scaffold** (described below). Walk through each part briefly using a low-stakes example topic:

**Example Topic:** *"Should the school cafeteria offer a vegetarian option every day?"*

Model the structure on the board:

**1. CLAIM** *(What do I believe?)*
> *"The school cafeteria should offer a vegetarian option every single day."*

**2. REASON + EVIDENCE** *(Why? What proves it?)*
> *"Many students have dietary restrictions for medical, religious, or ethical reasons. According to a 2022 Gallup poll, about 5% of Americans identify as vegetarian — meaning in a school of 600 students, roughly 30 students may have no appropriate meal option on non-vegetarian days."*

**3. REASON + EVIDENCE** *(A second "why")*
> *"Additionally, plant-based meals are often less expensive to prepare, which could save the school district money that could be spent on other programs."*

**4. COUNTERCLAIM + REBUTTAL** *(What would someone who disagrees say, and how do I respond?)*
> *"Some might argue that vegetarian food is less popular and would go to waste. However, cafeterias could offer these options alongside other choices, ensuring that all students are served without increasing waste."*

**5. CONCLUSION** *(Bring it home)*
> *"Offering daily vegetarian options is a simple, cost-effective way to make sure every student can eat a nutritious school lunch."*

**Ask the class:**
> *"Where did curiosity show up in this argument?"*

Guide students to notice that **the counterclaim required the writer to genuinely ask: "Why might someone disagree with me?"** That's curiosity in action.

---

### **ACTIVITY 1 — Curiosity Cards: Question Before You Claim**
**(0:20 – 0:30 | 10 minutes)**

Hand out **Curiosity Cards**. Each card has a debate topic printed at the top and blank space below (see template below).

**Topic options** (assign one per student or let them choose):
- Should students be allowed to use their phones during lunch?
- Should gym class be required every day?
- Should schools eliminate letter grades?
- Should students have a say in choosing their teachers?
- Should junk food be banned from school fundraisers?

**Instructions (read aloud and written on board):**

> *Step 1: Read your topic.*
> *Step 2: Before you pick a side — write FIVE genuine questions you have about this topic. These should be questions you don't already know the answer to.*
> *Step 3: Circle the ONE question that you think is most important.*
> *Step 4: Now, based on thinking through those questions, pick your side and write one sentence: your CLAIM.*

Give students **7 minutes** to work silently.

With the remaining 2–3 minutes, ask two or three volunteers to share:
- What was one question they asked?
- How did asking that question affect which side they chose (or how confident they felt)?

**Debrief:**
> *"Notice: the question came first. The claim came second. That's the curious writer's process."*

---

### **ACTIVITY 2 — Drafting the Argument**
**(0:30 – 0:48 | 18 minutes)**

Students now use the **Argument Scaffold handout** to draft a short argument on their topic from the Curiosity Card.

**Say:**
> *"You've already done the hardest part — you asked good questions and you found your claim. Now fill in the rest of the scaffold. You don't need to write full paragraphs yet. You can use bullet points to get your ideas down. The goal is to hit all five parts: Claim, two Reasons with Evidence, one Counterclaim with Rebuttal, and a Conclusion."*

**Circulate the room** during this time. Offer targeted prompts:

- *"What evidence would make someone actually believe you?"*
- *"Who would disagree with you? Why would a reasonable person think differently?"*
- *"Is your rebuttal just saying 'they're wrong'? Or are you actually explaining why your evidence is stronger?"*
- *"You have two reasons — are they actually different reasons, or the same reason said twice?"*

With **3 minutes remaining**, give a heads-up: *"Start wrapping up your counterclaim and conclusion."*

---

### **ACTIVITY 3 — Pair Share: Stress-Test the Argument**
**(0:48 – 0:53 | 5 minutes)**

Students turn to a partner (or the person behind them).

**Instructions:**
> *"You are going to be your partner's most curious critic. Here's your job: Read their claim. Then ask them ONE question they haven't answered in their argument yet. That's it. One good question."*

Students exchange papers or read their claims aloud. Partners ask their question. Students jot the question down at the bottom of their scaffold to consider later.

**Debrief (1 minute):**
> *"Did anyone hear a question from their partner that made them think — 'oh, I should address that'? That's what a curious reader does. And a curious writer anticipates those questions before the reader even has to ask."*

---

### **CLOSING — The Curiosity-Argument Connection**
**(0:53 – 0:55 | 2 minutes)**

Bring students back to the whole group. Ask:

> *"Finish this sentence in your head: 'Before today, when I argued, I used to ________. Now I know I should _________ first.'"*

Ask two students to share aloud.

**Final message (say this directly and clearly):**

> *"The best writers — and the best thinkers — are curious before they are certain. Curiosity doesn't make you weak or wishy-washy. It makes your argument bulletproof, because you've already thought about every angle. Starting today, every time you sit down to write an argument, make yourself ask at least three questions before you write your claim. That habit will change how you write — and how you think."*

**Exit Ticket** (students hand in as they leave or leave on desk):
> Write one sentence: *"One question I want to keep thinking about from today's topic is ___________."*

## Differentiation

**For students who need support:**
- Provide a partially completed Argument Scaffold with sentence starters already filled in
- Allow students to choose from a shorter, two-option list of topics (fewer variables to consider)
- Pair with a stronger writer during Activity 3 who can model the "stress-test" question

**For advanced students:**
- Require two counterclaims with two separate rebuttals
- Ask them to evaluate the quality of their own evidence: *"Is this evidence strong? What would make it stronger?"*
- Challenge them to write a second short paragraph from the opposing side's perspective — then explain in writing why they still prefer their original claim

**For ELL students:**
- Provide a bilingual vocabulary card with key terms: *claim, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal, curiosity*
- Allow Curiosity Card questions to be written in their home language first, then translated
- Pair with a supportive partner for Activity 3

## Assessment

|---|---|

| **Claim** | Is it a debatable statement (not a fact)? |
| **Evidence** | Is it specific and relevant, not vague? |
| **Counterclaim** | Does the student fairly represent the other side, or dismiss it unfairly? |
| **Rebuttal** | Does it logically respond to the counterclaim, not just repeat the claim? |
| **Exit Ticket** | Does the question show continued engagement with the topic? |

---

## Teacher Notes

- The mysterious object at the opening is essential — it creates a felt experience of curiosity, not just a definition. Choose something genuinely ambiguous.
- Resist the urge to resolve the object's mystery too quickly. Let students sit with not knowing for a full minute.
- During Activity 2, the most common struggle will be the counterclaim. Students often write: *"Some people think I'm wrong."* Coach them toward specificity: *"What exactly do they think, and why would a reasonable person think it?"*
- The exit ticket question (not an answer — a question) is intentional. It signals to students that curiosity doesn't end when class ends. Follow up the next day by asking one or two students what they found out.

> *\"Today's writing standard — CCSS W.7.1 — asks you to write arguments that include clear claims, logical reasoning, AND opposing viewpoints. That last part? That's a curiosity skill. You can't acknowledge the other side if you've never been curious enough to understand it.\"*

---

### **MINI-LESSON PART 2 — The Anatomy of an Argument**
**(0:12 – 0:20 | 8 minutes)**

Hand out the **Argument Scaffold** (described below). Walk through each part briefly using a low-stakes example topic:

**Example Topic:** *\"Should the school cafeteria offer a vegetarian option every day?\"*

Model the structure on the board:

**1. CLAIM** *(What do I believe?)*
> *\"The school cafeteria should offer a vegetarian option every single day.\"*

**2. REASON + EVIDENCE** *(Why? What proves it?)*
> *\"Many students have dietary restrictions for medical, religious, or ethical reasons, and on days when no vegetarian option is available, some of these students may have no appropriate meal choice.\"*

**3. REASON + EVIDENCE** *(A second \"why\")*
> *\"Additionally, plant-based meals are often less expensive to prepare, which could save the school district money that could be spent on other programs.\"*

**4. COUNTERCLAIM + REBUTTAL** *(What would someone who disagrees say, and how do I respond?)*
> *\"Some might argue that vegetarian food is less popular and would go to waste. However, cafeterias could offer these options alongside other choices, ensuring that all students are served without increasing waste.\"*

**5. CONCLUSION** *(Bring it home)*
> *\"Offering daily vegetarian options is a simple, cost-effective way to make sure every student can eat a nutritious school lunch.\"*

**Ask the class:**
> *\"Where did curiosity show up in this argument?\"*

Guide students to notice that **the counterclaim required the writer to genuinely ask: \"Why might someone disagree with me?\"** That's curiosity in action.

---

### **ACTIVITY 1 — Curiosity Cards: Question Before You Claim**
**(0:20 – 0:30 | 10 minutes)**

Hand out **Curiosity Cards**. Each card has a debate topic printed at the top and blank space below (see template below).

**Topic options** (assign one per student or let them choose):
- Should students be allowed to use their phones during lunch?
- Should gym class be required every day?
- Should schools eliminate letter grades?
- Should students have a say in choosing their teachers?
- Should junk food be banned from school fundraisers?

**Instructions (read aloud and written on board):**

> *Step 1: Read your topic.*
> *Step 2: Before you pick a side — write FIVE genuine questions you have about this topic. These should be questions you don't already know the answer to.*
> *Step 3: Circle the ONE question that you think is most important.*
> *Step 4: Now, based on thinking through those questions, pick your side and write one sentence: your CLAIM.*

Give students **7 minutes** to work silently.

With the remaining 2–3 minutes, ask two or three volunteers to share:
- What was one question they asked?
- How did asking that question affect which side they chose (or how confident they felt)?

**Debrief:**
> *\"Notice: the question came first. The claim came second. That's the curious writer's process.\"*

---

### **ACTIVITY 2 — Drafting the Argument**
**(0:30 – 0:48 | 18 minutes)**

Students now use the **Argument Scaffold handout** to draft a short argument on their topic from the Curiosity Card.

**Say:**
> *\"You've already done the hardest part — you asked good questions and you found your claim. Now fill in the rest of the scaffold. You don't need to write full paragraphs yet. You can use bullet points to get your ideas down. The goal is to hit all five parts: Claim, two Reasons with Evidence, one Counterclaim with Rebuttal, and a Conclusion.\"*

**Circulate the room** during this time. Offer targeted prompts:

- *\"What evidence would make someone actually believe you?\"*
- *\"Who would disagree with you? Why would a reasonable person think differently?\"*
- *\"Is your rebuttal just saying 'they're wrong'? Or are you actually explaining why your evidence is stronger?\"*
- *\"You have two reasons — are they actually different reasons, or the same reason said twice?\"*

With **3 minutes remaining**, give a heads-up: *\"Start wrapping up your counterclaim and conclusion.\"*

---

### **ACTIVITY 3 — Pair Share: Stress-Test the Argument**
**(0:48 – 0:53 | 5 minutes)**

Students turn to a partner (or the person behind them).

**Instructions:**
> *\"You are going to be your partner's most curious critic. Here's your job: Read their claim. Then ask them ONE question they haven't answered in their argument yet. That's it. One good question.\"*

Students exchange papers or read their claims aloud. Partners ask their question. Students jot the question down at the bottom of their scaffold to consider later.

**Debrief (1 minute):**
> *\"Did anyone hear a question from their partner that made them think — 'oh, I should address that'? That's what a curious reader does. And a curious writer anticipates those questions before the reader even has to ask.\"*

---

### **CLOSING — The Curiosity-Argument Connection**
**(0:53 – 0:55 | 2 minutes)**

Bring students back to the whole group. Ask:

> *\"Finish this sentence in your head: 'Before today, when I argued, I used to ________. Now I know I should _________ first.'\"*

Ask two students to share aloud.

**Final message (say this directly and clearly):**

> *\"The best writers — and the best thinkers — are curious before they are certain. Curiosity doesn't make you weak or wishy-washy. It makes your argument bulletproof, because you've already thought about every angle. Starting today, every time you sit down to write an argument, make yourself ask at least three questions before you write your claim. That habit will change how you write — and how you think.\"*

**Exit Ticket** (students hand in as they leave or leave on desk):
> Write one sentence: *\"One question I want to keep thinking about from today's topic is ___________.\"*
