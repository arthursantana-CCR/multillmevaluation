## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Writing Arguments That Think Outside the Box
**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1

---

## Lesson Overview
Students will craft a short argumentative paragraph on a genuinely debatable topic of their choosing, practicing claim-making, evidence use, counterargument acknowledgment, and logical organization — while developing creative habits of mind throughout the process.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Write a claim supported by clear reasons and relevant evidence
- Acknowledge and address an opposing viewpoint
- Organize argument writing logically and cohesively
- Take intellectual risks by defending an unconventional or personally meaningful position

**Materials:** Whiteboard, student notebooks, printed "Argument Builder" graphic organizer, timer, colored pens/markers for revision

---

## Lesson Sequence

### Warm-Up: "Hot Take Gallery" (8 minutes)
Post five provocative, low-stakes debate prompts around the room on large paper:
- *Homework should be optional for all students.*
- *Lunch should be 45 minutes longer than class periods.*
- *Schools should have no assigned seating — anywhere.*
- *Every student should learn a musical instrument.*
- *Reading fiction is more important than reading nonfiction.*

Students rotate and write their gut reaction (agree/disagree + one word why) on sticky notes placed on each prompt. After rotation, the class briefly shares which prompts sparked the most disagreement.

**CRE1a – Developing Personal Tastes:** Students express genuine opinions rather than copying peers. *Observe:* Do students write their own reaction before reading others' sticky notes? Do they change their answer after seeing classmates' responses, or hold their ground?

**CRE2b – Seeking New Ideas:** The gallery exposes students to unexpected angles on familiar topics. *Observe:* Do students engage with prompts that surprise them, or only with topics they already know well?

---

### Direct Instruction: The Argument Blueprint (10 minutes)
Teacher models a brief argumentative paragraph using the "CREE" structure on the board:
- **C**laim — your position
- **R**eason — why you believe it
- **E**vidence — facts, examples, or logical support
- **E**xpect the opposite — acknowledge the counterargument and respond to it

Teacher drafts a live, imperfect example in front of students, intentionally crossing out a weak sentence and rewriting it aloud, narrating: *"That didn't work — let me try a stronger approach."*

**CRE3b – Being Comfortable with Failure:** The teacher models productive revision as a normal part of writing. *Observe later:* When students draft, do they cross out and try again, or freeze when their first sentence feels wrong?

**CRE3a – Being Comfortable with Risks and Uncertainty:** Teacher chooses a slightly edgy prompt to model, showing that defending an unpopular position is intellectually legitimate. *Observe:* Students who select bold or unusual positions during independent work are demonstrating this competency.

---

### Independent Work: Choose Your Fight (25 minutes)

**Phase 1 — Topic Selection (5 minutes):** Students choose *any* debatable topic they genuinely care about — from the gallery prompts or one of their own. They must write a one-sentence explanation of *why this topic matters to them personally.*

**CRE1b – Developing Personal Aesthetics and Style:** Students choosing their own topic (rather than an assigned one) begin shaping a writerly identity. *Observe:* Does the student's topic choice reflect a distinct perspective or personal experience?

**CRE2a – Generating New Ideas:** *Observe:* Do students invent their own topic? Do they take a familiar prompt in an unexpected direction, or default to the most obvious position?

**Phase 2 — Drafting with the Graphic Organizer (15 minutes):** Students complete the Argument Builder:
- Write their claim
- List two reasons with supporting evidence
- Write one counterargument and a rebuttal
- Number their ideas in the order they'll appear in the paragraph

**CRE4a – Connecting Ideas for Cohesiveness:** *Observe:* Does the student's evidence actually connect to their claim, or do ideas feel random and disconnected?

**CRE4b – Reorganizing Ideas for Cohesiveness:** *Observe:* When students number their points, do they consider logical sequencing (e.g., saving the strongest reason for last)?

**CRE5b – Working with Constraints:** The one-paragraph constraint forces compression and prioritization. *Observe:* Do students make deliberate choices about what to include, or do they try to cram in everything and lose focus?

**Phase 3 — Revision with Color (5 minutes):** Students re-read their paragraph and use a colored pen to underline one sentence they want to improve. They rewrite it directly below.

**CRE4c – Refining Ideas for Cohesiveness:** *Observe:* Is the revision a meaningful improvement in clarity or logic, or is it a surface-level word swap?

---

### Sharing & Wrap-Up (12 minutes)

**Pair Share (5 minutes):** Partners exchange paragraphs and identify: *What is the claim? Is the counterargument addressed?*

**CRE5a – Realizing Ideas:** *Observe:* Does the finished paragraph function as a real argument? Is the claim identifiable and supported?

**Whole-Class Debrief (7 minutes):** Teacher asks: *"Did anyone defend a position they weren't totally sure about? What happened?"* This surfaces the experience of intellectual risk-taking and validates uncertainty as productive.

---

## Assessment
Students self-assess their graphic organizer using a three-question exit slip:
1. What is my clearest reason?
2. Where does my argument feel weakest?
3. What would I change with five more minutes?

## Candidate 3
**55-Minute Lesson Plan: Writing Arguments to Support Claims with Clear Reasons and Relevant Evidence (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1)**  
*Focus: Integrating Critical Thinking Creativity Subcompetencies*

---

### Lesson Objective:  
Students will write a structured argumentative paragraph supporting a claim with clear reasons and relevant evidence, acknowledge an opposing claim, and organize their reasoning logically. Throughout the lesson, students will engage in activities that foster creativity as defined by the CCR Creativity subcompetencies (CRE1–CRE5).

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Sample argumentative prompt (e.g., “Should school uniforms be mandatory?”)  
- Graphic organizer for argument structure (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Sticky notes or index cards  
- Timer  

---

### Lesson Outline:

#### 1. **Introduction & Warm-Up (5 minutes)**  
- Briefly explain the lesson goal: writing a strong argument with reasons, evidence, and acknowledging opposing views.  
- Pose a quick question to the class: “What is something you strongly believe in? Why?”  
- Ask 2-3 students to share their personal opinions and reasons briefly.

**Targets:**  
- *CRE1a (Developing personal tastes)*: Students begin expressing personal opinions.  
- *Observation:* Listen for students’ expression of personal preferences and emerging reasons.

---

#### 2. **Mini-Lesson: Argument Structure & Creativity in Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Present the components of an argument: claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal.  
- Show a sample paragraph on the board, highlighting each part.  
- Discuss how writers can develop their own style and voice when presenting arguments (CRE1b).  
- Emphasize creativity in generating original reasons and evidence, and in acknowledging opposing views thoughtfully (CRE2a, CRE2b).  
- Introduce the idea that taking risks in writing (e.g., presenting a unique viewpoint or counterclaim) is part of creativity (CRE3a, CRE3b).

**Targets:**  
- *CRE1b (Developing personal aesthetics and style)*: Encourage students to think about their unique voice.  
- *CRE2a (Generating new ideas)* and *CRE2b (Seeking new ideas)*: Highlight originality and exploring diverse perspectives.  
- *CRE3a (Being comfortable with risks and uncertainty)* and *CRE3b (Being comfortable with failure)*: Normalize creative risks and learning from mistakes.

**Observation:**  
- Gauge student engagement and questions about originality and risk-taking.  
- Note students’ willingness to consider new ideas or express unique styles.

---

#### 3. **Guided Practice: Brainstorming & Organizing Ideas (15 minutes)**  
- Present the prompt: “Should school uniforms be mandatory?”  
- Students individually brainstorm claims and reasons on sticky notes or index cards. Encourage them to think beyond common ideas (CRE2a).  
- Then, in pairs, students share their ideas and combine them, seeking new insights by integrating diverse perspectives (CRE2b).  
- Using a graphic organizer, students begin to arrange their claim, reasons, evidence, and a counterclaim logically (CRE4a, CRE4b).  
- Teacher circulates, prompting students to reorganize ideas for clarity and stronger flow (CRE4b), and to refine their reasoning (CRE4c).

**Targets:**  
- *CRE2a (Generating new ideas)*: Students produce original or less typical reasons.  
- *CRE2b (Seeking new ideas)*: Students combine ideas from peers.  
- *CRE4a (Connecting ideas for cohesiveness)* and *CRE4b (Reorganizing ideas for cohesiveness)*: Students structure their argument logically.  
- *CRE4c (Refining ideas for cohesiveness)*: Students improve clarity and coherence.

**Observation:**  
- Review students’ sticky notes and graphic organizers for originality and integration of ideas.  
- Note how students reorganize and refine their arguments based on peer discussion and teacher prompts.

---

#### 4. **Independent Writing: Drafting the Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Students write a paragraph presenting their claim, supporting reasons with evidence, and acknowledging an opposing claim with a rebuttal.  
- Encourage students to apply their personal voice and style (CRE1b), take creative risks in their argument (CRE3a), and not fear making mistakes (CRE3b).  
- Remind them to use the graphic organizer as a guide.

**Targets:**  
- *CRE1a (Developing personal tastes)*: Students express clear personal opinions.  
- *CRE1b (Developing personal aesthetics and style)*: Students demonstrate unique writing style.  
- *CRE3a (Being comfortable with risks and uncertainty)* and *CRE3b (Being comfortable with failure)*: Students attempt creative approaches despite uncertainty.  
- *CRE5a (Realizing ideas)*: Students implement their planned ideas into a written product.  
- *CRE5b (Working with constraints)*: Students write within time and format constraints, using them to focus their argument.

**Observation:**  
- Collect drafts to assess clarity of claim, use of evidence, acknowledgment of counterclaims, and personal voice.  
- Note students’ willingness to try new ideas or styles and how they handle constraints.

---

#### 5. **Peer Review & Reflection (8 minutes)**  
- Students exchange drafts with a partner.  
- Using a checklist, they identify strengths and suggest one area for improvement related to argument clarity, evidence, or acknowledging opposing views.  
- Encourage students to reflect on how they took creative risks or refined their ideas during writing (CRE4c, CRE5a).  
- Invite a few volunteers to share how they used creativity or overcame challenges in their writing.

**Targets:**  
- *CRE4c (Refining ideas for cohesiveness)*: Students suggest refinements.  
- *CRE5a (Realizing ideas)*: Students reflect on execution quality.  
- *CRE5b (Working with constraints)*: Students discuss how constraints shaped their writing.  
- *CRE3b (Being comfortable with failure)*: Students view feedback as growth opportunity.

**Observation:**  
- Listen to peer feedback for evidence of thoughtful critique and constructive suggestions.  
- Note students’ reflections on creative risk-taking and handling challenges.

---
