## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with scenarios, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (8 minutes)
Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A store sells 3 apples for $2. How much for 12 apples?"
- **Scenario B:** "A baby weighs 8 lbs at birth and 16 lbs at 6 months. Will she weigh 32 lbs at 12 months?"

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? Why?"* Students discuss with a partner (Think-Pair-Share).

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means and identify the relevant quantities (price per apple, weight over time).
**Assessment:** Listen during pair-share for precise language like "constant rate" or "same ratio." Cold-call 2–3 students; rate clarity of explanations.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (12 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities where **y/x = k** (constant of proportionality). Show all five representations using the apple example:

- **Table:** (1, $0.67), (3, $2), (6, $4), (12, $8)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = (2/3)x
- **Diagram:** Tape diagram showing equal groupings
- **Verbal:** "Each apple costs about 67 cents."

Emphasize the **three tests** for proportionality: (1) constant ratio, (2) graph is a straight line, (3) line passes through origin.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students copy a graphic organizer linking all five representations to one relationship.
**Assessment:** Collect graphic organizers; check whether students connect representations logically (e.g., arrows showing how table → equation → graph).

---

### 3. Guided Practice — "Proportional or Not?" Investigation (15 minutes)
Distribute a handout with four scenarios:
1. Pizza: 2 pies feed 6 people; 5 pies feed 15.
2. Phone plan: $20 flat fee + $0.10/minute.
3. Distance: A car drives 60 miles in 1 hour, 180 miles in 3 hours.
4. Plant growth: 2 inches in week 1, 5 inches by week 2, 11 inches by week 3.

In groups of 3, students determine which are proportional, building a table and graph for each.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Groups must cross-check by testing **all three criteria** (ratio, line, origin)—not just one. Require students to verify using at least two representations.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you KNOW it's proportional? Can you confirm with another representation?"* Note groups using multiple verification methods.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Some scenarios contain "trap" information (the phone plan looks linear but isn't proportional due to flat fee). Students must select which data points to test.
**Assessment:** Review group answers; students who flag the flat fee as disqualifying demonstrate quality judgment.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision Task (10 minutes)
Present a real-world problem: *"You're planning a class fundraiser. Two vendors offer T-shirts:*
- *Vendor A: $8 per shirt (proportional)*
- *Vendor B: $50 setup fee + $5 per shirt (not proportional)*

*Which should we choose for 10 shirts? For 50 shirts? For 200 shirts?"*

Groups graph both, compute costs, and recommend a choice with justification.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students must analyze trade-offs across different order sizes—Vendor A is better for small orders, Vendor B for large.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation citing at least two trade-offs. Look for phrases like "depends on quantity," "break-even point," or "long-term."

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify their answer using the equations y = 8x vs. y = 5x + 50, identifying where the lines intersect.
**Assessment:** Score reasoning on a 0–2 rubric: 2 = uses equations/graphs to find intersection (~17 shirts); 1 = makes a choice with partial logic; 0 = guesses.

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection — Exit Ticket (10 minutes)
Students individually complete an exit ticket with two parts:

**Part 1 (Content):** *"Given the table (2, 10), (5, 25), (8, 40)—is this proportional? Write the equation and explain."*

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"Describe one moment today when your thinking changed. What confused you at first about proportional relationships? What helped clarify it?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Part 2 explicitly asks students to engage in metacognition.
**Assessment:** Read reflections for evidence of self-awareness (e.g., "I thought any straight line was proportional, but I learned it must pass through the origin"). Rate 2 = identifies specific shift in thinking; 1 = surface mention; 0 = no reflection.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables; sentence stems for reflection.
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to create their own non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional.

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship at home (recipes, bills, schedules). Represent each in two formats and explain reasoning.

---

This lesson integrates all six critical thinking subcompetencies through inquiry, group analysis, real-world decision-making, and structured metacognitive reflection.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Proportionality Puzzle: Connecting Representations
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to identify, represent, and verify proportional relationships across multiple formats while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, weigh problem-solving options, and reflect on their mathematical reasoning.

---

### **Part 1: Introduction and The Hook (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher presents a verbal description of a real-world scenario: "A local theme park charges $15 per ticket. However, a billboard advertises a 'Group Rate' of 5 tickets for $80, and a website claims you can get 10 tickets for $150." Students are asked to determine if the pricing is proportional. 

*   **Targeting CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must extract the specific pricing data from the verbal description and clarify the mathematical definition of proportionality (a constant unit rate). They rewrite the verbal claims into precise mathematical ratios to support a deeper understanding of the problem.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher will observe students as they write down the ratios (1:15, 5:80, 10:150). Students achieve a Level 2 if they can precisely clarify that the information provided shows a fluctuating unit rate ($15, $16, $15), thereby proving the relationship is *not* proportional.

### **Part 2: Guided Practice & Organization (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher introduces a true proportional scenario: "A car travels at a constant speed of 60 miles per hour." Students are tasked with representing this relationship in four ways: a table, a graph, an equation ($y = 60x$), and a diagram. 

*   **Targeting CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students use a "Rule of Four" graphic organizer. They must arrange the data to strengthen the logical flow, showing exactly how the constant of proportionality ($k=60$) connects the table's ratios, the graph's steepness, and the equation's coefficient.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews the graphic organizers. A Level 2 is demonstrated when a student logically arranges the table sequentially, plots the graph with properly scaled axes, and explicitly draws arrows or color-codes the organizer to show how the unit rate flows consistently through all four representations.

### **Part 3: The "Data Detective" Collaborative Task (15 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students work in pairs. They are given a folder containing three different "sources" of information regarding a local bakery's cookie pricing: a handwritten promotional flyer (verbal description), a printed cash register receipt (table of values), and a graph published on the bakery's official website. Some data points conflict. Students must determine the true proportional relationship of the cookie prices.

*   **Targeting CRI2a (Assessing validity of information):** Students must rigorously cross-check the information across the three sources. They calculate the unit rate from the receipt and cross-reference it with the slope of the website's graph to see if the flyer's claims are valid.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher listens to pair discussions. Level 2 is achieved when students actively cross-check the math across all three sources, identifying exactly where the flyer's math breaks the proportional rule established by the receipt and graph.
*   **Targeting CRI2b (Assessing quality of information):** Students must decide which source is the most reliable to base their final equation on. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** Students demonstrate Level 2 by showing sophisticated judgment, arguing that the printed receipt and official website graph are high-quality, authoritative sources of objective data, whereas the handwritten flyer is of lower quality and contains a mathematical error.

### **Part 4: Deep Thinking and Decision Making (10 minutes)**
**Activity:** The teacher poses a new challenge based on the bakery data: "If we need to find the exact cost of 3,450 cookies for a corporate event, which representation (table, graph, or equation) should we use?"

*   **Targeting CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices):** Students systematically evaluate the trade-offs of each representation. They must discuss the long-term implications of their choice (e.g., extending a table or graph to 3,450 is highly inefficient and prone to error, whereas an equation provides immediate accuracy).
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses this during a whole-class share-out. Level 2 is met when students articulate a comprehensive evaluation of the trade-offs, concluding that while graphs are great for quick visual checks, equations are the superior choice for large-scale predictions.
*   **Targeting CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making):** Students must make a final decision on the cost and justify it using the equation they built. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** Level 2 is observed when students use sophisticated, flawless logic to justify their conclusion, clearly explaining how the constant of proportionality in their equation guarantees an accurate prediction regardless of the input size.

### **Part 5: Conclusion and Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**
**Activity:** Students complete an independent Exit Ticket containing one final proportional word problem and a metacognitive reflection prompt: *"Think about a moment today when your graph, table, or equation did not match up. How did you realize your mistake, and how did you adjust your thinking to fix it?"*

*   **Targeting CRI5a (Reflecting on one's thinking processes):** Students reflect on their internal problem-solving process, specifically how they monitored their own comprehension and corrected mathematical errors during the lesson.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher grades the written exit tickets. A Level 2 is awarded to students who demonstrate deep metacognitive awareness—for example, writing, "I initially assumed the relationship was proportional because the numbers were increasing, but when my graph didn't start at the origin (0,0), I realized my assumption was wrong. I adjusted my thinking by recalculating the unit rate to check for a constant."

## 55-Minute Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Learning Target:** Students will determine whether two quantities are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.

### Materials
- Projector/board  
- Graph paper  
- Student handout with tables, graphs, and scenarios  
- Colored pencils  
- Exit ticket  

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes
Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $2.40  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $4.50  

Ask students: “Which deal is better? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Invite a few students to share strategies, such as finding cost per bar.

**Teacher emphasis:** “When we compare quantities by finding a constant rate, we are looking for proportional relationships.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify what the quantities are: number of bars and total cost.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clearly stating, “I need cost per bar,” or “I am comparing dollars to bars.” Check whether students separate relevant information from extra wording.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Teach the key idea:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities stays constant. The constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**, often written as **k**.

Use the example:

| Apples | Cost |
|---|---|
| 3 | $2.40 |
| 6 | $4.80 |
| 9 | $7.20 |

Ask: “What is the cost per apple?”

Students calculate:

$2.40 ÷ 3 = $0.80  
$4.80 ÷ 6 = $0.80  
$7.20 ÷ 9 = $0.80  

Since the unit rate is always $0.80, the relationship is proportional.

Represent it in multiple ways:

- **Verbal:** Each apple costs $0.80.  
- **Equation:** \( y = 0.80x \), where \( x \) is apples and \( y \) is cost.  
- **Table:** Shows equivalent ratios.  
- **Graph:** Points form a straight line through the origin.  
- **Diagram:** Double number line showing apples and cost increasing by the same factor.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students organize the relationship into table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check if students arrange information logically, label quantities correctly, and connect each representation to the same unit rate.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Proportional or Not? — 12 minutes
Give students two examples.

**Example 1:**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $12 |
| 2 | $24 |
| 3 | $36 |
| 4 | $48 |

Students determine that the relationship is proportional because the rate is $12 per hour.

Equation: \( y = 12x \)

**Example 2:**

A carnival charges a $3 entry fee plus $2 per ride.

| Rides | Total Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $5 |
| 2 | $7 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $11 |

Students test ratios:

5 ÷ 1 = 5  
7 ÷ 2 = 3.5  
9 ÷ 3 = 3  

The ratios are not constant, so the relationship is not proportional.

Also point out that the graph would not pass through the origin because there is an initial fee.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students verify proportionality using more than one representation: table ratios, equation pattern, and graph behavior.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to justify: “How do you know?” Look for evidence that they cross-check instead of relying on one number.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students decide which information is most useful: unit rate, constant ratio, origin, and linear pattern.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Students should focus on mathematically relevant evidence, not surface details such as “the numbers go up” or “the table looks organized.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Match — 14 minutes
Give each pair a set of cards with mixed representations:

- Tables  
- Graphs  
- Equations  
- Verbal descriptions  
- Double number line diagrams  

Students match cards that represent the same proportional relationship. Include at least one non-proportional relationship as a distractor.

Example set:

- Verbal: “A cyclist travels 15 miles every hour.”  
- Equation: \( y = 15x \)  
- Table: 1 hour = 15 miles, 2 hours = 30 miles, 3 hours = 45 miles  
- Graph: Straight line through \((0,0)\), \((1,15)\), \((2,30)\)

Students must write one sentence explaining each match.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students compare possible matches and decide which representations belong together.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for partner talk such as, “This graph looks close, but it does not go through the origin,” or “This equation has the same unit rate as the table.” Students should consider alternatives before choosing.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students defend why a set of representations matches or why a card does not belong.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Require evidence-based explanations using vocabulary: constant of proportionality, unit rate, equivalent ratios, straight line, and origin.

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes
Students complete three problems:

1. Determine whether a table is proportional and explain.  
2. Write an equation from a verbal description.  
3. Graph a proportional relationship from a table and identify the constant of proportionality.

Teacher circulates and asks prompting questions:

- “What two quantities are being compared?”  
- “Is the ratio constant?”  
- “Does your graph pass through the origin?”  
- “What does \( k \) mean in this situation?”

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “Which representation helps you most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”  
3. Determine if this relationship is proportional:

| Minutes | Pages Read |
|---|---|
| 10 | 8 |
| 20 | 16 |
| 30 | 24 |

Students explain using at least one complete sentence.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify which strategy helped them and why.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for reflection beyond an answer, such as “I used the table first because dividing pages by minutes showed the same rate each time.”

---

## Assessment Summary
Students demonstrate mastery if they can:

- Identify proportional relationships using constant ratios.  
- Represent a proportional relationship in a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.  
- Explain that a proportional graph is a straight line through the origin.  
- Use reasoning and evidence to justify whether a relationship is proportional.

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Fair?” — 7 minutes
Display two snack deals:

- Deal A: 3 granola bars for $2.40  
- Deal B: 5 granola bars for $4.50  

Ask students: “Which deal is better? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then discuss with a partner. Invite a few students to share strategies, such as finding cost per bar.

**Teacher emphasis:** “When we compare quantities by finding a constant rate, we are looking for proportional relationships.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify what the quantities are: number of bars and total cost.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students clearly stating, “I need cost per bar,” or “I am comparing dollars to bars.” Check whether students separate relevant information from extra wording.

---

### 2. Mini-Lesson: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes
Teach the key idea:

A relationship is **proportional** if the ratio between two quantities stays constant. The constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**, often written as **k**.

Use the example:

| Apples | Cost |
|---|---|
| 3 | $2.40 |
| 6 | $4.80 |
| 9 | $7.20 |

Ask: “What is the cost per apple?”

Students calculate:

$2.40 ÷ 3 = $0.80  
$4.80 ÷ 6 = $0.80  
$7.20 ÷ 9 = $0.80  

Since the unit rate is always $0.80, the relationship is proportional.

Represent it in multiple ways:

- **Verbal:** Each apple costs $0.80.  
- **Equation:** \( y = 0.80x \), where \( x \) is apples and \( y \) is cost.  
- **Table:** Shows equivalent ratios.  
- **Graph:** Points form a straight line through the origin.  
- **Diagram:** Double number line showing apples and cost increasing by the same factor.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students organize the relationship into table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Check if students arrange information logically, label quantities correctly, and connect each representation to the same unit rate.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Proportional or Not? — 12 minutes
Give students two examples.

**Example 1:**

| Hours | Money Earned |
|---|---|
| 1 | $12 |
| 2 | $24 |
| 3 | $36 |
| 4 | $48 |

Students determine that the relationship is proportional because the rate is $12 per hour.

Equation: \( y = 12x \)

**Example 2:**

A carnival charges a $3 entry fee plus $2 per ride.

| Rides | Total Cost |
|---|---|
| 1 | $5 |
| 2 | $7 |
| 3 | $9 |
| 4 | $11 |

Students test ratios:

5 ÷ 1 = 5  
7 ÷ 2 = 3.5  
9 ÷ 3 = 3  

The ratios are not constant, so the relationship is not proportional.

Also point out that the graph would not pass through the origin because there is an initial fee.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students verify proportionality using more than one representation: table ratios, equation pattern, and graph behavior.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Ask students to justify: “How do you know?” Look for evidence that they cross-check instead of relying on one number.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students decide which information is most useful: unit rate, constant ratio, origin, and linear pattern.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Students should focus on mathematically relevant evidence, not surface details such as “the numbers go up” or “the table looks organized.”

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Representation Match — 14 minutes
Give each pair a set of cards with mixed representations:

- Tables  
- Graphs  
- Equations  
- Verbal descriptions  
- Double number line diagrams  

Students match cards that represent the same proportional relationship. Include at least one non-proportional relationship as a distractor.

Example set:

- Verbal: “A cyclist travels 15 miles every hour.”  
- Equation: \( y = 15x \)  
- Table: 1 hour = 15 miles, 2 hours = 30 miles, 3 hours = 45 miles  
- Graph: Straight line through \((0,0)\), \((1,15)\), \((2,30)\)

Students must write one sentence explaining each match.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students compare possible matches and decide which representations belong together.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for partner talk such as, “This graph looks close, but it does not go through the origin,” or “This equation has the same unit rate as the table.” Students should consider alternatives before choosing.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students defend why a set of representations matches or why a card does not belong.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Require evidence-based explanations using vocabulary: constant of proportionality, unit rate, equivalent ratios, straight line, and origin.

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 7 minutes
Students complete three problems:

1. Determine whether a table is proportional and explain.  
2. Write an equation from a verbal description.  
3. Graph a proportional relationship from a table and identify the constant of proportionality.

Teacher circulates and asks prompting questions:

- “What two quantities are being compared?”  
- “Is the ratio constant?”  
- “Does your graph pass through the origin?”  
- “What does \( k \) mean in this situation?”

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 5 minutes
Students answer:

1. “How can you tell if a relationship is proportional?”  
2. “Which representation helps you most: table, graph, equation, diagram, or words? Why?”  
3. Determine if this relationship is proportional:

| Minutes | Pages Read |
|---|---|
| 10 | 8 |
| 20 | 16 |
| 30 | 24 |

Students explain using at least one complete sentence.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
- **Targeted moment:** Students identify which strategy helped them and why.  
- **Observation/Assessment:** Look for reflection beyond an answer, such as “I used the table first because dividing pages by minutes showed the same rate each time.”

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Lesson Objective
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills around clarifying, organizing, evaluating, and reflecting on mathematical information.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, scenario handouts, "Data Detective" source folders, exit tickets, whiteboard.

---

## Lesson Flow

### 1. Warm-Up & Hook (7 minutes)
Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A store sells 3 granola bars for $2.40. How much for 12 bars?"
- **Scenario B:** "A baby weighs 8 lbs at birth and 16 lbs at 6 months. Will she weigh 32 lbs at 12 months?"

Ask: *"Which situation is proportional? Why?"* Students Think-Pair-Share.

**Targets CRI1a (Clarifying information):** Students must clarify what "proportional" means and identify relevant quantities (cost per bar, weight over time), rewriting verbal claims as precise mathematical ratios.
**Assessment:** Listen for precise language like "constant rate," "unit rate," or "same ratio." Cold-call 2–3 students; Level 2 = clearly distinguishes constant vs. fluctuating rates.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction (10 minutes)
Define proportional relationships: two quantities where **y/x = k** (constant of proportionality). Show all five representations using the granola bar example:

- **Table:** (3, $2.40), (6, $4.80), (9, $7.20)
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Equation:** y = 0.80x
- **Diagram:** Double number line showing bars and cost scaling together
- **Verbal:** "Each bar costs $0.80."

Emphasize the **three tests** for proportionality: (1) constant ratio, (2) graph is a straight line, (3) line passes through origin.

**Targets CRI1b (Organizing information):** Students complete a "Rule of Four" graphic organizer linking all five representations.
**Assessment:** Collect organizers; Level 2 = student uses arrows or color-coding to show how the unit rate flows consistently through table, equation, graph, and diagram.

---

### 3. "Data Detective" Investigation (13 minutes)
In pairs, students receive a folder with three conflicting "sources" about a bakery's cookie pricing:
1. A handwritten flyer: "12 cookies for $9!"
2. A printed receipt table: (4, $3), (8, $6), (12, $9)
3. A graph from the bakery's website showing a line through origin with slope $0.75/cookie

Some flyer claims contain errors. Students determine the true proportional relationship.

**Targets CRI2a (Assessing validity):** Pairs cross-check the unit rate across all three sources—testing all three proportionality criteria, not just one.
**Assessment:** Circulate and ask, *"How do you KNOW? Can you confirm with another representation?"* Level 2 = students identify exactly where a source breaks the proportional rule established by the others.

**Targets CRI2b (Assessing quality):** Students judge which source is most authoritative.
**Assessment:** Level 2 = students argue the printed receipt and official graph are high-quality, objective data, while flagging the flyer as lower quality due to mathematical inconsistency.

---

### 4. Collaborative Decision Task (10 minutes)
Present: *"You're planning a class fundraiser. Two vendors offer T-shirts:*
- *Vendor A: $8 per shirt (proportional)*
- *Vendor B: $50 setup fee + $5 per shirt (not proportional)*

*Which should we choose for 10 shirts? 50 shirts? 200 shirts?"*

Groups graph both, compute costs, and recommend a choice.

**Targets CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons):** Students systematically evaluate trade-offs across order sizes—Vendor A wins for small orders, Vendor B for large—and consider long-term implications.
**Assessment:** Each group submits a written recommendation citing at least two trade-offs. Level 2 = identifies break-even point (~17 shirts) and reasons about scale.

**Targets CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning):** Students justify using equations y = 8x vs. y = 5x + 50, identifying where the lines intersect.
**Assessment:** Score reasoning on a 0–2 rubric: 2 = uses equations/graphs to find intersection with flawless logic; 1 = makes a choice with partial logic; 0 = guesses.

---

### 5. Closure & Reflection — Exit Ticket (15 minutes including independent work)
Students individually complete:

**Part 1 (Content):** *"Given the table (2, 10), (5, 25), (8, 40)—is this proportional? Write the equation and explain using at least two representations."*

**Part 2 (Reflection):** *"Think about a moment today when your graph, table, or equation did not match up—or when your thinking changed. How did you realize, and how did you adjust?"*

**Targets CRI5a (Reflecting on thinking):** Part 2 prompts explicit metacognition.
**Assessment:** Rate 2 = identifies a specific shift in thinking (e.g., "I thought any straight line was proportional, but I realized it must pass through the origin, so I recalculated the unit rate"); 1 = surface mention; 0 = no reflection.

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables and sentence stems for reflection.
- **Challenge:** Ask advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional.

## Homework
Find one proportional and one non-proportional relationship at home (recipes, bills, schedules). Represent each in two formats and explain reasoning.

This lesson integrates all critical thinking subcompetencies (CRI1a–CRI5a) through inquiry, source evaluation, real-world decision-making, and structured metacognitive reflection.
