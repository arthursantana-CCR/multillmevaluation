## Candidate 1
# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with sample scenarios, exit ticket slips.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Hook & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a*
**Activity:** Display two scenarios on the board:
- **Scenario A:** "A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 miles in 3 hours."
- **Scenario B:** "A baby weighs 8 lbs at birth, 15 lbs at 6 months, 30 lbs at 2 years."

Ask: "Which describes a proportional relationship? What information do we need to *clarify* to decide?"

Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide them to articulate that proportionality requires a *constant ratio*. Write the definition precisely: **y = kx**, where k is the constant of proportionality.

**Assessment for CRI1a:** Listen for students who identify the relevant feature (constant ratio) and articulate it clearly. Note responses on a clipboard checklist — Level 2 if students precisely distinguish proportional from non-proportional reasoning.

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Organizing Information (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b*
Introduce the **four representations**: table, graph, equation, verbal description. Using Scenario A, model how to organize the same relationship across all four formats on a graphic organizer (4-quadrant chart).

Demonstrate:
- **Table:** Hours/Miles columns
- **Equation:** y = 60x
- **Graph:** Straight line through origin
- **Verbal:** "Miles equal 60 times hours"

**Assessment for CRI1b:** Collect students' completed graphic organizers. Look for logical flow — does information across representations align? Level 2 students arrange data so each representation reinforces the others.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Assessing Validity (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b*
**Activity:** Distribute a handout with **four "student work samples"** (fictional) claiming various relationships are proportional. One sample includes a graph that doesn't pass through the origin but claims proportionality; another uses a table with inconsistent ratios.

In pairs, students must **cross-check each claim** using at least two representations (e.g., verify the table's ratios AND check whether the graph passes through origin).

Prompt: "Don't trust the claim — verify using multiple methods."

**Assessment for CRI2a:** Circulate and ask pairs, "How did you confirm this?" Level 2 students use *multiple* representations to cross-check (table ratios + graph + equation).

**Assessment for CRI2b:** Review written justifications. Level 2 students cite the most authoritative evidence (e.g., "The ratio y/x is not constant: 3/1 ≠ 7/2, so it's not proportional") rather than vague reasoning.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Weighing Alternatives (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
**Real-world problem:** "You're choosing between two phone plans:
- **Plan X:** $0.10 per minute, no monthly fee
- **Plan Y:** $5 monthly fee + $0.05 per minute"

In groups of 3, students must:
1. Determine which plan represents a proportional relationship (Plan X).
2. Create tables, graphs, and equations for both.
3. Recommend a plan for a user who talks 50 min/month vs. 200 min/month.
4. Write a justification weighing trade-offs.

**Assessment for CRI3a:** Read group justifications. Level 2 groups systematically evaluate trade-offs across multiple usage scenarios and consider long-term implications (e.g., "For heavy users, Plan Y saves money despite the fee").

**Assessment for CRI4a:** Look for logical reasoning chains in justifications. Level 2 students anticipate complexities — e.g., identifying the break-even point where plans cost equally.

---

### 5. Independent Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a*
Students complete an exit ticket with two parts:

**Part A (Content):** Given the equation y = 4x, create a table, graph, and verbal description.

**Part B (Metacognitive Reflection):** Answer in 3–4 sentences:
- "Which representation (table, graph, equation, or words) was easiest for you to understand proportional relationships? Why?"
- "What confused you at the start of class that makes sense now? What strategy helped you?"
- "If you saw a new proportional problem tomorrow, what would you do differently?"

**Assessment for CRI5a:** Collect exit tickets. Level 2 responses demonstrate genuine metacognition — students name specific strategies they adjusted (e.g., "I used to just check one ratio, but now I check all of them and look at whether the graph passes through zero"). Level 1 responses mention reasoning without depth ("Graphs were easier"). Level -1 responses show no awareness of growth or errors.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Quick whole-class debrief: Call on 3 students to share one insight from their reflection. Reinforce the key takeaway: **Proportional relationships have a constant ratio (k), pass through the origin on a graph, and can be written as y = kx.**

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables for struggling learners.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional and explain why it's deceptive.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.) and represent it in all four formats.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Power of Proportions: Representing Real-World Relationships
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Duration:** 55 Minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Lesson Objective:** Students will be able to recognize, represent, and translate proportional relationships across verbal descriptions, tables, graphs, and equations while applying critical thinking skills to evaluate data sources, organize information, and justify mathematical decisions.

---

### **Part 1: Warm-Up and Information Processing (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: The Road Trip Scenario**
The teacher presents a verbal description: *"You are planning a cross-country road trip. A travel blog claims a car gets 30 miles per gallon, but the official manufacturer’s website states the car uses 2 gallons to travel 60 miles, 3 gallons for 90 miles, and 5 gallons for 150 miles."* 

**CRI1a: Clarifying information**
*   **Activity/Moment:** Before doing any math, students participate in a "Think-Pair-Share" to dissect the verbal description. They must precisely clarify the core question: What are the two quantities being compared, and what is the constant of proportionality (unit rate)? 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher circulates and listens for students asking precise clarifying questions (e.g., "Are we comparing miles to gallons or gallons to miles?") and stating the unit rate clearly to support a deeper understanding of the relationship.

**CRI1b: Organizing information**
*   **Activity/Moment:** Students are tasked with translating the verbal description into a structured T-table (Gallons vs. Miles). They must arrange the manufacturer's data sequentially.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews the students' whiteboards or scratch paper to ensure they have arranged the data in a logical, ascending structure (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 5 gallons) that strengthens the logical flow and makes the proportional pattern ($y = 30x$) immediately visible.

---

### **Part 2: Guided Practice and Data Evaluation (15 Minutes)**

**Activity: Graphing and Fact-Checking**
The teacher projects two graphs representing the road trip data. Graph A is from a random social media post, showing a jagged line with inconsistent intervals. Graph B is generated using data from the official Department of Transportation, showing a perfectly straight line passing through the origin $(0,0)$. 

**CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
*   **Activity/Moment:** Students must cross-check the points on both graphs against their organized tables and the original manufacturer's verbal description. They discuss why Graph B is mathematically valid (straight line through the origin) and why the government source is more authoritative than the social media post.
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher assesses students' verbal responses and written notes to see if they are rigorously cross-checking the visual data against multiple authoritative sources (the manufacturer's numbers and the DOT graph) rather than blindly accepting the flawed social media graph.

**CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
*   **Activity/Moment:** The teacher introduces a new data point from an unreliable source: "A friend says if you drive downhill, you get 100 miles per gallon." Students must evaluate the quality and relevance of this information regarding the established proportional equation ($y = 30x$).
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher observes whether students demonstrate sophisticated judgment by rejecting this outlier. Students should articulate that while downhill driving affects mileage, it introduces a variable that breaks the constant proportional relationship needed for their current mathematical model.

---

### **Part 3: Collaborative Problem Solving (20 Minutes)**

**Activity: The Vendor Dilemma**
Students are placed in small groups and given a new scenario: The school is buying custom t-shirts. 
*   *Vendor A* provides a table of costs.
*   *Vendor B* provides a graph.
*   *Vendor C* provides an equation ($c = 12t$).
Students must translate all vendors into equations to find the constant of proportionality and decide which vendor to use for an order of 250 shirts.

**CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
*   **Activity/Moment:** Before solving, groups must fill out a "Representation Trade-Off" chart. They systematically evaluate the pros and cons of using a table, a graph, or an equation to predict the cost of 250 shirts. 
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher collects the charts to verify that students are systematically evaluating trade-offs. A high-level response will note: "Graphs are great for quick visual comparisons of who is cheaper initially, but equations are much better for calculating exact long-term implications, like the cost of 250 shirts, without having to draw a massive graph."

**CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
*   **Activity/Moment:** Groups must write a final proposal to the principal recommending a vendor. They must use their equations to justify their choice mathematically, while also anticipating real-world complexities (e.g., shipping costs or bulk discounts).
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reads the proposals to assess if the reasoning is sophisticated. Students should not only use the math correctly (e.g., "Vendor C is $12 per shirt, Vendor A is $15") but also anticipate complexities (e.g., "We chose Vendor C because the equation $c = 12t$ proves it is the cheapest for 250 shirts, assuming there are no hidden flat-rate shipping fees that would make it non-proportional").

---

### **Part 4: Closure and Metacognition (10 Minutes)**

**Activity: Exit Ticket and Reflection**
Students independently complete a brief exit ticket where they must draw a diagram (like a double number line) representing $y = 4x$. After completing the math, they answer a reflection prompt.

**CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
*   **Activity/Moment:** The exit ticket includes the prompt: *"Think about how you solved proportional problems at the beginning of class versus now. How did translating the data into different forms (tables, graphs, equations) change the way you understand the problem? What will you do differently next time you encounter a confusing math scenario?"*
*   **Observation/Assessment:** The teacher reviews the exit tickets for deep metacognitive awareness. A strong assessment shows the student strategically adjusting their thinking (e.g., "I used to just guess based on the verbal description, but I realized my brain processes the pattern better when I organize it into a table first, and then turn it into an equation to check my work. Next time, I will immediately build a table to clarify my thoughts.").

## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** Middle School / Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Identify the constant of proportionality.
3. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
4. Explain their reasoning using mathematical evidence.

### Materials
- Projector or board
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- “Proportional or Not?” task handout
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Deal Is Better? — 7 minutes

Display the following:

- Store A: 3 notebooks for $6  
- Store B: 5 notebooks for $12  
- Store C: 4 notebooks for $8

Ask students:  
“Which store gives the best deal? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite a few students to explain. Guide students toward finding the unit rate, or cost per notebook.

Expected responses:
- Store A: $6 ÷ 3 = $2 per notebook
- Store B: $12 ÷ 5 = $2.40 per notebook
- Store C: $8 ÷ 4 = $2 per notebook

Explain: “Today we are studying proportional relationships. One way to recognize them is by checking whether the ratio between two quantities stays the same.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Introduce the definition:

A **proportional relationship** is a relationship between two quantities where the ratio between them is constant. This constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**.

Write the equation form:

\[
y = kx
\]

Explain:
- \(x\) and \(y\) are related quantities.
- \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.
- The graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line that passes through the origin \((0,0)\).

Use this example:

A runner travels 2 miles every 20 minutes.

Create a table:

| Time, x | Distance, y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 20 | 2 |
| 40 | 4 |
| 60 | 6 |

Ask: “What stays the same?”  
Students should notice that the runner travels 1 mile every 10 minutes, or 0.1 mile per minute.

Show the equation:

\[
y = 0.1x
\]

Then sketch the graph, emphasizing that the points form a straight line through the origin.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A car wash charges $7 for each car washed.”

As a class, create five representations.

1. **Verbal description:** Each car costs $7 to wash.
2. **Table:**

| Cars, x | Cost, y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 7 |
| 2 | 14 |
| 3 | 21 |
| 4 | 28 |

3. **Equation:**

\[
y = 7x
\]

4. **Graph:** Students plot the points on graph paper and draw a line through the points and the origin.

5. **Diagram:** Draw groups of $7 for each car, such as one car matched with seven dollar symbols.

Ask students:
- “Where do you see the constant of proportionality?”
- “How do the table, graph, equation, and diagram show the same relationship?”
- “Why does the graph need to pass through \((0,0)\)?”

Clarify that if zero cars are washed, the cost should be zero dollars. That is why the origin matters in a proportional relationship.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs on four examples. They must decide whether each relationship is proportional and represent at least one proportional relationship in two different ways.

Example A:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |
| 8 | 24 |

Example B:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 13 |
| 4 | 17 |

Example C:  
A babysitter charges $10 per hour plus a one-time $5 transportation fee.

Example D:  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

Students answer:
1. Is it proportional? Why or why not?
2. What is the constant of proportionality, if there is one?
3. How could you represent the relationship with a table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description?

Bring the class together to discuss. Emphasize:
- Example A is proportional because \(y/x = 3\) every time.
- Example B is not proportional because the ratio does not stay constant.
- Example C is not proportional because the equation would be \(y = 10x + 5\), not \(y = kx\), and it does not pass through the origin.
- Example D is proportional because the flour-to-sugar ratio stays the same.

---

## Critical Thinking Connections

### CRI1a: Clarifying Information
**Lesson moment:** During partner work, students identify what the quantities represent and label \(x\), \(y\), and the constant of proportionality.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for students clearly stating ideas such as, “\(x\) represents hours, \(y\) represents cost, and the $5 fee means it is not proportional.”

### CRI1b: Organizing Information
**Lesson moment:** Students organize the same relationship into a table, equation, graph, diagram, and verbal description.  
**Observe/assess:** Check whether their representations are consistent and logically connected, such as a table matching the equation and graph.

### CRI2a: Assessing Validity of Information
**Lesson moment:** Students test whether a relationship is proportional by checking ratios and whether the graph passes through the origin.  
**Observe/assess:** Ask students to justify claims with mathematical evidence, such as “This is valid because every \(y/x\) ratio equals 3.”

### CRI2b: Assessing Quality of Information
**Lesson moment:** Students decide which information is most useful for proving proportionality: unit rate, constant ratio, equation form, or graph.  
**Observe/assess:** Listen for students using relevant evidence instead of surface patterns, such as “The numbers go up by 4 each time” versus “The ratio stays constant.”

### CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices
**Lesson moment:** Students compare representations and decide which one best shows proportionality.  
**Observe/assess:** Students explain trade-offs, such as “A table makes the ratio easy to check, but a graph makes it easy to see if the line passes through the origin.”

### CRI4a: Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making
**Lesson moment:** Students decide whether each example is proportional and defend their conclusion.  
**Observe/assess:** Look for logical explanations using multiple pieces of evidence, such as ratio, equation, and graph.

### CRI5a: Reflecting on Thinking Processes
**Lesson moment:** Before the exit ticket, students complete this reflection: “At first I thought ____, but then I checked ____ and realized ____.”  
**Observe/assess:** Review whether students can describe how their thinking changed or what strategy helped them avoid an error.

---

### 5. Exit Ticket — 8 minutes

Students complete independently:

1. Is this relationship proportional? Explain.

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 10 |
| 4 | 20 |
| 6 | 30 |
| 8 | 40 |

2. Write an equation for the relationship if it is proportional.

3. Describe one way to tell from a graph whether a relationship is proportional.

Expected answers:
1. Yes, because \(y/x = 5\) for each pair.
2. \[
y = 5x
\]
3. The graph is a straight line that passes through the origin.

Collect exit tickets to determine who can recognize proportional relationships and who needs more practice distinguishing proportional from non-proportional relationships.

## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade Level:** Middle School / Grade 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.

### Learning Targets
Students will be able to:
1. Determine whether two quantities are proportional.
2. Identify the constant of proportionality.
3. Represent a proportional relationship using a table, graph, equation, diagram, and verbal description.
4. Explain their reasoning using mathematical evidence.

### Materials
- Projector or board
- Student notebooks
- Graph paper
- Rulers
- “Proportional or Not?” task handout
- Exit ticket

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: Which Deal Is Better? — 7 minutes

Display the following:

- Store A: 3 notebooks for $6  
- Store B: 5 notebooks for $12  
- Store C: 4 notebooks for $8

Ask students:  
“Which store gives the best deal? How do you know?”

Students work independently for 2 minutes, then share with a partner. Invite a few students to explain. Guide students toward finding the unit rate, or cost per notebook.

Expected responses:
- Store A: $6 ÷ 3 = $2 per notebook
- Store B: $12 ÷ 5 = $2.40 per notebook
- Store C: $8 ÷ 4 = $2 per notebook

Explain: “Today we are studying proportional relationships. One way to recognize them is by checking whether the ratio between two quantities stays the same.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Introduce the definition:

A **proportional relationship** is a relationship between two quantities where the ratio between them is constant. This constant ratio is called the **constant of proportionality**.

Write the equation form:

\[
y = kx
\]

Explain:
- \(x\) and \(y\) are related quantities.
- \(k\) is the constant of proportionality.
- The graph of a proportional relationship is a straight line that passes through the origin \((0,0)\).

Use this example:

A runner travels 2 miles every 20 minutes.

Create a table:

| Time, x | Distance, y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 20 | 2 |
| 40 | 4 |
| 60 | 6 |

Ask: “What stays the same?”  
Students should notice that the runner travels 1 mile every 10 minutes, or 0.1 mile per minute.

Show the equation:

\[
y = 0.1x
\]

Then sketch the graph, emphasizing that the points form a straight line through the origin.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Multiple Representations — 12 minutes

Give students this situation:

“A car wash charges $7 for each car washed.”

As a class, create five representations.

1. **Verbal description:** Each car costs $7 to wash.
2. **Table:**

| Cars, x | Cost, y |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0 |
| 1 | 7 |
| 2 | 14 |
| 3 | 21 |
| 4 | 28 |

3. **Equation:**

\[
y = 7x
\]

4. **Graph:** Students plot the points on graph paper and draw a line through the points and the origin.

5. **Diagram:** Draw groups of $7 for each car, such as one car matched with seven dollar symbols.

Ask students:
- “Where do you see the constant of proportionality?”
- “How do the table, graph, equation, and diagram show the same relationship?”
- “Why does the graph need to pass through \((0,0)\)?”

Clarify that if zero cars are washed, the cost should be zero dollars. That is why the origin matters in a proportional relationship.

---

### 4. Partner Activity: Proportional or Not? — 15 minutes

Students work in pairs on four examples. They must decide whether each relationship is proportional and represent at least one proportional relationship in two different ways.

Example A:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 2 | 6 |
| 4 | 12 |
| 6 | 18 |
| 8 | 24 |

Example B:

| x | y |
|---|---|
| 1 | 5 |
| 2 | 9 |
| 3 | 13 |
| 4 | 17 |

Example C:  
A babysitter charges $10 per hour plus a one-time $5 transportation fee.

Example D:  
A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.

Students answer:
1. Is it proportional? Why or why not?
2. What is the constant of proportionality, if there is one?
3. How could you represent the relationship with a table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal description?

Bring the class together to discuss. Emphasize:
- Example A is proportional because \(y/x = 3\) every time.
- Example B is not proportional because the ratio does not stay constant.
- Example C is not proportional because the equation would be \(y = 10x + 5\), not \(y = kx\), and it does not pass through the origin.
- Example D is proportional because the flour-to-sugar ratio stays the same.

### CRI3a: Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices

\]

Collect exit tickets to determine who can recognize proportional relationships and who needs more practice distinguishing proportional from non-proportional relationships.

# Lesson Plan: Proportional Relationships (CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2)

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Setting:** Traditional Classroom

## Learning Objectives
Students will recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions, while developing critical thinking skills.

## Materials
Graph paper, rulers, calculators, handout with sample scenarios, exit ticket slips.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up & Clarifying Information (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI1a*
**Activity:** Display two scenarios:
- **Scenario A:** "A car travels 60 miles in 1 hour, 120 miles in 2 hours, 180 miles in 3 hours."
- **Scenario B:** "A baby weighs 8 lbs at birth, 15 lbs at 6 months, 30 lbs at 2 years."

Ask: "Which describes a proportional relationship? What information do we need to *clarify* to decide?"

Students turn-and-talk for 2 minutes, then share. Guide them to articulate that proportionality requires a *constant ratio* between two quantities. Write the definition precisely: **y = kx**, where k is the constant of proportionality.

**Assessment for CRI1a:** Listen for students who identify the relevant feature (constant ratio) and articulate it clearly. Use a clipboard checklist — Level 2 if students precisely distinguish proportional from non-proportional reasoning (e.g., "Are we comparing miles to hours, and does the ratio stay the same?").

---

### 2. Direct Instruction & Organizing Information (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI1b*
Introduce the **five representations**: verbal description, table, graph, equation, and diagram. Using Scenario A, model how to organize the same relationship across all formats on a graphic organizer.

Demonstrate:
- **Verbal:** "Miles equal 60 times hours"
- **Table:** Hours/Miles columns (0,0), (1,60), (2,120), (3,180)
- **Equation:** y = 60x
- **Graph:** Straight line through the origin
- **Diagram:** A double number line or grouped icons showing 60 miles per hour

Emphasize *why* the graph must pass through (0,0): if zero hours pass, zero miles are traveled.

**Assessment for CRI1b:** Collect students' graphic organizers. Look for logical flow — does information across representations align? Level 2 students arrange data so each representation reinforces the others.

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Assessing Validity & Quality (10 minutes) — *Targets CRI2a & CRI2b*
**Activity:** Project two graphs of a "30 miles per gallon" claim. Graph A is a jagged line with inconsistent intervals; Graph B is a straight line through the origin matching the data. Also include a fictional student work sample using a table with inconsistent ratios.

In pairs, students must **cross-check each claim** by verifying table ratios AND checking whether the graph passes through the origin.

Then introduce an unreliable claim: *"A friend says if you drive downhill, you get 100 miles per gallon."* Students evaluate whether this fits the proportional model y = 30x.

**Assessment for CRI2a:** Circulate and ask, "How did you confirm this?" Level 2 students use *multiple* representations to cross-check (table ratios + graph + equation).

**Assessment for CRI2b:** Review written justifications. Level 2 students reject the outlier claim, articulating that introducing variable conditions breaks the constant proportional relationship — citing specific evidence ("y/x is not constant: 3/1 ≠ 7/2") rather than vague reasoning.

---

### 4. Collaborative Task: Weighing Alternatives & Reasoning (12 minutes) — *Targets CRI3a & CRI4a*
**Real-world problem:** "You're choosing between two phone plans:
- **Plan X:** $0.10 per minute, no monthly fee
- **Plan Y:** $5 monthly fee + $0.05 per minute"

In groups of 3, students must:
1. Determine which plan represents a proportional relationship (Plan X) and explain why Plan Y is not (y = 0.05x + 5 does not pass through the origin).
2. Create tables, graphs, and equations for both.
3. Recommend a plan for a user who talks 50 min/month vs. 200 min/month.
4. Write a justification weighing trade-offs.

**Assessment for CRI3a:** Read group justifications. Level 2 groups systematically evaluate trade-offs across multiple usage scenarios and consider long-term implications (e.g., "For heavy users, Plan Y saves money despite the fee; the break-even point is at 100 minutes").

**Assessment for CRI4a:** Look for logical reasoning chains. Level 2 students anticipate complexities — identifying the break-even point and noting assumptions such as taxes or overage fees that could alter the relationship.

---

### 5. Independent Reflection & Exit Ticket (8 minutes) — *Targets CRI5a*
Students complete a two-part exit ticket:

**Part A (Content):** Given the equation y = 4x, create a table, graph, and verbal description.

**Part B (Metacognitive Reflection):** Answer in 3–4 sentences:
- "Which representation was easiest for you to understand proportional relationships? Why?"
- "What confused you at the start of class that makes sense now? What strategy helped?"
- "If you saw a new proportional problem tomorrow, what would you do differently?"

**Assessment for CRI5a:** Level 2 responses demonstrate genuine metacognition — students name specific strategies they adjusted (e.g., "I used to just check one ratio, but now I check all of them and look at whether the graph passes through zero"). Level 1 responses mention reasoning without depth. Level -1 responses show no awareness of growth.

---

### 6. Closure (5 minutes)
Quick whole-class debrief: Call on 3 students to share one insight. Reinforce the key takeaway: **Proportional relationships have a constant ratio (k), pass through the origin on a graph, and can be written as y = kx.**

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide partially completed tables for struggling learners.
- **Extension:** Challenge advanced students to create a non-proportional scenario that "looks" proportional (e.g., a babysitter charging $10/hour plus a $5 transportation fee) and explain why it's deceptive.

## Homework
Find one real-world proportional relationship at home (recipes, prices, etc.) and represent it in all five formats.
