## Candidate 3
## 55-Minute Lesson: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships

**Grade:** 7  
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.  
**Objective:** Students will determine whether relationships are proportional and represent proportional relationships using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and words.  
**Materials:** Graph paper, rulers, calculators, chart paper or board, student handout, exit ticket.

---

### Learning Targets

Students will be able to:

1. Identify whether a relationship is proportional.
2. Find and interpret the constant of proportionality.
3. Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
4. Explain their reasoning using evidence from multiple representations.

---

## Lesson Sequence

### 1. Warm-Up: “Is It Proportional?” — 5 minutes

Display two situations:

**Situation A:** A taxi charges $3 to start and $2 per mile.  
**Situation B:** Apples cost $2 per pound.

Ask students to silently answer:

- Which relationship is proportional?
- How do you know?

Students share with a partner, then the class discusses. Guide students toward the idea that proportional relationships have a constant ratio and can be written as `y = kx`. Situation B is proportional because the cost per pound is constant and there is no starting fee. Situation A is not proportional because of the initial $3 fee.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1a: Clarifying information**  
Activity moment: Students identify the important information: starting fee, cost per mile, cost per pound.  
Observation/assessment: Listen for students clarifying relevant details, such as “The taxi has a starting fee, so the total is not just miles times a constant.” Written evidence might include: “Apples are proportional because every pound adds $2 and it starts at 0 pounds, $0.”

---

### 2. Direct Instruction: What Makes a Relationship Proportional? — 10 minutes

Explain that a proportional relationship means two quantities increase or decrease at the same rate. It can be represented by:

- **Table:** The ratio `y/x` is constant.
- **Graph:** The graph is a straight line through the origin.
- **Equation:** It can be written as `y = kx`.
- **Diagram:** Equal groups or scale diagrams show repeated copies of the same ratio.
- **Verbal description:** The situation describes “for each” or “per” with no added starting amount.

Use the example: “A cyclist travels 12 miles in 2 hours.”

Create a table:

| Hours | Miles |
|---|---|
| 1 | 6 |
| 2 | 12 |
| 3 | 18 |
| 4 | 24 |

Ask students to find the constant of proportionality: `k = 6`. Then write the equation: `m = 6h`. Sketch the graph and emphasize that it goes through `(0,0)`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI1b: Organizing information**  
Activity moment: Students record the same relationship in multiple organized forms.  
Observation/assessment: Check whether students arrange information logically: table values match the equation, graph points match the table, and the verbal description matches the constant rate. Evidence might include: “I organized hours as x-values and miles as y-values, then used the rule miles = 6 × hours.”

---

### 3. Guided Practice: Representation Stations — 15 minutes

Give students a handout with three relationships:

1. **Relationship 1:** A recipe uses 3 cups of flour for every 2 cups of sugar.  
2. **Relationship 2:** A gym charges a $10 sign-up fee plus $5 per visit.  
3. **Relationship 3:** A download uses 4 megabytes every 2 seconds.

For each relationship, students complete:

- Is it proportional? Why or why not?
- Table
- Equation if proportional
- Graph sketch
- Verbal explanation

Work through Relationship 1 as a class. Students then complete Relationships 2 and 3 with partners.

For Relationship 2, students should conclude it is not proportional because of the $10 initial fee. Its equation would be `y = 5x + 10`, not `y = kx`.

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**  
Activity moment: Students cross-check whether a relationship is proportional using more than one representation.  
Observation/assessment: Look for students verifying their decision with table ratios, graph behavior, and equation form. Evidence might include: “I checked the table and the equation. The gym is not proportional because the graph would not go through the origin.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**  
Activity moment: Students decide which information is most useful for proving proportionality.  
Observation/assessment: Students should choose mathematical evidence, not vague impressions. Strong evidence: “The ratio megabytes/seconds is always 2, so the relationship is proportional.” Weak evidence: “It looks like it goes up.”

---

### 4. Partner Challenge: Choose the Best Representation — 10 minutes

Pose this problem:

“A school store sells notebooks in packs. 2 packs cost $5, 4 packs cost $10, and 6 packs cost $15.”

Partners must decide which representation best proves the relationship is proportional: table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal explanation. They must explain the pros and cons of at least two choices.

Example response: “A table is useful because we can easily compare `cost/packs`. A graph is useful because a straight line through the origin shows proportionality. The equation `c = 2.5p` is most efficient once we know the constant.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**  
Activity moment: Students compare representations and decide which is most convincing.  
Observation/assessment: Look for comparison language: “The table helps because… but the graph helps because….” Evidence might include: “The graph is visual, but the equation is faster for predicting new values.”

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**  
Activity moment: Students justify their selected representation using mathematical reasoning.  
Observation/assessment: Check that conclusions are supported by facts: constant ratio, origin, or equation form. Evidence might include: “I chose the equation because every cost equals 2.5 times the number of packs, so the constant of proportionality is 2.5.”

---

### 5. Independent Practice — 8 minutes

Students complete two problems independently.

**Problem A:** A car travels 180 miles in 3 hours at a constant speed. Represent the relationship with a table, equation, graph, and sentence.

Expected: `m = 60h`; proportional.

**Problem B:** A phone plan costs $25 per month plus $0.10 per text. Is this proportional? Explain.

Expected: Not proportional; there is a starting monthly fee.

Teacher circulates and asks probing questions:

- “What is the constant of proportionality?”
- “How does your graph show proportionality?”
- “Does this situation start at zero?”

---

### 6. Reflection and Exit Ticket — 7 minutes

Exit ticket:

1. Explain one way to tell if a relationship is proportional from a table.  
2. Explain one way to tell from a graph.  
3. Which representation helped you most today, and why?  
4. What mistake might someone make when deciding if a relationship is proportional?

**Critical Thinking Focus — CRI5a: Reflecting on one’s thinking processes**  
Activity moment: Students reflect on how they decided and what errors to avoid.  
Observation/assessment: Look for metacognitive statements about strategy and adjustment. Evidence might include: “At first I thought the gym plan was proportional because it increased by 5, but then I noticed it did not start at 0.”

---

## Assessment

Use student handouts, partner explanations, and exit tickets to assign evidence-based levels from -1 to 2 for each critical thinking subcompetency:

- **CRI1a:** Did the student clarify relevant information accurately?
- **CRI1b:** Did the student organize representations logically?
- **CRI2a:** Did the student check proportionality using multiple methods?
- **CRI2b:** Did the student use high-quality mathematical evidence?
- **CRI3a:** Did the student compare strengths and weaknesses of representations?
- **CRI4a:** Did the student justify conclusions with sound reasoning?
- **CRI5a:** Did the student reflect on their own thinking or correct misconceptions?

Final success criterion: Students can accurately recognize and represent proportional relationships across tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions.
