## Candidate 1
# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Five Ways

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify whether a relationship is proportional using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions
- Justify their reasoning using the constant of proportionality (unit rate)
- Evaluate and compare multiple representations of the same relationship

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard / projector
- "Lemonade Stand" activity sheet (one per student)
- Graph paper
- Colored markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Hook – Pose the Problem (5 minutes)

Display two scenarios side by side:

> **Scenario A:** A lemonade stand charges $2 per cup.
> **Scenario B:** A lemonade stand charges $1 for the first cup, then $3 for each additional cup.

Ask: *"Are either of these proportional relationships? How do you know?"* Allow 60 seconds of partner talk, then take two or three quick responses. Do not confirm answers yet — tell students they will have the tools to decide by the end of class.

**CRI1a – Clarifying Information**
*Activity:* Students must articulate what "proportional" means in their own words before instruction begins. Push them with: *"What exactly do you mean by that?"*
*Assessment:* Listen for whether students distinguish between a constant ratio and a changing ratio. Partial clarity (Level 1) sounds like "the numbers go up together." Precise clarity (Level 2) sounds like "the ratio between cups and cost stays the same every time."

---

### Direct Instruction – The Five Representations (12 minutes)

Walk through Scenario A using all five representations on the board simultaneously, building each one deliberately:

1. **Verbal:** "Two dollars for every cup."
2. **Table:** Cups (1, 2, 3, 4) → Cost ($2, $4, $6, $8). Check that cost ÷ cups = 2 every time.
3. **Equation:** *y = 2x*, where *k = 2* is the constant of proportionality.
4. **Graph:** Plot the ordered pairs; draw the line through the origin. Emphasize: *a proportional graph always passes through (0, 0).*
5. **Diagram:** A ratio diagram showing cups : cost as 1 : 2, scaling up.

Then display Scenario B's table alongside Scenario A's. Ask: *"Where does the proportional test break down for Scenario B?"*

**CRI1b – Organizing Information**
*Activity:* The five representations are introduced in a deliberate sequence — verbal → table → equation → graph → diagram — so each one reinforces the last.
*Assessment:* Ask students to copy the representations into their notes in the same sequence and label each. Students at Level 2 will annotate connections between them (e.g., drawing an arrow from *k* in the equation to the slope on the graph). Students at Level 1 will copy without connecting.

---

### Guided Practice – "Is It Proportional?" Card Sort (15 minutes)

Pairs receive six cards, each showing a different representation (two tables, two graphs, two equations). Students sort them into "Proportional" or "Not Proportional" and record one sentence of evidence for each card.

**CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information**
*Activity:* Students must verify each card rather than guess by appearance. A graph that looks linear but does not pass through the origin is intentionally included as a trap.
*Assessment:* Circulate and ask: *"How did you check that one?"* Level 2 students cross-check — they verify the table ratio AND confirm the graph passes through (0, 0). Level 1 students check only one condition.

**CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information**
*Activity:* After sorting, students choose their two most convincing pieces of evidence and explain why those pieces are more reliable than the others.
*Assessment:* Level 2 students articulate why a consistent ratio across all rows of a table is stronger evidence than checking only the first row. Level 1 students pick evidence without explaining its strength.

---

### Collaborative Discussion – Weigh the Representations (10 minutes)

Whole-class discussion prompt: *"If you had to convince a skeptical friend that a relationship is proportional, which representation would you use — and which would you avoid? Why?"*

**CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
*Activity:* Students debate which representation is most persuasive and acknowledge weaknesses in their chosen format (e.g., a table only shows selected values; a graph can be misread if unlabeled).
*Assessment:* Level 2 responses name a trade-off explicitly: *"A graph is easy to see, but it doesn't prove every ratio is equal — the table proves it more rigorously."*

**CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
*Activity:* Students must justify their representation choice with a logical argument, not just personal preference.
*Assessment:* Level 2 students anticipate a counter-argument: *"You might say the equation is simplest, but if someone doesn't know algebra, the table communicates more clearly."* Level 1 students give a reason without anticipating pushback.

---

### Exit Ticket – Reflect and Self-Assess (8 minutes)

Students independently complete a three-part exit ticket:

1. Create your own proportional relationship and show it in three representations.
2. Circle the one representation you found hardest to produce.
3. Write two sentences: *What confused you, and what would you do differently next time?*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
*Activity:* Part 3 explicitly asks students to name a confusion and a correction strategy — not just what they got wrong, but how they would adjust.
*Assessment:* Level 2 responses demonstrate genuine metacognition: *"I kept forgetting to check whether the graph crossed (0, 0). Next time I'll check the origin first, before I check the slope."* Level 1 responses name the error without a strategy: *"I messed up the graph."*

---

### Closing (5 minutes)

Return to the opening hook. Cold-call two students to now definitively answer whether Scenario A and B are proportional, citing at least one representation as evidence. Confirm answers and preview: *"Tomorrow we apply this to real data — which may be messy."*

---

## Differentiation
- **Support:** Provide a partially completed table and a checklist of the three proportionality tests.
- **Extension:** Ask students to create a scenario that *looks* proportional in a table but fails on the graph, and explain why.

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Plan: Recognizing and Representing Proportional Relationships**

**Subject:** Mathematics
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Time Allotment:** 55 minutes
**Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2 — Recognize and represent proportional relationships between quantities (in tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions).

**Learning Objectives:**
Students will be able to:
1.  Define a proportional relationship.
2.  Identify proportional relationships from tables, graphs, and verbal descriptions.
3.  Represent proportional relationships in tables, graphs, and equations.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers/pens
*   Student handouts with practice problems (including tables, graphs, and verbal scenarios)
*   Graph paper
*   Rulers
*   Exit tickets

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**I. Introduction & Hook (7 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Good morning, mathematicians! Today we're going to explore relationships between quantities. Imagine you're baking cookies. For every 1 cup of flour, you need 2 eggs. If you use 2 cups of flour, how many eggs do you need? What if you use 3 cups?"
*   **Students:** (Respond with 4 eggs, 6 eggs)
*   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about how the number of eggs changes compared to the amount of flour?"
*   **Teacher:** Introduce the term "proportional relationship" as a special kind of relationship where two quantities change at a constant rate.

    *   **CRI1a: Clarifying information**
        *   **Activity:** After the initial cookie example, ask students to work in pairs for 1 minute to define "proportional" in their own words based on the example. Then, have a few pairs share their definitions, and guide the class to refine a clear, concise definition of a proportional relationship, emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can accurately rephrase the problem and identify the two quantities being compared (flour and eggs). Listen for precise language in their definitions, such as "when one quantity doubles, the other doubles" or "there's a consistent multiplier."

**II. Direct Instruction & Guided Practice (18 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Proportional relationships can be shown in many ways: tables, graphs, and equations. Let's look at our cookie example."
*   **Tables:**
    *   Create a table on the board:
        | Flour (cups) | Eggs |
        | :----------- | :--- |
        | 1            | 2    |
        | 2            | 4    |
        | 3            | 6    |
    *   **Teacher:** "What do you notice about the ratio of eggs to flour in each row? (2/1, 4/2, 6/3). They all simplify to 2. This constant ratio is called the 'constant of proportionality'."
*   **Graphs:**
    *   **Teacher:** "Let's plot these points on a graph. What do you notice about the shape of the graph?" (It's a straight line passing through the origin (0,0)). Explain that this is a key characteristic of proportional relationships on a graph.
*   **Equations:**
    *   **Teacher:** "How can we write an equation to represent this? If 'y' is eggs and 'x' is flour, how do we get 'y' from 'x'?" (y = 2x). Explain that 'k' (the constant of proportionality) is the number we multiply 'x' by, so the general form is y = kx.

    *   **CRI1b: Organizing information**
        *   **Activity:** As the class moves from the verbal description to a table, then a graph, then an equation, guide students to systematically record the information. For the graph, explicitly instruct them on labeling axes, choosing an appropriate scale, and plotting points accurately.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Check student notes or a quick whiteboard check for correctly structured tables (e.g., independent variable first, consistent increments) and properly labeled graphs with clear scales and plotted points that accurately reflect the data.

**III. Collaborative Activity: "Proportionality Puzzles" (20 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** "Now it's your turn to be detectives of proportionality!"
*   Divide students into small groups (3-4 students).
*   Provide each group with a handout containing 3-4 different scenarios. Each scenario will include one representation (e.g., a verbal description, a table, or a graph).
*   **Task:** For each scenario, students must:
    1.  Determine if the relationship is proportional.
    2.  If it is proportional, represent it in the *other two* forms (e.g., if given a table, create a graph and an equation).
    3.  If it is *not* proportional, explain why.

    *   **CRI2a: Assessing validity of information**
        *   **Activity:** One of the scenarios will present a table and a *mismatched* graph (e.g., the graph doesn't accurately represent the table's data, or it's for a different scenario). Students must identify this inconsistency and explain why one representation is invalid compared to the other.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can identify discrepancies between representations and explain *why* one might be invalid or inconsistent with another (e.g., "The points on this graph don't match the (x,y) pairs in our table," or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio").

    *   **CRI2b: Assessing quality of information**
        *   **Activity:** Provide one scenario with a verbal description that is vague or includes irrelevant information (e.g., "A baker makes cookies. He uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He also bakes cakes on Tuesdays, and his oven temperature is 350 degrees."). Students must identify the relevant information needed to determine proportionality and disregard the irrelevant details.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Observe if students can filter out the extraneous information and focus only on the quantities that form a potential proportional relationship. Ask groups to articulate *why* certain pieces of information were ignored (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio, so it's not relevant to proportionality").

    *   **CRI3a: Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices**
        *   **Activity:** After completing the representations for a proportional scenario, ask groups to discuss: "Which representation (table, graph, or equation) do you think is most useful for *this specific scenario* and why? What are the advantages and disadvantages of using a graph versus an equation for this problem?"
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Listen for students comparing the utility of different representations. For example, they might say, "The graph is good because you can quickly visualize the trend and estimate values," or "The equation is better for precisely calculating the number of eggs for 100 cups of flour without extending the table or graph."

    *   **CRI4a: Applying sound reasoning to decision-making**
        *   **Activity:** For each scenario, students must make a decision: "Is this proportional or not?" and then justify their answer using evidence from the given representation (e.g., "It is proportional because the ratio y/x is constant for all points," or "It is not proportional because the graph does not pass through the origin and is not a straight line").
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Evaluate the clarity and logical soundness of their justifications. Do they correctly apply the definition of proportionality (constant ratio, straight line through origin) to support their conclusion? Look for explanations that directly link evidence to the definition.

**IV. Wrap-up & Exit Ticket (10 minutes)**

*   **Teacher:** Bring the class back together. Review one or two of the scenarios from the group activity, having groups share their findings and reasoning.
*   **Teacher:** "Today we learned about proportional relationships and how to recognize and represent them in different ways."
*   **Exit Ticket:** Provide students with an exit ticket.
    *   **Question 1:** "A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not."
    *   **Question 2:** "What was the most challenging part of identifying or representing proportional relationships today? What strategy did you use to overcome it, or what would you do differently next time?"

    *   **CRI5a: Reflecting on one's thinking processes**
        *   **Activity:** Question 2 on the exit ticket directly prompts students to reflect on their learning process, identify difficulties, and consider strategies for improvement.
        *   **Observation/Assessment:** Read student responses. Look for evidence of metacognitive awareness, such as identifying specific conceptual hurdles (e.g., "I struggled with remembering that graphs must go through the origin, not just be a straight line") and proposing concrete strategies (e.g., "Next time, I will always check the ratio y/x for every point in the table before deciding if it's proportional"). Avoid generic answers like "it was hard" or "I'll try harder."

  - **Graphs:** points forming a straight line through the origin  
  - **Equations:** y = kx, where k is the constant of proportionality

- Circulate and prompt students to explain their reasoning.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetencies:**  
- *CRI2a (Assessing validity of information)*: Students evaluate if the given data truly represents a proportional relationship by checking for a constant ratio.  
- *CRI4a (Applying sound reasoning to decision-making)*: Students justify their representations and decisions about proportionality.

---

**4. Class Discussion (10 minutes)**  
- Invite pairs to share one example and explain how they recognized the proportional relationship and converted it between forms.  
- Discuss common misconceptions (e.g., relationships that are not proportional because the ratio changes).  
- Highlight the importance of checking the constant ratio and the graph passing through the origin.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetency:**  
- *CRI3a (Weighing pros and cons of alternative choices)*: Students compare different representations and discuss which might be easier or more useful in various contexts.

---

**5. Independent Practice (10 minutes)**  
- Students individually complete a short set of problems requiring them to:  
  - Identify proportional relationships from verbal descriptions or tables  
  - Create graphs and write equations representing those relationships  
- Encourage students to reflect on their thinking process as they work.

**Targeted Critical Thinking Subcompetency:**  
- *CRI5a (Reflecting on one’s thinking processes)*: Students write a brief note on how they decided if a relationship was proportional and how they chose which representations to use.

---

**6. Exit Ticket (5 minutes)**  
- Prompt: “Explain in your own words what makes two quantities proportional and give an example in any form (table, graph, equation, diagram, or verbal).”  
- Collect to assess understanding and critical thinking.

# Proportional Relationships: Recognizing and Representing Them Five Ways

**Grade:** 7 | **Duration:** 55 minutes | **Standard:** CCSS.Math.Content.7.RP.A.2

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of the lesson, students will be able to:
- Identify whether a relationship is proportional using tables, graphs, equations, diagrams, and verbal descriptions
- Justify their reasoning using the constant of proportionality (unit rate)
- Evaluate and compare multiple representations of the same relationship

---

## Materials
- Whiteboard / projector
- "Lemonade Stand" activity sheet (one per student)
- "Proportionality Puzzles" group handout (one per group)
- Graph paper and rulers
- Colored markers
- Exit ticket slips

---

## Lesson Outline

### Hook – Pose the Problem (7 minutes)

Display two scenarios side by side:

> **Scenario A:** A lemonade stand charges $2 per cup.
> **Scenario B:** A lemonade stand charges $1 for the first cup, then $3 for each additional cup.

Ask: *"Are either of these proportional relationships? How do you know?"* Allow 60 seconds of partner talk, then take two or three quick responses. Do not confirm answers yet — tell students they will have the tools to decide by the end of class.

**CRI1a – Clarifying Information**
*Activity:* Students must articulate what "proportional" means in their own words before formal instruction begins. Push them with: *"What exactly do you mean by that?"* Guide the class to refine a precise definition emphasizing the idea of a constant ratio.
*Assessment:* Listen for whether students distinguish between a constant ratio and a changing ratio. Partial clarity (Level 1) sounds like "the numbers go up together." Precise clarity (Level 2) sounds like "the ratio between cups and cost stays the same every time, no matter how many cups you buy."

---

### Direct Instruction – The Five Representations (13 minutes)

Walk through Scenario A using all five representations on the board simultaneously, building each one deliberately in a logical sequence from concrete to abstract:

1. **Verbal:** "Two dollars for every cup."
2. **Table:** Cups (1, 2, 3, 4) → Cost ($2, $4, $6, $8). Check that cost ÷ cups = 2 every time.
3. **Diagram:** A ratio/tape diagram showing cups : cost as 1 : 2, scaling up.
4. **Graph:** Plot the ordered pairs; draw the line through the origin. Emphasize: *a proportional graph always passes through (0, 0).*
5. **Equation:** *y = 2x*, where *k = 2* is the constant of proportionality and the general form is *y = kx*.

Then display Scenario B's table alongside Scenario A's. Ask: *"Where does the proportional test break down for Scenario B?"*

**CRI1b – Organizing Information**
*Activity:* The five representations are introduced in a deliberate sequence — verbal → table → diagram → graph → equation — so each one reinforces the last, moving from concrete to abstract.
*Assessment:* Ask students to copy the representations into their notes in the same sequence and label each. Students at Level 2 will annotate connections between them (e.g., drawing an arrow from *k* in the equation to the slope on the graph, or noting that the table's constant ratio matches the graph's passage through the origin). Students at Level 1 will copy without connecting the representations to one another.

---

### Collaborative Activity – "Proportionality Puzzles" (15 minutes)

Groups of 3–4 receive a handout with four scenarios, each presenting one representation (a verbal description, a table, or a graph). For each scenario, students must:
1. Determine if the relationship is proportional.
2. If proportional, represent it in two additional forms.
3. If not proportional, explain why using specific evidence.

**Built-in traps and challenges:**
- **Trap card (CRI2a):** One card shows a table with a constant ratio alongside a *mismatched graph* that does not pass through the origin. Students must identify the inconsistency and explain which representation is invalid.
- **Noise card (CRI2b):** One card provides a verbal description containing irrelevant details (e.g., "A baker uses 2 cups of flour for every 4 eggs. He bakes on Tuesdays, and his oven runs at 350°F."). Students must isolate the relevant quantities and disregard extraneous information.

**CRI2a – Assessing Validity of Information**
*Activity:* Students verify each card rather than guess by appearance. The trap graph is intentionally included to expose the difference between a graph that looks linear and one that is truly proportional.
*Assessment:* Circulate and ask: *"How did you check that one?"* Level 2 students cross-check — they verify the table ratio AND confirm the graph passes through (0, 0). Level 1 students check only one condition. Listen for explanations such as "The points on this graph don't match the pairs in our table" or "The graph doesn't go through the origin, but the table shows a constant ratio."

**CRI2b – Assessing Quality of Information**
*Activity:* After completing the noise card, students articulate which pieces of information were relevant and why the rest was discarded.
*Assessment:* Level 2 students explain why certain details are irrelevant (e.g., "The oven temperature doesn't affect the flour-to-egg ratio"). Level 1 students filter the information but cannot explain the reasoning behind their selection.

---

### Collaborative Discussion – Weigh the Representations (8 minutes)

Whole-class discussion prompt: *"If you had to convince a skeptical friend that a relationship is proportional, which representation would you use — and which would you avoid? What are the trade-offs?"*

**CRI3a – Weighing Pros and Cons of Alternative Choices**
*Activity:* Students debate which representation is most persuasive and acknowledge weaknesses in their chosen format (e.g., a table only shows selected values; a graph can be misread if unlabeled; an equation requires algebraic literacy).
*Assessment:* Level 2 responses name a trade-off explicitly: *"A graph is easy to see, but it doesn't prove every ratio is equal — the table proves it more rigorously."* Or: *"The equation is best for calculating exact values, but if someone doesn't know algebra, the table communicates more clearly."* Level 1 responses express a preference without comparing trade-offs.

**CRI4a – Applying Sound Reasoning to Decision-Making**
*Activity:* Students must justify their representation choice with a logical argument, not just personal preference, and anticipate a counter-argument from a classmate.
*Assessment:* Level 2 students demonstrate anticipatory reasoning: *"You might say the equation is simplest, but if someone doesn't know algebra, the table communicates more directly."* Level 1 students give a reason without anticipating pushback or counter-evidence.

---

### Exit Ticket – Reflect and Self-Assess (8 minutes)

Students independently complete a three-part exit ticket:

1. A car travels 60 miles in 2 hours, 90 miles in 3 hours, and 120 miles in 4 hours. Is this a proportional relationship? Explain why or why not using at least one representation.
2. Create your own proportional relationship and show it in two representations of your choice.
3. Write two sentences: *What was the most challenging part of today's lesson, and what strategy would you use differently next time?*

**CRI5a – Reflecting on One's Thinking Processes**
*Activity:* Part 3 explicitly asks students to name a specific confusion and a concrete correction strategy — not just what they got wrong, but how they would adjust their approach.
*Assessment:* Level 2 responses demonstrate genuine metacognition: *"I kept forgetting to check whether the graph crossed (0, 0). Next time I'll check the origin first, before I check whether the line is straight."* Level 1 responses name the error without a forward-looking strategy: *"I messed up the graph."* Level -1 responses show no awareness of error at all.
