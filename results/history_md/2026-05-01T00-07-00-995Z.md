## Candidate 1
# The Curiosity Argument: Why Questions Make Better Writers

**Grade Level:** 7th Grade | **Time:** 55 Minutes | **Materials:** Notebook, pencil, sticky notes (3 per student)

---

## HOOK — *What You Don't Know Can Help You* (8 minutes)

Walk in and write this single sentence on the board:

**"Dogs make better pets than cats."**

Ask students: *"Raise your hand if you agree. Now raise your hand if you disagree."*

Then say: *"Everyone in this room just had an opinion. But here's the problem — an opinion without curiosity is just noise. Today we're going to turn your opinions into arguments, and the secret ingredient is asking better questions."*

Tell students: Curiosity isn't just a personality trait. It's a **writing tool**. The most powerful arguments aren't written by people who already have all the answers — they're written by people who kept asking questions until they understood something deeply enough to defend it.

---

## DIRECT INSTRUCTION — *The Anatomy of a Curious Argument* (12 minutes)

Introduce the **4-Part Argument Framework** on the board:

1. **Claim** — What do you believe?
2. **Reasons & Evidence** — What makes you believe it?
3. **Opposing Claim** — What would someone who disagrees say?
4. **Rebuttal** — Why does your argument still hold up?

Explain: Most students nail parts 1 and 2 and completely skip parts 3 and 4. Why? Because looking at the other side feels uncomfortable. But here's the truth — **the opposing claim is where curiosity lives.**

Model this with the dogs vs. cats example:

- *Claim:* Dogs make better pets for most families.
- *Evidence:* Studies show dog owners walk more, report lower stress, and dogs can be trained for safety.
- *Opposing Claim:* Some argue cats are better because they're lower maintenance and ideal for small living spaces.
- *Rebuttal:* While cats suit certain lifestyles, the social and health benefits dogs provide make them the stronger choice for most households.

Ask students: *"How could I have written that opposing claim if I never got curious about why cat lovers feel the way they do?"*

Answer: You couldn't. Acknowledging the other side requires you to genuinely wonder, *"What do they know that I might be missing?"*

---

## GUIDED PRACTICE — *The Sticky Note Curiosity Challenge* (15 minutes)

Give each student 3 sticky notes.

Present this prompt on the board:

**"Middle school students should be required to take a class on financial literacy."**

**Round 1 (3 minutes):** On sticky note #1, students write their gut-reaction claim — agree or disagree — in one sentence.

**Round 2 (5 minutes):** On sticky note #2, students write 3 questions they'd need answered before they could fully argue their side. Push them: *"What don't you know yet? What would change your mind? Who would disagree and why?"*

Share out. Write the most interesting student questions on the board. Point out: **these questions are the engine of a great argument.**

**Round 3 (5 minutes):** On sticky note #3, students write one opposing claim — the strongest possible argument against their own position.

Pair-share. Have two or three students read their opposing claims aloud. Celebrate the ones that are genuinely strong. Say: *"If your opposing claim is weak, your rebuttal means nothing. Curious writers take the other side seriously."*

---

## INDEPENDENT PRACTICE — *Draft Your Argument* (15 minutes)

Students write a structured paragraph using this prompt:

**"Schools should eliminate homework on weekends."**

Require all four parts of the framework. Circulate the room. Look for students who are skipping the opposing claim and coach them with questions: *"What would a parent who disagrees say? What would a teacher worried about falling grades argue?"*

Remind them: *"If you're only talking to people who already agree with you, you're not arguing — you're just talking to a mirror."*

---

## CLOSING — *The Curiosity Commitment* (5 minutes)

Bring students back together. Ask: *"What was harder — writing your own claim or writing the opposing claim?"*

Most will say the opposing claim. Validate that. Then say:

*"That discomfort is exactly where good thinking happens. Curiosity means you're willing to sit in that uncomfortable place long enough to understand something you didn't before. That's not just good writing — that's good thinking for the rest of your life."*

**Exit Ticket:** Students answer in one sentence — *"What question did today's lesson make you want to ask that you didn't have before?"*

Collect exit tickets. Read them tomorrow to open the next class.

---

*Lesson targets CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 through structured argument writing with explicit attention to opposing claims and logical reasoning.*

## Candidate 2
**Lesson Title:** The Curious Case of the Convincing Argument

**Time:** 55 minutes
**Grade Level:** 7th Grade
**Subject:** ELA
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.
**Learning Objective:** Students will understand how curiosity drives the process of developing a strong argumentative claim and initial reasons.

**Materials:**
*   Whiteboard or projector
*   Markers
*   Handout 1: Intriguing image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   Handout 2: Short, intriguing text (e.g., a brief article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   Index cards or small slips of paper (one per student).

---

**Lesson Procedure:**

**1. Hook: What's Going On Here? (7 minutes)**
*   **Display Handout 1 (Intriguing Image):** Project or distribute a captivating image (e.g., a blurry photo of a supposed cryptid, an unusual historical artifact, a strange natural phenomenon).
*   **Teacher:** "Look closely at this image. What's the first thing that pops into your mind? What questions do you have about it?"
*   **Individual Work (2 min):** Students silently jot down initial thoughts and questions.
*   **Share Out (5 min):** "Share some of your questions aloud. Don't worry about answers, just the questions." Facilitate a quick class share-out, highlighting the variety of questions.
*   **Teacher:** "That feeling of wanting to know more, of asking 'why?' or 'how?' or 'what if?' – that's **curiosity**! It's a powerful tool, especially when we're trying to convince someone of something."

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argumentative Writing (10 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we'll explore how curiosity helps us become better argumentative writers. Argumentative writing isn't just stating an opinion; it's convincing others your opinion is valid using strong reasons and evidence."
*   **Introduce Standard (CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1):** "This standard asks us to write arguments that support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, and even consider other viewpoints. Curiosity is the engine for this process."
*   **Teacher Explanation:**
    *   **Finding a topic:** Curiosity helps us find something we care enough about to argue for or against.
    *   **Forming a claim:** Curiosity makes us ask 'Is this true?' or 'Should this be different?' leading to a debatable claim.
    *   **Finding reasons & evidence:** Curiosity pushes us to research, investigate, and dig deeper for facts and examples.
    *   **Considering other views:** Curiosity makes us wonder, 'What do others think? Why might they disagree?'
*   **Teacher:** "Curiosity isn't just a feeling; it's the driving force behind strong arguments."

**3. Activity: The Curiosity Question Generator (15 minutes)**
*   **Distribute Handout 2 (Intriguing Text):** Provide a new handout (e.g., a short article about a local school policy debate, a description of a historical mystery, a controversial public art piece).
*   **Teacher:** "For the next 7 minutes, write down every curious question that comes to mind about this topic. Think 'who, what, when, where, why, how, what if, should...'"
*   **Individual Work (7 min):** Students generate questions.
*   **Pair Share (3 min):** "In pairs, share your questions. See if your partner's questions spark even more curiosity. Add any new questions to your list."
*   **Class Share (5 min):** "Let's hear a few questions from different pairs. What did you wonder?" *Goal: Demonstrate the breadth of questions curiosity can uncover.*

**4. Activity: From Question to Claim & Reason (15 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "From these curious questions, we can build an argument. An argument starts with a **claim** – your main point or position on an issue. It's an answer to one of your curious questions, stated as a debatable statement."
*   **Formulate Claim (3 min):** "Look at your list of questions. Choose ONE question that you feel strongly about, or that you think you could potentially argue for or against. Now, turn that question into a **claim**. For example, if your question was 'Should our school have a longer lunch break?', your claim might be 'Our school *should* implement a longer lunch break.'"
*   **Identify Reason (5 min):** "Once you have a claim, you need a **reason** to support it. Why do you believe your claim is true? What's one main idea that backs it up?"
*   **Index Card Task:** "On an index card, write your claim clearly on one side. On the other side, write down one strong reason that supports your claim."
*   **Partner Share (4 min):** "Share your claim and reason with a partner. Does their reason make sense? Does it directly support the claim?"
*   **Teacher:** "We've just taken our curiosity, turned it into a question, then into a claim, and found an initial reason. This is the foundation of argumentative writing!"

**5. Wrap-up & Look Ahead (8 minutes)**
*   **Teacher:** "Today, we saw how curiosity is the spark that ignites strong arguments. It helps us ask the right questions, form clear claims, and start thinking about reasons."
*   **Reflection (3 min):** "What was one new thing you learned about curiosity or argumentative writing today?" (Quick share-out or think-pair-share).
*   **Preview (2 min):** "Next time, we'll take these claims and reasons and explore how to find **evidence** to make them even stronger, and how to consider those **alternate or opposing claims** – all driven by our continued curiosity!"
*   **Collect (Optional):** Collect index cards for a quick check of understanding.

## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### Objective:  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a short argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging an opposing viewpoint.

---

### Materials Needed:  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Printed handout with a simple argumentative prompt (provided below)  
- Example paragraph (provided below)  
- Sticky notes or index cards  

---

### Lesson Outline:

**1. Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- Begin by asking students: *“What does curiosity mean to you?”*  
- Write their responses on the board.  
- Explain: Curiosity is the desire to learn, ask questions, and explore new ideas. It helps us discover new things and understand the world better.  
- Share a brief story or example of a famous curious person (e.g., Marie Curie or Thomas Edison) who used curiosity to solve problems.  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science but also in writing and thinking critically.

**2. Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (5 minutes)**  
- Explain that when we write arguments, curiosity helps us ask questions like:  
  - *Why do I believe this?*  
  - *What evidence supports my claim?*  
  - *What might someone who disagrees say?*  
- This curiosity makes our writing stronger and more convincing.

**3. Mini-Lesson: Elements of a Strong Argument (10 minutes)**  
- Write the following on the board:  
  1. **Claim:** Your main point or opinion.  
  2. **Reasons:** Why you believe your claim.  
  3. **Evidence:** Facts, examples, or details that support your reasons.  
  4. **Acknowledging Opposing Claims:** Recognizing other viewpoints.  
  5. **Logical Organization:** Presenting ideas clearly and in order.  
- Read a short example paragraph aloud:  

  *Example:*  
  *Claim:* School uniforms should be required.  
  *Reason 1:* Uniforms reduce bullying because students dress alike.  
  *Evidence:* A study showed schools with uniforms had 30% fewer bullying incidents.  
  *Reason 2:* Uniforms save time in the morning.  
  *Evidence:* Students don’t have to decide what to wear every day.  
  *Opposing claim:* Some say uniforms limit self-expression, but students can express themselves through accessories and activities.  

- Discuss how the writer used curiosity to think about the opposing claim and respond to it.

**4. Guided Practice: Exploring Curiosity Through Questions (10 minutes)**  
- Give students a simple prompt to argue about, for example:  
  *“Should homework be banned in middle school?”*  
- In pairs, students brainstorm:  
  - What questions do they have about homework?  
  - What reasons might support banning homework?  
  - What reasons might oppose banning homework?  
- Encourage them to be curious and ask “why” and “how” questions.  
- Have pairs share a few questions and ideas with the class.

**5. Independent Writing: Crafting a Short Argument (15 minutes)**  
- Students write a short paragraph (5-7 sentences) stating their claim about the homework prompt.  
- They must include:  
  - A clear claim  
  - At least two reasons with evidence or examples  
  - Acknowledge one opposing claim and respond to it  
- Remind them to organize their ideas logically.

**6. Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite 2-3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class to identify the claim, reasons, evidence, and opposing claim in each.  
- End with a quick reflection:  
  *“How did being curious help you write your argument today?”*  
- Collect writing for review.

---

### Homework (Optional):  
Ask students to observe something they are curious about at home or school and write down three questions they have about it. They will bring these questions to the next class to explore further.

---

### Summary:  
This lesson encourages students to embrace curiosity as a tool for deeper thinking and stronger argument writing. By asking questions and considering different viewpoints, students learn to support their claims with clear reasons and evidence, meeting the CCSS writing standard in an engaging and meaningful way.
