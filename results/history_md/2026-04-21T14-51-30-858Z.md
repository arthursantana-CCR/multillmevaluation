## Candidate 3
**Lesson Plan: Cultivating Curiosity and Writing Strong Arguments**  
**Grade:** 7th Grade  
**Duration:** 55 minutes  
**Standard:** CCSS.ELA-Literacy.W.7.1 — Write arguments to support claims with clear reasons and relevant evidence, including acknowledging alternate or opposing claims and organizing reasoning logically.

---

### **Lesson Objective:**  
By the end of the lesson, students will understand the value of curiosity in learning and be able to write a well-organized argumentative paragraph that supports a claim with clear reasons and evidence, while acknowledging opposing viewpoints.

---

### **Materials Needed:**  
- Whiteboard and markers  
- Student notebooks or writing paper  
- Projector or chart paper (optional)  
- Handout: Argument Writing Graphic Organizer (Claim, Reasons, Evidence, Counterclaim, Rebuttal)  
- Short curiosity-themed prompt (provided below)  

---

### **Lesson Outline:**

#### 1. **Introduction to Curiosity (10 minutes)**  
- **Engage:** Begin by asking students:  
  *“What does curiosity mean to you? Can you think of a time when being curious helped you learn something new or solve a problem?”*  
- Write a few student responses on the board.  
- Briefly explain:  
  *Curiosity is the desire to learn or know more about something. It drives us to ask questions, explore new ideas, and think deeply.*  
- Share a quick, relatable example (e.g., a famous inventor or scientist whose curiosity led to an important discovery).  
- Emphasize that curiosity is important not only in science or school but also in everyday life and in forming strong arguments.

---

#### 2. **Connecting Curiosity to Argument Writing (10 minutes)**  
- Explain that curiosity helps us become better thinkers and writers because it encourages us to ask questions like:  
  - *Why do I believe this?*  
  - *What evidence supports my idea?*  
  - *What might someone who disagrees say?*  
- Introduce the standard: Writing arguments means making a claim and supporting it with reasons and evidence, while also considering other viewpoints.  
- Show a simple example on the board:  
  **Claim:** School uniforms should be required.  
  **Reason:** They help reduce bullying.  
  **Evidence:** Studies show schools with uniforms have fewer bullying incidents.  
  **Counterclaim:** Some say uniforms limit self-expression.  
  **Rebuttal:** But students can express themselves through accessories and behavior.  
- Highlight how curiosity helps us think about the counterclaim and rebuttal.

---

#### 3. **Guided Practice: Exploring a Curiosity-Themed Prompt (15 minutes)**  
- Present the prompt:  
  *“Curiosity is the most important quality a student can have.”*  
- Ask students to brainstorm reasons why they agree or disagree with this statement.  
- Use a graphic organizer to help students jot down:  
  - Their claim (agree or disagree)  
  - At least two reasons supporting their claim  
  - Evidence or examples for each reason  
  - One possible opposing claim  
  - A rebuttal to that opposing claim  
- Circulate and assist students as they work.

---

#### 4. **Writing Activity (15 minutes)**  
- Have students write a clear, organized argumentative paragraph based on their graphic organizer.  
- Remind them to:  
  - State their claim clearly in the first sentence  
  - Use reasons and evidence to support their claim  
  - Acknowledge an opposing viewpoint and respond to it  
  - Organize their ideas logically  
- Encourage students to use curiosity-driven questions to deepen their reasoning (e.g., *Why is this important? What evidence can I find? What might someone else think?*).

---

#### 5. **Sharing and Reflection (5 minutes)**  
- Invite a few volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.  
- Ask the class:  
  *“How did thinking curiously help you write your argument? Did considering the other side change your thinking?”*  
- Summarize key takeaways:  
  - Curiosity helps us ask good questions and find strong evidence.  
  - Good arguments consider different viewpoints.  
  - Being curious makes us better learners and communicators.

---

### **Extension/Homework (Optional):**  
- Have students find a news article or opinion piece on a topic they are curious about and write a short argument supporting or opposing the author’s claim, using reasons and evidence.
