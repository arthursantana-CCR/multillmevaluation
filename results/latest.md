## Generator
# Argument Writing: Building a Case That Holds Up

## 7th Grade ELA — 55-Minute Lesson

---

## LESSON OVERVIEW

**Central Question:** What makes an argument convincing enough to change someone's mind?

**Learning Objectives:**
By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:
1. Identify the essential components of a written argument (claim, reasons, evidence, counterclaim, rebuttal)
2. Distinguish between strong and weak evidence
3. Draft a structured argumentative paragraph that includes a claim, at least one reason with evidence, and an acknowledgment of an opposing view

**Materials Needed:**
- Whiteboard or projector
- Printed "Argument Anatomy" graphic organizer (one per student)
- Printed "Strong or Weak Evidence?" sorting cards (one set per pair)
- Printed mentor text: "Should Students Have Homework?" (sample argument — provided below)
- Lined paper or composition notebooks
- Colored pens or highlighters (3 colors per student)

---

## BEFORE CLASS — TEACHER PREPARATION

Print and cut the evidence sorting cards. Write the following five terms on the board before students arrive — leave space next to each for student contributions:

> **CLAIM — REASON — EVIDENCE — COUNTERCLAIM — REBUTTAL**

---

## LESSON SEQUENCE

---

### SEGMENT 1: HOOK — THE COURTROOM CHALLENGE (5 minutes)

**Open with this scenario (read aloud or display):**

> *You have been accused of eating the last piece of pizza. You did not do it. You have 60 seconds to convince the class you are innocent. Go.*

Call on 2–3 volunteers. Let them give their mini-defenses. The class listens, then votes: convinced or not convinced?

**Brief debrief — ask the class:**
- *What did the convincing ones do that worked?*
- *What did the not-so-convincing ones do that fell flat?*
- *Did anyone mention what someone else might say — and then shoot it down?*

**Bridge to the lesson:**

> "What you just did is exactly what argument writers do every single day. A strong argument doesn't just say 'trust me.' It gives reasons, backs them up with evidence, and deals honestly with the other side. Today, you're going to learn how to build that kind of argument on paper."

---

### SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — ARGUMENT ANATOMY (12 minutes)

**Return to the five terms on the board. Build definitions collaboratively.**

Go through each term. Give the definition, then ask students for their own example using a familiar topic: *"Should the school day start later?"*

---

**① CLAIM**
> The main position or opinion you are arguing. It answers the question: *What do I believe, and what am I asking my reader to accept?*

- Weak claim: *"I think school should start later."* (states opinion but gives reader nothing to hold onto)
- Strong claim: *"Middle schools should push their start time to 8:30 a.m. because adolescents' sleep patterns make early rising harmful to their health and academic performance."*

**Ask:** *What's the difference? What does the stronger one do that the weaker one doesn't?* (It signals the reasons; it sounds authoritative; it tells the reader where the argument is going.)

---

**② REASON**
> A logical explanation for WHY your claim is true. Reasons answer: *Why should my reader believe this?*

Example: *"Teenagers' bodies are biologically wired to fall asleep later and wake up later than adults."*

**Point out:** A reason is still the writer's thinking. It needs something behind it.

---

**③ EVIDENCE**
> Facts, statistics, research, expert opinions, or real examples that PROVE your reason is true.

Example: *"According to the American Academy of Pediatrics, teens need 8–10 hours of sleep per night, and studies show that school start times before 8:30 a.m. are associated with higher rates of depression, obesity, and poor academic performance."*

**Ask:** *Why does this feel different from just having a reason alone?*

**Introduce the "So What?" rule:** After any piece of evidence, a strong writer explains how it connects back to the claim. Never drop evidence and walk away.

---

**④ COUNTERCLAIM**
> The opposing argument — what someone who disagrees would say. Acknowledging it makes you look honest and credible.

Example: *"Some argue that changing school start times creates scheduling problems for parents who need to be at work early."*

**Ask:** *Why would you bring up the other side? Doesn't that hurt your argument?*

Guide students to the insight: Ignoring the other side makes you look like you haven't thought it through. Addressing it shows confidence and intelligence.

---

**⑤ REBUTTAL**
> Your response to the counterclaim — showing why your argument is still stronger despite the objection.

Example: *"While scheduling adjustments would be necessary, the long-term academic and health benefits to students far outweigh the short-term logistical challenges for families."*

**Check for understanding — quick thumbs up/down/sideways:**
> *"Give me a thumbs up if you could explain what a counterclaim is to someone who missed class today."*

Address any confusion before moving on.

---

### SEGMENT 3: GUIDED PRACTICE — MENTOR TEXT ANALYSIS (10 minutes)

**Distribute the mentor text (see below) and three highlighters.**

**MENTOR TEXT: "Should Students Have Homework?"**

---

*Students today are busier than ever, juggling extracurriculars, family responsibilities, and social lives. For this reason, middle schools should significantly reduce the amount of homework assigned each night. Research consistently shows that excessive homework beyond one to two hours produces diminishing returns on learning and increases student anxiety. A 2006 study by Harris Cooper, a leading expert on homework, found that for middle school students, homework beyond 90 minutes per night shows no academic benefit and may actually be counterproductive.*

*Opponents of homework reduction often argue that practice at home reinforces classroom learning and builds discipline. This is a reasonable concern; repetition does strengthen memory. However, the quality of practice matters far more than the quantity. Short, focused assignments accomplish the same reinforcement goals without overwhelming students. A student who completes 20 focused minutes of math practice retains more than a student who grudgingly works through 90 minutes of busywork. Reducing homework does not mean eliminating meaningful practice — it means making every assignment count.*

*Middle schools that have piloted reduced-homework policies, such as those in the Marion County School District in Florida, reported higher student engagement, improved teacher-student relationships, and no significant drop in test scores. The evidence is clear: smarter, fewer assignments benefit students more than piling on work. Schools that prioritize student well-being will ultimately produce stronger, more motivated learners.*

---

**Color-coding instructions (display on board):**
- **Yellow:** Highlight the CLAIM
- **Blue:** Highlight any EVIDENCE (facts, statistics, research, real examples)
- **Green:** Highlight the COUNTERCLAIM and REBUTTAL

**Students work for 4 minutes independently, then compare with a partner for 2 minutes.**

**Whole-class debrief (4 minutes):**
- Call on students to share what they highlighted.
- Discuss: *"Where is the 'So What?' — where does the writer explain how the evidence connects to the claim?"*
- Ask: *"Is this argument convincing? What makes it strong? What could make it stronger?"*

---

### SEGMENT 4: COLLABORATIVE PRACTICE — EVIDENCE SORTING (8 minutes)

**Distribute the evidence sorting cards to each pair.**

**Each card contains one piece of "evidence." Students sort them into three categories:**

| STRONG EVIDENCE | WEAK EVIDENCE | NOT EVIDENCE AT ALL |
|---|---|---|
| Specific, sourced, relevant | Vague or unverifiable | Opinion, feeling, or personal anecdote presented as fact |

**Sample cards (print and cut):**

1. *"A 2022 Pew Research study found that 72% of teenagers report feeling 'consistently anxious' about academic pressure."*
2. *"Everyone knows that too much homework is bad for kids."*
3. *"My cousin had so much homework last year that she cried every night."*
4. *"Dr. Denise Pope of Stanford University found in her research that high homework loads correlate with increased rates of stress-related physical symptoms in teens."*
5. *"Some teachers give a lot of homework and some don't."*
6. *"The National Sleep Foundation recommends that teenagers get 8–10 hours of sleep per night."*
7. *"Homework is just busywork."*
8. *"In 2019, the school district of Somerville, MA reported a 30% drop in student stress after implementing a no-homework-on-weekends policy."*

**Debrief as a class:** Go through each card. Call on different pairs. Discuss disagreements — there are no trick answers, but some are debatable (especially Card 3, which can spark good discussion about the difference between anecdote and data).

**Key takeaway to write on the board:**

> *"Evidence is only as strong as how specific, sourced, and relevant it is."*

---

### SEGMENT 5: INDEPENDENT WRITING — DRAFT AN ARGUMENTATIVE PARAGRAPH (15 minutes)

**Distribute the Argument Anatomy graphic organizer.**

**ARGUMENT ANATOMY GRAPHIC ORGANIZER**

*Topic: Should middle school students be allowed to use their phones during lunch?*

| Component | Your Writing |
|---|---|
| **CLAIM** (Your position + a hint of your reasons) | |
| **REASON** (Why is your claim true?) | |
| **EVIDENCE** (What proof supports your reason?) | |
| **SO WHAT?** (How does this evidence connect back to your claim?) | |
| **COUNTERCLAIM** (What would someone who disagrees say?) | |
| **REBUTTAL** (Why is your argument still stronger?) | |
| **Closing sentence** (Restate your position with confidence) | |

**Instructions (display on board):**

> Using your graphic organizer as a road map, write a single, structured argumentative paragraph. Your paragraph must include:
> - A clear claim
> - At least one reason supported by evidence (you may use real knowledge or reasonable examples)
> - A counterclaim and rebuttal
> - A closing sentence that reinforces your position
>
> You do not have to agree with your own claim — you may argue either side.

**Teacher circulation notes:**
- Look for students who write claims without reasons (prompt: *"Tell me WHY — now write that down"*)
- Look for students who drop evidence without explaining it (prompt: *"So what does that prove? Add that sentence"*)
- Look for students who skip the counterclaim (prompt: *"What would someone who disagrees with you say right now? Write that down, then argue back"*)
- Offer sentence starters to struggling writers (see below)

**SENTENCE STARTERS FOR SUPPORT:**

> **Claim:** *"Middle school students should [or should not] be allowed to use their phones during lunch because..."*
>
> **Evidence intro:** *"According to... / Research shows... / For example, [school/place] found that..."*
>
> **So what:** *"This demonstrates that... / This matters because... / This shows that..."*
>
> **Counterclaim:** *"Some may argue that... / Opponents of this view claim that... / While it is true that..."*
>
> **Rebuttal:** *"However... / Despite this concern... / Even so, the evidence suggests..."*

---

### SEGMENT 6: SHARE OUT AND SYNTHESIS (4 minutes)

**Ask 2–3 volunteers to read their paragraphs aloud.**

After each reading, ask the class one targeted question:
- *"Did they deal with the other side? How?"*
- *"Where was the strongest piece of evidence?"*
- *"What one thing could make this argument even more convincing?"*

**Keep feedback specific and constructive — model this yourself before opening it to students.**

---

### SEGMENT 7: CLOSE — THE ARGUMENT WRITER'S PLEDGE (1 minute)

**Read aloud together (display on board):**

> *"As an argument writer, I promise to state my position clearly, back it up with real evidence, never ignore the other side, and always explain why my argument is stronger. I argue not to win — but to inform, persuade, and be taken seriously."*

**Exit ticket (students hand in before leaving):**

> On a sticky note or half-sheet, answer:
> *"What is ONE thing that makes an argument strong, and ONE mistake that makes an argument weak? Use what you learned today."*

---

## DIFFERENTIATION

**For students who need additional support:**
- Provide a partially completed graphic organizer with the claim already written
- Allow students to argue a topic with a personal connection (a rule at home, a policy at school)
- Provide a sentence frame for every component, not just selected ones
- Pair with a stronger writer during the drafting segment for verbal rehearsal before writing

**For students who need additional challenge:**
- Require two separate reasons, each with its own evidence
- Require addressing two counterclaims instead of one
- Ask: *"How would you structure this as a full multi-paragraph essay? Draft a quick outline."*
- Challenge them to find a real source they could cite (if devices are available)

**For English Language Learners:**
- Provide the mentor text with key structural terms labeled in the margins
- Allow graphic organizer responses in bullet points before converting to full sentences
- Pair with a bilingual partner during the analysis segment
- Pre-teach the five key vocabulary terms with visual anchors (e.g., a diagram of argument structure as a building: claim = foundation, reasons = walls, evidence = bricks, counterclaim = a window showing the other view, rebuttal = shutting the window)

- *What did the not-so-convincing ones do that fell flat?*
- *Did anyone mention what someone else might say — and then shoot it down?*

**Bridge to the lesson:**

> \"What you just did is exactly what argument writers do every single day. A strong argument doesn't just say 'trust me.' It gives reasons, backs them up with evidence, and deals honestly with the other side. Today, you're going to learn how to build that kind of argument on paper.\"

### SEGMENT 2: DIRECT INSTRUCTION — ARGUMENT ANATOMY (12 minutes)

**Return to the five terms on the board. Build definitions collaboratively.**

Go through each term. Give the definition, then ask students for their own example using a familiar topic: *\"Should the school day start later?\"*

---

**① CLAIM**
> The main position or opinion you are arguing. It answers the question: *What do I believe, and what am I asking my reader to accept?*

- Weak claim: *\"I think school should start later.\"* (states opinion but gives reader nothing to hold onto)
- Strong claim: *\"Middle schools should push their start time to 8:30 a.m. because adolescents' sleep patterns make early rising harmful to their health and academic performance.\"*

**Ask:** *What's the difference? What does the stronger one do that the weaker one doesn't?* (It signals the reasons; it sounds authoritative; it tells the reader where the argument is going.)

---

**② REASON**
> A logical explanation for WHY your claim is true. Reasons answer: *Why should my reader believe this?*

Example: *\"Teenagers' bodies are biologically wired to fall asleep later and wake up later than adults.\"*

**Point out:** A reason is still the writer's thinking. It needs something behind it.

---

**③ EVIDENCE**
> Facts, statistics, research, expert opinions, or real examples that PROVE your reason is true.

Example: *\"Health and sleep researchers have found that teens need 8–10 hours of sleep per night, and studies have linked school start times before 8:30 a.m. to negative health and academic outcomes.\"*

**Ask:** *Why does this feel different from just having a reason alone?*

**Introduce the \"So What?\" rule:** After any piece of evidence, a strong writer explains how it connects back to the claim. Never drop evidence and walk away.

---

**④ COUNTERCLAIM**
> The opposing argument — what someone who disagrees would say. Acknowledging it makes you look honest and credible.

Example: *\"Some argue that changing school start times creates scheduling problems for parents who need to be at work early.\"*

**Ask:** *Why would you bring up the other side? Doesn't that hurt your argument?*

Guide students to the insight: Ignoring the other side makes you look like you haven't thought it through. Addressing it shows confidence and intelligence.

---

**⑤ REBUTTAL**
> Your response to the counterclaim — showing why your argument is still stronger despite the objection.

Example: *\"While scheduling adjustments would be necessary, the long-term academic and health benefits to students far outweigh the short-term logistical challenges for families.\"*

**Check for understanding — quick thumbs up/down/sideways:**
> *\"Give me a thumbs up if you could explain what a counterclaim is to someone who missed class today.\"*

Address any confusion before moving on.

### SEGMENT 3: GUIDED PRACTICE — MENTOR TEXT ANALYSIS (10 minutes)

**Distribute the mentor text (see below) and three highlighters.**

**MENTOR TEXT: \"Should Students Have Homework?\"**

*Note to teacher: The following is a model argumentative paragraph written for instructional purposes. The arguments and any referenced findings are illustrative examples, not verified citations. Encourage students to find real sources when writing their own arguments.*

---

*Students today are busier than ever, juggling extracurriculars, family responsibilities, and social lives. For this reason, middle schools should significantly reduce the amount of homework assigned each night. Research in the field of education has raised questions about whether large amounts of homework benefit middle school students, with some findings suggesting that beyond a certain threshold, additional homework time shows little academic benefit and may increase student stress and anxiety.*

*Opponents of homework reduction often argue that practice at home reinforces classroom learning and builds discipline. This is a reasonable concern; repetition does strengthen memory. However, the quality of practice matters far more than the quantity. Short, focused assignments accomplish the same reinforcement goals without overwhelming students. A student who completes 20 focused minutes of math practice retains more than a student who grudgingly works through 90 minutes of busywork. Reducing homework does not mean eliminating meaningful practice — it means making every assignment count.*

*Some schools that have experimented with reduced-homework policies have reported benefits including higher student engagement and improved teacher-student relationships. The evidence suggests: smarter, fewer assignments may benefit students more than piling on work. Schools that prioritize student well-being will ultimately produce stronger, more motivated learners.*

---

**Color-coding instructions (display on board):**
- **Yellow:** Highlight the CLAIM
- **Blue:** Highlight any EVIDENCE (facts, statistics, research, real examples)
- **Green:** Highlight the COUNTERCLAIM and REBUTTAL

**Students work for 4 minutes independently, then compare with a partner for 2 minutes.**

**Whole-class debrief (4 minutes):**
- Call on students to share what they highlighted.
- Discuss: *\"Where is the 'So What?' — where does the writer explain how the evidence connects to the claim?\"*
- Ask: *\"Is this argument convincing? What makes it strong? What could make it stronger?\"*

### SEGMENT 4: COLLABORATIVE PRACTICE — EVIDENCE SORTING (8 minutes)

**Distribute the evidence sorting cards to each pair.**

**Each card contains one piece of \"evidence.\" Students sort them into three categories:**

| STRONG EVIDENCE | WEAK EVIDENCE | NOT EVIDENCE AT ALL |
|---|---|---|
| Specific, sourced, relevant | Vague or unverifiable | Opinion, feeling, or personal anecdote presented as fact |

**Sample cards (print and cut):**

1. *\"A national survey on teen mental health found that a large majority of teenagers report feeling consistently anxious about academic pressure.\"* *(Note: Students should discuss — is this strong? What would make it stronger? A specific, named, verifiable source with a date.)*
2. *\"Everyone knows that too much homework is bad for kids.\"*
3. *\"My cousin had so much homework last year that she cried every night.\"*
4. *\"Education researchers have found that high homework loads correlate with increased rates of stress-related physical symptoms in teens.\"* *(Note: Students should discuss — what would strengthen this? Naming the specific study and researcher.)*
5. *\"Some teachers give a lot of homework and some don't.\"*
6. *\"Health organizations recommend that teenagers get 8–10 hours of sleep per night.\"*
7. *\"Homework is just busywork.\"*
8. *\"A school district that piloted a no-homework-on-weekends policy reported a significant drop in student stress and no decline in academic performance.\"* *(Note: Students should discuss — what is missing that would make this stronger? The name of the district, the year, and a source.)*

**Debrief as a class:** Go through each card. Call on different pairs. Discuss disagreements — there are no trick answers, but some are debatable (especially Card 3, which can spark good discussion about the difference between anecdote and data). Use Cards 1, 4, and 8 to discuss what makes a citation credible: a named source, a date, and a way to verify it.

**Key takeaway to write on the board:**

> *\"Evidence is only as strong as how specific, sourced, and relevant it is.\"*
